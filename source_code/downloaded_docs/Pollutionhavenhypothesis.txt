     <p>
      The
      <b>
       pollution haven hypothesis
      </b>
      posits that, when large industrialized nations seek to set up factories or offices abroad, they will often look for the cheapest option in terms of resources and labor that offers the land and material access they require.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unmaskingthe_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Unmaskingthe-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, this often comes at the cost of environmentally sound practices.
      <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">
       Developing nations
      </a>
      with cheap resources and labor tend to have less stringent
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">
       environmental regulations
      </a>
      , and conversely, nations with stricter environmental regulations become more expensive for companies as a result of the costs associated with meeting these standards. Thus, companies that choose to physically invest in foreign countries tend to (re)locate to the countries with the lowest
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_standard" title="Environmental standard">
       environmental standards
      </a>
      or weakest enforcement.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Three_scales_of_the_hypothesis">
       Three scales of the hypothesis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollution_haven_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Three scales of the hypothesis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      1. Pollution control costs have an impact at the margins, where they exert some effect on investment decisions and trade flows.
     </p>
     <p>
      2. Pollution control costs are important enough to measurably influence trade and investment.
     </p>
     <p>
      3. Countries set their environmental standards below socially-efficient levels in order to attract investment or to promote their exports.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Four_New_Empirical_Tests_of_the_PHH_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Four_New_Empirical_Tests_of_the_PHH-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Scales 1 and 2 have empirical support, but the significance of the hypothesis relative to other investment and trade factors is still controversial. One study found that environmental regulations have a strong negative effect on a country's
      <a href="/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment" title="Foreign direct investment">
       FDI
      </a>
      , particularly in pollution-intensive industries when measured by employment. However, that same study found that the environmental regulations present in a country's neighbors have an insignificant impact on that country's trade flows.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Four_New_Empirical_Tests_of_the_PHH_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Four_New_Empirical_Tests_of_the_PHH-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Formula_and_variations">
       Formula and variations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollution_haven_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formula and variations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Yi = αRi + XiβI + εi
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      In the above formula, Y is economic activity, R is regulatory stringency, X is an aggregate of other characteristics that affect Y and ε is an error term.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unmaskingthe_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Unmaskingthe-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Theoretically, by changing your value of R, analysts will be able to calculate the expected effect on economic activity. According to the Pollution Haven Hypothesis, this equation shows that environmental regulations and economic activity are negatively correlated, because regulations raise the cost of key inputs to goods with pollution-intensive productions and reduce jurisdictions' comparative advantage in these goods. This lack of comparative advantage causes firms to move to countries with lower environmental standards, decreasing Y.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is also an expanded formula, as shown below:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Yit = vi + αRit + γTit + θRitTit + X’βit + εit
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This expanded formula takes into account whether trade liberalization (i.e. the level of trade barriers that exist in a country, labeled as T) increases the negative correlation between economic activity (Y) and regulatory stringency (R). Some authors claim that trade barriers disproportionately effect the environment, and this equation attempts to quantify the interaction between trade barriers and regulatory stringency, and the corresponding effect with respect to output in an economy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unmaskingthe_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Unmaskingthe-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       environmental Kuznets curve
      </b>
      (EKC) is a conceptual model that suggests that a country's pollution concentrations rise with development and industrialization up to a turning point, after which they fall again as the country uses its increased affluence to reduce pollution concentrations, suggesting that the cleaner environment in developed countries comes at the expense of a dirtier environment in developing countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Exploring_the_Causality_between_the_Pollution_Haven_Hypothesis_and_the_Environmental_Kuznets_Curve_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Exploring_the_Causality_between_the_Pollution_Haven_Hypothesis_and_the_Environmental_Kuznets_Curve-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this sense, the EKC is potentially a reflection of the Pollution Haven Hypothesis, because one of the factors that may drive the increase in environmental degradation seen in
      <a href="/wiki/Pre-industrial_society" title="Pre-industrial society">
       pre-industrial economies
      </a>
      is an influx of waste from
      <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_economy" title="Post-industrial economy">
       post-industrial economies
      </a>
      . This same transfer of polluting firms through trade and foreign investment could lead to the decrease in environmental degradation seen in downward-sloping section of the EKC, which models post-industrial (service) economies.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Real-world_example">
       Real-world example
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollution_haven_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Real-world example">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Spent batteries that Americans turn in to be recycled are increasingly being sent to Mexico, where the lead inside them is extracted by crude methods that are illegal in the United States. This increased export flow is a result of strict new
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">
       Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      standards on lead pollution, which make domestic recycling more difficult and expensive in the United States, but do not prohibit companies from exporting the work and danger to countries where environmental standards are low and enforcement is lax. In this sense, Mexico is becoming a pollution haven for the United States battery industry because Mexican environmental officials acknowledge that they lack the money, manpower, and technical capacity to police the flow. According to the New York Times, 20% of spent American vehicle and industrial batteries are now exported to Mexico, up from 6% in 2007, meaning that approximately 20 million batteries will cross the border this year. A significant proportion of this flow is smuggled in after being mislabeled as metal scrap.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times_Lead_Export_Article_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NY_Times_Lead_Export_Article-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_of_controversy">
       Areas of controversy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollution_haven_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Areas of controversy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The first area of controversy with respect to the Pollution Haven Theory has to do with the formulas above. Finding an appropriate measure of regulatory stringency (R) is not simple, because we want to know how much more costly production is in a given jurisdiction relative to others due to that jurisdiction's environmental regulations. The compliance costs stemming from these regulations, however, could come in the form of environmental taxes, regulatory delays, the threat (or execution) of lawsuits, product redesign, or emissions limits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unmaskingthe_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Unmaskingthe-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This proliferation of cost styles makes R hard to quantify.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another major critique of the second formula is that it is difficult to measure regulatory stringency and trade barriers because the two effects are likely endogenous, so few studies have attempted to estimate the indirect effect of trade liberalization on pollution havens. Furthermore, governments at times engage in inefficient competition to actually attract polluting industries through weakening their environmental standards. However, as per conventional economic theory, welfare-maximizing governments should set standards so that the benefits justify the costs at the margin. This does not mean that environmental standards will be equal everywhere, as jurisdictions have different assimilative capacities, costs of abatement, and social attitudes regarding the environment, meaning heterogeneity in pollution standards is to be expected.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unmaskingthe_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Unmaskingthe-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      By extension, this means that industry migration to less stringent jurisdictions may not raise efficiency concerns in an economic sense.
     </p>
     <p>
      A final area of controversy is whether the Pollution Haven Hypothesis has empirical support. For example, studies have found
      <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">
       statistically significant
      </a>
      evidence that countries with poor air quality do have higher net factor exports of coal, but the magnitude of the impact is small relative to other variables.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Krugman" title="Paul Krugman">
       Paul Krugman
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences" title="Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences">
       Nobel Prize
      </a>
      –winning economist, is skeptical as to whether pollution havens have empirical support in economic theory, as he writes, "At this point it's hard to come up with major examples of industries in which the pollution haven phenomenon, to the extent that it occurs, leads to international negative externalities. This does not, however, say that such examples cannot arise in the future."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_Economics_Theory_.26_Policy_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-International_Economics_Theory_.26_Policy-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Scale 3 above has had empirical arguments made specifically against it, especially in the last 20 years. Some economists argue that once higher environmental standards are introduced in a country, larger multinational firms present in the country are likely to push for enforcement so as to reduce the cost advantage of smaller local firms. This effect would make countries with strict environmental standards a haven for the large companies often associated with higher levels of pollution, meaning the polluting agents may be smaller companies, rather than the larger MNCs as theorized by other proponents of the Pollution Haven Hypothesis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Where_Are_The_Pollution_Havens.3F_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Where_Are_The_Pollution_Havens.3F-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
