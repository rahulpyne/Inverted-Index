   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Turboexpander
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:274px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ExpansionTurbine.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="179" data-file-width="272" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/ExpansionTurbine.png" width="272"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ExpansionTurbine.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 1: Schematic diagram of a turboexpander driving a compressor.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       turboexpander
      </b>
      , also referred to as a
      <b>
       turbo-expander
      </b>
      or an
      <b>
       expansion turbine
      </b>
      , is a centrifugal or axial flow
      <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">
       turbine
      </a>
      through which a high
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
       gas
      </a>
      is expanded to produce work that is often used to drive a
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">
       compressor
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerry_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kerry-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flynn_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Flynn-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Because work is extracted from the expanding high pressure gas, the expansion is approximated by an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isentropic" title="Isentropic">
       isentropic
      </a>
      process (i.e., a constant
      <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">
       entropy
      </a>
      process) and the low pressure exhaust gas from the turbine is at a very low
      <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
       temperature
      </a>
      , −150 °C or less depending upon the operating pressure and gas properties. Partial liquefaction of the expanded gas is not uncommon.
     </p>
     <p>
      Turboexpanders are very widely used as sources of
      <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">
       refrigeration
      </a>
      in industrial processes such as the extraction of
      <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">
       ethane
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_processing" title="Natural gas processing">
       natural gas liquids
      </a>
      (NGLs) from
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction_of_gases" title="Liquefaction of gases">
       liquefaction of gases
      </a>
      (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
       helium
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">
       argon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">
       krypton
      </a>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and other low-temperature processes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Turboexpanders currently in operation range in size from about 750
      <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
       W
      </a>
      to about 7.5 MW (1
      <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">
       hp
      </a>
      to about 10,000 hp).
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
       Applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboexpander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although turboexpanders are very commonly used in low-temperature processes, they are used in many other applications as well. This section discusses one of the low temperature processes as well as some of the other applications.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Extracting_hydrocarbon_liquids_from_natural_gas">
       Extracting hydrocarbon liquids from natural gas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboexpander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extracting hydrocarbon liquids from natural gas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:342px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Demethanizer.PNG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="364" data-file-width="340" height="364" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/69/Demethanizer.PNG" width="340"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Demethanizer.PNG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 2: A schematic diagram of a demethanizer extracting hydrocarbon liquids from natural gas.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Raw natural gas consists primarily of methane (CH
      <sub>
       4
      </sub>
      ), the shortest and lightest
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">
       hydrocarbon
      </a>
      molecule, as well as various amounts of heavier hydrocarbon gases such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">
       ethane
      </a>
      (C
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      H
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">
       propane
      </a>
      (C
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      H
      <sub>
       8
      </sub>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">
       normal butane
      </a>
      (n-C
      <sub>
       4
      </sub>
      H
      <sub>
       10
      </sub>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Isobutane" title="Isobutane">
       isobutane
      </a>
      (i-C
      <sub>
       4
      </sub>
      H
      <sub>
       10
      </sub>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Pentane" title="Pentane">
       pentanes
      </a>
      and even higher
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_weight" title="Molecular weight">
       molecular weight
      </a>
      hydrocarbons. The raw gas also contains various amounts of
      <a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">
       acid gases
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      (CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">
       hydrogen sulfide
      </a>
      (H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      S) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercaptan" title="Mercaptan">
       mercaptans
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Methanethiol" title="Methanethiol">
       methanethiol
      </a>
      (CH
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      SH) and
      <a href="/wiki/Ethanethiol" title="Ethanethiol">
       ethanethiol
      </a>
      (C
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      H
      <sub>
       5
      </sub>
      SH).
     </p>
     <p>
      When processed into finished by-products (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_processing" title="Natural gas processing">
       Natural gas processing
      </a>
      ), these heavier hydrocarbons are collectively referred to as NGL (natural gas liquids). The extraction of the NGL often involves a turboexpander
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a low-temperature
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distillation_column" title="Distillation column">
       distillation column
      </a>
      (called a
      <i>
       demethanizer
      </i>
      ) as shown in Figure 2. The inlet gas to the demethanizer is first cooled to about −51 °C in a
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchanger
      </a>
      (referred to as a
      <i>
       cold box
      </i>
      ) which partially
      <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">
       condenses
      </a>
      the inlet gas. The resultant gas-liquid mixture is then separated into a gas stream and a liquid stream.
     </p>
     <p>
      The liquid stream from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vapor-liquid_separator" title="Vapor-liquid separator">
       gas-liquid separator
      </a>
      flows through a valve and undergoes a
      <a href="/wiki/Flash_evaporation#Flash_evaporation_of_a_single-component_liquid" title="Flash evaporation">
       <i>
        throttling expansion
       </i>
      </a>
      from an absolute pressure of 62 bar to 21 bar (6.2 to 2.1 MPa), which is an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isenthalpic" title="Isenthalpic">
       isenthalpic
      </a>
      process (i.e., a constant enthalpy process) that results in lowering the temperature of the stream from about −51 °C to about −81 °C as the stream enters the demethanizer.
     </p>
     <p>
      The gas stream from the gas-liquid separator enters the turboexpander where it undergoes an
      <a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process" title="Isentropic process">
       isentropic
      </a>
      expansion from an absolute pressure of 62 bar to 21 bar (6.2 to 2.1 MPa) that lowers the gas stream temperature from about −51 °C to about −91 °C as it enters the demethanizer to serve as distillation
      <a href="/wiki/Reflux" title="Reflux">
       reflux
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Liquid from the top
      <a href="/wiki/Tray" title="Tray">
       tray
      </a>
      of the demethanizer (at about −90 °C) is routed through the cold box where it is warmed to about 0 °C as it cools the inlet gas, and is then returned to the lower section of the demethanizer. Another liquid stream from the lower section of the demethanizer (at about 2 °C) is routed through the cold box and returned to the demethanizer at about 12 °C. In effect, the inlet gas provides the
      <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">
       heat
      </a>
      required to "reboil" the bottom of the demethanizer and the turboexpander removes the heat required to provide reflux in the top of the demethanizer.
     </p>
     <p>
      The overhead gas product from the demethanizer at about −90 °C is processed natural gas that is of suitable quality for distribution to end-use consumers by
      <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">
       pipeline
      </a>
      . It is routed through the cold box where it is warmed as it cools the inlet gas. It is then compressed in the gas compressor which is driven by the turbo expander and further compressed in a second-stage gas compressor driven by an
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">
       electric motor
      </a>
      before entering the distribution pipeline.
     </p>
     <p>
      The bottom product from the demethanizer is also warmed in the cold box, as it cools the inlet gas, before it leaves the system as NGL.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Power_generation">
       Power generation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboexpander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Power generation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:224px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Expansion_turbine_power_generation.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="218" data-file-width="222" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Expansion_turbine_power_generation.png" width="222"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Expansion_turbine_power_generation.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 3: Schematic diagram of power generation system using a turboexpander.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Figure 3 depicts an electric power generation system that uses a heat source, a cooling medium (air, water or other), a circulating working fluid and a turboexpander. The system can accommodate a wide variety of heat sources such as:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Geothermal hot water
      </li>
      <li>
       Exhaust gas from internal combustion engines burning a variety of fuels (
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
        natural gas
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">
        landfill gas
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diesel_oil" title="Diesel oil">
        diesel oil
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">
        fuel oil
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       A variety of waste heat sources (in the form of either gas or liquid)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Referring to Figure 3, the circulating working fluid (usually an
      <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">
       organic compound
      </a>
      such as R-134a) is pumped to a high pressure and then vaporized in the evaporator by
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchange
      </a>
      with the available heat source. The resulting high-pressure vapor flows to the turboexpander where it undergoes an isentropic expansion and exits as a vapor-liquid mixture which is then condensed into a liquid by heat exchange with the available cooling medium. The condensed liquid is pumped back to the evaporator to complete the cycle.
     </p>
     <p>
      The system in Figure 3 is a
      <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">
       Rankine cycle
      </a>
      as is used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil-fuel power plant">
       fossil-fuel power plants
      </a>
      where water is the working fluid and the heat source is derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      of natural gas,
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">
       fuel oil
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      used to generate high-pressure steam. The high-pressure steam then undergoes an isentropic expansion in a conventional
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">
       steam turbine
      </a>
      . The steam turbine exhaust steam is next condensed into liquid water which is then pumped back to steam generator to complete the cycle.
     </p>
     <p>
      When an organic working fluid such as R-134a is used in the Rankine cycle, the cycle is sometimes referred to as an
      <a href="/wiki/Organic_Rankine_cycle" title="Organic Rankine cycle">
       Organic Rankine cycle
      </a>
      (ORC).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigeration_system">
       Refrigeration system
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboexpander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Refrigeration system">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Expansion_turbine%2Bcompressor_refrigeration_system.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="241" data-file-width="256" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/Expansion_turbine%2Bcompressor_refrigeration_system.png" width="256"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Expansion_turbine%2Bcompressor_refrigeration_system.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 4: Schematic diagram of a refrigeration system using a turboexpander, compressor and a motor.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Figure 4 depicts a refrigeration system with a capacity of about 100 to 1000
      <a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration#Miscellany" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">
       tons of refrigeration
      </a>
      (i.e., 350 to 3,500 kW). The system utilizes a compressor, a turboexpander and an electric motor.
     </p>
     <p>
      Depending on the operating conditions, the turboexpander reduces the load on the electric motor by some 6 to 15% as compared to a conventional
      <a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">
       vapor-compression refrigeration
      </a>
      system that uses a
      <a href="/wiki/Flash_evaporation#Flash_evaporation_of_a_single-component_liquid" title="Flash evaporation">
       <i>
        throttling expansion
       </i>
      </a>
      valve rather than a turboexpander.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brasz_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brasz-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Basically, this can be seen as a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Turbo-compound_engine" title="Turbo-compound engine">
       turbo compounding
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The system employs a high-pressure refrigerant (i.e., one with a low
      <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point#Relation_between_the_normal_boiling_point_and_the_vapor_pressure_of_liquids" title="Boiling point">
       normal boiling point
      </a>
      ) such as:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brasz_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brasz-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Chlorodifluoromethane" title="Chlorodifluoromethane">
        Chlorodifluoromethane
       </a>
       (CHClF
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       ) known as R-22, with a normal boiling point of −47 °C
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/1,1,1,2-Tetrafluoroethane" title="1,1,1,2-Tetrafluoroethane">
        1,1,1,2-Tetrafluoroethane
       </a>
       (C
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       F
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       ) known as R-134a, with a normal boiling point of −26 °C.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      As shown in Figure 4, refrigerant vapor is compressed to a higher pressure resulting in a higher temperature as well. The hot, compressed vapor is then
      <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">
       condensed
      </a>
      into a liquid. The
      <a href="/wiki/Condenser_(heat_transfer)" title="Condenser (heat transfer)">
       condenser
      </a>
      is where heat is expelled from the circulating refrigerant and is carried away by whatever cooling medium is used in the condenser (air, water, etc.).
     </p>
     <p>
      The refrigerant liquid flows through the turboexpander where it is vaporized and the vapor undergoes an isentropic expansion which results in a low-temperature mixture of vapor and liquid. The vapor-liquid mixture is then routed through the evaporator where it is vaporized by heat absorbed from the space being cooled. The vaporized refrigerant flows to the compressor inlet to complete the cycle.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Power_recovery_in_fluid_catalytic_cracker">
       Power recovery in fluid catalytic cracker
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboexpander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Power recovery in fluid catalytic cracker">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fluid_catalytic_cracker_power_recovery.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="218" data-file-width="225" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/Fluid_catalytic_cracker_power_recovery.png" width="225"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fluid_catalytic_cracker_power_recovery.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 5: A schematic diagram of the power recovery system in a fluid catalytic cracking unit.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gas
      </a>
      from the catalyst regenerator of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_catalytic_cracker" title="Fluid catalytic cracker">
       fluid catalytic cracker
      </a>
      is at a temperature of about 715 °C and at a pressure of about 2.4
      <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)#Absolute_pressure_and_gauge_pressure" title="Bar (unit)">
       barg
      </a>
      (240 kPa gauge). Its gaseous components are mostly
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      (CO),
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      (CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      (N
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ). Although the flue gas has been through two stages of
      <a href="/wiki/Cyclonic_separation" title="Cyclonic separation">
       cyclones
      </a>
      (located within the regenerator) to remove entrained catalyst fines, it still contains some residual catalyst fines.
     </p>
     <p>
      Figure 5 depicts how power is recovered and utilized by routing the regenerator flue gas through a turboexpander. After the flue gas exits the regenerator, it is routed through a secondary catalyst separator containing
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Swirl_tubes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Swirl tubes (page does not exist)">
        swirl tubes
       </a>
      </i>
      designed to remove 70 to 90 percent of the residual catalyst fines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is required to prevent erosion damage to the turboexpander.
     </p>
     <p>
      As shown in Figure 5, expansion of the flue gas through a turboexpander provides sufficient power to drive the regenerator's combustion air compressor. The electrical
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor-generator" title="Motor-generator">
       motor-generator
      </a>
      in the power recovery system can consume or produce electrical power. If the expansion of the flue gas does not provide enough power to drive the air compressor, the electric motor-generator provides the needed additional power. If the flue gas expansion provides more power than needed to drive the air compressor, than the electric motor-generator converts the excess power into electric power and exports it to the refinery's electrical system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reza_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reza-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">
       steam turbine
      </a>
      shown in Figure 5 is used to drive the regenerator's combustion air compressor during start-ups of the fluid catalytic cracker until there is sufficient combustion flue gas to take over that task.
     </p>
     <p>
      The expanded flue gas is then routed through a steam-generating
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">
       boiler
      </a>
      (referred to as a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Condensing_boiler" title="Condensing boiler">
        CO boiler
       </a>
      </i>
      ) where the carbon monoxide in the flue gas is burned as fuel to provide steam for use in the refinery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reza_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reza-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The flue gas from the CO boiler is processed through an
      <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitator" title="Electrostatic precipitator">
       electrostatic precipitator
      </a>
      (ESP) to remove residual
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">
       particulate matter
      </a>
      . The ESP removes particulates in the size range of 2 to 20
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrometer_(unit)" title="Micrometer (unit)">
       micrometers
      </a>
      from the flue gas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reza_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reza-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboexpander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The possible use of an expansion machine for isentropically creating low temperatures was suggested by
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Siemens" title="Carl Wilhelm Siemens">
       Carl Wilhelm Siemens
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Siemens_cycle" title="Siemens cycle">
       Siemens cycle
      </a>
      ), a
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       German
      </a>
      engineer in 1857. About three decades later, in 1885, Ernest Solvay of
      <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
       Belgium
      </a>
      attempted to use a reciprocating expander machine but could not attain any temperatures lower than −98 °C because of problems with lubrication of the machine at such temperatures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerry_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kerry-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1902,
      <a href="/wiki/Georges_Claude" title="Georges Claude">
       Georges Claude
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       French
      </a>
      engineer, successfully used a reciprocating expansion machine to liquefy air. He used a degreased, burnt leather packing as a piston seal without any lubrication. With an air pressure of only 40 bar (4 MPa), Claude achieved an almost isentropic expansion resulting in a lower temperature than had before been possible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerry_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kerry-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first turboexpanders seem to have been designed in about 1934 or 1935 by Guido Zerkowitz, an Italian engineer working for the German firm of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linde_AG" title="Linde AG">
       Linde AG
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Almqvist_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Almqvist-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1939, the
      <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">
       Russian
      </a>
      physicist
      <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Kapitsa" title="Pyotr Kapitsa">
       Pyotr Kapitsa
      </a>
      perfected the design of centrifugal turboexpanders. His first practical prototype was made of
      <a href="/wiki/Monel" title="Monel">
       Monel
      </a>
      metal, had an outside diameter of only 8 cm (3.1 in), operated at 40,000 revolutions per minute and expanded 1,000 cubic metres of air per hour. It used a water pump as a brake and had an efficiency of 79 to 83 percent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerry_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kerry-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Almqvist_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Almqvist-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most turboexpanders in industrial use since then have been based on Kapitsa's design and centrifugal turboexpanders have taken over almost 100 percent of the industrial gas liquefaction and low temperature process requirements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerry_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kerry-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Almqvist_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Almqvist-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The availability of liquid
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      revolutionized the production of steel using the
      <a href="/wiki/Basic_oxygen_steelmaking" title="Basic oxygen steelmaking">
       basic oxygen steelmaking
      </a>
      process.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1978, Pyotr Kapitsa was awarded a Nobel physics prize for his body of work in the area of low-temperature physics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1983, San Diego Gas and Electric was among the first to install a turboexpander in a natural gas letdown station for
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery" title="Energy recovery">
       energy recovery
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
       Types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboexpander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Types">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Turboexpanders can be classified by loading device or bearings.
     </p>
     <p>
      Three main loading devices used in turboexpanders are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrifugal_compressors" title="Centrifugal compressors">
       centrifugal compressors
      </a>
      , electrical generators or hydraulic brakes. With centrifugal compressors and electrical generators the shaft power from the turboexpander is recouped either to recompress the process gas or to generate electrical energy lowering utility bills.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hydraulic brakes are used when the turboexpander is very small and harvesting the shaft power is not economically justifiable.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bearings used are either oil bearings or
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_bearing" title="Magnetic bearing">
       magnetic bearings
      </a>
      .
     </p>
