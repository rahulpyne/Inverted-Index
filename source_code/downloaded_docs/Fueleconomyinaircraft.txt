     <p>
      Aircraft must consume fuel to supply the energy needed to move the vehicles and their passengers.
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">
       Fuel economy
      </a>
      is a measure of how much fuel an aircraft needs to operate, and can be expressed in several ways, for example by the liters of fuel consumed per passenger per kilometer. Aerodynamic
      <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">
       drag
      </a>
      , which exerts a force on the aircraft in the opposite direction from the velocity, is a principal determinant of energy consumption in aircraft because they operate at such high speeds.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Factors_in_aircraft_fuel_economy">
       Factors in aircraft fuel economy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Factors in aircraft fuel economy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Each model of aircraft has a maximum range speed for a given total load (fuel plus payload), which is the speed at which it is most fuel efficient.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skybrary_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-skybrary-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Flying slower or faster than this optimum speed increases fuel consumption per mile flown. There is an optimum speed for efficiency because the component of drag resulting from airframe skin friction against the air increases at a square function of air speed, but the drag resulting from generating lift decreases with air speed. (These are technically called parasitic drag and induced drag, respectively.)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skybrary_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-skybrary-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The desirability of a low maximum range speed to reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">
       environmental and climate impacts
      </a>
      is at odds in aircraft design with the benefit from the revenue generated by making the design speed higher thereby increasing the passenger miles flown per day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peeters-2005_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peeters-2005-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Aircraft weight is also a factor in fuel economy, because more lift-generating drag (induced drag) results as weight increases. If the airframe weight is reduced, engines that are smaller and lighter can be used, and for a given range the fuel capacity can be reduced. Thus some weight savings can be compounded for an increase in fuel efficiency. A rule-of-thumb being that a 1% weight reduction corresponds to around a 0.75% reduction in fuel consumption.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Capehart_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Capehart-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Flight altitude affects engine efficiency. Jet-engine efficiency increases at altitude up to the tropopause, the temperature minimum of the atmosphere; at lower temperatures, the engine efficiency is higher.[1] Jet engine efficiency is also increased at high speeds, but above about Mach 0.85 the aerodynamic drag on the airframe overwhelms this effect.
     </p>
     <p>
      Above that speed, shockwaves begin to form that greatly increase drag. For supersonic flight (Mach 1.0 or higher), fuel consumption is increased tremendously.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_commercial_aircraft_fuel_economy_since_the_1950s">
       Changes in commercial aircraft fuel economy since the 1950s
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Changes in commercial aircraft fuel economy since the 1950s">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although modern jet aircraft have twice the fuel efficiency of the earliest
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_airliner" title="Jet airliner">
       jet airliners
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      they are only slightly more fuel efficient than the latest piston engine airliners of the late 1950s such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-1649_Starliner" title="Lockheed L-1649 Starliner">
       Lockheed L-1649 Starliner
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-7" title="Douglas DC-7">
       Douglas DC-7
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peeters-2005_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peeters-2005-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nonetheless, jets have about twice the cruise speed. The early jet airliners were designed at a time when air crew labor costs were higher relative to fuel costs than today. Despite the high fuel consumption, because fuel was inexpensive in that era the higher speed resulted in favorable economical returns since crew costs and amortization of capital investment in the aircraft could be spread over more seat miles flown per day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Today's turboprop airliners have better fuel efficiency than current jet airliners, in part because of their lower cruising speed and
      <a href="/wiki/Propeller_(aeronautics)" title="Propeller (aeronautics)">
       propellers
      </a>
      that are more efficient than those of the 1950s-era piston-powered airlines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICCT-Kwan_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ICCT-Kwan-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among major airlines, those with turboprop equipped regional carrier subsidiaries typically rank high in overall fleet fuel efficiency. For example, although
      <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Airlines" title="Alaska Airlines">
       Alaska Airlines
      </a>
      scored at the top of a 2011-2012 fuel efficiency ranking, if its regional carrier—turbo-prop equipped
      <a href="/wiki/Horizon_Air" title="Horizon Air">
       Horizon Air
      </a>
      —were dropped from the consideration, the airline's ranking would be lower.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICCT-Kwan_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ICCT-Kwan-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_aircraft_efficiency">
       Jet aircraft efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jet aircraft efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Jet aircraft efficiency is improving: Between 1960 and 2000 there was a 55% overall fuel efficiency gain (if one were to consider the
      <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">
       Boeing 707
      </a>
      as the base case).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peeters-2005_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peeters-2005-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most of the improvements in efficiency were gained in the first decade when jet craft first came into widespread commercial use. Between 1971 and 1998 the fleet-average annual improvement per available seat-kilometre was estimated at 2.4%.
      <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">
       Concorde
      </a>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_transport" title="Supersonic transport">
       supersonic transport
      </a>
      managed about 17 passenger-miles to the Imperial gallon, which is 16.7 L/100 km per passenger; similar to a business jet, but much worse than a subsonic turbofan aircraft.
      <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">
       Airbus
      </a>
      states a fuel rate consumption of their
      <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">
       A380
      </a>
      at less than 3 L/100 km per passenger (78 passenger-miles per US gallon),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      however this is for an unspecified number and configuration of seats. The maximum configuration is 850 economy passengers, but with a typical 525-seat multi-class configuration the fuel consumption is "comparable to that of a B747-400 ER and even about 15% worse than a B777-300ER on a passenger-mile basis."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Weight_effect">
       Weight effect
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Weight effect">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      As over 80% of the fully laden take-off weight of a modern aircraft such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">
       Airbus A380
      </a>
      is craft and fuel,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      there remains considerable room for future improvements in fuel efficiency. The weight of an aircraft can be reduced by using light-weight materials such as
      <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">
       titanium
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">
       carbon fiber
      </a>
      and other composite plastics. Expensive materials may be used, if the reduction of mass justifies the price of materials through improved fuel efficiency. The improvements achieved in fuel efficiency by mass reduction, reduce the amount of fuel an aircraft must carry. This further reduces the mass of the aircraft, and therefore provides further gains in fuel efficiency. For example, the Airbus A380 design includes multiple light-weight materials. The
      <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">
       Boeing 787 Dreamliner
      </a>
      was the first major commercial airplane to have a composite fuselage, composite wings, and use composites in most other airframe components.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Composites_flying_prt1_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Composites_flying_prt1-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Very long non-stop flights suffer from the weight penalty of the large quantity of fuel required, limiting the number of available seats to compensate. For such flights, the critical fiscal factor is the quantity of fuel burnt per seat-nautical mile.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      For these reasons the world's longest commercial flights were cancelled circa 2013. An example is Singapore Air's former New York to Singapore flight, which could carry only 100 passengers (all business class) on the 10,300 mile flight. According to an industry analyst, "It [was] pretty much a fuel tanker in the air."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynamics">
       Aerodynamics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aerodynamics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Lift-to-drag_ratio" title="Lift-to-drag ratio">
       Lift-to-drag ratio
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Airbus has showcased
      <a href="/wiki/Wingtip_device" title="Wingtip device">
       wingtip devices
      </a>
      (sharklets or winglets) that can achieve 3.5 percent reduction in fuel consumption.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are wingtip devices on the Airbus A380. Further developed Minix winglets have been said to offer 6 percent reduction in fuel consumption.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gizmag1_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gizmag1-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Winglets at the tip of an aircraft wing, can be retrofitted to any airplane, and smooths out the wing-tip vortex, reducing the aircraft's wing drag.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gizmag1_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gizmag1-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      NASA and Boeing are conducting tests on a 500 lb (230 kg) "
      <a href="/wiki/Blended_wing_body" title="Blended wing body">
       blended wing
      </a>
      " aircraft. This design provides greater fuel efficiency, since the whole craft produces lift, not just the wings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The blended wing body (BWB) concept offers advantages in structural, aerodynamic and operating efficiencies over today's more conventional fuselage-and-wing designs. These features translate into greater range, fuel economy, reliability and life cycle savings, as well as lower manufacturing costs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      NASA has created a cruise efficient STOL (CESTOL) concept.
     </p>
     <p>
      Fraunhofer Institute for Manufacturing Engineering and Applied Materials Research (IFAM) have researched a
      <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">
       shark
      </a>
      skin imitating paint that would reduce drag through a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Riblet_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Riblet effect (page does not exist)">
       riblet effect
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Aircraft is a major potential application for new technologies such as aluminium
      <a href="/wiki/Metal_foam" title="Metal foam">
       metal foam
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">
       nanotechnology
      </a>
      such as the shark skin imitating paint.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_consumption_factors">
       Fuel consumption factors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fuel consumption factors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      To save fuel, Simon Weselby presented the following measures while flying, in his example of an A330 flying 4,600 kilometres (2,900 mi):
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weselby2012_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-weselby2012-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       direct routing: 40 km (25 mi) less distance saves 190 kg (420 lb) fuel
      </li>
      <li>
       vertical flight profile optimization: fly 600 m (2,000 ft) below optimum altitude, 600 kg (1,300 lb) more fuel consumed
      </li>
      <li>
       cruising speed: 0.01 mach above optimum, 800 kg (1,800 lb) more fuel consumed
      </li>
      <li>
       aircraft weight: 1,000 kg (2,200 lb) more fuel on board, 150 kg (330 lb) more fuel consumed. 100 litres (22 imp gal; 26 US gal) of unused potable water, 15 kg (33 lb) more fuel consumed.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Also operational procedures can save fuel:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weselby2012_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-weselby2012-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       10 minutes less
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_power_unit" title="Auxiliary power unit">
        APU
       </a>
       use, 35 kg (77 lb) fuel saved
      </li>
      <li>
       reduced flap approach, 15 kg (33 lb) fuel saved
      </li>
      <li>
       reduced thrust reversal, 30 kg (66 lb) fuel saved
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Maintenance saves fuel as well:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weselby2012_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-weselby2012-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       no engine wash schedule: 100 kg (220 lb) more fuel consumed
      </li>
      <li>
       slat rigging, 5 mm (0.20 in) gap, 50 kg (110 lb) more fuel consumed
      </li>
      <li>
       spoiler rigging, 10 mm (0.39 in) gap, 40 kg (88 lb) more fuel consumed
      </li>
      <li>
       damaged door seal, 15 kg (33 lb) more fuel consumed
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Propellers_versus_jets">
       Propellers versus jets
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Propellers versus jets">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Propfan" title="Propfan">
       Propfans
      </a>
      are a more fuel efficient technology than
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">
       jets
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">
       turboprops
      </a>
      , but turboprops have an optimum speed below about 450 mph (700 km/h).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This speed is less than used with jets by major airlines today. However, the decrease in speed reduces drag. With the current
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Precise_language" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The text near this tag is dated.">
          needs update
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      high price for
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">
       jet fuel
      </a>
      and the emphasis on engine/airframe efficiency to reduce emissions, there is renewed interest in the propfan concept for jetliners that might come into service beyond the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_787" title="Boeing 787">
       Boeing 787
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A350" title="Airbus A350">
       Airbus A350
      </a>
      XWB. For instance, Airbus has patented aircraft designs with twin rear-mounted counter-rotating propfans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      NASA has conducted an Advanced Turboprop Project (ATP), where they researched a variable pitch propfan that produced less noise and achieved high speeds.
     </p>
     <p>
      For private aircraft in
      <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">
       general aviation
      </a>
      , the current record is 37 km/kg fuel or 3.7 L/100 km in a
      <a href="/wiki/Monnett_Sonerai" title="Monnett Sonerai">
       Monnett Sonerai
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Related to fuel efficiency is the
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">
       impact of aviation emissions on climate
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Range">
       Range
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Range">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For very
      <a href="/wiki/Flight_length#long_haul" title="Flight length">
       long haul flights
      </a>
      , the airplane is heavier to transport additional fuel, leading to a higher fuel consumption. Above a certain distance, it becomes more fuel efficient to make a halfway stop to refuel even if it leads to an additional less efficient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descent_(aircraft)" title="Descent (aircraft)">
       descent
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Climb_(aeronautics)" title="Climb (aeronautics)">
       climb
      </a>
      . For example, for a
      <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777" title="Boeing 777">
       Boeing 777
      </a>
      -300 the tipping point is at 3,000 nautical miles (5,600 km) : it is more fuel efficient to make a
      <a href="/wiki/Non-stop_flight" title="Non-stop flight">
       non-stop flight
      </a>
      below this distance and to make a stop above.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Airline_fuel_efficiency">
       Airline fuel efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_economy_in_aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Airline fuel efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Fuel economy in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_transport" title="Air transport">
       air transport
      </a>
      comes from aircraft fuel efficiency combined with
      <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">
       airlines
      </a>
      efficiency :
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_seat_map" title="Aircraft seat map">
       seating configuration
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Passenger_load_factor" title="Passenger load factor">
       passenger load factor
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Air_cargo" title="Air cargo">
       air cargo
      </a>
      . For instance, over the
      <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight" title="Transatlantic flight">
       transatlantic
      </a>
      route, the most active intercontinental market, in 2014 the average fuel consumption was 32 pax-km per L - 3.13 litres per 100 kilometres (75 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -US
       </small>
      </sub>
      ) per passenger. The most fuel efficient airline was
      <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Air_Shuttle" title="Norwegian Air Shuttle">
       Norwegian Air Shuttle
      </a>
      with 40 pax-km/L - 2.5 litres per 100 kilometres (94 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -US
       </small>
      </sub>
      ) per passenger, thanks to its fuel efficient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_787" title="Boeing 787">
       Boeing 787
      </a>
      -8, a high 86% passenger load factor and a high density of 1.18 seat/m² due to a low 11% premium seating. On the other side, the least efficient was
      <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">
       British Airways
      </a>
      at 27 pax-km/L - 3.7 litres per 100 kilometres (64 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -US
       </small>
      </sub>
      ) per passenger, using fuel inefficient
      <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747-400" title="Boeing 747-400">
       Boeing 747-400s
      </a>
      with a low density of 0.79 seat/m² due to a high 24% premium seating, in spite of a high 83% load factor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICCT1511_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ICCT1511-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Research projects such as Boeing's
      <a href="/wiki/EcoDemonstrator" title="EcoDemonstrator">
       ecoDemonstrator
      </a>
      program have sought to identify ways of improving the fuel economy of commercial aircraft operations. The U.S. government has encouraged such research through grant programs, including the FAA's Continuous Lower Energy, Emissions and Noise (CLEEN) program, and NASA's Environmentally Responsible Aviation (ERA) Project.

       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
