     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
       geology
      </a>
      , the
      <b>
       crust
      </b>
      is the outermost solid shell of a rocky
      <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
       planet
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
       natural satellite
      </a>
      , which is chemically distinct from the underlying
      <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
       mantle
      </a>
      . The crusts of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_(planet)" title="Earth (planet)">
       Earth
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       Moon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
       Mercury
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
       Venus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
       Mars
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
       Io
      </a>
      , and other planetary bodies have been generated largely by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous" title="Igneous">
       igneous
      </a>
      processes, and these crusts are richer in
      <a href="/wiki/Incompatible_element" title="Incompatible element">
       incompatible elements
      </a>
      than their respective mantles.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_crust">
       Earth's crust
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_poster.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2200" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/350px-Earth_poster.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/525px-Earth_poster.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/700px-Earth_poster.svg.png 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_poster.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The internal structure of Earth
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The crust of the
      <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
       Earth
      </a>
      is composed of a great variety of
      <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">
       igneous
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rocks" title="Metamorphic rocks">
       metamorphic
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">
       sedimentary rocks
      </a>
      . The crust is underlain by the mantle. The upper part of the mantle is composed mostly of
      <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">
       peridotite
      </a>
      , a rock denser than rocks common in the overlying crust. The boundary between the crust and mantle is conventionally placed at the
      <a href="/wiki/Mohorovi%C4%8Di%C4%87_discontinuity" title="Mohorovičić discontinuity">
       Mohorovičić discontinuity
      </a>
      , a boundary defined by a contrast in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic" title="Seismic">
       seismic
      </a>
      velocity. The crust occupies less than 1% of Earth's volume.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">
       oceanic crust
      </a>
      of the sheet is different from its
      <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">
       continental crust
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The oceanic crust is 5 km (3 mi) to 10 km (6 mi) thick
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and is composed primarily of
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Diabase" title="Diabase">
        diabase
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">
        gabbro
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The continental crust is typically from 30 km (20 mi) to 50 km (30 mi) thick and is mostly composed of slightly less dense rocks than those of the oceanic crust. Some of these less dense rocks, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">
        granite
       </a>
       , are common in the continental crust but rare to absent in the oceanic crust.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Both the continental and oceanic crust
      <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
       "float" on the mantle
      </a>
      . Because the continental crust is thicker, it extends both to greater elevations and greater depth than the oceanic crust. The slightly lower density of felsic continental rock compared to basaltic oceanic rock contributes to the higher relative elevation of the top of the continental crust. As the top of the continental crust reaches elevations higher than that of the oceanic, water runs off the continents and collects above the oceanic crust. Because of the change in velocity of
      <a href="/wiki/Seismic_wave" title="Seismic wave">
       seismic waves
      </a>
      it is believed that beneath continents at a certain depth continental crust (
      <a href="/wiki/Sial" title="Sial">
       sial
      </a>
      ) becomes close in its physical properties to oceanic crust (
      <a href="/wiki/Sima_(geology)" title="Sima (geology)">
       sima
      </a>
      ), and the transition zone is referred to as the
      <a href="/wiki/Conrad_discontinuity" title="Conrad discontinuity">
       Conrad discontinuity
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The temperature of the crust increases with depth, reaching values typically in the range from about 200 °C (392 °F) to 400 °C (752 °F) at the boundary with the underlying mantle. The crust and underlying relatively rigid uppermost mantle make up the
      <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">
       lithosphere
      </a>
      . Because of
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      in the underlying
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_deformation" title="Plastic deformation">
       plastic
      </a>
      (although non-molten) upper mantle and
      <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">
       asthenosphere
      </a>
      , the lithosphere is broken into
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">
       tectonic plates
      </a>
      that move. The temperature increases by as much as 30 °C (about 50 °F) for every kilometer locally in the upper part of the crust, but the
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">
       geothermal gradient
      </a>
      is smaller in deeper crust.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plates_tect2_en.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2844" data-file-width="4167" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/350px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/525px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/700px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plates_tect2_en.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Plates in the crust of Earth
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Partly by analogy to what is known about the Moon, Earth is considered to have differentiated from an aggregate of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetesimals" title="Planetesimals">
       planetesimals
      </a>
      into its core, mantle and crust within about 100 million years of the formation of the planet, 4.6 billion years ago. The
      <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/primordial" title="wikt:primordial">
       primordial
      </a>
      crust was very thin and was probably recycled by much more vigorous
      <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
       plate tectonics
      </a>
      and destroyed by significant
      <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
       asteroid
      </a>
      impacts, which were much more common in the early stages of the solar system.
     </p>
     <p>
      Earth has probably always had some form of basaltic crust, but the age of the oldest oceanic crust today is only about 200 million years. In contrast, the bulk of the continental crust is much older. The oldest continental crustal rocks on Earth have ages in the range from about 3.7 to 4.28 billion years
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and have been found in the
      <a href="/wiki/Narryer_Gneiss_Terrane" title="Narryer Gneiss Terrane">
       Narryer Gneiss Terrane
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">
       Western Australia
      </a>
      , in the
      <a href="/wiki/Acasta_Gneiss" title="Acasta Gneiss">
       Acasta Gneiss
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">
       Northwest Territories
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Shield" title="Canadian Shield">
       Canadian Shield
      </a>
      , and on other cratonic regions such as those on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fennoscandian_Shield" title="Fennoscandian Shield">
       Fennoscandian Shield
      </a>
      . Some
      <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">
       zircon
      </a>
      with age as great as 4.3 billion years has been found in the
      <a href="/wiki/Narryer_Gneiss_Terrane" title="Narryer Gneiss Terrane">
       Narryer Gneiss Terrane
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The average age of the current Earth's continental crust has been estimated to be about 2.0 billion years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most crustal rocks formed before 2.5 billion years ago are located in
      <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">
       cratons
      </a>
      . Such old continental crust and the underlying mantle asthenosphere are less dense than elsewhere in Earth and so are not readily destroyed by
      <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">
       subduction
      </a>
      . Formation of new continental crust is linked to periods of intense
      <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">
       orogeny
      </a>
      ; these periods coincide with the formation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">
       supercontinents
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">
       Rodinia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">
       Pangaea
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">
       Gondwana
      </a>
      . The crust forms in part by aggregation of
      <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">
       island arcs
      </a>
      including granite and metamorphic fold belts, and it is preserved in part by depletion of the underlying mantle to form buoyant lithospheric mantle.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Moon.27s_crust">
       Moon's crust
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">
       Geology of the Moon
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A theoretical
      <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">
       protoplanet
      </a>
      named "
      <a href="/wiki/Theia_(planet)" title="Theia (planet)">
       Theia
      </a>
      " is thought to have collided with the forming Earth, and part of the material ejected into space by the collision accreted to form the Moon. As the Moon formed, the outer part of it is thought to have been molten, a “
      <a href="/wiki/Lunar_magma_ocean" title="Lunar magma ocean">
       lunar magma ocean
      </a>
      .”
      <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">
       Plagioclase
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">
       feldspar
      </a>
      crystallized in large amounts from this
      <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">
       magma
      </a>
      ocean and floated toward the surface. The
      <a href="/wiki/Cumulate_rock" title="Cumulate rock">
       cumulate rocks
      </a>
      form much of the crust. The upper part of the crust probably averages about 88% plagioclase (near the lower limit of 90% defined for
      <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
       anorthosite
      </a>
      ): the lower part of the crust may contain a higher percentage of ferromagnesian minerals such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">
       pyroxenes
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
       olivine
      </a>
      , but even that lower part probably averages about 78% plagioclase.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The underlying mantle is denser and olivine-rich.
     </p>
     <p>
      The thickness of the crust ranges between about 20 and 120 km. Crust on the
      <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
       far side of the Moon
      </a>
      averages about 12 km thicker than that on the
      <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">
       near side
      </a>
      . Estimates of average thickness fall in the range from about 50 to 60 km. Most of this plagioclase-rich crust formed shortly after formation of the moon, between about 4.5 and 4.3 billion years ago. Perhaps 10% or less of the crust consists of igneous rock added after the formation of the initial plagioclase-rich material. The best-characterized and most voluminous of these later additions are the mare
      <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
       basalts
      </a>
      formed between about 3.9 and 3.2 billion years ago. Minor volcanism continued after 3.2 billion years, perhaps as recently as 1 billion years ago. There is no evidence of
      <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
       plate tectonics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Study of the Moon has established that a crust can form on a rocky planetary body significantly smaller than Earth. Although the radius of the Moon is only about a quarter that of Earth, the lunar crust has a significantly greater average thickness. This thick crust formed almost immediately after formation of the Moon. Magmatism continued after the period of intense meteorite impacts ended about 3.9 billion years ago, but igneous rocks younger than 3.9 billion years make up only a minor part of the crust.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
