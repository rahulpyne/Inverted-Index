   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Landfill gas
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Landfill gas
      </b>
      is a complex mix of different gases created by the action of
      <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">
       microorganisms
      </a>
      within a
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">
       landfill
      </a>
      . Landfill gas is approximately forty to sixty percent
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      , with the remainder being mostly
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      . Trace amounts of other volatile organic compounds comprise the remainder (&lt;1%). These trace gases include a large array of species, mainly simple
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">
       hydrocarbons
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production_of_landfill_gases">
       Production of landfill gases
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfill_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production of landfill gases">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Landfill gases are the result of three processes:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       evaporation of volatile organic compounds (e.g., solvents)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">
        chemical reactions
       </a>
       between waste components
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">
        microbial action
       </a>
       , especially
       <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">
        methanogenesis
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The first two depend strongly on the nature of the waste. The dominant process in most landfills is the third process whereby
      <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">
       anaerobic bacteria
      </a>
      decompose organic waste to produce biogas, which consists of methane and carbon dioxide together with traces of other compounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EIA_Landfill_Gas_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EIA_Landfill_Gas-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite the heterogeneity of waste, the evolution of gases follows well defined
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">
       kinetic pattern
      </a>
      . Formation of methane and CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      commences about six months after depositing the landfill material. The evolution of gas reaches a maximum at about 20 years, then declines over the course of decades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Variation_with_landfill_content">
       Variation with landfill content
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfill_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variation with landfill content">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The nature of the material in a landfill affects the landfill gas at the trace levels. For example, gases from municipal landfills contain traces of
      <a href="/wiki/Octamethylcyclotetrasiloxane" title="Octamethylcyclotetrasiloxane">
       octamethylcyclotetrasiloxane
      </a>
      , since such
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organosilicon_compound" title="Organosilicon compound">
       organosilicon compounds
      </a>
      are used in personal care products.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring">
       Monitoring
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfill_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Monitoring">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas_monitoring" title="Landfill gas monitoring">
       Landfill gas monitoring
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Because gases produced by landfills are both valuable and sometimes hazardous, monitoring techniques have been developed. Flame ionization detectors can be used to measure methane levels as well as total VOC levels. Surface monitoring and sub-surface monitoring as well as monitoring of the ambient air is carried out. In the U.S., under the Clean Air Act of 1996, it is required that many large landfills install gas collection and control systems, which means that at the very least the facilities must collect and flare the gas.
     </p>
     <p>
      U.S. Federal regulations under Subtitle D of RCRA formed in October 1979 regulate the siting, design, construction, operation, monitoring, and closure of MSW landfills. Subtitle D now requires controls on the migration of methane in landfill gas. Monitoring requirements must be met at landfills during their operation, and for an additional 30 years after. The landfills affected by Subtitle D of RCRA are required to control gas by establishing a way to check for methane emissions periodically and therefore prevent off-site migration. Landfill owners and operators must make sure the concentration of methane gas does not exceed 25% of the LEL for methane in the facilities' structures and the LEL for methane at the facility boundary.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Landfill_gas_use">
       Landfill gas use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfill_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Landfill gas use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas_utilization" title="Landfill gas utilization">
       Landfill gas utilization
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Landfill_gas_collection_system.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="226" data-file-width="375" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Landfill_gas_collection_system.JPG/220px-Landfill_gas_collection_system.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Landfill_gas_collection_system.JPG/330px-Landfill_gas_collection_system.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bb/Landfill_gas_collection_system.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Landfill_gas_collection_system.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Landfill gas collection system
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      U.S.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">
       Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      data shows that more than 950 municipal solid waste landfills are operating in the United States as of 2013. Decomposing waste in these landfills produce landfill gas, which is a mixture of about half methane and half carbon dioxide. These landfills are the third largest source of human-made methane emissions in the United States.
     </p>
     <p>
      The gases produced within a landfill can be collected and used in various ways. The landfill gas can be utilized directly on site by a boiler or any type of combustion system, providing heat. Electricity can also be generated on site through the use of microturbines, steam turbines, or fuel cells.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The landfill gas can also be sold off site and sent into natural gas pipelines. This approach requires the gas to be processed into pipeline quality, e.g., by removing various contaminants and components.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The efficiency of gas collection at landfills directly impacts the amount of energy that can be recovered - closed landfills (those no longer accepting waste) collect gas more efficiently than open landfills (those that are still accepting waste). A comparison of collection efficiency at closed and open landfills found about a 17 percentage point difference between the two.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Landfill gas can also be used to evaporate leachate, another byproduct of the landfill process. This application displaces another fuel that was previously used for the same thing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leachate_evaporation_system.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="320" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Leachate_evaporation_system.JPG/220px-Leachate_evaporation_system.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f9/Leachate_evaporation_system.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leachate_evaporation_system.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Leachate" title="Leachate">
         Leachate
        </a>
        evaporation system
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the U.S., the number of landfill gas projects increased from 399 in 2005, to 594 in 2012
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      according to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">
       Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      . These projects are popular because they control energy costs and reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      emissions. These projects collect the methane gas and treat it, so it can be used for electricity or upgraded to pipeline-grade gas. (Methane gas has twenty-one times the
      <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">
       global warming potential
      </a>
      of carbon dioxide).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koch_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Koch-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The U.S.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">
       Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      , which estimates that hundreds of landfills could support gas to energy projects, has also established the Landfill Methane Outreach Program. This program was developed to reduce methane emissions from landfills in a cost-effective manner by encouraging the development of environmentally and economically beneficial landfill gas-to-energy projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landfill_Gas_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Landfill_Gas-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the U.S.,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_Management,_Inc" title="Waste Management, Inc">
       Waste Management
      </a>
      uses landfill gas as an energy source. Their landfill gas-to-energy projects create sufficient energy to power four hundred thousand homes every day. This energy production offsets almost two million tons of coal per year. These projects also reduce greenhouse gas emissions into the atmosphere. Waste Management currently has 110 landfill gas-to-energy facilities. This gas is equivalent to natural gas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">
       Opposition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfill_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Opposition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Capture and use of landfill gas can be expensive. Some environmental groups claim that the projects do not produce "renewable power" because trash (their source) is not renewable. The
      <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">
       Sierra Club
      </a>
      opposes government subsidies for such projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koch_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Koch-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">
       Natural Resources Defense Council
      </a>
      (NRDC) argues that government incentives should be directed more towards solar, wind, and energy-efficiency efforts.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">
       Environmental effects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfill_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Landfill gases have an influence on
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
       climate change
      </a>
      . The major components are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      , both of which are
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      . In terms of global warming potential, methane is over 25 times more detrimental to the atmosphere than carbon dioxide. Landfills are the third largest source of methane in the US.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Microbial_oxidation">
       Microbial oxidation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfill_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Microbial oxidation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      When landfill gas permeates through a soil cover, a fraction of the
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      in the gas is oxidized microbially to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">
       Safety
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landfill_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Safety">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Landfill gas emissions can lead to
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">
       environmental
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">
       hygiene
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Security" title="Security">
       security
      </a>
      problems in the landfill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several accidents have occurred, for example at
      <a href="/wiki/Loscoe" title="Loscoe">
       Loscoe
      </a>
      , England in 1986,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      where migrating landfill gas accumulated and partially destroyed a property. An accident causing two deaths occurred from an explosion in a house adjacent to Skellingsted landfill in Denmark in 1991.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Due to the risk presented by landfill gas, there is a clear need to monitor gas produced by landfills. In addition to the risk of fire and explosion, gas migration in the subsurface can result in contact of landfill gas with
      <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">
       groundwater
      </a>
      . This, in turn, can result in contamination of groundwater by organic compounds present in nearly all landfill gas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although usually evolved only in trace amounts, landfills do release some
      <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">
       aromatics
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorocarbon" title="Chlorocarbon">
       chlorocarbons
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas_migration" title="Landfill gas migration">
       Landfill gas migration
      </a>
      , due to pressure differentials and diffusion, can occur. This can create an explosion hazard if the gas reaches sufficiently high concentrations in adjacent buildings.
     </p>
