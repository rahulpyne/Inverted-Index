     <p>
      <b>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating" title="Heating">
        Heating
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">
        ventilation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">
        air conditioning
       </a>
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       HVAC
      </b>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      is the technology of indoor and vehicular environmental comfort. Its goal is to provide
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">
       thermal comfort
      </a>
      and acceptable
      <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">
       indoor air quality
      </a>
      . HVAC system design is a subdiscipline of
      <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">
       mechanical engineering
      </a>
      , based on the principles of
      <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">
       thermodynamics
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">
       fluid mechanics
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
       heat transfer
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">
       Refrigeration
      </a>
      is sometimes added to the field's abbreviation as HVAC&amp;R or HVACR, or ventilating is dropped as in HACR (such as the designation of HACR-rated
      <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">
       circuit breakers
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      HVAC is an important part of residential structures such as single family homes, apartment buildings, hotels and senior living facilities, medium to large industrial and office buildings such as
      <a href="/wiki/Skyscraper" title="Skyscraper">
       skyscrapers
      </a>
      and hospitals,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Onboard_vessel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Onboard vessel (page does not exist)">
       onboard vessels
      </a>
      , and in marine environments, where safe and
      <a href="/wiki/Sick_building_syndrome" title="Sick building syndrome">
       healthy building
      </a>
      conditions are regulated with respect to temperature and humidity, using fresh air from outdoors.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ventilating or
      <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">
       ventilation
      </a>
      (the
      <i>
       V
      </i>
      in HVAC) is the process of exchanging or replacing air in any space to provide high
      <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">
       indoor air quality
      </a>
      which involves temperature control, oxygen replenishment, and removal of moisture, odors, smoke, heat, dust, airborne bacteria, carbon dioxide, and other gases. Ventilation removes unpleasant smells and excessive moisture, introduces outside air, keeps interior building air circulating, and prevents stagnation of the interior air.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ventilation includes both the exchange of air to the outside as well as circulation of air within the building. It is one of the most important factors for maintaining acceptable indoor air quality in buildings. Methods for ventilating a building may be divided into
      <i>
       mechanical/forced
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_ventilation" title="Natural ventilation">
        natural
       </a>
      </i>
      types.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The three central functions of heating, ventilation, and air conditioning are interrelated, especially with the need to provide
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">
       thermal comfort
      </a>
      and acceptable
      <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">
       indoor air quality
      </a>
      within reasonable installation, operation, and maintenance costs. HVAC systems can provide
      <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">
       ventilation
      </a>
      , reduce air
      <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(HVAC)" title="Infiltration (HVAC)">
       infiltration
      </a>
      , and maintain pressure relationships between spaces. The means of air delivery and removal from spaces is known as
      <a href="/wiki/Room_air_distribution" title="Room air distribution">
       room air distribution
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rock_and_Zhu_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rock_and_Zhu-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_systems">
       Individual systems
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Individual systems">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In modern buildings the design, installation, and
      <a href="/wiki/Control_system" title="Control system">
       control systems
      </a>
      of these functions are integrated into one or more HVAC systems. For very small buildings, contractors normally estimate the capacity, engineer,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2015)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and select HVAC systems and equipment. For larger buildings, building service designers, mechanical engineers, or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Building_engineering" title="Building engineering">
       building services
      </a>
      engineers analyze, design, and specify the HVAC systems. Specialty mechanical contractors then fabricate and commission the systems. Building permits and code-compliance inspections of the installations are normally required for all sizes of building.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="District_networks">
       District networks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: District networks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although HVAC is executed in individual buildings or other enclosed spaces (like
      <a href="/wiki/North_American_Aerospace_Defense_Command" title="North American Aerospace Defense Command">
       NORAD's
      </a>
      underground headquarters), the equipment involved is in some cases an extension of a larger
      <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">
       district heating
      </a>
      (DH) or
      <a href="/wiki/District_cooling" title="District cooling">
       district cooling
      </a>
      (DC) network, or a combined DHC network. In such cases, the operating and maintenance aspects are simplified and metering becomes necessary to bill for the energy that is consumed, and in some cases energy that is returned to the larger system. For example, at a given time one building may be utilizing chilled water for air conditioning and the warm water it returns may be used in another building for heating, or for the overall heating-portion of the DHC network (likely with energy added to boost the temperature).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Basing HVAC on a larger network helps to provide an economy of scale that is often not possible for individual buildings, for utilizing renewable energy sources such as solar heat,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      winter's cold,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the cooling potential in some places of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_cooling" title="Lake cooling">
       lakes or seawater
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Free_cooling" title="Free cooling">
       free cooling
      </a>
      , and the enabling function of
      <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">
       seasonal thermal energy storage
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning#History" title="Air conditioning">
       Air conditioning § History
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      HVAC is based on inventions and discoveries made by
      <a href="/wiki/Nikolay_Lvov" title="Nikolay Lvov">
       Nikolay Lvov
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">
       Michael Faraday
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Willis_Carrier" title="Willis Carrier">
       Willis Carrier
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Ruud" title="Edwin Ruud">
       Edwin Ruud
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Reuben_Trane" title="Reuben Trane">
       Reuben Trane
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Joule" title="James Joule">
       James Joule
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Rankine" title="William Rankine">
       William Rankine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot" title="Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot">
       Sadi Carnot
      </a>
      , and many others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Multiple inventions within this time frame preceded the beginnings of first comfort air conditioning system, which was designed in 1902 by Alfred Wolff (Cooper, 2003) for the New York Stock Exchange, while Willis Carrier equipped the Sacketts-Wilhems Printing Company with the process AC unit the same year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The invention of the components of HVAC systems went hand-in-hand with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">
       industrial revolution
      </a>
      , and new methods of modernization, higher efficiency, and system control are constantly being introduced by companies and inventors worldwide.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heating">
       Heating
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heating">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">
       Central heating
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      "Heaters" redirects here. For the community in the United States, see
      <a href="/wiki/Heaters,_West_Virginia" title="Heaters, West Virginia">
       Heaters, West Virginia
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      "Heater" redirects here. For other uses, see
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Heater_(disambiguation)" title="Heater (disambiguation)">
       Heater (disambiguation)
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <p>
      Heaters are appliances whose purpose is to generate heat (i.e. warmth) for the building. This can be done via
      <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">
       central heating
      </a>
      . Such a system contains a
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">
       boiler
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">
       furnace
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">
       heat pump
      </a>
      to heat water, steam, or air in a central location such as a
      <a href="/wiki/Furnace_room" title="Furnace room">
       furnace room
      </a>
      in a home, or a
      <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_room" title="Mechanical room">
       mechanical room
      </a>
      in a large building. The heat can be transferred by
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">
       conduction
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
       radiation
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Generation">
       Generation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Generation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Panna.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="405" height="435" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Panna.jpg/220px-Panna.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Panna.jpg/330px-Panna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/Panna.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Panna.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Central heating unit
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Heaters exist for various types of fuel, including
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel#Solid_fuel" title="Fuel">
       solid fuels
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel#Liquid_fuel" title="Fuel">
       liquids
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel#Gaseous_fuels" title="Fuel">
       gases
      </a>
      . Another type of heat source is
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_heating" title="Electric heating">
       electricity
      </a>
      , normally heating ribbons composed of high resistance wire (see
      <a href="/wiki/Nichrome" title="Nichrome">
       Nichrome
      </a>
      ). This principle is also used for baseboard heaters and
      <a href="/wiki/Space_heater" title="Space heater">
       portable heaters
      </a>
      . Electrical heaters are often used as backup or supplemental heat for
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">
       heat pump
      </a>
      systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">
       heat pump
      </a>
      gained popularity in the 1950s in Japan and the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Heat pumps can extract heat from
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump#Heat_sources_and_sinks" title="Heat pump">
       various sources
      </a>
      , such as environmental air, exhaust air from a building, or from the ground. Initially, heat pump HVAC systems were only used in moderate climates, but with improvements in low temperature operation and reduced loads due to more efficient homes, they are increasing in popularity in cooler climates.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">
       Distribution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distribution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Water_.2F_steam">
       Water / steam
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Water / steam">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In the case of heated water or steam, piping is used to transport the heat to the rooms. Most modern hot water boiler heating systems have a circulator, which is a pump, to move hot water through the distribution system (as opposed to older
      <a href="/wiki/Water_heating#Gravity-fed_system" title="Water heating">
       gravity-fed systems
      </a>
      ). The heat can be transferred to the surrounding air using
      <a href="/wiki/Radiator_(heating)" title="Radiator (heating)">
       radiators
      </a>
      , hot water coils (hydro-air), or other heat exchangers. The radiators may be mounted on walls or installed within the floor to produce floor heat.
     </p>
     <p>
      The use of water as the heat transfer medium is known as
      <a href="/wiki/Hydronics" title="Hydronics">
       hydronics
      </a>
      . The heated water can also supply an auxiliary heat exchanger to supply hot water for bathing and washing.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Air">
       Air
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Air">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Warm air systems distribute heated air through duct work systems of supply and return air through metal or fiberglass ducts. Many systems use the same ducts to distribute air cooled by an evaporator coil for air conditioning. The air supply is normally filtered through air cleaners to remove dust and pollen particles.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers">
       Dangers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dangers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The use of furnaces, space heaters, and boilers as a method of indoor heating could result in incomplete combustion and the emission of
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">
       nitrogen oxides
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">
       formaldehyde
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">
       volatile organic compounds
      </a>
      , and other combustion byproducts. Incomplete combustion occurs when there is insufficient oxygen; the inputs are fuels containing various contaminants and the outputs are harmful byproducts, most dangerously carbon monoxide, which is a tasteless and odorless gas with serious adverse health effects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Without proper ventilation, carbon monoxide can be lethal at concentrations of 1000 ppm (0.1%). However, at several hundred ppm, carbon monoxide exposure induces headaches, fatigue, nausea, and vomiting. Carbon monoxide binds with hemoglobin in the blood, forming carboxyhemoglobin, reducing the blood's ability to transport oxygen. The primary health concerns associated with carbon monoxide exposure are its cardiovascular and neurobehavioral effects. Carbon monoxide can cause atherosclerosis (the hardening of arteries) and can also trigger heart attacks. Neurologically, carbon monoxide exposure reduces hand to eye coordination, vigilance, and continuous performance. It can also affect time discrimination.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ventilation">
       Ventilation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ventilation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">
       Ventilation (architecture)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">
       Ventilation
      </a>
      is the process of changing or replacing air in any space to control temperature or remove any combination of moisture, odors, smoke, heat, dust, airborne bacteria, or carbon dioxide, and to replenish oxygen. Ventilation includes both the exchange of air with the outside as well as circulation of air within the building. It is one of the most important factors for maintaining acceptable
      <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">
       indoor air quality
      </a>
      in buildings. Methods for ventilating a building may be divided into
      <i>
       mechanical/forced
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       natural
      </i>
      types.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_or_forced_ventilation">
       Mechanical or forced ventilation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mechanical or forced ventilation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HVAC_Ventilation_Exhaust.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/HVAC_Ventilation_Exhaust.jpg/220px-HVAC_Ventilation_Exhaust.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/HVAC_Ventilation_Exhaust.jpg/330px-HVAC_Ventilation_Exhaust.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/HVAC_Ventilation_Exhaust.jpg/440px-HVAC_Ventilation_Exhaust.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HVAC_Ventilation_Exhaust.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        HVAC ventilation exhaust for a 12-story building
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Mechanical, or forced, ventilation is provided by an
      <a href="/wiki/Air_handler" title="Air handler">
       air handler
      </a>
      (AHU) and used to control
      <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">
       indoor air quality
      </a>
      . Excess
      <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">
       humidity
      </a>
      , odors, and contaminants can often be controlled via dilution or replacement with outside air. However, in humid climates more energy is required to remove excess moisture from ventilation air.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kitchens and bathrooms typically have mechanical exhausts to control odors and sometimes humidity. Factors in the design of such systems include the flow rate (which is a function of the fan speed and exhaust vent size) and noise level.
      <a href="/wiki/Direct_drive_mechanism" title="Direct drive mechanism">
       Direct drive
      </a>
      fans are available for many applications, and can reduce maintenance needs.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ceiling
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fan_(mechanical)" title="Fan (mechanical)">
       fans
      </a>
      and table/floor fans circulate air within a room for the purpose of reducing the perceived temperature by increasing evaporation of perspiration on the skin of the occupants. Because hot air rises, ceiling fans may be used to keep a room warmer in the winter by circulating the warm stratified air from the ceiling to the floor.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_ventilation">
       Natural ventilation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Natural ventilation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:N%26AMOV_-_Schoolroom_-_Impulsion.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="468" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/N%26AMOV_-_Schoolroom_-_Impulsion.png/220px-N%26AMOV_-_Schoolroom_-_Impulsion.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/N%26AMOV_-_Schoolroom_-_Impulsion.png/330px-N%26AMOV_-_Schoolroom_-_Impulsion.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/N%26AMOV_-_Schoolroom_-_Impulsion.png/440px-N%26AMOV_-_Schoolroom_-_Impulsion.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:N%26AMOV_-_Schoolroom_-_Impulsion.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">
         Ventilation
        </a>
        on the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdraught" title="Downdraught">
         downdraught
        </a>
        system, by impulsion, or the
        <a href="/wiki/Plenum_space" title="Plenum space">
         'plenum' principle
        </a>
        , applied to schoolrooms (1899)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_ventilation" title="Natural ventilation">
       Natural ventilation
      </a>
      is the ventilation of a building with outside air without using fans or other mechanical systems. It can be via operable windows, louvers, or
      <a href="/wiki/Trickle_vent" title="Trickle vent">
       trickle vents
      </a>
      when spaces are small and the architecture permits. In more complex schemes, warm air is allowed to rise and flow out high building openings to the outside (
      <a href="/wiki/Stack_effect" title="Stack effect">
       stack effect
      </a>
      ), causing cool outside air to be drawn into low building openings. Natural ventilation schemes can use very little energy, but care must be taken to ensure comfort. In warm or humid climates, maintaining
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">
       thermal comfort
      </a>
      solely via natural ventilation might not be possible.
      <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">
       Air conditioning
      </a>
      systems are used, either as backups or supplements. Air-side
      <a href="/wiki/Economizer#HVAC" title="Economizer">
       economizers
      </a>
      also use outside air to condition spaces, but do so using fans, ducts, dampers, and control systems to introduce and distribute cool outdoor air when appropriate.
     </p>
     <p>
      An important component of natural ventilation is air change rate or
      <a href="/wiki/Air_changes_per_hour" title="Air changes per hour">
       air changes per hour
      </a>
      : the hourly rate of ventilation divided by the volume of the space. For example, six air changes per hour means an amount of new air, equal to the volume of the space, is added every ten minutes. For human comfort, a minimum of four air changes per hour is typical, though warehouses might have only two. Too high of an air change rate may be uncomfortable, akin to a
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">
       wind tunnel
      </a>
      which have thousands of changes per hour. The highest air change rates are for crowded spaces, bars, night clubs, commercial kitchens at around 30 to 50 air changes per hour.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Room pressure can be either positive or negative with respect to outside the room. Positive pressure occurs when there is more air being supplied than exhausted, and is common to reduce the infiltration of outside contaminants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Airborne_diseases">
       Airborne diseases
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Airborne diseases">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Natural ventilation is a key factor in reducing the spread of airborne illnesses such as tuberculosis, the common cold, influenza and meningitis. Opening doors, windows, and using ceiling fans are all ways to maximize natural ventilation and reduce the risk of airborne contagion. Natural ventilation requires little maintenance and is inexpensive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Air_conditioning">
       Air conditioning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Air conditioning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">
       Air conditioning
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      An air conditioning system, or a standalone air conditioner, provides cooling and
      <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning#Humidity_control" title="Air conditioning">
       humidity control
      </a>
      for all or part of a building. Air conditioned buildings often have sealed windows, because open windows would work against the system intended to maintain constant indoor air conditions. Outside, fresh air is generally drawn into the system by a vent into the indoor heat exchanger section, creating positive air pressure. The percentage of return air made up of fresh air can usually be manipulated by adjusting the opening of this vent. Typical fresh air intake is about 10%.
     </p>
     <p>
      Air conditioning and refrigeration are provided through the removal of heat. Heat can be removed through
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
       radiation
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">
       conduction
      </a>
      . Refrigeration conduction media such as water, air, ice, and chemicals are referred to as
      <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">
       refrigerants
      </a>
      . A refrigerant is employed either in a
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">
       heat pump
      </a>
      system in which a
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump_and_refrigeration_cycle" title="Heat pump and refrigeration cycle">
       compressor
      </a>
      is used to drive thermodynamic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigeration_cycle" title="Refrigeration cycle">
       refrigeration cycle
      </a>
      , or in a
      <a href="/wiki/Free_cooling" title="Free cooling">
       free cooling
      </a>
      system which uses pumps to circulate a cool refrigerant (typically water or a glycol mix).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigeration_cycle">
       Refrigeration cycle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Refrigeration cycle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigeration_cycle" title="Refrigeration cycle">
       Refrigeration cycle
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heatpump.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="438" data-file-width="750" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Heatpump.svg/220px-Heatpump.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Heatpump.svg/330px-Heatpump.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Heatpump.svg/440px-Heatpump.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heatpump.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A simple stylized diagram of the refrigeration cycle: 1)
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condensing_coil" title="Condensing coil">
         condensing coil
        </a>
        , 2)
        <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion_valve" title="Thermal expansion valve">
         expansion valve
        </a>
        , 3)
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporator_coil" title="Evaporator coil">
         evaporator coil
        </a>
        , 4)
        <a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">
         compressor
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The refrigeration cycle uses four essential elements to cool.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The system refrigerant starts its cycle in a gaseous state. The
       <b>
        compressor
       </b>
       pumps the refrigerant gas up to a high pressure and temperature.
      </li>
      <li>
       From there it enters a heat exchanger (sometimes called a
       <b>
        condensing coil
       </b>
       or condenser) where it loses energy (heat) to the outside, cools, and condenses into its liquid phase.
      </li>
      <li>
       An
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion_valve" title="Thermal expansion valve">
         expansion valve
        </a>
       </b>
       (also called metering device) regulates the refrigerant liquid to flow at the proper rate.
      </li>
      <li>
       The liquid refrigerant is returned to another heat exchanger where it is allowed to evaporate, hence the heat exchanger is often called an
       <b>
        evaporating coil
       </b>
       or evaporator. As the liquid refrigerant evaporates it absorbs energy (heat) from the inside air, returns to the compressor, and repeats the cycle. In the process, heat is absorbed from indoors and transferred outdoors, resulting in cooling of the building.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In variable climates, the system may include a
      <a href="/wiki/Reversing_valve" title="Reversing valve">
       reversing valve
      </a>
      that switches from heating in winter to cooling in summer. By reversing the flow of refrigerant, the heat pump refrigeration cycle is changed from cooling to heating or vice versa. This allows a facility to be heated and cooled by a single piece of equipment by the same means, and with the same hardware.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Free_cooling">
       Free cooling
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Free cooling">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Free_cooling" title="Free cooling">
       Free cooling
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Free cooling systems can have very high efficiencies, and are sometimes combined with seasonal thermal energy storage so the cold of winter can be used for summer air conditioning. Common storage mediums are deep aquifers or a natural underground rock mass accessed via a cluster of small-diameter, heat-exchanger-equipped boreholes. Some systems with small storages are hybrids, using free cooling early in the cooling season, and later employing a heat pump to chill the circulation coming from the storage. The heat pump is added-in because the storage acts as a
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_sink" title="Heat sink">
       heat sink
      </a>
      when the system is in cooling (as opposed to charging) mode, causing the temperature to gradually increase during the cooling season.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some systems include an "economizer mode", which is sometimes called a "free-cooling mode". When economizing, the control system will open (fully or partially) the outside air damper and close (fully or partially) the return air damper. This will cause fresh, outside air to be supplied to the system. When the outside air is cooler than the demanded cool air, this will allow the demand to be met without using the mechanical supply of cooling (typically chilled water or a direct expansion "DX" unit), thus saving energy. The control system can compare the temperature of the outside air vs. return air, or it can compare the
      <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">
       enthalpy
      </a>
      of the air, as is frequently done in climates where humidity is more of an issue. In both cases, the outside air must be less energetic than the return air for the system to enter the economizer mode.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Central_vs._split_system">
       Central vs. split system
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Central vs. split system">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Central, "all-air" air-conditioning systems (or package systems) with a combined outdoor condenser/evaporator unit are often installed in modern residences, offices, and public buildings, but are difficult to retrofit (install in a building that was not designed to receive it) because of the bulky air ducts required. (Minisplit ductless systems are used in these situations.)
     </p>
     <p>
      An alternative to central systems is the use of separate indoor and outdoor coils in split systems. These systems, although most often seen in residential applications, are gaining popularity in small commercial buildings. The evaporator coil is connected to a remote condenser unit using refrigerant piping between an indoor and outdoor unit instead of ducting air directly from the outdoor unit. Indoor units with directional vents mount onto walls, suspended from ceilings, or fit into the ceiling. Other indoor units mount inside the ceiling cavity, so that short lengths of duct handle air from the indoor unit to vents or diffusers around the rooms.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dehumidification">
       Dehumidification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Dehumidification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Dehumidification (air drying) in an air conditioning system is provided by the evaporator. Since the evaporator operates at a temperature below the
      <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">
       dew point
      </a>
      , moisture in the air condenses on the evaporator coil tubes. This moisture is collected at the bottom of the evaporator in a pan and removed by piping to a central drain or onto the ground outside.
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Dehumidifier" title="Dehumidifier">
       dehumidifier
      </a>
      is an air-conditioner-like device that controls the humidity of a room or building. It is often employed in basements which have a higher
      <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">
       relative humidity
      </a>
      because of their lower temperature (and propensity for damp floors and walls). In food retailing establishments, large open chiller cabinets are highly effective at dehumidifying the internal air. Conversely, a humidifier increases the
      <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">
       humidity
      </a>
      of a building.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">
       Maintenance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Maintenance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      All modern air conditioning systems, even small window package units, are equipped with internal air filters. These are generally of a lightweight gauzy material, and must be replaced or washed as conditions warrant. For example, a building in a high dust environment, or a home with furry pets, will need to have the filters changed more often than buildings without these dirt loads. Failure to replace these filters as needed will contribute to a lower heat exchange rate, resulting in wasted energy, shortened equipment life, and higher energy bills; low air flow can result in iced-over evaporator coils, which can completely stop air flow. Additionally, very dirty or plugged filters can cause overheating during a heating cycle, and can result in damage to the system or even fire.
     </p>
     <p>
      Because an air conditioner moves heat between the indoor coil and the outdoor coil, both must be kept clean. This means that, in addition to replacing the air filter at the evaporator coil, it is also necessary to regularly clean the condenser coil. Failure to keep the condenser clean will eventually result in harm to the compressor, because the condenser coil is responsible for discharging both the indoor heat (as picked up by the evaporator) and the heat generated by the electric motor driving the compressor.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_efficiency">
       Energy efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Energy efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Since the 1980s, manufacturers of HVAC equipment have been making an effort to make the systems they manufacture more efficient. This was originally driven by rising energy costs, and has more recently been driven by increased awareness of environmental issues. Additionally, improvements to the HVAC system efficiency can also help increase occupant health and productivity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the US, the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">
       EPA
      </a>
      has imposed tighter restrictions over the years. There are several methods for making HVAC systems more efficient.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heating_energy">
       Heating energy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Heating energy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the past, water heating was more efficient for heating buildings and was the standard in the United States. Today,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced_air" title="Forced air">
       forced air
      </a>
      systems can double for air conditioning and are more popular.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some benefits of forced air systems, which are now widely used in churches, schools and high-end residences, are
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Better air conditioning effects
      </li>
      <li>
       Energy savings of up to 15-20%
      </li>
      <li>
       Even conditioning
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A drawback is the installation cost, which can be slightly higher than traditional HVAC systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      Energy efficiency can be improved even more in central heating systems by introducing zoned heating. This allows a more granular application of heat, similar to non-central heating systems. Zones are controlled by multiple
      <a href="/wiki/Thermostat" title="Thermostat">
       thermostats
      </a>
      . In water heating systems the thermostats control
      <a href="/wiki/Zone_valve" title="Zone valve">
       zone valves
      </a>
      , and in forced air systems they control
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zone_damper" title="Zone damper">
       zone dampers
      </a>
      inside the vents which selectively block the flow of air. In this case, the control system is very critical to maintaining a proper temperature.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Forecasting_(heating)" title="Forecasting (heating)">
       Forecasting
      </a>
      is another method of controlling building heating by calculating demand for heating energy that should be supplied to the building in each time unit.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_source_heat_pump">
       Ground source heat pump
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Ground source heat pump">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">
       Geothermal heat pump
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Ground source, or geothermal, heat pumps are similar to ordinary heat pumps, but instead of transferring heat to or from outside air, they rely on the stable, even temperature of the earth to provide heating and air conditioning. Many parts of the country experience seasonal temperature extremes, which would require large-capacity heating and cooling equipment to heat or cool buildings. For example, a conventional heat pump system used to heat a building in Montana's −70
      <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">
       °F
      </a>
      (−57
      <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">
       °C
      </a>
      ) low temperature or cool a building in the highest temperature ever recorded in the US—134 °F (57 °C) in
      <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley" title="Death Valley">
       Death Valley
      </a>
      , California, in 1913 would require a large amount of energy due to the extreme difference between inside and outside air temperatures. A few feet below the earth's surface, however, the ground remains at a relatively constant temperature. Utilizing this large source of relatively moderate temperature earth, a heating or cooling system's capacity can often be significantly reduced. Although ground temperatures vary according to latitude, at 6 feet (1.8 m) underground, temperatures generally only range from 45 to 75 °F (7 to 24 °C).
     </p>
     <p>
      An example of a geothermal heat pump that uses a body of water as the heat sink, is the system used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Trump_International_Hotel_and_Tower_(Chicago)" title="Trump International Hotel and Tower (Chicago)">
       Trump International Hotel and Tower
      </a>
      in Chicago, Illinois. This building is situated on the
      <a href="/wiki/Chicago_River" title="Chicago River">
       Chicago River
      </a>
      , and uses cold river water by pumping it into a recirculating cooling system, where heat exchangers transfer heat from the building into the water, and then the now-warmed water is pumped back into the Chicago River.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While they may be more costly to install than regular heat pumps, geothermal heat pumps can produce markedly lower energy bills – 30 to 40 percent lower, according to estimates from the US Environmental Protection Agency.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ventilation_energy_recovery">
       Ventilation energy recovery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Ventilation energy recovery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery" title="Energy recovery">
       Energy recovery
      </a>
      systems sometimes utilize
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_ventilation" title="Heat recovery ventilation">
       heat recovery ventilation
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery_ventilation" title="Energy recovery ventilation">
       energy recovery ventilation
      </a>
      systems that employ
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchangers
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_wheel" title="Thermal wheel">
       enthalpy wheels
      </a>
      to recover
      <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">
       sensible
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">
       latent heat
      </a>
      from exhausted air. This is done by transfer of energy to the incoming outside fresh air.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Air_conditioning_energy">
       Air conditioning energy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Air conditioning energy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The performance of vapor compression refrigeration cycles is limited by
      <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">
       thermodynamics
      </a>
      . These air conditioning and
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">
       heat pump
      </a>
      devices
      <i>
       move
      </i>
      heat rather than convert it from one form to another, so
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">
        thermal efficiencies
       </a>
      </i>
      do not appropriately describe the performance of these devices. The Coefficient-of-Performance (COP) measures performance, but this dimensionless measure has not been adopted. Instead, the Energy Efficiency Ratio (
      <i>
       EER
      </i>
      ) has traditionally been used to characterize the performance of many HVAC systems. EER is the Energy Efficiency Ratio based on a 35 °C (95 °F) outdoor temperature. To more accurately describe the performance of air conditioning equipment over a typical cooling season a modified version of the EER, the Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio (
      <i>
       SEER
      </i>
      ), or in Europe the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESEER" title="ESEER">
       ESEER
      </a>
      , is used. SEER ratings are based on seasonal temperature averages instead of a constant 35 °C (95 °F) outdoor temperature. The current industry minimum SEER rating is 14 SEER.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Engineers have pointed out some areas where efficiency of the existing hardware could be improved. For example, the fan blades used to move the air are usually stamped from sheet metal, an economical method of manufacture, but as a result they are not aerodynamically efficient. A well-designed blade could reduce electrical power required to move the air by a third.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Air_filtration_and_cleaning">
       Air filtration and cleaning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Air filtration and cleaning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Air_handling_unit.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="738" data-file-width="1109" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Air_handling_unit.JPG/220px-Air_handling_unit.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Air_handling_unit.JPG/330px-Air_handling_unit.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Air_handling_unit.JPG/440px-Air_handling_unit.JPG 2x" width="220"/>

         </a>

         Air handling unit
        </a>
        , used for heating, cooling, and filtering the air
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Air cleaning and filtration removes particles, contaminants, vapors and gases from the air. The filtered and cleaned air then is used in heating, ventilation and air conditioning. Air cleaning and filtration should be taken in account when protecting our building environments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_2003_1.E2.80.9363_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_2003_1.E2.80.9363-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_air_delivery_rate_and_filter_performance">
       Clean air delivery rate and filter performance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Clean air delivery rate and filter performance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Clean air delivery rate is the amount of clean air an air cleaner provides to a room or space. When determining CADR, the amount of airflow in a space is taken into account. For example, an air cleaner with a flow rate of 100 cfm (cubic feet per minute) and an efficiency of 50% has a CADR of 50 cfm. Along with CADR, filtration performance is very important when it comes to the air in our indoor environment. Filter performance depends on the size of the particle or fiber, the filter packing density and depth and also the air flow rate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_2003_1.E2.80.9363_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_2003_1.E2.80.9363-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="HVAC_industry_and_standards">
       HVAC industry and standards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: HVAC industry and standards">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The HVAC industry is a worldwide enterprise, with roles including operation and maintenance, system design and construction, equipment manufacturing and sales, and in education and research. The HVAC industry was historically regulated by the manufacturers of HVAC equipment, but regulating and standards organizations such as
      <a href="/wiki/HARDI" title="HARDI">
       HARDI
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE" title="ASHRAE">
       ASHRAE
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMACNA" title="SMACNA">
       SMACNA
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Air_Conditioning_Contractors_of_America" title="Air Conditioning Contractors of America">
       ACCA
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Mechanical_Code" title="Uniform Mechanical Code">
       Uniform Mechanical Code
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/International_Building_Code" title="International Building Code">
       International Mechanical Code
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Air_Movement_and_Control_Association" title="Air Movement and Control Association">
       AMCA
      </a>
      have been established to support the industry and encourage high standards and achievement.
     </p>
     <p>
      The starting point in carrying out an estimate both for cooling and heating depends on the exterior climate and interior specified conditions. However, before taking up the heat load calculation, it is necessary to find fresh air requirements for each area in detail, as
      <a href="/wiki/Pressurization" title="Pressurization">
       pressurization
      </a>
      is an important consideration.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="International">
       International
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: International">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ISO_16813&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ISO 16813 (page does not exist)">
       ISO 16813
      </a>
      :2006 is one of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">
       ISO
      </a>
      building environment standards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It establishes the general principles of building environment design. It takes into account the need to provide a healthy indoor environment for the occupants as well as the need to protect the environment for future generations and promote collaboration among the various parties involved in building environmental design for sustainability. ISO16813 is applicable to new construction and the retrofit of existing buildings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iso16813_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-iso16813-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The building environmental design standard aims to:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iso16813_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-iso16813-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       provide the constraints concerning sustainability issues from the initial stage of the design process, with building and plant life cycle to be considered together with owning and operating costs from the beginning of the design process;
      </li>
      <li>
       assess the proposed design with rational criteria for indoor air quality, thermal comfort, acoustical comfort, visual comfort, energy efficiency and HVAC system controls at every stage of the design process;
      </li>
      <li>
       iterate decisions and evaluations of the design throughout the design process.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">

      <span class="mw-editsection">

     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
       United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: United States">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Heating,_Refrigerating_and_Air-Conditioning_Engineers" title="American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers">
       American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the United States, HVAC engineers generally are members of the American Society of Heating, Refrigerating, and Air-Conditioning Engineers (
      <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE" title="ASHRAE">
       ASHRAE
      </a>
      ), EPA Universal CFC certified, or locally engineer certified such as a Special to Chief Boilers License issued by the state or, in some jurisdictions, the city. ASHRAE is an international technical society for all individuals and organizations interested in HVAC. The Society, organized into regions, chapters, and student branches, allows exchange of HVAC knowledge and experiences for the benefit of the field's practitioners and the public. ASHRAE provides many opportunities to participate in the development of new knowledge via, for example, research and its many technical committees. These committees typically meet twice per year at the ASHRAE Annual and Winter Meetings. A popular product show, the AHR Expo, is held in conjunction with each winter meeting. The Society has approximately 50,000 members and has headquarters in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">
       Atlanta, Georgia
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The most recognized standards for HVAC design are based on ASHRAE data. The most general of four volumes of the ASHRAE Handbook is Fundamentals; it includes heating and cooling calculations. Each volume of the ASHRAE Handbook is updated every four years. The design professional must consult ASHRAE data for the standards of design and care as the typical building codes provide little to no information on HVAC design practices; codes such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Mechanical_Code" title="Uniform Mechanical Code">
       UMC
      </a>
      and IMC do include much detail on installation requirements, however. Other useful reference materials include items from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMACNA" title="SMACNA">
       SMACNA
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACGIH" title="ACGIH">
       ACGIH
      </a>
      , and technical trade journals.
     </p>
     <p>
      American design standards are legislated in the
      <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Mechanical_Code" title="Uniform Mechanical Code">
       Uniform Mechanical Code
      </a>
      or International Mechanical Code. In certain states, counties, or cities, either of these codes may be adopted and amended via various legislative processes. These codes are updated and published by the International Association of Plumbing and Mechanical Officials (
      <a href="/wiki/IAPMO" title="IAPMO">
       IAPMO
      </a>
      ) or the International Code Council (
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Code_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Code Council (page does not exist)">
       ICC
      </a>
      ) respectively, on a 3-year code development cycle. Typically, local building permit departments are charged with enforcement of these standards on private and certain public properties.
     </p>
     <p>
      HVAC professionals in the US can receive training through formal training institutions, where most earn
      <a href="/wiki/Associate_degree" title="Associate degree">
       associate degrees
      </a>
      . Training for HVAC technicians includes classroom lectures and hands-on tasks, and can be followed by an apprenticeship wherein the recent graduate works alongside a professional HVAC technician for a temporary period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      HVAC techs who have been trained can also be certified in areas such as air conditioning, heat pumps, gas heating, and commercial refrigeration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Institution_of_Building_Services_Engineers" title="Chartered Institution of Building Services Engineers">
       Chartered Institution of Building Services Engineers
      </a>
      is a body that covers the essential
      <a href="/wiki/Service_(systems_architecture)" title="Service (systems architecture)">
       Service (systems architecture)
      </a>
      that allow buildings to operate. It includes the electrotechnical,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating" title="Heating">
       heating
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventilating" title="Ventilating">
       ventilating
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">
       air conditioning
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">
       refrigeration
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Plumbing" title="Plumbing">
       plumbing
      </a>
      industries. To
      <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">
       train
      </a>
      as a
      <a href="/wiki/Building_services_engineering" title="Building services engineering">
       building services engineer
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic" title="Academic">
       academic
      </a>
      requirements are GCSEs (A-C) / Standard Grades (1-3) in Maths and Science, which are important in measurements, planning and theory. Employers will often want a
      <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">
       degree
      </a>
      in a branch of engineering, such as building environment
      <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">
       engineering
      </a>
      , electrical engineering or mechanical engineering. To become a full member of CIBSE, and so also to be registered by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_Council_UK" title="Engineering Council UK">
       Engineering Council UK
      </a>
      as a chartered engineer, engineers must also attain an Honours Degree and a master's degree in a relevant engineering subject.
     </p>
     <p>
      CIBSE publishes several guides to HVAC design relevant to the UK market, and also the Republic of Ireland, Australia, New Zealand and Hong Kong. These guides include various recommended design criteria and standards, some of which are cited within the UK building regulations, and therefore form a legislative requirement for major building services works. The main guides are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Guide A: Environmental Design
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide B: Heating, Ventilating, Air Conditioning and Refrigeration
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide C: Reference Data
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide D: Transportation systems in Buildings
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide E: Fire Safety Engineering
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide F: Energy Efficiency in Buildings
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide G: Public Health Engineering
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide H: Building Control Systems
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide J: Weather, Solar and Illuminance Data
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide K: Electricity in Buildings
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide L: Sustainability
      </li>
      <li>
       Guide M: Maintenance Engineering and Management
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Within the
      <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">
       construction
      </a>
      sector, it is the job of the building services engineer to
      <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">
       design
      </a>
      and oversee the installation and maintenance of the essential services such as gas,
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>
      , water, heating and
      <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">
       lighting
      </a>
      , as well as many others. These all help to make buildings comfortable and healthy places to live and work in. Building Services is part of a sector that has over 51,000 businesses and employs represents 2%-3% of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">
       GDP
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">
       Asia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=HVAC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Asia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Asian architectural temperature-control have different priorities than European methods. For example, Asian heating traditionally focuses on maintaining
      <a href="/wiki/Underfloor_heating#History" title="Underfloor heating">
       temperatures of objects such as the floor
      </a>
      or furnishings such as
      <a href="/wiki/Kotatsu" title="Kotatsu">
       Kotatsu
      </a>
      tables and directly warming people, as opposed to the Western focus, in modern periods, on designing air systems.
     </p>
