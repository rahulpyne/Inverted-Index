     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Elaeis
       </b>
      </i>
      (from
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      , meaning "oil") is a genus of
      <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">
       palms
      </a>
      containing two species, called
      <b>
       oil palms
      </b>
      . They are used in commercial
      <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">
       agriculture
      </a>
      in the production of
      <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">
       palm oil
      </a>
      . The African oil palm
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Elaeis_guineensis" title="Elaeis guineensis">
        Elaeis guineensis
       </a>
      </i>
      (the species name
      <i>
       guineensis
      </i>
      referring to its country of origin) is the principal source of palm oil, it is native to west and southwest
      <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
       Africa
      </a>
      , occurring between
      <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">
       Angola
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambia" title="Gambia">
       Gambia
      </a>
      . The American oil palm
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Elaeis_oleifera" title="Elaeis oleifera">
        Elaeis oleifera
       </a>
      </i>
      (from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
       Latin
      </a>
      <i>
       oleifer
      </i>
      , meaning "oil-producing")
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      is native to tropical
      <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
       Central
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
       South America
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is used locally for oil production.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elaeis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Mature palms are single-stemmed, and can grow well over 20 m (66 ft) tall. The
      <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">
       leaves
      </a>
      are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinnate" title="Pinnate">
       pinnate
      </a>
      , and reach between 3–5 m (10–16 ft) long. The
      <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">
       flowers
      </a>
      are produced in dense clusters; each individual flower is small, with three sepals and three petals.
     </p>
     <p>
      The palm fruit is reddish, about the size of a large plum, and grows in large bunches. Each fruit is made up of an oily, fleshy outer layer (the pericarp), with a single
      <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">
       seed
      </a>
      (the
      <a href="/wiki/Palm_kernel" title="Palm kernel">
       palm kernel
      </a>
      ), also rich in oil.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Species">
       Species
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elaeis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Species">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The two species,
      <i>
       E. guineensis
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       E. oleifera
      </i>
      can produce fertile
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">
       hybrids
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">
       genome
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       E. guineensis
      </i>
      has been sequenced, which has important implications for breeding improved strains of the crop plants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_palm_oil_cultivation">
       Commercial palm oil cultivation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elaeis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial palm oil cultivation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palm_Oil" title="Palm Oil">
       Palm Oil
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Since palm oil contains more
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturated_fats" title="Saturated fats">
       saturated fats
      </a>
      than oils made from canola, corn, linseed, soybeans, safflower, and sunflowers, it can withstand extreme deep-frying heat and resists oxidation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It contains no
      <a href="/wiki/Trans_fat" title="Trans fat">
       trans fat
      </a>
      , and its use in food has increased as food-labelling laws have changed to specify trans fat content. Oil from
      <i>
       Elaeis guineensis
      </i>
      is also used as
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
       biofuel
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Human use of oil palms may date back about 5,000 years in coastal west Africa. Palm oil was also discovered in the late 19th century by archaeologists in a tomb at
      <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">
       Abydos
      </a>
      dating back to 3000 BCE.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiple_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kiple-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is thought that Arab traders brought the oil palm to Egypt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obahiagbon_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Obahiagbon-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Elaeis guineensis
      </i>
      is now extensively cultivated in tropical countries outside Africa, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
       Malaysia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
       Indonesia
      </a>
      which together produce most of the world supply. Palm oil plantations are under increasing scrutiny for
      <a href="/wiki/Social_and_environmental_impact_of_palm_oil" title="Social and environmental impact of palm oil">
       social and environmental harm
      </a>
      , particularly because
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainforests" title="Rainforests">
       rainforests
      </a>
      with high
      <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">
       biodiversity
      </a>
      are destroyed,
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      output is increased, and because people are displaced by unscrupulous palm-oil enterprises. Especially in Indonesia, there is also growing pressure for palm oil producers to prove that they are not harming rare animals in the cultivation process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
