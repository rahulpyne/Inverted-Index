   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Atmospheric dispersion modeling
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:153px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AirPollutionSource.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="203" data-file-width="151" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b9/AirPollutionSource.jpg" width="151"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AirPollutionSource.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Industrial air pollution source
       </div>

     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Atmospheric dispersion modeling
      </b>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">
       mathematical simulation
      </a>
      of how
      <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">
       air pollutants
      </a>
      disperse in the ambient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
       atmosphere
      </a>
      . It is performed with computer programs that solve the mathematical equations and
      <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">
       algorithms
      </a>
      which simulate the pollutant dispersion. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollution_dispersion_terminology" title="Air pollution dispersion terminology">
       dispersion models
      </a>
      are used to estimate the downwind ambient
      <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">
       concentration
      </a>
      of air pollutants or toxins emitted from sources such as industrial plants, vehicular traffic or accidental chemical releases. They can also be used to predict future concentrations under specific scenarios (i.e. changes in emission sources). Therefore, they are the dominant type of model used in air quality policy making. They are most useful for pollutants that are dispersed over large distances and that may react in the atmosphere. For pollutants that have a very high spatio-temporal variability (i.e. have very steep distance to source decay such as
      <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">
       black carbon
      </a>
      ) and for
      <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">
       epidemiological
      </a>
      studies statistical land-use regression models are also used.
     </p>
     <p>
      Dispersion models are important to governmental agencies tasked with protecting and managing the ambient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">
       air quality
      </a>
      . The models are typically employed to determine whether existing or proposed new industrial facilities are or will be in compliance with the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Ambient_Air_Quality_Standards" title="National Ambient Air Quality Standards">
       National Ambient Air Quality Standards
      </a>
      (NAAQS) in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      and other nations. The models also serve to assist in the design of effective control strategies to reduce
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_standards" title="Emission standards">
       emissions
      </a>
      of harmful air pollutants. During the late 1960s, the Air Pollution Control Office of the U.S. EPA initiated research projects that would lead to the development of models for the use by urban and transportation planners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A major and significant application of a roadway dispersion model that resulted from such research was applied to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spadina_Expressway" title="Spadina Expressway">
       Spadina Expressway
      </a>
      of Canada in 1971.
     </p>
     <p>
      Air dispersion models are also used by public safety responders and emergency management personnel for emergency planning of accidental chemical releases. Models are used to determine the consequences of accidental releases of hazardous or toxic materials, Accidental releases may result in fires, spills or explosions that involve hazardous materials, such as chemicals or radionuclides. The results of dispersion modeling, using worst case
      <a href="/wiki/Accidental_release_source_terms" title="Accidental release source terms">
       accidental release source terms
      </a>
      and meteorological conditions, can provide an estimate of location impacted areas, ambient concentrations, and be used to determine protective actions appropriate in the event a release occurs. Appropriate protective actions may include evacuation or
      <a href="/wiki/Shelter_in_place" title="Shelter in place">
       shelter in place
      </a>
      for persons in the downwind direction. At industrial facilities, this type of consequence assessment or emergency planning is required under the
      <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">
       Clean Air Act (United States)
      </a>
      (CAA) codified in Part 68 of Title 40 of the
      <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">
       Code of Federal Regulations
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The dispersion models vary depending on the mathematics used to develop the model, but all require the input of data that may include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">
        Meteorological
       </a>
       conditions such as wind speed and direction, the amount of atmospheric
       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
        turbulence
       </a>
       (as characterized by what is called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollution_dispersion_terminology#Characterization_of_atmospheric_turbulence" title="Air pollution dispersion terminology">
        "stability class"
       </a>
       ), the ambient air temperature, the height to the bottom of any
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_inversion" title="Temperature inversion">
        inversion
       </a>
       aloft that may be present, cloud cover and solar radiation.
      </li>
      <li>
       Source term (the concentration or quantity of toxins in emission or
       <a href="/wiki/Accidental_release_source_terms" title="Accidental release source terms">
        accidental release source terms
       </a>
       ) and temperature of the material
      </li>
      <li>
       Emissions or release parameters such as source location and height, type of source (i.e., fire, pool or vent stack)and exit
       <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
        velocity
       </a>
       , exit temperature and
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow_rate" title="Mass flow rate">
        mass flow rate
       </a>
       or release rate.
      </li>
      <li>
       Terrain elevations at the source location and at the receptor location(s), such as nearby homes, schools, businesses and hospitals.
      </li>
      <li>
       The location, height and width of any obstructions (such as buildings or other structures) in the path of the emitted gaseous plume, surface roughness or the use of a more generic parameter "rural" or "city" terrain.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Many of the modern, advanced dispersion modeling programs include a
      <a href="/wiki/Preprocessor_(CAE)" title="Preprocessor (CAE)">
       pre-processor
      </a>
      module for the input of meteorological and other data, and many also include a post-processor module for graphing the output data and/or
      <a href="/wiki/Plot_(graphics)" title="Plot (graphics)">
       plotting
      </a>
      the area impacted by the air pollutants on maps. The plots of areas impacted may also include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isopleths" title="Isopleths">
       isopleths
      </a>
      showing areas of minimal to high concentrations that define areas of the highest health risk. The isopleths plots are useful in determining protective actions for the public and responders.
     </p>
     <p>
      The atmospheric dispersion models are also known as atmospheric diffusion models, air dispersion models, air quality models, and air pollution dispersion models.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_layers">
       Atmospheric layers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_dispersion_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Atmospheric layers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Discussion of the layers in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
       Earth's atmosphere
      </a>
      is needed to understand where airborne pollutants disperse in the atmosphere. The layer closest to the Earth's surface is known as the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
        troposphere
       </a>
      </i>
      . It extends from sea-level to a height of about 18 km and contains about 80 percent of the mass of the overall atmosphere. The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
        stratosphere
       </a>
      </i>
      is the next layer and extends from 18 km to about 50 km. The third layer is the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
        mesosphere
       </a>
      </i>
      which extends from 50 km to about 80 km. There are other layers above 80 km, but they are insignificant with respect to atmospheric dispersion modeling.
     </p>
     <p>
      The lowest part of the troposphere is called the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">
        atmospheric boundary layer (ABL)
       </a>
      </i>
      or the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">
        planetary boundary layer (PBL)
       </a>
      </i>
      and extends from the Earth's surface to about 1.5 to 2.0 km in height. The air temperature of the atmospheric boundary layer decreases with increasing altitude until it reaches what is called the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Capping_inversion" title="Capping inversion">
        inversion layer
       </a>
      </i>
      (where the temperature increases with increasing altitude) that caps the atmospheric boundary layer. The upper part of the troposphere (i.e., above the inversion layer) is called the
      <i>
       free troposphere
      </i>
      and it extends up to the 18 km height of the troposphere.
     </p>
     <p>
      The ABL is of the most important with respect to the emission, transport and dispersion of airborne pollutants. The part of the ABL between the Earth's surface and the bottom of the inversion layer is known as the mixing layer. Almost all of the airborne pollutants emitted into the ambient atmosphere are transported and dispersed within the mixing layer. Some of the emissions penetrate the inversion layer and enter the free troposphere above the ABL.
     </p>
     <p>
      In summary, the layers of the Earth's atmosphere from the surface of the ground upwards are: the ABL made up of the mixing layer capped by the inversion layer; the free troposphere; the stratosphere; the mesosphere and others. Many atmospheric dispersion models are referred to as
      <i>
       boundary layer models
      </i>
      because they mainly model air pollutant dispersion within the ABL. To avoid confusion, models referred to as
      <i>
       mesoscale models
      </i>
      have dispersion modeling capabilities that extend horizontally up to a few hundred kilometres. It does not mean that they model dispersion in the mesosphere.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gaussian_air_pollutant_dispersion_equation">
       Gaussian air pollutant dispersion equation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_dispersion_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gaussian air pollutant dispersion equation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The technical literature on air pollution dispersion is quite extensive and dates back to the 1930s and earlier. One of the early air pollutant plume dispersion equations was derived by Bosanquet and Pearson.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their equation did not assume
      <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">
       Gaussian distribution
      </a>
      nor did it include the effect of ground reflection of the pollutant plume.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sir Graham Sutton derived an air pollutant plume dispersion equation in 1947
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which did include the assumption of Gaussian distribution for the vertical and crosswind dispersion of the plume and also included the effect of ground reflection of the plume.
     </p>
     <p>
      Under the stimulus provided by the advent of stringent
      <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">
       environmental control regulations
      </a>
      , there was an immense growth in the use of air pollutant plume dispersion calculations between the late 1960s and today. A great many computer programs for calculating the dispersion of air pollutant emissions were developed during that period of time and they were called "air dispersion models". The basis for most of those models was the
      <b>
       Complete Equation For Gaussian Dispersion Modeling Of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollution_dispersion_terminology" title="Air pollution dispersion terminology">
        Continuous, Buoyant Air Pollution Plumes
       </a>
      </b>
      shown below:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beychok-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>

       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       H
      </i>
      <sub>
       s
      </sub>
      (the actual physical height of the pollutant plume's emission source point) plus Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      (the plume rise due the plume's buoyancy).
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:335px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gaussian_Plume_(SVG).svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="259" data-file-width="333" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gaussian_Plume_%28SVG%29.svg/333px-Gaussian_Plume_%28SVG%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gaussian_Plume_%28SVG%29.svg/500px-Gaussian_Plume_%28SVG%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gaussian_Plume_%28SVG%29.svg/666px-Gaussian_Plume_%28SVG%29.svg.png 2x" width="333"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gaussian_Plume_(SVG).svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Visualization of a buoyant Gaussian air pollutant dispersion plume
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      To determine Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      , many if not most of the air dispersion models developed between the late 1960s and the early 2000s used what are known as "the Briggs equations." G.A. Briggs first published his plume rise observations and comparisons in 1965.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1968, at a symposium sponsored by CONCAWE (a Dutch organization), he compared many of the plume rise models then available in the literature.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In that same year, Briggs also wrote the section of the publication edited by Slade
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      dealing with the comparative analyses of plume rise models. That was followed in 1969 by his classical critical review of the entire plume rise literature,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      in which he proposed a set of plume rise equations which have become widely known as "the Briggs equations". Subsequently, Briggs modified his 1969 plume rise equations in 1971 and in 1972.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Briggs divided air pollution plumes into these four general categories:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Cold jet plumes in calm ambient air conditions
      </li>
      <li>
       Cold jet plumes in windy ambient air conditions
      </li>
      <li>
       Hot, buoyant plumes in calm ambient air conditions
      </li>
      <li>
       Hot, buoyant plumes in windy ambient air conditions
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Briggs considered the trajectory of cold jet plumes to be dominated by their initial velocity momentum, and the trajectory of hot, buoyant plumes to be dominated by their buoyant momentum to the extent that their initial velocity momentum was relatively unimportant. Although Briggs proposed plume rise equations for each of the above plume categories,
      <i>
       <b>
        it is important to emphasize that "the Briggs equations" which become widely used are those that he proposed for bent-over, hot buoyant plumes
       </b>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In general, Briggs's equations for bent-over, hot buoyant plumes are based on observations and data involving plumes from typical combustion sources such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gas stacks
      </a>
      from steam-generating boilers burning
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuels
      </a>
      in large power plants. Therefore, the stack exit velocities were probably in the range of 20 to 100 ft/s (6 to 30 m/s) with exit temperatures ranging from 250 to 500 °F (120 to 260 °C).
     </p>
     <p>
      A logic diagram for using the Briggs equations
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beychok-4">
        [4]

     </p>

      </a>
