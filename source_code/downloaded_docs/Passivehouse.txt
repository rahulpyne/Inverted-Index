   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Passive house
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      <b>
       Passive house
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
       <i>
        Passivhaus
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is a rigorous, voluntary standard for
      <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">
       energy efficiency
      </a>
      in a
      <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">
       building
      </a>
      , reducing its
      <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">
       ecological footprint
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It results in
      <a href="/wiki/Low-energy_house" title="Low-energy house">
       ultra-low energy buildings
      </a>
      that require little energy for space heating or cooling.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25b_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25b-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A similar standard,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Minergie" title="Minergie">
        MINERGIE-P
       </a>
      </i>
      , is used in
      <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The standard is not confined to residential properties; several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Office_building" title="Office building">
       office buildings
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">
       schools
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">
       kindergartens
      </a>
      and a
      <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">
       supermarket
      </a>
      have also been constructed to the standard. Passive design is not an attachment or supplement to architectural design, but a design process that is integrated with architectural design.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plainiotis_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-plainiotis-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although it is mostly applied to new buildings, it has also been used for refurbishments.
     </p>
     <p>
      Estimates of the number of Passivhaus buildings around the world in late 2008 ranged from 15,000 to 20,000 structures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of August 2010, there were approximately 25,000 such certified structures of all types in Europe, while in the United States there were only 13, with a few dozen more under construction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The vast majority of passive structures have been built in German-speaking countries and
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
       Scandinavia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tmulti tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:308px;max-width:308px">
       <div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:152px;max-width:152px">
        <div class="thumbimage">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bo_Adamson.jpg">
          <img alt="" data-file-height="156" data-file-width="117" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Bo_Adamson.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
         Bo Adamson, co-originator of the passive house concept
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:152px;max-width:152px">
        <div class="thumbimage">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wolfgang_Feist.jpg">
          <img alt="" data-file-height="156" data-file-width="117" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/Wolfgang_Feist.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
         Wolfgang Feist, co-originator of the passive house concept, and founder of the Passivhaus Institut in Germany
        </div>
       </div>
       <div style="clear:left">
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Passivhaus standard originated from a conversation in May 1988 between Bo Adamson of
      <a href="/wiki/Lund_University" title="Lund University">
       Lund University
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      , and Wolfgang Feist of the Institut für Wohnen und Umwelt (Institute for Housing and the Environment,
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their concept was developed through a number of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Research_project" title="Research project">
       research projects
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      aided by financial assistance from the German state of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hessen" title="Hessen">
       Hessen
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_examples">
       First examples
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First examples">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The eventual building of four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Row_house" title="Row house">
       row houses
      </a>
      (terraced houses or town homes) was designed for four private clients by the
      <a href="/wiki/Architectural_firm" title="Architectural firm">
       architectural firm
      </a>
      of Bott, Ridder and Westermeyer. The first Passivhaus
      <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">
       residences
      </a>
      were built in
      <a href="/wiki/Darmstadt" title="Darmstadt">
       Darmstadt
      </a>
      , Germany in 1990, and occupied by the clients the following year.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Further_implementation_and_councils">
       Further implementation and councils
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Further implementation and councils">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In September 1996 the
      <a href="/wiki/Passivhaus-Institut" title="Passivhaus-Institut">
       Passivhaus-Institut
      </a>
      was founded, also in Darmstadt, to promote and control the standards. Since then, thousands of Passivhaus structures have been built, to an estimated 25,000+ as of 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most are located in Germany and
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Austria
      </a>
      , with others in various countries worldwide.
     </p>
     <p>
      After the concept had been validated at Darmstadt, with space heating 90% less than required for a standard new building of the time, the Economical Passive Houses Working Group was created in 1996. This group developed the planning package and initiated the production of the innovative components that had been used, notably the windows and the high-efficiency ventilation systems. Meanwhile, further passive houses were built in
      <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">
       Stuttgart
      </a>
      (1993),

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Standards">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Passivhaus_thermogram_gedaemmt_ungedaemmt.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="460" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Passivhaus_thermogram_gedaemmt_ungedaemmt.png/300px-Passivhaus_thermogram_gedaemmt_ungedaemmt.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Passivhaus_thermogram_gedaemmt_ungedaemmt.png/450px-Passivhaus_thermogram_gedaemmt_ungedaemmt.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f2/Passivhaus_thermogram_gedaemmt_ungedaemmt.png 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Passivhaus_thermogram_gedaemmt_ungedaemmt.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The dark colours on this
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermogram" title="Thermogram">
         thermogram
        </a>
        of a
        <b>
         Passive house
        </b>
        , at right, shows how little heat is escaping compared to a traditional building to the left

     <p>
      While some techniques and technologies were specifically developed for the Passive House standard, others, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Superinsulation" title="Superinsulation">
       superinsulation
      </a>
      , already existed, and the concept of
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">
       passive solar building design
      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-energy_building" title="Low-energy building">
       low-energy building
      </a>

      <i>
       Niedrigenergiehaus
      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
       Denmark
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Standards_2">
       Standards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Standards">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Passivhaus standard requires that the building fulfills the following requirements:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The building must be designed to have an annual heating and cooling demand as calculated with the Passivhaus Planning Package of not more than 15 kWh/m
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (4,755
       <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">
        BTU
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Square_foot" title="Square foot">
        sq ft
       </a>
       ; 5.017
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">
        MJ
       </a>
       /sq ft) per year in heating or cooling energy OR be designed with a peak heat load of 10 W/m
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (1.2 hp/1000 sq ft)
      </li>
      <li>
       Total
       <a href="/wiki/Primary_energy" title="Primary energy">
        primary energy
       </a>
       (source energy for electricity, etc.) consumption (primary energy for
       <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">
        heating
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Water_heating" title="Water heating">
        hot water
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
        electricity
       </a>
       ) must not be more than 120 kWh/m
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (38,040 BTU/sq ft; 40.13 MJ/sq ft) per year
      </li>
      <li>
       The building must not leak more air than 0.6 times the house volume per hour (n
       <sub>
        50
       </sub>
       ≤ 0.6 / hour) at 50 Pa (0.0073 psi) as tested by a
       <a href="/wiki/Blower_door" title="Blower door">
        blower door
       </a>
       , or alternatively when looked at the surface area of the enclosure, the leakage rate must be less than 0.05 cubic feet per minute
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recommendations">
       Recommendations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recommendations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Further, the specific heat load for the heating source at design temperature is recommended, but not required, to be less than 10
       <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
        W
       </a>
       /m² (3.17
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Btu" title="Btu">
        btu
       </a>
       /h.ft²).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      These standards are much higher than houses built to most normal building codes. For comparisons, see
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_house#International_comparisons" title="Passive house">
       the international comparisons section
      </a>
      below.
     </p>
     <p>
      National partners within the 'consortium for the Promotion of European Passive Houses' are thought to have some flexibility to adapt these limits locally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Space_heating_requirement">
       Space heating requirement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Space heating requirement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      By achieving the Passivhaus standards, qualified buildings are able to dispense with conventional heating systems. While this is an underlying objective of the Passivhaus standard, some type of heating will still be required and most Passivhaus buildings do include a system to provide supplemental space heating. This is normally distributed through the low-volume
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_ventilation" title="Heat recovery ventilation">
       heat recovery ventilation
      </a>
      system that is required to maintain air quality, rather than by a conventional hydronic or high-volume
      <a href="/wiki/Forced-air" title="Forced-air">
       forced-air
      </a>
      heating system, as described in the
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_house#Space_heating" title="Passive house">
       space heating
      </a>
      section below.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_costs">
       Construction costs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Construction costs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In Passivhaus buildings, the cost savings from dispensing with the conventional heating system can be used to fund the upgrade of the building envelope and the heat recovery ventilation system. With careful design and increasing competition in the supply of the specifically designed Passivhaus building products, in Germany it is now possible to construct buildings for the same cost as those built to normal German
      <a href="/wiki/Building_code" title="Building code">
       building standards
      </a>
      , as was done with the Passivhaus apartments at
      <a href="/wiki/Vauban,_Freiburg" title="Vauban, Freiburg">
       Vauban, Freiburg
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On average passive houses are reported to be more expensive upfront than conventional buildings - 5% to 8% in Germany,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      8% to 10% in UK
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and 5% to 10% in USA.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Evaluations have indicated that while it is technically possible, the costs of meeting the Passivhaus standard increase significantly when building in
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">
       Northern Europe
      </a>
      above 60°
      <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
       latitude
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
      European cities at approximately 60° include Helsinki in Finland and Bergen in Norway. London is at 51°; Moscow is at 55°.
     </p>
     <p>
      These facts have led a number of architects to construct buildings that use the ground under the building for massive heat storage to shift heat production from the winter to the summer. Some buildings can also shift cooling from the summer to the winter. At least one designer uses a passive thermosiphon carrying only air, so the process can be accomplished without expensive, unreliable machinery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (See also
      <a href="/wiki/Annualized_geo_solar" title="Annualized geo solar">
       Annualized geo solar
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">
       Design and construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Design and construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Passive_house_scheme_1.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="740" data-file-width="870" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Passive_house_scheme_1.svg/300px-Passive_house_scheme_1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Passive_house_scheme_1.svg/450px-Passive_house_scheme_1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Passive_house_scheme_1.svg/600px-Passive_house_scheme_1.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Passive_house_scheme_1.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <b>
         Passivhaus
        </b>
        uses a combination of
        <a href="/wiki/Category:Low-energy_building" title="Category:Low-energy building">
         low-energy building
        </a>
        techniques and technologies
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Achieving the major decrease in heating energy consumption required by the standard involves a shift in approach to building design and construction. Design may be assisted by use of the 'Passivhaus Planning Package' (PHPP),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which uses specifically designed
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">
       computer simulations
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      To achieve the standards, a number of techniques and technologies are used in combination:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25b_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25b-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_solar_design_and_landscape">
       Passive solar design and landscape
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Passive solar design and landscape">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">
       Passive solar building design
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Energy-efficient_landscaping" title="Energy-efficient landscaping">
       energy-efficient landscaping
      </a>
      support the Passive house energy conservation and can integrate them into a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood" title="Neighborhood">
       neighborhood
      </a>
      and environment. Following
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_low-energy_building_techniques" title="List of low-energy building techniques">
       passive solar building techniques
      </a>
      , where possible buildings are compact in shape to reduce their surface area, with principal windows oriented towards the equator - south in the northern hemisphere and north in the southern hemisphere - to maximize passive
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_gain" title="Solar gain">
       solar gain
      </a>
      . However, the use of solar gain, especially in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">
       temperate
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
       climate
      </a>
      regions, is secondary to minimizing the overall house energy requirements. In climates and regions needing to reduce excessive summer passive solar heat gain, whether from direct or reflected sources,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Brise_soleil" title="Brise soleil">
        Brise soleil
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">
       trees
      </a>
      , attached
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pergola" title="Pergola">
        pergolas
       </a>
      </i>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">
       vines
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_garden" title="Vertical garden">
       vertical gardens
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">
       green roofs
      </a>
      , and other techniques are implemented.
     </p>
     <p>
      Passive houses can be constructed from dense or lightweight materials, but some internal
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      is normally incorporated to reduce summer peak temperatures, maintain stable winter temperatures, and prevent possible overheating in spring or autumn before the higher
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">
       sun angle
      </a>
      "shades" mid-day wall exposure and window penetration. Exterior wall color, when the surface allows choice, for reflection or absorption
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">
       insolation
      </a>
      qualities depends on the predominant year-round ambient outdoor temperature. The use of
      <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">
       deciduous
      </a>
      trees and wall
      <a href="/wiki/Trellis_(architecture)" title="Trellis (architecture)">
       trellised
      </a>
      or self attaching vines can assist in climates not at the temperature extremes.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Superinsulation">
       Superinsulation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Superinsulation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Passivhaus buildings employ
      <a href="/wiki/Superinsulation" title="Superinsulation">
       superinsulation
      </a>
      to significantly reduce the heat transfer through the walls, roof and floor compared to conventional buildings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A wide range of
      <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">
       thermal insulation
      </a>
      materials can be used to provide the required high
      <a href="/wiki/R-value_(insulation)" title="R-value (insulation)">
       R-values
      </a>
      (low
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U-value" title="U-value">
       U-values
      </a>
      , typically in the 0.10 to 0.15 W/(m².K) range). Special attention is given to eliminating
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_bridge" title="Thermal bridge">
       thermal bridges
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A disadvantage resulting from the thickness of wall insulation required is that, unless the external dimensions of the building can be enlarged to compensate, the internal floor area of the building may be less compared to traditional construction.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Sweden, to achieve passive house standards, the insulation thickness would be 335 mm (about 13 in) (0.10 W/(m².K)) and the roof 500 mm (about 20 in) (U-value 0.066 W/(m².K)).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_window_technology">
       Advanced window technology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Advanced window technology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Passivhaus_Fenster_Beispiele_(blank).svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="530" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Passivhaus_Fenster_Beispiele_%28blank%29.svg/300px-Passivhaus_Fenster_Beispiele_%28blank%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Passivhaus_Fenster_Beispiele_%28blank%29.svg/450px-Passivhaus_Fenster_Beispiele_%28blank%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Passivhaus_Fenster_Beispiele_%28blank%29.svg/600px-Passivhaus_Fenster_Beispiele_%28blank%29.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Passivhaus_Fenster_Beispiele_(blank).svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Typical
        <b>
         Passive House
        </b>
        windows
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      To meet the requirements of the Passivhaus standard, windows are manufactured with exceptionally high
      <a href="/wiki/R-value_(insulation)" title="R-value (insulation)">
       R-values
      </a>
      (low U-values, typically 0.85 to 0.70 W/(m².K) for the entire window including the frame). These normally combine triple-pane
      <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">
       insulated glazing
      </a>
      (with a good solar heat-gain coefficient,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25b_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25b-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_40-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-emissivity" title="Low-emissivity">
       low-emissivity
      </a>
      coatings,
      <a href="/wiki/Hermetic_seal" title="Hermetic seal">
       sealed
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">
       argon
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">
       krypton
      </a>
      gas filled inter-pane voids, and 'warm edge' insulating glass spacers) with air-seals and specially developed thermally broken window frames.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">
       Central Europe
      </a>
      and most of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , for unobstructed south-facing Passivhaus windows, the heat gains from the sun are, on average, greater than the heat losses, even in mid-winter.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Airtightness">
       Airtightness
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Airtightness">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Building envelopes under the Passivhaus standard are required to be extremely
      <a href="/wiki/Hermetic_seal" title="Hermetic seal">
       airtight
      </a>
      compared to conventional construction. They are required to meet either 0.60 ACH50 (air changes per hour at 50 pascals) based on the building's volume, or 0.05 CFM50/sf (cubic feet per minute at 50 pascals, per square foot of building enclosure surface area). In order to achieve these metrics, recommended best practice is to test the building air barrier enclosure with a
      <a href="/wiki/Blower_door" title="Blower door">
       blower door
      </a>
      at
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.ecoachievers.com/notable-projects/pleasantly-tight-times-mf-passive-house/" rel="nofollow">
       mid-construction
      </a>
      if possible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25b_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25b-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Passive house is designed so that most of the air exchange with exterior is done by controlled ventilation through a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat-exchanger" title="Heat-exchanger">
       heat-exchanger
      </a>
      in order to minimize heat loss (or gain, depending on climate), so uncontrolled air leaks are best avoided.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25b_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25b-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another reason is the passive house standard makes extensive use of insulation which usually requires a careful management of
      <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">
       moisture
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">
       dew points
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        [41]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is achieved through air barriers, careful sealing of every construction joint in the building envelope, and sealing of all service penetrations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_40-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ventilation">
       Ventilation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ventilation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Use of passive
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_ventilation" title="Natural ventilation">
       natural ventilation
      </a>
      is an integral component of passive house design where ambient temperature is conducive — either by singular or cross ventilation, by a simple opening or enhanced by the
      <a href="/wiki/Stack_effect" title="Stack effect">
       stack effect
      </a>
      from smaller ingress with larger egress windows and/or
      <a href="/wiki/Clerestory" title="Clerestory">
       clerestory
      </a>
      -operable
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skylight_(window)" title="Skylight (window)">
       skylight
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      When ambient climate is not conducive, mechanical
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_ventilation" title="Heat recovery ventilation">
       heat recovery ventilation
      </a>
      systems, with a heat recovery rate of over 80% and high-efficiency
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronically_commutated_motor" title="Electronically commutated motor">
       electronically commutated motors
      </a>
      (ECM), are employed to maintain air quality, and to recover sufficient heat to dispense with a conventional central heating system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25b_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25b-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since passively designed buildings are essentially
      <a href="/wiki/Hermetic_seal" title="Hermetic seal">
       air-tight
      </a>
      , the rate of air change can be optimized and carefully controlled at about 0.4
      <a href="/wiki/Air_changes_per_hour" title="Air changes per hour">
       air changes per hour
      </a>
      . All ventilation ducts are insulated and sealed against leakage.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some Passivhaus builders promote the use of
      <a href="/wiki/Ground-coupled_heat_exchanger" title="Ground-coupled heat exchanger">
       earth warming tubes
      </a>
      (typically ≈200 mm (~7,9 in) diameter, ≈40 m (~130 ft) long at a depth of ≈1.5 m (~5 ft)). These are buried in the soil to act as earth-to-air heat exchangers and pre-heat (or pre-cool) the intake air for the ventilation system. In cold weather the warmed air also prevents
      <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
       ice
      </a>
      formation in the heat recovery system's
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchanger
      </a>
      . Concerns about this technique have arisen in some climates due to problems with condensation and mold.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uninhabitable_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Uninhabitable-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Alternatively, an earth to air heat exchanger can use a liquid circuit instead of an air circuit, with a heat exchanger (battery) on the supply air.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Space_heating">
       Space heating
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Space heating">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Passivhaus_heating_de_Kompakt.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="631" data-file-width="666" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Passivhaus_heating_de_Kompakt.png/300px-Passivhaus_heating_de_Kompakt.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Passivhaus_heating_de_Kompakt.png/450px-Passivhaus_heating_de_Kompakt.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Passivhaus_heating_de_Kompakt.png/600px-Passivhaus_heating_de_Kompakt.png 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Passivhaus_heating_de_Kompakt.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <b>
         Passivhaus
        </b>
        : In addition to the heat exchanger (centre), a micro-heat pump extracts heat from the exhaust air (left) and hot water heats the ventilation air (right). The ability to control building temperature using only the normal volume of ventilation air is fundamental.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In addition to using passive
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_gain" title="Solar gain">
       solar gain
      </a>
      , Passivhaus buildings make extensive use of their intrinsic heat from internal sources—such as waste heat from lighting,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_goods" title="White goods">
       white goods
      </a>
      (major appliances) and other electrical devices (but not dedicated heaters)—as well as body heat from the people and other animals inside the building. This is due to the fact that people, on average, emit heat equivalent to 100
      <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
       watts
      </a>
      each of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Body#Radiation_emitted_by_a_human_body" title="Black Body">
       radiated thermal energy
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Together with the comprehensive
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">
       energy conservation
      </a>
      measures taken, this means that a conventional
      <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">
       central heating
      </a>
      system is not necessary, although they are sometimes installed due to client skepticism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-2010.09.25.d_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-2010.09.25.d-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Instead, Passive houses sometimes have a dual purpose 800 to 1,500
      <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
       watt
      </a>
      heating and/or cooling element integrated with the supply air duct of the ventilation system, for use during the coldest days. It is fundamental to the design that all the heat required can be transported by the normal low air volume required for ventilation. A maximum air temperature of 50 °C (122 °F) is applied, to prevent any possible smell of scorching from dust that escapes the filters in the system.
     </p>
     <p>
      The air-heating element can be heated by a small
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">
       heat pump
      </a>
      , by direct
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">
       solar thermal energy
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annualized_geothermal_solar" title="Annualized geothermal solar">
       annualized geothermal solar
      </a>
      , or simply by a
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_burner" title="Oil burner">
       oil burner
      </a>
      . In some cases a micro-heat pump is used to extract additional heat from the exhaust ventilation air, using it to heat either the incoming air or the
      <a href="/wiki/Hot_water_storage_tank" title="Hot water storage tank">
       hot water storage tank
      </a>
      . Small wood-burning stoves can also be used to heat the water tank, although care is required to ensure that the room in which stove is located does not overheat.
     </p>
     <p>
      Beyond the recovery of heat by the heat recovery ventilation unit, a well designed Passive house in the European climate should not need any supplemental heat source if the heating load is kept under 10W/m².
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Because the heating capacity and the heating energy required by a passive house both are very low, the particular
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_energy_resources" title="List of energy resources">
       energy source
      </a>
      selected has fewer financial implications than in a traditional building, although
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      sources are well suited to such low loads.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Passive House Standards in Europe determine a Space Heating and cooling Energy Demand of 15 kilowatt hours per square meter of Treated Floor Area per year or 10 Watts per square meter peak demand. (Or in Imperial units 4.75 kBTU/sf*yr and 3.2 BTU/hr*sf respectively.) In addition, the total energy to be used in the building operations including heating, cooling, lighting, equipment, hot water, plug loads, etc. is limited to 120 kilowatt hours per square meter of Treated Floor Area per year. (Or in Imperial units 38.0 BTU/sf*yr.)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Traits_of_passive_houses">
       Traits of passive houses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Traits of passive houses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Typically, passive houses feature:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Fresh, clean air: Note that for the parameters tested, and provided the filters (minimum F6) are maintained,
       <a href="/wiki/HEPA" title="HEPA">
        HEPA
       </a>
       quality air is provided. 0.3 air changes per hour (ACH) are recommended, otherwise the air can become "stale" (excess CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , flushing of indoor air pollutants) and any greater, excessively dry (less than 40% humidity). This implies careful selection of interior finishes and furnishings, to minimize indoor air pollution from
       <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">
        VOC
       </a>
       's (e.g.,
       <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">
        formaldehyde
       </a>
       ). This can be counteracted somewhat by opening a window for a very brief time, by plants, and by indoor fountains.
      </li>
      <li>
       Because of the high resistance to heat flow (high R-value insulation), there are no "outside walls" which are colder than other walls.
      </li>
      <li>
       Homogeneous interior temperature: it is impossible to have single rooms (e.g. the sleeping rooms) at a different temperature from the rest of the house. Note that the relatively high temperature of the sleeping areas is physiologically not considered desirable by some building scientists. Bedroom windows can be cracked open slightly to alleviate this when necessary.
      </li>
      <li>
       Slow temperature changes: with ventilation and heating systems switched off, a passive house typically loses less than 0.5 °C (1 °F) per day (in winter), stabilizing at around 15 °C (59 °F) in the central European climate.
      </li>
      <li>
       Quick return to normal temperature: opening windows or doors for a short time has only a limited effect; after apertures are closed, the air very quickly returns to the "normal" temperature.
      </li>
      <li>
       Some have voiced concerns that Passivhaus is not a general approach as the occupant has to behave in a prescribed way, for example not opening windows too often. However modelling shows that such concerns are not valid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="International_comparisons">
       International comparisons
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: International comparisons">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       , a house built to the Passive House standard results in a building that requires space heating energy of 1
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BTU" title="BTU">
        BTU
       </a>
       per square foot (11 kJ/m²) per
       <a href="/wiki/Heating_degree_day" title="Heating degree day">
        heating degree day
       </a>
       , compared with about 5 to 15 BTUs per square foot (56-170 kJ/m²) per heating degree day for a similar building built to meet the 2003 Model Energy Efficiency Code. This is between 75 and 95% less energy for space heating and cooling than current new buildings that meet today's US energy efficiency codes. The Passivhaus in the German-language camp of
       <a href="/wiki/Waldsee_(camp)" title="Waldsee (camp)">
        Waldsee
       </a>
       , Minnesota was designed under the guidance of architect Stephan Tanner of INTEP, LLC, a Minneapolis- and Munich-based consulting company for high performance and sustainable construction. Waldsee BioHaus is modeled on Germany’s Passivhaus standard: beyond that of the U.S. LEED standard which improves quality of life inside the building while using 85% less energy than a house built to Minnesota building codes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       VOLKsHouse 1.0 was the first certified Passive House offered and sold in Santa Fe New Mexico.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       , an average new house built to the Passive House standard would use 77% less energy for space heating, compared to the circa-2006
       <a href="/wiki/Energy_efficiency_in_British_housing" title="Energy efficiency in British housing">
        Building Regulations
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">
        Ireland
       </a>
       , it is calculated that a typical house built to the Passive House standard instead of the 2002 Building Regulations would consume 85% less energy for space heating and cut space-heating related
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" title="Carbon emissions">
        carbon emissions
       </a>
       by 94%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_zero_energy_buildings">
       Comparison with zero energy buildings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Comparison with zero energy buildings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Zero-energy_building" title="Zero-energy building">
       Zero-energy building
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A net zero-energy building (ZEB) is a building that over a year does not use more energy than it generates. The first 1979 Zero Energy Design building used passive solar heating and cooling techniques with air-tight construction and super insulation. A few ZEB’s fail to fully exploit more affordable conservation technology and all use onsite active
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      technologies like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Building-integrated_photovoltaic" title="Building-integrated photovoltaic">
       photovoltaic
      </a>
      to offset the building's primary energy consumption. Passive House and ZEB are complementary synergistic technology approaches, based on the same physics of thermal energy transfer and storage: ZEBs drive the annual energy consumption down to 0 kWh/m² with help from on-site renewable energy sources and can benefit from materials and methods which are used to meet the Passive House demand constraint of 120 kWh/m² which will minimize the need for the often costly on-site renewable energy sources.
      <a href="/wiki/Energy-plus-house" title="Energy-plus-house">
       Energy Plus houses
      </a>
      are similar to both PassivHaus and ZEB but emphasize the production of more energy per year than they consume, e.g., annual energy performance of -25 kWh/m² is an Energy Plus house.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_climate_needs">
       Tropical climate needs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Tropical climate needs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In a tropical climate, it could be helpful for ideal internal conditions to use
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery_ventilation" title="Energy recovery ventilation">
       Energy Recovery Ventilation
      </a>
      instead of
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_ventilation" title="Heat recovery ventilation">
       Heat Recovery Ventilation
      </a>
      to reduce the humidity load of ventilation on the mechanical dehumidification system. Although dehumidifiers might be used, heat pump hot water heaters also will act to cool and condense interior humidity (where it can be dumped into
      <a href="/wiki/Drain_(plumbing)" title="Drain (plumbing)">
       drains
      </a>
      ) and dump the heat into the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_water_tank" title="Hot water tank">
       hot water tank
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_cooling" title="Passive cooling">
       Passive cooling
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_air_conditioning" title="Solar air conditioning">
       solar air conditioning
      </a>
      , and other solutions in
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">
       passive solar building design
      </a>
      need to be studied to adapt the Passive house concept for use in more regions of the world.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a certified Passive House in the hot and humid climate of
      <a href="/wiki/Lafayette,_Louisiana" title="Lafayette, Louisiana">
       Lafayette
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">
       Louisiana
      </a>
      , USA, which uses
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery_ventilation" title="Energy recovery ventilation">
       Energy Recovery Ventilation
      </a>
      and an efficient one ton air-conditioner to provide cooling and dehumidification.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        [52]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Solar access is a very important factor in any design of a passive house as it allows the structure to use the solar energy to heat and light the space naturally, replace electrical water heaters with solar-energy-based water heaters.
     </p>
