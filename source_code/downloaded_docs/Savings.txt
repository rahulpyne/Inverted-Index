     <p>
      <b>
       Wealth
      </b>
      is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abundance_(economics)" title="Abundance (economics)">
       abundance
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">
       valuable
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">
       resources
      </a>
      or valuable material
      <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">
       possessions
      </a>
      . This includes the core meaning as held in the originating old English word
      <i>
       weal
      </i>
      , which is from an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European" title="Indo-European">
       Indo-European
      </a>
      word stem.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      An individual, community, region or country that possesses an abundance of such possessions or resources to the benefit of the common good is known as wealthy.
     </p>
     <p>
      The modern concept of wealth is of significance in all areas of
      <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
       economics
      </a>
      , and clearly so for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Growth_economics" title="Growth economics">
       growth economics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Development_economics" title="Development economics">
       development economics
      </a>
      yet the meaning of wealth is context-dependent. At the most general level, economists may define wealth as "anything of value" that captures both the subjective nature of the idea and the idea that it is not a fixed or static concept. Various definitions and concepts of wealth have been asserted by various individuals and in different contexts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goulet_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-goulet-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Defining wealth can be a
      <a href="/wiki/Normative" title="Normative">
       normative
      </a>
      process with various
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethical" title="Ethical">
       ethical
      </a>
      implications, since often wealth maximization is seen as a goal or is thought to be a normative principle of its own.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heilbroner_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Heilbroner-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
       United Nations
      </a>
      definition of
      <i>
       inclusive wealth
      </i>
      is a monetary measure which includes the sum of natural, human, and physical assets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wealth_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wealth-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inclusive_Wealth_Report_-_IHDP_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Inclusive_Wealth_Report_-_IHDP-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Natural capital includes land, forests,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">
       fossil fuels
      </a>
      , and minerals. Human capital is the population's education and skills. Physical (or "manufactured") capital includes such things as machinery, buildings, and infrastructure.
      <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">
       Qatar
      </a>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_wealth" title="List of countries by wealth">
       wealthiest country
      </a>
      in the world per capital.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wealth">
       Wealth
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Wealth">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">
       Adam Smith
      </a>
      , in his seminal work
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">
        The Wealth of Nations
       </a>
      </i>
      , described wealth as "the annual produce of the land and labour of the society". This "produce" is, at its simplest, that which satisfies human needs and wants of
      <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">
       utility
      </a>
      . In popular usage, wealth can be described as an abundance of items of economic
      <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">
       value
      </a>
      , or the state of controlling or possessing such items, usually in the form of
      <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">
       money
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">
       real estate
      </a>
      and personal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_(ownership_right)" title="Property (ownership right)">
       property
      </a>
      . An individual who is considered wealthy, affluent, or rich is someone who has accumulated substantial wealth relative to others in their society or reference group.
     </p>
     <p>
      In economics,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wealth_(economics)" title="Wealth (economics)">
       net worth
      </a>
      refers to the value of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assets" title="Assets">
       assets
      </a>
      owned minus the value of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liability_(accounting)" title="Liability (accounting)">
       liabilities
      </a>
      owed at a point in time.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Wealth can be categorized into three principal categories:
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_property" title="Personal property">
       personal property
      </a>
      , including homes or automobiles; monetary savings, such as the accumulation of past
      <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">
       income
      </a>
      ; and the
      <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">
       capital
      </a>
      wealth of income producing assets, including
      <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">
       real estate
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Stocks" title="Stocks">
       stocks
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">
       bonds
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">
       businesses
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      All these delineations make wealth an especially important part of
      <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">
       social stratification
      </a>
      . Wealth provides a type of individual safety net of protection against an unforeseen decline in one's living standard in the event of job loss or other emergency and can be transformed into home ownership, business ownership, or even a college education.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      'Wealth' refers to some
      <i>
       accumulation
      </i>
      of resources (net asset value), whether abundant or not. 'Richness' refers to an
      <i>
       abundance
      </i>
      of such resources (income or flow). A wealthy individual, community, or nation thus has more accumulated resources (capital) than a poor one. The opposite of wealth is destitution. The opposite of richness is
      <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">
       poverty
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The term implies a
      <a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">
       social contract
      </a>
      on establishing and maintaining
      <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">
       ownership
      </a>
      in relation to such items which can be invoked with little or no effort and expense on the part of the owner. The concept of wealth is relative and not only varies between societies, but varies between different sections or regions in the same society. A personal
      <a href="/wiki/Net_worth" title="Net worth">
       net worth
      </a>
      of US $10,000 in most parts of the United States would certainly not place a person among the wealthiest citizens of that locale. However, such an amount would constitute an extraordinary amount of wealth in impoverished
      <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">
       developing countries
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Concepts of wealth also vary across time. Modern labor-saving inventions and the development of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sciences" title="Sciences">
       sciences
      </a>
      have vastly improved the standard of living in modern societies for even the poorest of people. This comparative wealth across time is also applicable to the future; given this trend
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      of human advancement, it is possible that the standard of living that the wealthiest enjoy today will be considered impoverished by future generations.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">
       Industrialization
      </a>
      emphasized the role of technology. Many jobs were automated. Machines replaced some workers while other workers became more specialized.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_specialization" title="Labour specialization">
       Labour specialization
      </a>
      became critical to economic success. However,
      <a href="/wiki/Physical_capital" title="Physical capital">
       physical capital
      </a>
      , as it came to be known, consisting of both the
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">
       natural capital
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrastructural_capital" title="Infrastructural capital">
       infrastructural capital
      </a>
      , became the focus of the
      <i>
       analysis of wealth
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">
       Adam Smith
      </a>
      saw wealth creation as the combination of materials, labour, land, and technology in such a way as to capture a profit (excess above the cost of production).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gutenberg1_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gutenberg1-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The theories of
      <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">
       David Ricardo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">
       John Locke
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">
       John Stuart Mill
      </a>
      , in the 18th century and 19th century built on these views of wealth that we now call
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">
       classical economics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxian" title="Marxian">
       Marxian
      </a>
      economics (
      <i>
       see
       <a href="/wiki/Labor_theory_of_value" title="Labor theory of value">
        labor theory of value
       </a>
      </i>
      ) distinguishes in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Grundrisse" title="Grundrisse">
        Grundrisse
       </a>
      </i>
      between material wealth and human wealth, defining human wealth as "wealth in human relations"; land and labour were the source of all material wealth. The German cultural historian Silvio Vietta links wealth/poverty to rationality. Having a leading position in the development of rational sciences, in new technologies and in economic production leads to wealth, while the opposite can be correlated with
      <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">
       poverty
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_Vietta_2013_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Silvio_Vietta_2013-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_Vietta_2012_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Silvio_Vietta_2012-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Amount_of_wealth_in_the_world">
       Amount of wealth in the world
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amount of wealth in the world">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The wealth of households amounts to USD 241
      <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#1012" title="Orders of magnitude (numbers)">
       trillion
      </a>
      (2013) and is estimated to increase by 40% in five years. The U.S. net worth of $100 trillion and Switzerland's USD 0.51 million/inhabitant are the highest numbers in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Credit Suisse Wealth Report (mid-2013) estimated that, once debts had been subtracted, an adult required just USD 4,000 in assets to be within the wealthiest 50% of world citizens. However, at least USD 75,000 was needed to reach the top 10%, and USD 753,000 to belong to the most wealthy 1%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Tim_Harford" title="Tim Harford">
       Tim Harford
      </a>
      has asserted that a small child has greater wealth than the 2 billion poorest people in the world combined, since a small child has no debt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophical_analysis">
       Philosophical analysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Philosophical analysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In Western civilization, wealth is connected with a quantitative type of thought, invented in the ancient Greek "revolution of rationality", involving for instance the quantitative analysis of nature, the rationalization of warfare, and measurement in economics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_Vietta_2013_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Silvio_Vietta_2013-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_Vietta_2012_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Silvio_Vietta_2012-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The invention of coined money and banking was particularly important. Aristotle describes the basic function of money as a universal instrument of quantitative measurement – “for it measures all things […]”– making things alike and comparable due to a social "agreement" of acceptance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In that way, money also enables a new type of economic society and the definition of wealth in measurable quantities. In the Roman Empire, just as in modern colonialism, the main force behind the conquest of countries was the exploitation and accumulation of wealth in quantitative values like gold and money. Modern philosophers like Nietzsche criticized the fixation on measurable wealth: "Unsere ‘Reichen' – das sind die Ärmsten! Der eigentliche Zweck alles Reichtums ist vergessen!" (“Our 'rich people' – those are the poorest! The real purpose of all wealth has been forgotten!”)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_analysis">
       Economic analysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic analysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        "Savings" redirects here. For the concept of non-expenditure of income per unit of time, see
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Saving" title="Saving">
        Saving
       </a>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
       economics
      </a>
      , wealth (in a commonly applied
      <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">
       accounting
      </a>
      sense) (sometimes
      <b>
       savings
      </b>
      ) is the
      <a href="/wiki/Net_worth" title="Net worth">
       net worth
      </a>
      of a person, household, or nation, that is, the value of all
      <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">
       assets
      </a>
      owned net of all
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liability_(accounting)" title="Liability (accounting)">
       liabilities
      </a>
      owed at a point in time. For national wealth as measured in the
      <a href="/wiki/National_accounts" title="National accounts">
       national accounts
      </a>
      , the net liabilities are those owed to the rest of the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The term may also be used more broadly as referring to the productive capacity of a society or as a contrast to
      <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">
       poverty
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Analytical emphasis may be on its determinants or
      <a href="/wiki/Distribution_of_wealth" title="Distribution of wealth">
       distribution
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Economic terminology distinguishes between wealth and income. Wealth or savings is a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Stock_and_flow" title="Stock and flow">
        stock
       </a>
      </i>
      variable, that is, measurable
      <i>
       at a date
      </i>
      in time, for example the value of an orchard on December 31 minus debt owed on the orchard. For a given amount of wealth, say at the beginning of the year,
      <a href="/wiki/Income#Economic_definitions" title="Income">
       income
      </a>
      from that wealth, as measurable
      <i>
       over
      </i>
      say a year is a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Stock_and_flow" title="Stock and flow">
        flow
       </a>
      </i>
      variable. What marks the income as a flow is its measurement per unit of time, such as the value of apples yielded from the orchard per year.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" title="Macroeconomic">
       macroeconomic
      </a>
      theory the '
      <a href="/wiki/Wealth_effect" title="Wealth effect">
       wealth effect
      </a>
      ' may refer to the increase in aggregate consumption from an increase in national wealth. One measure of it is the
      <a href="/wiki/Wealth_elasticity_of_demand" title="Wealth elasticity of demand">
       wealth elasticity of demand
      </a>
      . It is the percentage change in the amount demanded of
      <a href="/wiki/National_Income_and_Product_Accounts#Accounting_for_national_product:_the_right_side_of_the_report" title="National Income and Product Accounts">
       consumption
      </a>
      for each one-percent change in wealth.
     </p>
     <p>
      Wealth may be measured in
      <a href="/wiki/Real_versus_nominal_value_(economics)" title="Real versus nominal value (economics)">
       nominal or real values
      </a>
      , that is in money value as of a given date or adjusted to net out price changes. The assets include those that are tangible (
      <a href="/wiki/Land_(economics)" title="Land (economics)">
       land
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">
       capital
      </a>
      ) and
      <i>
       financial
      </i>
      (money, bonds, etc.). Measurable wealth typically excludes intangible or nonmarketable assets such as
      <a href="/wiki/Human_capital" title="Human capital">
       human capital
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Social_capital" title="Social capital">
       social capital
      </a>
      . In economics, 'wealth' corresponds to the
      <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">
       accounting
      </a>
      term '
      <a href="/wiki/Net_worth" title="Net worth">
       net worth
      </a>
      '. But analysis may adapt typical accounting conventions for economic purposes in social accounting (such as in
      <a href="/wiki/National_accounts" title="National accounts">
       national accounts
      </a>
      ). An example of the latter is
      <a href="/wiki/Generational_accounting" title="Generational accounting">
       generational accounting
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">
       social security
      </a>
      systems to include the
      <a href="/wiki/Present_value" title="Present value">
       present value
      </a>
      projected future outlays considered to be liabilities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Macroeconomic questions include whether the issuance of government bonds affects
      <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">
       investment
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/National_Income_and_Product_Accounts#Accounting_for_national_product:_the_right_side_of_the_report" title="National Income and Product Accounts">
       consumption
      </a>
      through the
      <a href="/wiki/Wealth_effect" title="Wealth effect">
       wealth effect
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_assets" title="Environmental assets">
       Environmental assets
      </a>
      are not usually counted in measuring wealth, in part due to the difficulty of valuation for a
      <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">
       non-market good
      </a>
      . Environmental or
      <a href="/wiki/Green_accounting" title="Green accounting">
       green accounting
      </a>
      is a method of
      <a href="/wiki/National_accounts" title="National accounts">
       social accounting
      </a>
      for formulating and deriving such measures on the argument that an educated valuation is superior to a value of zero (as the implied valuation of environmental assets).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wealth_and_social_class">
       Wealth and social class
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wealth and social class">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">
       Social class
      </a>
      is not identical to wealth, but the two concepts are related (particularly in
      <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">
       Marxist theory
      </a>
      ), leading to the combined concept of
      <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_status" title="Socioeconomic status">
       Socioeconomic status
      </a>
      . Wealth refers to value of everything a person or family owns. This includes tangible items such as jewelry, housing, cars, and other personal property. Financial assets such as stocks and bonds, which can be traded for cash, also contribute to wealth. Wealth is measured as “net assets,” minus how much debt one owes. Wealth is a restrictive agent for people of different classes because some hobbies can only be participated in by the affluent, such as world travel.
     </p>
     <p>
      Partly as a result of different economic conditions of life, members of different
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_classes" title="Social classes">
       social classes
      </a>
      often have different value systems and view the world in different ways. As such, there exist different "conceptions of social reality, different aspirations and hopes and fears, different conceptions of the desirable."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The way the various social classes in society view wealth vary and these diverse characteristics are a fundamental dividing line among the classes. According to Richard H Ropers, the
      <a href="/wiki/Wealth_inequality_in_the_United_States" title="Wealth inequality in the United States">
       concentration of wealth in the United States
      </a>
      is inequitably distributed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1996, the United States federal government reported that the net worth of the top 1 percent of people in the United States was approximately equal to that of the bottom 90 percent. Cross-nationally, the United States has greater wealth inequality than other developed nations.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_upper_class">
       The upper class
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The upper class">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Upper class encompasses the top end of the income spectrum relative members of society as a whole. Since they have more wealth and privacy, the upper class has more personal autonomy than the rest of the population.
      <a href="/wiki/Upper_class" title="Upper class">
       Upper class
      </a>
      values include higher education, and the wealthiest people the accumulation and maintenance of wealth, the maintenance of social networks and the power that accompanies such networks. Children of the upper class are typically schooled on how to manage this power and channel this privilege in different forms. It is in large part by accessing various edifices of information,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2011)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      associates, procedures and auspices that the upper class are able to maintain their wealth and pass it to future generations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherraden.2C_Michael_1991_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sherraden.2C_Michael_1991-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Usually, people of the upper class participate as partisans in elections and have more political power than those of lower classes due to their abundance of resources and influence.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_middle_class">
       The middle class
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The middle class">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Middle class encompasses the individuals who fall in between the upper and lower class relative to their financial situation. Generally, the population of America associates themselves as middle class. Lifestyle is a means for which individuals or families decide what to consume with their money and their way of living. The middle class places a greater emphasis on income: unlike the upper class, the middle class measures success and potential in the form of money [relative pennies] rather than influence and power. The
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">
       middle class
      </a>
      views wealth as something for emergencies and it is seen as more of a cushion. This class comprises people that were raised with families that typically owned their own home, planned ahead and stressed the importance of education and achievement. They earn a significant amount of income and also have significant amounts of consumption. However, there is very limited savings (deferred consumption) or investments, besides retirement
      <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">
       pensions
      </a>
      and home ownership. They have been socialized to accumulate wealth through structured, institutionalized arrangements. Without this set structure, asset accumulation would likely not occur.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherraden.2C_Michael_1991_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sherraden.2C_Michael_1991-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_lower_class">
       The lower class
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The lower class">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Those with the least amount of wealth are the poor. Most of the institutions that the poor encounter discourage any accumulation of assets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherraden.2C_Michael_1991_24-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sherraden.2C_Michael_1991-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lower class members feel more restrictive in their options due to their lack of wealth. This could lead to complications in solving their personal dilemmas, as predicted by the Class Structure Hypothesis. There are many societal standards and designs intentional sabotage and shortcomings to explain the persistent state of yearning and want the lower classes generally experience with their lower quality and quantity of assets. Typical causes are persistent unethical/harmful mentalities and criminal tendencies: misguidedly similar to the upper class in some cases.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">
       Distribution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Distribution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Distribution_of_wealth" title="Distribution of wealth">
       Distribution of wealth
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although precise data are not available, the total
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Household_wealth" title="Household wealth">
       household wealth
      </a>
      in the world, excluding
      <a href="/wiki/Human_capital" title="Human capital">
       human capital
      </a>
      , has been estimated at $125
      <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#1012" title="Orders of magnitude (numbers)">
       trillion
      </a>
      (USD 125×10
      <sup>
       12
      </sup>
      ) in year 2000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Including human capital, the United Nations estimated it in 2008 to be $118 trillion in the United States alone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wealth_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wealth-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inclusive_Wealth_Report_-_IHDP_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Inclusive_Wealth_Report_-_IHDP-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to the Kuznet’s Hypothesis, inequality of wealth and income increases during the early phases of economic development, stabilizes and then becomes more equitable.
     </p>
     <p>
      About 90% of global wealth is distributed in North America, Europe, and "rich
      <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">
       Asia-Pacific
      </a>
      " countries (not including India),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and in 2008, 1% of adults were estimated to hold 40% of world wealth, a number which falls to 32% when adjusted for
      <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">
       purchasing power parity
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2013, 1% of adults were estimated to hold 46% of world wealth
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and around $18.5
      <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#1012" title="Orders of magnitude (numbers)">
       trillion
      </a>
      was estimated to be stored in
      <a href="/wiki/Tax_haven" title="Tax haven">
       tax havens
      </a>
      worldwide.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wealth_in_the_form_of_land">
       Wealth in the form of land
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wealth in the form of land">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Land_law" title="Land law">
       Land law
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the western tradition, the concepts of owning land and accumulating wealth in the form of land were engendered in the rise of the first
      <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">
       state
      </a>
      , for a primary service and power of government was, and is to this day, the awarding and
      <a href="/wiki/Adjudication" title="Adjudication">
       adjudication
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_use_rights" title="Land use rights">
       land use rights
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Land ownership was also justified according to
      <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">
       John Locke
      </a>
      . He claimed that because we mix
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2011)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      our labour with the land, we thereby deserve the right to control the use of the land and benefit from the product of that land (but subject to his
      <a href="/wiki/Lockean_proviso" title="Lockean proviso">
       Lockean proviso
      </a>
      of "at least where there is enough, and as good left in common for others.").
     </p>
     <p>
      Additionally, in developed countries post-
      <a href="/wiki/Agrarian_society" title="Agrarian society">
       agrarian society
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Industrial_society" title="Industrial society">
       industrial society
      </a>
      ) this argument has many critics (including those influenced by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgist" title="Georgist">
       Georgist
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geolibertarian" title="Geolibertarian">
       geolibertarian
      </a>
      ideas) who argue that since land, by definition, is not a product of human labor, any claim of
      <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">
       private property
      </a>
      in it is a form of theft; as
      <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">
       David Lloyd George
      </a>
      observed, "to prove a legal title to land one must trace it back to the man who stole it."
     </p>
     <p>
      Many older ideas have resurfaced in the modern notions of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_stewardship" title="Ecological stewardship">
       ecological stewardship
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bioregionalism" title="Bioregionalism">
       bioregionalism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">
       natural capital
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">
       ecological economics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropological_views">
       Anthropological views
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Anthropological views">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       Anthropology
      </a>
      characterizes societies, in part, based on a society's concept of wealth, and the institutional structures and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" title="Power (sociology)">
       power
      </a>
      used to protect this wealth.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Several types are defined below. They can be viewed as an evolutionary progression. Many young adolescents have become wealthy from the inheritance of their families.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_interpersonal_concept">
       The interpersonal concept
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The interpersonal concept">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Early
      <a href="/wiki/Hominidae" title="Hominidae">
       hominids
      </a>
      seem to have started with incipient ideas of wealth,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      similar to that of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_ape" title="Great ape">
       great apes
      </a>
      . But as tools,
      <a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">
       clothing
      </a>
      , and other mobile
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrastructural_capital" title="Infrastructural capital">
       infrastructural capital
      </a>
      became important to survival (especially in hostile
      <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">
       biomes
      </a>
      ), ideas such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">
       inheritance
      </a>
      of wealth, political positions,
      <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">
       leadership
      </a>
      , and ability to control group movements (to perhaps reinforce such power) emerged.
      <a href="/wiki/Neandertal" title="Neandertal">
       Neandertal
      </a>
      societies had pooled
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funerary_rite" title="Funerary rite">
       funerary rites
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">
       cave painting
      </a>
      which implies at least a notion of shared assets that could be spent for social purposes, or preserved for social purposes. Wealth may have been collective.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Accumulation_of_non-necessities">
       Accumulation of non-necessities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Accumulation of non-necessities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
       Humans
      </a>
      back to and including the
      <a href="/wiki/Cro-Magnon" title="Cro-Magnon">
       Cro-Magnons
      </a>
      seem to have had clearly defined rulers and status hierarchies.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">
       Digs
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Sungir" title="Sungir">
       Sungir
      </a>
      Archaeological Site have revealed elaborate funeral clothing on a man and a pair of children buried there approximately 28,000 years ago.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      This indicates a considerable accumulation of wealth by some individuals or families. The high
      <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">
       artisan
      </a>
      skill also suggest the capacity to direct
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_specialization" title="Labour specialization">
       specialized labor
      </a>
      to tasks that are not of any obvious utility to the group's survival.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Control_of_arable_land">
       Control of arable land
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Control of arable land">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">
       irrigation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">
       urbanization
      </a>
      , especially in ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">
       Sumer
      </a>
      and later
      <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
       Egypt
      </a>
      , unified the ideas of wealth and control of
      <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">
       land
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">
       agriculture
      </a>
      . To feed a large stable population, it was possible and necessary to achieve universal
      <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cultivation" title="wikt:cultivation">
       cultivation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">
       city-state
      </a>
      protection. The notion of the
      <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">
       state
      </a>
      and the notion of
      <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">
       war
      </a>
      are said to have emerged at this time. Tribal cultures were formalized into what we would call feudal systems, and many rights and obligations were assumed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">
       monarchy
      </a>
      and related
      <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">
       aristocracy
      </a>
      . Protection of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrastructural_capital" title="Infrastructural capital">
       infrastructural capital
      </a>
      built up over generations became critical:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_wall" title="City wall">
       city walls
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrigation_system" title="Irrigation system">
       irrigation systems
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sewage_system" title="Sewage system">
       sewage systems
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueduct_(watercourse)" title="Aqueduct (watercourse)">
       aqueducts
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">
       buildings
      </a>
      , all impossible to replace within a single generation, and thus a matter of social survival to maintain. The
      <a href="/wiki/Social_capital" title="Social capital">
       social capital
      </a>
      of entire societies was often defined in terms of its relation to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrastructural_capital" title="Infrastructural capital">
       infrastructural capital
      </a>
      (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">
       castles
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort" title="Fort">
       forts
      </a>
      or an allied
      <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">
       monastery
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">
       cathedral
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">
       temple
      </a>
      ), and
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">
       natural capital
      </a>
      , (i.e. the
      <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">
       land
      </a>
      that supplied locally grown food).
      <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_economics" title="Agricultural economics">
       Agricultural economics
      </a>
      continues these traditions in the analyses of modern
      <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_policy" title="Agricultural policy">
       agricultural policy
      </a>
      and related ideas of wealth, e.g. the ark of taste model of agricultural wealth.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_role_of_technology">
       The role of technology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wealth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: The role of technology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">
       Industrialization
      </a>
      emphasized the role of technology. Many jobs were automated. Machines replaced some workers while other workers became more specialized.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_specialization" title="Labour specialization">
       Labour specialization
      </a>
      became critical to economic success. However,
      <a href="/wiki/Physical_capital" title="Physical capital">
       physical capital
      </a>
      , as it came to be known, consisting of both the
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">
       natural capital
      </a>
      (raw materials from nature) and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrastructural_capital" title="Infrastructural capital">
       infrastructural capital
      </a>
      (facilitating technology), became the focus of the
      <b>
       analysis of wealth
      </b>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">
       Adam Smith
      </a>
      saw wealth creation as the combination of materials, labour, land, and technology in such a way as to capture a profit (excess above the cost of production).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gutenberg1_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gutenberg1-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
