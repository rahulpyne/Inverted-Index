     <p>
      A
      <b>
       power station
      </b>
      , also referred to as a
      <b>
       generating station
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       power plant
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       powerhouse
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       generating plant
      </b>
      , is an industrial facility for the
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">
       generation
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">
       electric power
      </a>
      . Most power stations contain one or more
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">
       generators
      </a>
      , a rotating machine that converts mechanical power into
      <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)#Electrical_power" title="Power (physics)">
       electrical power
      </a>
      . The relative motion between a
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
       magnetic field
      </a>
      and a
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">
       conductor
      </a>
      creates an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_current" title="Electrical current">
       electrical current
      </a>
      . The energy source harnessed to turn the generator varies widely. Most power stations in the world burn
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuels
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">
       oil
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      to generate electricity. Others use
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">
       nuclear power
      </a>
      , but there is an increasing use of cleaner
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable sources
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
       solar
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave_Power" title="Wave Power">
       wave
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" title="Hydroelectric">
       hydroelectric
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1868 a hydro electric power station was designed and built by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" title="William Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">
       Lord Armstrong
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Cragside" title="Cragside">
       Cragside
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      . It used water from lakes on his estate to power
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siemens_AG" title="Siemens AG">
       Siemens
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">
       dynamos
      </a>
      . The electricity supplied power to lights, heating, produced hot water, ran an elevator as well as labor-saving devices and farm buildings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early 1870s Belgian inventor
      <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%A9nobe_Gramme" title="Zénobe Gramme">
       Zénobe Gramme
      </a>
      invented a generator powerful enough to produce power on a commercial scale for industry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the autumn of 1882, a central station providing public power was built in
      <a href="/wiki/Godalming" title="Godalming">
       Godalming
      </a>
      , England. It was proposed after the town failed to reach an agreement on the rate charged by the gas company, so the town council decided to use electricity. It used hydroelectric power that was used to street and household lighting. The system was not a commercial success and the town reverted to gas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1882 a public power station, the Edison Electric Light Station, was built in London, was a project of
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">
       Thomas Edison
      </a>
      organized by
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hibberd_Johnson" title="Edward Hibberd Johnson">
       Edward Johnson
      </a>
      . A
      <a href="/wiki/Babcock_%26_Wilcox" title="Babcock &amp; Wilcox">
       Babcock &amp; Wilcox
      </a>
      boiler powered a 125-horsepower steam engine that drove a 27-ton generator. This supplied electricity to premises in the area that could be reached through the
      <a href="/wiki/Culvert" title="Culvert">
       culverts
      </a>
      of the viaduct without digging up the road, which was the monopoly of the gas companies. The customers included the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_Temple_(London)" title="City Temple (London)">
       City Temple
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Bailey" title="Old Bailey">
       Old Bailey
      </a>
      . Another important customer was the Telegraph Office of the
      <a href="/wiki/General_Post_Office#Headquarters" title="General Post Office">
       General Post Office
      </a>
      , but this could not be reached though the culverts. Johnson arranged for the supply cable to be run overhead, via Holborn Tavern and
      <a href="/wiki/Newgate" title="Newgate">
       Newgate
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In September 1882 in New York, the
      <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Street_Station" title="Pearl Street Station">
       Pearl Street Station
      </a>
      was established by Edison to provide electric lighting in the lower Manhattan Island area. The station ran until destroyed by fire in 1890. The station used reciprocating
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">
       steam engines
      </a>
      to turn direct-current generators. Because of the DC distribution, the service area was small, limited by voltage drop in the feeders. The
      <a href="/wiki/War_of_Currents" title="War of Currents">
       War of Currents
      </a>
      eventually resolved in favor of AC distribution and utilization, although some DC systems persisted to the end of the 20th century. DC systems with a service radius of a mile (kilometer) or so were necessarily smaller, less efficient of fuel consumption, and more labor-intensive to operate than much larger central AC generating stations.
     </p>
     <p>
      AC systems used a wide range of
      <a href="/wiki/Utility_frequency" title="Utility frequency">
       frequencies
      </a>
      depending on the type of load; lighting load using higher frequencies, and traction systems and heavy motor load systems preferring lower frequencies. The economics of central station generation improved greatly when unified light and power systems, operating at a common frequency, were developed. The same generating plant that fed large industrial loads during the day, could feed commuter railway systems during rush hour and then serve lighting load in the evening, thus improving the system
      <a href="/wiki/Load_factor_(electrical)" title="Load factor (electrical)">
       load factor
      </a>
      and reducing the cost of electrical energy overall. Many exceptions existed, generating stations were dedicated to power or light by the choice of frequency, and rotating
      <a href="/wiki/Frequency_changer" title="Frequency changer">
       frequency changers
      </a>
      and rotating converters were particularly common to feed electric railway systems from the general lighting and power network.
     </p>
     <p>
      Throughout the first few decades of the 20th century central stations became larger, using higher steam pressures to provide greater efficiency, and relying on interconnections of multiple generating stations to improve reliability and cost. High-voltage AC transmission allowed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power">
       hydroelectric power
      </a>
      to be conveniently moved from distant waterfalls to city markets. The advent of the
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">
       steam turbine
      </a>
      in central station service, around 1906, allowed great expansion of generating capacity. Generators were no longer limited by the power transmission of belts or the relatively slow speed of reciprocating engines, and could grow to enormous sizes. For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Ziani_de_Ferranti" title="Sebastian Ziani de Ferranti">
       Sebastian Ziani de Ferranti
      </a>
      planned what would have been the largest reciprocating steam engine ever built for a proposed new central station, but scrapped the plans when turbines became available in the necessary size. Building power systems out of central stations required combinations of engineering skill and financial acumen in equal measure. Pioneers of central station generation include
      <a href="/wiki/George_Westinghouse" title="George Westinghouse">
       George Westinghouse
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Insull" title="Samuel Insull">
       Samuel Insull
      </a>
      in the United States, Ferranti and
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hesterman_Merz" title="Charles Hesterman Merz">
       Charles Hesterman Merz
      </a>
      in UK, and many others.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_power_stations">
       Thermal power stations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thermal power stations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">
       Thermal power station
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dampfturbine_Laeufer01.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2126" data-file-width="2126" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Dampfturbine_Laeufer01.jpg/200px-Dampfturbine_Laeufer01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Dampfturbine_Laeufer01.jpg/300px-Dampfturbine_Laeufer01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Dampfturbine_Laeufer01.jpg/400px-Dampfturbine_Laeufer01.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dampfturbine_Laeufer01.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Rotor of a modern steam turbine, used in a power station
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In thermal power stations, mechanical power is produced by a
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">
       heat engine
      </a>
      that transforms
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">
       thermal energy
      </a>
      , often from
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      of a
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">
       fuel
      </a>
      , into rotational energy. Most thermal power stations produce steam, so they are sometimes called steam power stations. Not all thermal energy can be transformed into mechanical power, according to the
      <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">
       second law of thermodynamics
      </a>
      ; therefore, there is always heat lost to the environment. If this loss is employed as useful heat, for industrial processes or
      <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">
       district heating
      </a>
      , the power plant is referred to as a
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       cogeneration
      </a>
      power plant or CHP (combined heat-and-power) plant. In countries where district heating is common, there are dedicated heat plants called
      <a href="/wiki/Heat-only_boiler_station" title="Heat-only boiler station">
       heat-only boiler stations
      </a>
      . An important class of power stations in the Middle East uses by-product heat for the
      <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">
       desalination
      </a>
      of water.
     </p>
     <p>
      The efficiency of a thermal power cycle is limited by the maximum working fluid temperature produced. The efficiency is not directly a function of the fuel used. For the same steam conditions, coal-, nuclear- and gas power plants all have the same theoretical efficiency. Overall, if a system is on constantly (base load) it will be more efficient than one that is used intermittently (peak load). Steam turbines generally operate at higher efficiency when operated at full capacity.
     </p>
     <p>
      Besides use of reject heat for process or district heating, one way to improve overall efficiency of a power plant is to combine two different thermodynamic cycles in a
      <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">
       combined cycle
      </a>
      plant. Most commonly, exhaust gases from a gas turbine are used to generate steam for a boiler and a steam turbine. The combination of a "top" cycle and a "bottom" cycle produces higher overall efficiency than either cycle can attain alone.
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="By_heat_source">
       By heat source
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: By heat source">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_power_station" title="Fossil-fuel power station">
        Fossil-fuel power stations
       </a>
       may also use a steam turbine generator or in the case of
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
        natural gas
       </a>
       -fired plants may use a
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">
        combustion turbine
       </a>
       . A coal-fired power station produces heat by burning coal in a steam boiler. The steam drives a
       <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">
        steam turbine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">
        generator
       </a>
       that then produces
       <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
        electricity
       </a>
       . The waste products of combustion include ash,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">
        sulphur dioxide
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">
        nitrogen oxides
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
        carbon dioxide
       </a>
       . Some of the gases can be removed from the waste stream to reduce pollution.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">
        Nuclear power plants
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       use a
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">
        nuclear reactor
       </a>
       's heat that is transferred to steam which then operates a steam turbine and generator. About 20 percent of electric generation in the USA is produced by nuclear power plants.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">
        Geothermal power
       </a>
       plants use steam extracted from hot underground rocks. These rocks are heated by the decay of radioactive material in the Earth's crust.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Biomass#Biomass_conversion_process_to_useful_energy" title="Biomass">
        Biomass-fuelled power plants
       </a>
       may be fuelled by
       <a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">
        waste from sugar cane
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">
        municipal solid waste
       </a>
       , landfill
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       , or other forms of
       <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
        biomass
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       In integrated
       <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">
        steel mills
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace_gas" title="Blast furnace gas">
        blast furnace exhaust gas
       </a>
       is a low-cost, although low-energy-density, fuel.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
        Waste heat from industrial processes
       </a>
       is occasionally concentrated enough to use for power generation, usually in a steam boiler and turbine.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
        Solar thermal
       </a>
       electric plants use sunlight to boil water and produce steam which turns the generator.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="By_prime_mover">
       By prime mover
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: By prime mover">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">
        Steam turbine
       </a>
       plants use the dynamic pressure generated by expanding steam to turn the blades of a turbine. Almost all large non-hydro plants use this system. About 90 percent of all electric power produced in the world is through use of steam turbines.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiser_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wiser-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">
        Gas turbine
       </a>
       plants use the dynamic pressure from flowing gases (air and combustion products) to directly operate the turbine. Natural-gas fuelled (and oil fueled) combustion turbine plants can start rapidly and so are used to supply "peak" energy during periods of high demand, though at higher cost than base-loaded plants. These may be comparatively small units, and sometimes completely unmanned, being remotely operated. This type was pioneered by the UK, Princetown
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       being the world's first, commissioned in 1959.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">
        Combined cycle
       </a>
       plants have both a gas turbine fired by natural gas, and a steam boiler and steam turbine which use the hot exhaust gas from the gas turbine to produce electricity. This greatly increases the overall efficiency of the plant, and many new baseload power plants are combined cycle plants fired by natural gas.
      </li>
      <li>
       Internal combustion
       <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_engine" title="Reciprocating engine">
        reciprocating engines
       </a>
       are used to provide power for isolated communities and are frequently used for small cogeneration plants. Hospitals, office buildings, industrial plants, and other critical facilities also use them to provide backup power in case of a power outage. These are usually fuelled by diesel oil, heavy oil,
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
        natural gas
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">
        landfill gas
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine#Microturbines" title="Gas turbine">
        Microturbines
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">
        Stirling engine
       </a>
       and internal combustion reciprocating engines are low-cost solutions for using opportunity fuels, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">
        landfill gas
       </a>
       , digester gas from water treatment plants and waste gas from oil production.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="By_duty">
       By duty
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: By duty">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Power plants that can be dispatched (scheduled) to provide energy to a system include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_load_power_plant" title="Base load power plant">
        Base load power plants
       </a>
       run nearly continually to provide that component of system load that doesn't vary during a day or week. Baseload plants can be highly optimized for low fuel cost, but may not start or stop quickly during changes in system load. Examples of base-load plants would include large modern coal-fired and nuclear generating stations, or hydro plants with a predictable supply of water.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">
        Peaking power plants
       </a>
       meet the daily peak load, which may only be for one or two hours each day. While their incremental operating cost is always higher than base load plants, they are required to ensure security of the system during load peaks. Peaking plants include simple cycle gas turbines and sometimes reciprocating internal combustion engines, which can be started up rapidly when system peaks are predicted. Hydroelectric plants may also be designed for peaking use.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Load_following_power_plant" title="Load following power plant">
        Load following power plants
       </a>
       can economically follow the variations in the daily and weekly load, at lower cost than peaking plants and with more flexibility than baseload plants.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Non-dispatchable plants include such sources as wind and solar energy; while their long-term contribution to system energy supply is predictable, on a short-term (daily or hourly) base their energy must be used as available since generation cannot be deferred. Contractual arrangements ("take or pay") with independent power producers or system interconnections to other networks may be effectively non-dispatchable.
     </p>

     <p>
      All thermal power plants produce
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">
       waste heat
      </a>
      energy as a byproduct of the useful electrical energy produced. The amount of waste heat energy equals or exceeds the amount of energy converted into useful electricity. Gas-fired power plants can achieve as much as 65 percent conversion efficiency, while coal and oil plants achieve around 30 to 49 percent. The waste heat produces a temperature rise in the atmosphere, which is small compared to that produced by
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse-gas
      </a>
      emissions from the same power plant. Natural draft wet
      <a href="/wiki/Cooling_tower" title="Cooling tower">
       cooling towers
      </a>
      at many nuclear power plants and large fossil fuel-fired power plants use large
      <a href="/wiki/Hyperboloid_structure" title="Hyperboloid structure">
       hyperboloid
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Chimney" title="Chimney">
       chimney
      </a>
      -like structures (as seen in the image at the right) that release the waste heat to the ambient atmosphere by the
      <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">
       evaporation
      </a>
      of water.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the mechanical induced-draft or forced-draft wet cooling towers in many large thermal power plants, nuclear power plants, fossil-fired power plants,
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">
       petroleum refineries
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">
       petrochemical plants
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">
       geothermal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trash-to-energy_plant" title="Trash-to-energy plant">
       waste-to-energy plants
      </a>
      use
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fan_(mechanical)" title="Fan (mechanical)">
       fans
      </a>
      to provide air movement upward through downcoming water, and are not hyperboloid chimney-like structures. The induced or forced-draft cooling towers are typically rectangular, box-like structures filled with a material that enhances the mixing of the upflowing air and the downflowing water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beychok-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In areas with restricted water use, a dry cooling tower or directly air-cooled radiators may be necessary, since the cost or environmental consequences of obtaining make-up water for evaporative cooling would be prohibitive. These coolers have lower efficiency and higher energy consumption to drive fans, compared to a typical wet, evaporative cooling tower.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Once-through_cooling_systems">
       Once-through cooling systems
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Once-through cooling systems">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Electric companies often prefer to use cooling water from the ocean, a lake, or a river, or a cooling pond, instead of a cooling tower. This single pass or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Once-through_cooling" title="Once-through cooling">
       once-through cooling
      </a>
      system can save the cost of a cooling tower and may have lower energy costs for pumping cooling water through the plant's
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchangers
      </a>
      . However, the waste heat can cause
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_pollution" title="Thermal pollution">
       thermal pollution
      </a>
      as the water is discharged. Power plants using natural bodies of water for cooling are designed with mechanisms such as
      <a href="/wiki/Fish_screen" title="Fish screen">
       fish screens
      </a>
      , to limit intake of organisms into the cooling machinery. These screens are only partially effective and as a result billions of fish and other aquatic organisms are killed by power plants each year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, the cooling system at the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_Point_Energy_Center" title="Indian Point Energy Center">
       Indian Point Energy Center
      </a>
      in New York kills over a billion fish eggs and larvae annually.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A further environmental impact is that aquatic organisms which adapt to the warmer discharge water may be injured if the plant shuts down in cold weather.
     </p>
     <p>
      Water consumption by power stations is a developing issue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In recent years, recycled wastewater, or grey water, has been used in cooling towers. The Calpine Riverside and the Calpine Fox power stations in
      <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">
       Wisconsin
      </a>
      as well as the Calpine Mankato power station in
      <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">
       Minnesota
      </a>
      are among these facilities.
     </p>

      sources.
     </p>

      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In a hydroelectric power station water flows though turbines using
      <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">
       hydropower
      </a>
      to generate
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectricity
      </a>
      . Power is captured from the gravitational force of water falling through
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penstocks" title="Penstocks">
       penstocks
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">
       water turbines
      </a>
      connected to
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">
       storing water
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      is the largest hydroelectricity producer, with 721 terawatt-hours of production in 2010, representing around 17 percent of domestic electricity use.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pumped_storage">
       Pumped storage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pumped storage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">
       Pumped-storage hydroelectricity
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">
       pumped-storage
      </a>
      is a reversible hydroelectric power plant. They are a net consumer of energy but can be used for storage to smooth peaks and troughs in overall electricity demand. Pumped storage plants typically use "spare" electricity during off peak periods to pump freshwater or saltwater from a lower reservoir to an upper reservoir. Because the pumping takes place "off peak", electricity is typically cheaper than at peak times. This is because power sources such as coal-fired, solar and wind are not switched off and remain in service even when demand is low. During hours of peak demand, when the electricity price is high, the water pumped to the upper reservoir is allowed to flow back to the lower reservoir through a water turbine connected to an electricity generator. Unlike coal power stations, which can take more than 12 hours to start up from cold, the hydroelectric plant can be brought into service in a few minutes, ideal to meet a peak load demand. Two substantial pumped storage schemes are in South Africa,
      <a href="/wiki/Palmiet_Pumped_Storage_Scheme" title="Palmiet Pumped Storage Scheme">
       Palmiet Pumped Storage Scheme
      </a>
      and another in the Drakensberg,
      <a href="/wiki/Ingula_Pumped_Storage_Scheme" title="Ingula Pumped Storage Scheme">
       Ingula Pumped Storage Scheme
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">
       Solar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Giant_photovoltaic_array.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Nellis_Solar_Power_Plant" title="Nellis Solar Power Plant">

        <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">

        </a>
        , United States
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
       Solar energy
      </a>
      can be turned into electricity either directly in
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">
       solar cells
      </a>
      , or in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentrating_solar_power" title="Concentrating solar power">
       concentrating solar power
      </a>
      plant by focusing the light to run a heat engine.
     </p>
     <p>
      A solar photovoltaic power plant converts sunlight into direct current electricity using the
      <a href="/wiki/Photoelectric_effect" title="Photoelectric effect">
       photoelectric effect
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Power_inverter" title="Power inverter">
       Inverters
      </a>
      change the direct current into alternating current for connection to the electrical grid. This type of plant does not use rotating machines for energy conversion.
     </p>
     <p>
      Solar thermal power plants are another type of solar power plant. They use either parabolic troughs or
      <a href="/wiki/Heliostat" title="Heliostat">
       heliostats
      </a>
      to direct sunlight onto a pipe containing a heat transfer fluid, such as oil. The heated oil is then used to boil water into steam, which turns a turbine that drives an electrical generator. The central tower type of solar thermal power plant uses hundreds or thousands of mirrors, depending on size, to direct sunlight onto a receiver on top of a tower. Again, the heat is used to produce steam to turn turbines that drive electrical generators.
     </p>

     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">

      </a>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Marine">
       Marine
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Marine">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">
       Marine energy
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Marine energy
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       marine power
      </b>
      (also sometimes referred to as
      <b>
       ocean energy
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       ocean power
      </b>
      ) refers to the energy carried by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_wave" title="Ocean wave">
       ocean waves
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
       tides
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">
       salinity
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_thermal_energy" title="Ocean thermal energy">
       ocean temperature differences
      </a>
      . The movement of water in the world’s oceans creates a vast store of
      <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
       kinetic energy
      </a>
      , or energy in motion. This energy can be harnessed to
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">
       generate
      </a>
      electricity to power homes, transport and industries.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term marine energy encompasses both
      <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">
       wave power
      </a>
      — power from surface waves, and
      <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">
       tidal power
      </a>
      — obtained from the kinetic energy of large bodies of moving water.
      <a href="/wiki/Offshore_wind_power" title="Offshore wind power">
       Offshore wind power
      </a>
      is not a form of marine energy, as wind power is derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">
       wind
      </a>
      , even if the
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">
       wind turbines
      </a>
      are placed over water.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">
       oceans
      </a>
      have a tremendous amount of energy and are close to many if not most concentrated populations. Ocean energy has the potential of providing a substantial amount of new
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      around the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Osmosis">
       Osmosis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Osmosis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurum_osmosis_power_02.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Hurum_osmosis_power_02.JPG/220px-Hurum_osmosis_power_02.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Hurum_osmosis_power_02.JPG/330px-Hurum_osmosis_power_02.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Hurum_osmosis_power_02.JPG/440px-Hurum_osmosis_power_02.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurum_osmosis_power_02.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Osmotic Power Prototype at Tofte (Hurum), Norway
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_power" title="Osmotic power">
       Osmotic power
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Salinity gradient energy is called pressure-retarded osmosis. In this method, seawater is pumped into a pressure chamber that is at a pressure lower than the difference between the pressures of saline water and fresh water. Freshwater is also pumped into the pressure chamber through a membrane, which increases both the volume and pressure of the chamber. As the pressure differences are compensated, a turbine is spun creating energy. This method is being specifically studied by the Norwegian utility Statkraft, which has calculated that up to 25 TWh/yr would be available from this process in Norway. Statkraft has built the world's first prototype osmotic power plant on the Oslo fiord which was opened on November 24, 2009.
     </p>

      </span>

     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       Biomass
      </a>
      energy can be produced from combustion of waste green material to heat water into steam and drive a steam turbine. Bioenergy can also be processed through a range of temperatures and pressures in
      <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">
       gasification
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       pyrolysis
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Torrefaction" title="Torrefaction">

      </a>

     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Storage_power_stations">
       Storage power stations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">

      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      It is possible to store energy and produce the electricity at a later time like in
      <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">
       Pumped-storage hydroelectricity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy_storage" title="Thermal energy storage">

      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Flywheel_energy_storage" title="Flywheel energy storage">

      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Battery_storage_power_station" title="Battery storage power station">
       Battery storage power station
      </a>
      and so on.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_power_output">
       Typical power output
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Typical power output">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_power_stations_in_the_world" title="List of largest power stations in the world">
       List of largest power stations in the world
      </a>

         </a>

     <p>
      The power generated by a power station is measured in multiples of the
      <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
       watt
      </a>
      , typically
      <a href="/wiki/Mega-" title="Mega-">
       megawatts
      </a>
      (10
      <sup>
       6
      </sup>
      watts) or
      <a href="/wiki/Giga-" title="Giga-">
       gigawatts
      </a>
      (10
      <sup>
       9
      </sup>
      watts). Power stations vary greatly in capacity depending on the type of power plant and on historical, geographical and economic factors. The following examples offer a sense of the scale.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many of the largest operational onshore wind farms are located in the USA. As of 2011, the
      <a href="/wiki/Roscoe_Wind_Farm" title="Roscoe Wind Farm">
       Roscoe Wind Farm
      </a>
      is the second largest onshore wind farm in the world, producing 781.5
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>
      of power, followed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Horse_Hollow_Wind_Energy_Center" title="Horse Hollow Wind Energy Center">
       Horse Hollow Wind Energy Center
      </a>
      (735.5 MW). As of July 2013, the
      <a href="/wiki/London_Array" title="London Array">
       London Array
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      is the largest offshore wind farm in the world at 630
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>
      , followed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanet_Offshore_Wind_Project" title="Thanet Offshore Wind Project">
       Thanet Offshore Wind Project
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      at 300
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As of 2015
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_photovoltaic_power_stations" title="List of photovoltaic power stations">
       largest photovoltaic (PV) power plants in the world
      </a>
      are led by
      <a href="/wiki/Longyangxia_Dam" title="Longyangxia Dam">
       Longyangxia Dam
      </a>
      Solar Park in China, rated at 850 megawatts.
     </p>
     <p>
      Solar thermal power stations in the U.S. have the following output:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The country's largest solar facility at
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems" title="Solar Energy Generating Systems">
        Kramer Junction
       </a>
       has an output of 354 MW
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blythe_Solar_Power_Project" title="Blythe Solar Power Project">
        Blythe Solar Power Project
       </a>
       planned production is estimated at 485 MW
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aerial_of_Three_Mile_Island_-_NARA_-_540012.tif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2700" data-file-width="2700" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Aerial_of_Three_Mile_Island_-_NARA_-_540012.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Aerial_of_Three_Mile_Island_-_NARA_-_540012.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Aerial_of_Three_Mile_Island_-_NARA_-_540012.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Aerial_of_Three_Mile_Island_-_NARA_-_540012.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Aerial_of_Three_Mile_Island_-_NARA_-_540012.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Aerial_of_Three_Mile_Island_-_NARA_-_540012.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aerial_of_Three_Mile_Island_-_NARA_-_540012.tif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Aerial view of the
        <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Three Mile Island Nuclear Generating Station">
         Three Mile Island Nuclear Generating Station
        </a>
        , USA
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Large coal-fired, nuclear, and hydroelectric power stations can generate hundreds of megawatts to multiple gigawatts. Some examples:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Three Mile Island Nuclear Generating Station">
        Three Mile Island Nuclear Generating Station
       </a>
       in the USA has a rated capacity of 802 megawatts.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The coal-fired
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ratcliffe-on-Soar_Power_Station" title="Ratcliffe-on-Soar Power Station">
        Ratcliffe-on-Soar Power Station
       </a>
       in the UK has a rated capacity of 2 gigawatts.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Aswan_Dam" title="Aswan Dam">
        Aswan Dam
       </a>
       hydro-electric plant in Egypt has a capacity of 2.1 gigawatts.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">
        Three Gorges Dam
       </a>
       hydro-electric plant in China has a capacity of 22.5 gigawatts.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Gas turbine power plants can generate tens to hundreds of megawatts. Some examples:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Queens" title="Indian Queens">
        Indian Queens
       </a>
       simple-cycle peaking power station in Cornwall UK, with a single gas turbine is rated 140 megawatts.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Medway_Power_Station" title="Medway Power Station">
        Medway Power Station
       </a>
       , a combined-cycle power station in Kent, UK with two gas turbines and one steam turbine, is rated 700 megawatts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The rated capacity of a power station is nearly the maximum electrical power that that power station can produce. Some power plants are run at almost exactly their rated capacity all the time, as a non-load-following
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_load_power_plant" title="Base load power plant">
       base load power plant
      </a>
      , except at times of scheduled or unscheduled maintenance.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, many power plants usually produce much less power than their rated capacity.
     </p>
     <p>
      In some cases a power plant produces much less power than its rated capacity because it uses an
      <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source" title="Intermittent energy source">
       intermittent energy source
      </a>
      . Operators try to pull
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_power_point_tracker" title="Maximum power point tracker">
       maximum available power
      </a>
      from such power plants, because their
      <a href="/wiki/Marginal_cost" title="Marginal cost">
       marginal cost
      </a>
      is practically zero, but the available power varies widely—in particular, it may be zero during heavy storms at night.
     </p>
     <p>
      In some cases operators deliberately produce less power for economic reasons. The cost of fuel to run a
      <a href="/wiki/Load_following_power_plant" title="Load following power plant">
       load following power plant
      </a>
      may be relatively high, and the cost of fuel to run a
      <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">
       peaking power plant
      </a>
      is even higher—they have relatively high marginal costs. Operators keep power plants turned off ("operational reserve") or running at minimum fuel consumption
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      ("spinning reserve") most of the time. Operators feed more fuel into load following power plants only when the demand rises above what lower-cost plants (i.e., intermittent and base load plants) can produce, and then feed more fuel into peaking power plants only when the demand rises faster than the load following power plants can follow.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">
       Operations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Operations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Power_plant_control_room.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1848" data-file-width="2464" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Power_plant_control_room.jpg/220px-Power_plant_control_room.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Power_plant_control_room.jpg/330px-Power_plant_control_room.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Power_plant_control_room.jpg/440px-Power_plant_control_room.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Power_plant_control_room.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Control room of a power plant
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The term
      <b>
       Power station
      </b>
      is generally limited to those able to be despatched by a system operator (i.e. the system operator can, by one means or another, alter the planned output of the generating facility).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babcock_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Babcock-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Elliott-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       power station operator
      </b>
      has several duties in the electricity-generating facility. Operators are responsible for the safety of the work crews that frequently do repairs on the mechanical and electrical equipment. They maintain the equipment with periodic
      <a href="/wiki/Inspection" title="Inspection">
       inspections
      </a>
      and log temperatures, pressures and other important information at regular intervals. Operators are responsible for starting and stopping the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">
       generators
      </a>
      depending on need. They are able to synchronize and adjust the voltage output of the added generation with the running electrical system, without upsetting the system. They must know the electrical and mechanical systems in order to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troubleshoot" title="Troubleshoot">
       troubleshoot
      </a>
      solve/fix problems in the facility and add to the reliability of the facility. Operators must be able to respond to an emergency and know the procedures in place to deal with it.
     </p>
