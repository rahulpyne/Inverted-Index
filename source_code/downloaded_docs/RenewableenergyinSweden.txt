     <p>
      <br/>
      The emissions decline 7.7% in 2008–2009 was at least partly influenced by the European economic recession of 2008–2009 and not only by the sustainable changes in energy consumption. From 2008 to 2009 the change in the
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_United_States" title="Climate change in the United States">
       US
      </a>
      was a 7.0% decline and in
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Canada" title="Climate change in Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      was a 9.6% decline.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardianlist_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardianlist-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A report was published in 2011 by the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Energy_Council" title="World Energy Council">
       World Energy Council
      </a>
      in association with
      <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Wyman" title="Oliver Wyman">
       Oliver Wyman
      </a>
      , entitled
      <i>
       Policies for the Future: 2011 Assessment of Country Energy and Climate Policies
      </i>
      , which ranks country performance according to an energy sustainability index.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The best performers were Switzerland, Sweden and France.
     </p>
     <p>
      Buildings and the residential sector currently account for 40 percent of Sweden's energy consumption. Buildings have a long life-span. Thus energy efficiency is important for houses being built. Better energy efficiency for existing buildings is the biggest challenge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy">
       Renewable energy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Renewable energy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The share of renewable energy use is high in Sweden; hydro, wind, and solar power together accounted for 49.8% of the electricity produced in the country in 2014 (when measured against national electricity
      <i>
       consumption
      </i>
      , however, this amount rises to 55.5%).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since 2003, Sweden has supported renewable energy in the electricity sector with a "green electricity certificate" obligation for retail power suppliers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The current plan of the certificate system is to support 25 TWh of new renewable electricity generation by 2020.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Within the context of the European Union's 2009
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewables_Directive" title="Renewables Directive">
       Renewables Directive
      </a>
      , Sweden is working towards reaching a 50% share of renewable energy in gross final consumption of energy - electricity, heating/cooling, and transportation - by 2020.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Eurostat" title="Eurostat">
       Eurostat
      </a>
      reported that Sweden had already exceeded the Directive's 2020 target in 2014.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2013 renewable energy investment was more than US$1 billion in Sweden.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">
       Wind power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wind power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Sweden" title="Wind power in Sweden">
       Wind power in Sweden
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_the_European_Union" title="Wind power in the European Union">
       Wind power in the European Union
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       Wind power
      </a>
      accounted for 2.4% of the electricity production in Sweden in 2010, 5 % in 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sweden has
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Europe" title="Wind power in Europe">
       wind power
      </a>
      potential of 510 TWh/a at land and 46 TWh/a at sea.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Consumption was 140 TWh of power in 2010.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2013 Sweden was second top country for wind power capacity per inhabitant in the world: 488 W per person.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In correlation one must note that Swedish use of energy per inhabitant is much higher than average in Europe.
     </p>
     <table class="wikitable" style="text-align:right;">
      <tr>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_power">
       Wave power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wave power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Sweden has a
      <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">
       wave power
      </a>
      station outside
      <a href="/wiki/Lysekil" title="Lysekil">
       Lysekil
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectric_power">
       Hydroelectric power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hydroelectric power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power_in_Sweden" title="Hydroelectric power in Sweden">
       Hydroelectric power in Sweden
      </a>
      accounts for more than half of energy production. More than 1900 power stations operate all over the country. Forty-five produce 100 MW and over, 17 produce 200 MW and over, and 6 produce 400 MW and over. The largest station, which is located on the upper
      <a href="/wiki/Lule_River" title="Lule River">
       Lule River
      </a>
      , has a maximum production capacity of 977 MW. The
      <a href="/wiki/Lule_River" title="Lule River">
       Lule River
      </a>
      is also the most productive river, with almost 18% of the Swedish installed effect. Almost all of the medium to large plants are located in northern Sweden.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">
       Solar power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solar power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      While installations have historically been minimal, solar power has recently been growing quickly in Sweden with the country's cumulative
      <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">
       PV
      </a>
      capacity nearly doubling in 2014 to 79 MW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuels">
       Biofuels
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Biofuels">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel_in_Sweden" title="Biofuel in Sweden">
       Biofuel in Sweden
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Sweden aims for a fossil fuel free vehicle fleet by 2030.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sweden published the sustainability criteria for biofuels (2011) which consider the areas with high biological values to be protected in respect to fuels production. The feedstock origin used for production of bioliquids in Sweden during 2011 was Sweden 49% The Netherlands 17% United States 17% Finland 6% Belgium 3% and other 8% (Brazil, Malaysia and Russia).
      <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">
       Palm oil
      </a>
      is often pointed out as a dirty feed-stock for biofuels. None of the Swedish companies used palm oil in 2011. The largest share of feedstock for bioliquids comes from the forest industry in the form of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tall_oil_pitch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tall oil pitch (page does not exist)">
       tall oil pitch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tall_oil" title="Tall oil">
       tall oil
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">
       methanol
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2013 the bus fleets in more than a dozen cities relied entirely on biomethane, local plants produced more than 60% of the total biomethane used in Swedish natural gas vehicles, and more filling stations were opened in 2012 and 2013. Goteborg Energi (Gothenburg Energy) has a 20 MW facility that gasifies forest residues and then converts the synthesis gases—hydrogen and carbon monoxide—into biomethane.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_and_Nuclear">
       Thermal and Nuclear
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Thermal and Nuclear">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nuclear is dominating in this sector. The other operational plant is, in almost all cases, fueled with renewable fuels. Oil plants are few, and are either decommissioned or used as a reserve,
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_power">
       Nuclear power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nuclear power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Sweden" title="Nuclear power in Sweden">
       Nuclear power in Sweden
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      More than 35% of the Swedish electricity is produced by 10
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">
       nuclear reactors
      </a>
      , spread out on 3 power stations:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Ringhals_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Ringhals Nuclear Power Plant">
        Ringhals Nuclear Power Plant
       </a>
       - 3
       <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">
        Pressurized Water Reactor
       </a>
       , 1
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BWR" title="BWR">
        Boiling Water Reactor
       </a>
       , ~4,2
       <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
        GW
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Oskarshamn_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Oskarshamn Nuclear Power Plant">
        Oskarshamn Nuclear Power Plant
       </a>
       - 3 BWRs (One of them is also world's largest BWR), ~2,6 GW
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Forsmark_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant">
        Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant
       </a>
       - 3 BWRs, ~3,2 GW
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Before 2005, there were 12 reactors, but the two BWR reactors (~1,2 GW) at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barseb%C3%A4ck_nuclear_power_plant" title="Barsebäck nuclear power plant">
       Barsebäck nuclear power plant
      </a>
      were decommissioned in 1999 and 2005.
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Global warming">
        edit

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">

      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Sweden" title="Climate change in Sweden">
       Climate change in Sweden
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to Energy Information Administration the CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions from energy consumption of Sweden were in 2009 54.77 Mt, slightly below
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Finland" title="Energy in Finland">
       Finland
      </a>
      54.86 Mt, despite the difference in the population.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The emissions per capita were in Sweden 5.58 and in Finland 9.93 tonnes per capita in 2009.

       <a href="#cite_note-26">
