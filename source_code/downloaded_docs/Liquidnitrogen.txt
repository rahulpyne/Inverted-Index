     <p>
      <b>
       Liquid nitrogen
      </b>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      in a liquid state at an extremely low temperature. It is a colorless clear liquid with a density of 0.807 g/ml at its
      <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">
       boiling point
      </a>
      (−195.79 °C (77 K; −320 °F)) and a
      <a href="/wiki/Relative_permittivity" title="Relative permittivity">
       dielectric constant
      </a>
      of 1.43.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nitrogen was first liquefied at the
      <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_University" title="Jagiellonian University">
       Jagiellonian University
      </a>
      on 15 April 1883 by Polish physicists,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zygmunt_Wr%C3%B3blewski" title="Zygmunt Wróblewski">
       Zygmunt Wróblewski
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Karol_Olszewski" title="Karol Olszewski">
       Karol Olszewski
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is produced industrially by
      <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">
       fractional distillation
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">
       liquid air
      </a>
      . Liquid nitrogen is often referred to by the abbreviation,
      <b>
       LN
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </b>
      or "LIN" or "LN" and has the
      <a href="/wiki/UN_number" title="UN number">
       UN number
      </a>
      1977. Liquid nitrogen is a diatomic liquid, which means that the diatomic character of the covalent N bonding in N
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      gas is retained after
      <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction" title="Liquefaction">
       liquefaction
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Liquid nitrogen is a
      <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">
       cryogenic
      </a>
      fluid that can cause rapid
      <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">
       freezing
      </a>
      on contact with living tissue. When appropriately
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">
       insulated
      </a>
      from ambient
      <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">
       heat
      </a>
      , liquid nitrogen can be stored and transported, for example in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vacuum_flasks" title="Vacuum flasks">
       vacuum flasks
      </a>
      . The temperature is held constant at 77 K by slow boiling of the liquid, resulting in the evolution of nitrogen gas. Depending on the size and design, the holding time of vacuum flasks (
      <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_storage_dewar" title="Cryogenic storage dewar">
       Dewars
      </a>
      ) ranges from a few hours to a few weeks. The development of pressurised super-insulated vacuum vessels has enabled liquefied nitrogen to be stored and transported over longer time periods with losses reduced to 2% per day or less.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The temperature of liquid nitrogen can readily be reduced to its freezing point 63 K (−210 °C; −346 °F) by placing it in a vacuum chamber pumped by a vacuum pump.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Liquid nitrogen's efficiency as a coolant is limited by the fact that it boils immediately on contact with a warmer object, enveloping the object in insulating nitrogen gas. This effect, known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Leidenfrost_effect" title="Leidenfrost effect">
       Leidenfrost effect
      </a>
      , applies to any liquid in contact with an object significantly hotter than its boiling point. Faster cooling may be obtained by plunging an object into a slush of liquid and solid nitrogen rather than liquid nitrogen alone.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">
       Uses
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Liquid nitrogen is a compact and readily transported source of dry nitrogen gas, as it does not require pressurization. Further, its ability to maintain temperatures far below the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezing_point" title="Freezing point">
       freezing point
      </a>
      of water makes it extremely useful in a wide range of applications, primarily as an open-cycle
      <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">
       refrigerant
      </a>
      , including:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cryotherapy" title="Cryotherapy">
        cryotherapy
       </a>
       for removing unsightly or potentially
       <a href="/wiki/Skin_cancer" title="Skin cancer">
        malignant
       </a>
       skin lesions such as
       <a href="/wiki/Wart" title="Wart">
        warts
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Actinic_keratosis" title="Actinic keratosis">
        actinic keratosis
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       to store cells at low temperature for laboratory work
      </li>
      <li>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">
        cryogenics
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       in a
       <a href="/wiki/Cryophorus" title="Cryophorus">
        cryophorus
       </a>
       to demonstrate rapid
       <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">
        freezing
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">
        evaporation
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       as a backup nitrogen source in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypoxic_air_fire_prevention_system" title="Hypoxic air fire prevention system">
        hypoxic air fire prevention systems
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       as a source of very dry
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        nitrogen
       </a>
       gas
      </li>
      <li>
       for the immersion, freezing, and transportation of
       <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">
        food
       </a>
       products
      </li>
      <li>
       for the
       <a href="/wiki/Cryopreservation" title="Cryopreservation">
        cryopreservation
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">
        blood
       </a>
       , reproductive cells (
       <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">
        sperm
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">
        egg
       </a>
       ), and other
       <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">
        biological
       </a>
       samples and materials
       <ul>
        <li>
         to preserve tissue samples from surgical excisions for future studies
        </li>
        <li>
         to facilitate
         <a href="/wiki/Cryoconservation_of_animal_genetic_resources" title="Cryoconservation of animal genetic resources">
          Cryoconservation of animal genetic resources
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       as a method of freezing water and oil pipes in order to work on them in situations where a valve is not available to block fluid flow to the work area, method known as a cryogenic isolation (frequently used in industry and New York district steam pipework) – Electrical heat pumps are now often used for small pipe diameters.
      </li>
      <li>
       in the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Promession" title="Promession">
        promession
       </a>
       , a way to dispose of the dead
      </li>
      <li>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Cryonics" title="Cryonics">
        cryonic preservation
       </a>
       in hopes of future reanimation.
      </li>
      <li>
       to shrink-weld machinery parts together
      </li>
      <li>
       as a coolant
       <ul>
        <li>
         for
         <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device#Use_in_astronomy" title="Charge-coupled device">
          CCD
         </a>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary_use_of_liquid_nitrogen">
       Culinary use of liquid nitrogen
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream#Cryogenics" title="Ice cream">
       Ice cream § Cryogenics
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The culinary use of liquid nitrogen is mentioned in an 1890 recipe book titled
      <i>
       Fancy Ices
      </i>
      authored by Mrs.
      <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Marshall" title="Agnes Marshall">
       Agnes Marshall
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCMag-2012-10-09_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBCMag-2012-10-09-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      but has been employed in more recent times by restaurants in the preparation of frozen desserts, such as ice cream, which can be created within moments at the table because of the speed at which it cools food.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCMag-2012-10-09_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBCMag-2012-10-09-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The rapidity of chilling also leads to the formation of smaller ice crystals, which provides the dessert with a smoother texture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCMag-2012-10-09_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBCMag-2012-10-09-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The technique is employed by chef
      <a href="/wiki/Heston_Blumenthal" title="Heston Blumenthal">
       Heston Blumenthal
      </a>
      who has used it at his restaurant,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Fat_Duck" title="The Fat Duck">
       The Fat Duck
      </a>
      to create frozen dishes such as egg and bacon ice cream.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCMag-2012-10-09_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBCMag-2012-10-09-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph-2012-10-09_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-2012-10-09-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Liquid nitrogen has also become popular in the preparation of
      <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen_cocktail" title="Liquid nitrogen cocktail">
       cocktails
      </a>
      because it can be used to quickly chill glasses or freeze ingredients.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsbeat-2012-10-08_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Newsbeat-2012-10-08-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is also added to drinks to create a smoky effect, which occurs as tiny droplets of the liquid nitrogen come into contact with the surrounding air, condensing the vapour that is naturally present.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsbeat-2012-10-08_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Newsbeat-2012-10-08-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">
       Safety
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Liquid_Nitrogen_Tank.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3773" data-file-width="2592" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Liquid_Nitrogen_Tank.JPG/220px-Liquid_Nitrogen_Tank.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Liquid_Nitrogen_Tank.JPG/330px-Liquid_Nitrogen_Tank.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Liquid_Nitrogen_Tank.JPG/440px-Liquid_Nitrogen_Tank.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Liquid_Nitrogen_Tank.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Filling a liquid nitrogen
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dewar_flask" title="Dewar flask">
         Dewar
        </a>
        from a storage tank
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Because the liquid-to-gas
      <a href="/wiki/Expansion_ratio" title="Expansion ratio">
       expansion ratio
      </a>
      of nitrogen is 1:694 at 20 °C (68 °F), a tremendous amount of force can be generated if liquid nitrogen is rapidly vaporized in an enclosed space. In an incident on January 12, 2006 at
      <a href="/wiki/Texas_A%26M_University" title="Texas A&amp;M University">
       Texas A&amp;M University
      </a>
      , the pressure-relief devices of a tank of liquid nitrogen were malfunctioning and later sealed. As a result of the subsequent pressure buildup, the tank failed catastrophically. The force of the explosion was sufficient to propel the tank through the ceiling immediately above it, shatter a reinforced concrete beam immediately below it, and blow the walls of the laboratory 0.1–0.2 m off their foundations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Because of its extremely low temperature, careless handling of liquid nitrogen and any objects cooled by it may result in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_burn" title="Cold burn">
       cold burns
      </a>
      . In that case, special gloves should be used while handling. However, a small splash or even pouring down skin will not burn immediately, because the evaporating gas thermally insulates to some extent, like touching a hot element very briefly with a wet finger. If the liquid nitrogen pools anywhere, it will burn severely.
     </p>
     <p>
      As liquid nitrogen evaporates it reduces the
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      concentration in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">
       air
      </a>
      and can act as an
      <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas" title="Asphyxiant gas">
       asphyxiant
      </a>
      , especially in
      <a href="/wiki/Confined_space" title="Confined space">
       confined spaces
      </a>
      . Nitrogen is odorless, colorless, and tasteless and may produce
      <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">
       asphyxia
      </a>
      without any sensation or prior warning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCGACOPCP30_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BCGACOPCP30-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_sensor" title="Oxygen sensor">
       Oxygen sensors
      </a>
      are sometimes used as a safety precaution when working with liquid nitrogen to alert workers of gas spills into a confined space.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usn_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-usn-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Vessels containing liquid nitrogen can
      <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">
       condense oxygen
      </a>
      from air. The liquid in such a vessel becomes increasingly enriched in oxygen (boiling point 90 K; −183 °C; −298 °F) as the nitrogen evaporates, and can cause violent oxidation of organic material.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ingestion of liquid nitrogen can cause severe internal damage. For example, in 2012, a young woman in England had her stomach removed after ingesting a cocktail made with liquid nitrogen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">
       Air separation
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Liquid nitrogen is produced commercially from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">
       cryogenic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">
       distillation
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">
       liquified air
      </a>
      or from the liquefication of pure nitrogen derived from air using
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">
       pressure swing adsorption
      </a>
      . An
      <a href="/wiki/Air_compressor" title="Air compressor">
       air compressor
      </a>
      is used to compress filtered air to high pressure; the high-pressure gas is cooled back to ambient temperature, and allowed to expand to a low pressure. The expanding air cools greatly (the
      <a href="/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_effect" title="Joule–Thomson effect">
       Joule–Thomson effect
      </a>
      ), and oxygen, nitrogen, and argon are separated by further stages of expansion and distillation. Small-scale production of liquid nitrogen is easily achieved using this principle. Liquid nitrogen may be produced for direct sale, or as a byproduct of manufacture of liquid oxygen used for industrial processes such as
      <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">
       steelmaking
      </a>
      . Liquid-air plants producing on the order of tons per day of product started to be built in the 1930s but became very common after the Second World War; a large modern plant may produce 3000 tons/day of liquid air products.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
