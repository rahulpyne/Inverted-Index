   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Water use
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;redirect=no" title="Water footprint">
       Water footprint
      </a>
      )
     </span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Water use
      </b>
      can mean the amount of
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      used by a household or a country, or the amount used for a given task or for the production of a given quantity of some product or crop, or the amount allocated for a particular purpose.
     </p>
     <p>
      Globally, of
      <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">
       precipitation
      </a>
      falling on land each year (about 117,000 km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      (28,000 cu mi)),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      about 4 percent is used by rainfed agriculture and about half is subject to evaporation and transpiration in
      <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">
       forests
      </a>
      and other natural or quasi-natural landscapes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The remainder, which goes to
      <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">
       groundwater
      </a>
      replenishment and
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">
       surface runoff
      </a>
      , is sometimes called “total actual renewable freshwater resources”. Its magnitude was recently estimated at 52,579 km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      (12,614 cu mi)/year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frenken2012_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Frenken2012-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It represents water that can be used either instream or after withdrawal from surface and groundwater sources. Of this remainder, about 3,918 km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      were withdrawn in 2007, of which 2,722 km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      (69 percent) were used by agriculture, and 734 km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      (19 percent) by other industry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most agricultural use of withdrawn water is for irrigation, which uses about 5.1 percent of total actual renewable
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">
       freshwater
      </a>
      resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frenken2012_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Frenken2012-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      World water use has been growing rapidly in the last hundred years (see
      <a class="external text" href="http://environment.newscientist.com/data/images/archive/2670/26700101.jpg" rel="nofollow">
       graph
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">
       New Scientist
      </a>
      article
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      There are numerous measures of water use, including total water use,
      <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">
       drinking water
      </a>
      consumption, non-consumptive use, withdrawn water use (from surface and groundwater sources), instream use, water footprint, etc. Each of these (and other) measures of water use is appropriate for some purposes and inappropriate for others. Water “footprints” have become popular measures of use, e.g. in relation to personal consumption. The term "water footprint" is often used to refer to the amount of water used by an individual, community, business, or nation, or the amount of water use associated with (although not necessarily assignable to) a product.
     </p>
     <p>
      The total water footprint of a typical 3-person household in the U.S. is 23,360 l (5,140 imp gal; 6,170 US gal).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      By comparison, a typical single family home in the U.S. only uses about 262 l (58 imp gal; 69 US gal) of water per day (2008 estimate). This includes (in decreasing order)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_toilet" title="Water toilet">
       toilet
      </a>
      use,
      <a href="/wiki/Washing_machine" title="Washing machine">
       washing machine
      </a>
      use,
      <a href="/wiki/Shower" title="Shower">
       showers
      </a>
      , baths, faucet use, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_leak" title="Water leak">
       leaks
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS">
         <span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (September 2012)">
          better source needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Water_footprint">
       Water footprint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Water footprint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       water footprint
      </b>
      of an individual, community or business is defined as the total volume of freshwater used to produce the goods and services consumed by the individual or community or produced by the business. Water use is measured in water volume consumed (evaporated) and/or polluted per unit of time. A water footprint can be calculated for any well-defined group of consumers (e.g., an individual, family, village, city, province, state or nation) or producers (e.g., a public organization, private enterprise or economic sector). The water footprint is a geographically explicit indicator, not only showing volumes of water use and pollution, but also the locations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterfootprintorg_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-waterfootprintorg-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The water footprint of a country is related to what its people eat. For example, it is a common thought that the water involved in a cup of coffee is just the water in the cup.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterfootprintorg_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-waterfootprintorg-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is actually 140 litres of water involved. The 140 litres of water is the amount of water that was used to grow, produce, package, and ship the coffee beans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterfootprintorg_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-waterfootprintorg-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Hamburger" title="Hamburger">
       hamburger
      </a>
      needs an estimated 2,400 litres of water. This hidden water is technically called
      <a href="/wiki/Virtual_water" title="Virtual water">
       virtual water
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterfootprintorg_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-waterfootprintorg-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, eating a lot of meat means a large water footprint. However, care is needed to avoid misunderstanding the significance of water footprints of food. (See "Water footprint of products", below.)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The water footprint concept was introduced in 2002 by Arjen Y. Hoekstra from
      <a href="/wiki/UNESCO-IHE" title="UNESCO-IHE">
       UNESCO-IHE
      </a>
      as an alternative indicator of water use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Water footprint is one of a family of footprint indicators, which also includes
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">
       carbon footprint
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Land_footprint" title="Land footprint">
       land footprint
      </a>
      . The water footprint concept is further related to the idea of virtual water trade introduced by Professor
      <a href="/wiki/John_Anthony_Allan" title="John Anthony Allan">
       John Allan
      </a>
      (2008
      <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Water_Prize" title="Stockholm Water Prize">
       Stockholm Water Prize Laureate
      </a>
      ). The most elaborate publications on how to estimate water footprints are a 2004 report on the "Water footprint of nations" from UNESCO-IHE
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the 2008 book
      <i>
       Globalization of Water
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the 2011 manual “The water footprint assessment manual: Setting the global standard”.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cooperation between global leading institutions in the field has led to the establishment of the Water Footprint Network in 2008 that aims to coordinate efforts to further develop and disseminate knowledge on water footprint concepts, methods and tools.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Water_Footprint_Network_.28WFN.29">
       Water Footprint Network (WFN)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water Footprint Network (WFN)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Water Footprint Network is an international learning community (non-profit foundation under Dutch law) that serves as a platform for connecting communities interested in sustainability, equitability and efficiency of water use. The organization has two work programmes: a Technical Work Programme and a Policy Work Programme. In addition, there is a Partner Forum which offer partners of the WFN a way of receiving, contributing and exchanging knowledge and experience on water footprint. Its mission and activities are listed below and taken directly from the Water Footprint website.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="blue_water_footprint">
       blue water footprint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: blue water footprint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       blue water footprint
      </i>
      is the volume of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">
       freshwater
      </a>
      that evaporated from the global blue water resources (surface water and ground water) to produce the goods and services consumed by the individual or community (either lost through evapotranspiration, incorporated in products or transferred to non-blue catchments).
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="green_water_footprint">
       green water footprint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: green water footprint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       green water footprint
      </i>
      is the volume of water evaporated from the global green water resources (rainwater stored in the soil as soil moisture) during production or those incorporated in products.
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="sustainable_water_use">
       sustainable water use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: sustainable water use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">
       Sustainable
      </a>
      water use involves the rigorous assessment of all source of clean water to establish the current and future rates of use, the impacts of that use both downstream and in the wider area where the water may be used and the impact of contaminated water streams on the environment and economic well being of the area. It also involves the implementation of
      <a href="/wiki/Social_policy" title="Social policy">
       social policies
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Water_pricing" title="Water pricing">
       water pricing
      </a>
      in order to manage water demand.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some localities, water may also have spiritual relevance and the use of such water may need to take account of such interests. For example, the
      <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">
       Maori
      </a>
      believe that water is the source and foundation of all life and have many spiritual associations with water and places associated with water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On a national and global scale, water sustainability requires strategic and long term planning to ensure appropriate sources of clean water are identified and the environmental and economic impact of such choices are understood and accepted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The re-use and reclamation of water is also part of sustainability including downstream impacts on both surface waters and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_water" title="Ground water">
       ground waters
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naumann_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-naumann-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Calculation_for_different_actors">
       Calculation for different actors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Calculation for different actors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The water footprint of a process is expressed as
      <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">
       volumetric flow rate
      </a>
      of water. That of a product is the whole footprint (sum) of processes in its complete supply chain divided by the number of product units. For consumers, businesses and geographic area, water footprint is indicated as volume of water per time, in particular:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheWaterFootprintAssessmentManual_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-TheWaterFootprintAssessmentManual-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       That of a consumer is the sum of footprint of all consumed products.
      </li>
      <li>
       That of a community or a nation is the sum for all of its members resp. inhabitants.
      </li>
      <li>
       That of a business is the footprint of all produced goods.
      </li>
      <li>
       That of a geographically delineated area is the footprint of all processes undertaken in this area. The virtual water balance of an area is the net import of virtual water
       <i>
        V
        <sub>
         i, net
        </sub>
       </i>
       , defined as the difference of the gross import
       <i>
        V
        <sub>
         i
        </sub>
       </i>
       of virtual water from its gross export
       <i>
        V
        <sub>
         e
        </sub>
       </i>
       . The water footprint of national consumption
       <i>
        WF
        <sub>
         area,nat
        </sub>
       </i>
       results from this as the sum of the water footprint of national area and its virtual water balance.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="International_standard">
       International standard
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International standard">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In February 2011, the Water Footprint Network, in a global collaborative effort of environmental organizations, companies, research institutions and the UN, launched the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.waterfootprint.org/?page=files/GlobalWaterFootprintStandard" rel="nofollow">
       Global Water Footprint Standard
      </a>
      . On July 24, 2014, ISO issued ISO 14046:2014, Environmental management—Water footprint—Principles, requirements and guidelines.
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.iso.org/iso/catalogue_detail?csnumber=43263" rel="nofollow">
       [4]
      </a>
      The ISO standard is based on Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) principles and can be applied for different sorts of assessment of products and companies.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_Cycle_Analysis_of_water_use">
       Life Cycle Analysis of water use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Life Cycle Analysis of water use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Life Cycle Analysis (LCA) is a systematic, phased approach to assessing the environmental aspects and potential impacts that are associated with a product, process or service. “Life cycle” “refers to the major activities in the course of the product’s life-span from its manufacture, use, and maintenance, to its final disposal, including the raw material acquisition required to manufacture the product”.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus a method for assessing the environmental impacts of freshwater consumption was developed. It specifically looks at the damage to three areas of protection: human health, ecosystem quality, and resources. The consideration of water consumption is crucial where water-intensive products (for example agricultural goods) are concerned and need to therefore undergo a life-cycle assessment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, regional assessments are equally as necessary as the impact of water use depends on its location. In short, LCA is important as it identifies the impact of water use in certain products, consumers, companies, nations, etc. which can help reduce the amount of water used.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Water_footprint_of_companies">
       Water footprint of companies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Water footprint of companies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The water footprint of a business, the 'corporate water footprint', is defined as the total volume of freshwater that is used directly or indirectly to run and support a business. It is the total volume of water use to be associated with the use of the business outputs. The water footprint of a business consists of water used for producing/manufacturing or for supporting activities and the indirect water use in the producer’s supply chain.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Carbon_Trust" title="The Carbon Trust">
       The Carbon Trust
      </a>
      argue that a more robust approach is for businesses to go beyond simple volumetric measurement to assess the full range of water impact from all sites. Its work with leading global pharmaceutical company
      <a href="/wiki/GlaxoSmithKline" title="GlaxoSmithKline">
       GlaxoSmithKline
      </a>
      (GSK) analysed four key categories: water availability, water quality, health impacts, and licence to operate (including reputational and regulatory risks) in order to enable GSK to quantitatively measure, and credibly reduce, its year-on-year water impact.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coca-Cola_Company" title="Coca-Cola Company">
       Coca-Cola Company
      </a>
      operates over a thousand manufacturing plants in about 200 countries. Making its drink uses a lot of water. Critics say its water footprint has been large. Coca-Cola has started to look at its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_sustainability" title="Water sustainability">
       water sustainability
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has now set out goals to reduce its water footprint such as treating the water it uses so it goes back into the environment in a clean state. Another goal is to find sustainable sources for the raw materials it uses in its drinks, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">
       sugarcane
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oranges" title="Oranges">
       oranges
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">
       corn
      </a>
      . By making its water footprint better, the company can reduce costs, improve the environment, and benefit the communities in which it operates.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naumann_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-naumann-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Water_footprint_of_nations">
       Water footprint of nations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Water footprint of nations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The water footprint of a nation is the water used to produce the goods and services consumed by the inhabitants of the nation. The internal water footprint is the appropriation of domestic water resources; the external water footprint is the appropriation of water resources in other countries. About 65% of Japan's total water footprint comes from outside the country; about 7% of the Chinese water footprint falls outside China.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterfootprintorg_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-waterfootprintorg-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       nuclear power
      </a>
      plants. Energy production annual water consumption in the EU 27 in 2011 was as billion m3 for gas 0.53, coal 1.54 and nuclear 2.44.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_energy" title="Wind energy">
       Wind energy
      </a>
      avoided the use of 387 million cubic metres (mn m³) of water in 2012 avoiding a cost of €743 mn. The cost of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Droughts" title="Droughts">
       droughts
      </a>
      in Europe over the past thirty years is according to the European Commission €100 billion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_water_footprint_and_virtual_water">
       Criticism of water footprint and virtual water
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticism of water footprint and virtual water">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Insufficient_consideration_of_consequences_of_proposed_water_saving_policies_to_farm_households">
       Insufficient consideration of consequences of proposed water saving policies to farm households
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Insufficient consideration of consequences of proposed water saving policies to farm households">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      According to Dennis Wichelns of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Water_Management_Institute" title="International Water Management Institute">
       International Water Management Institute
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       Although one goal of virtual water analysis is to describe opportunities for improving water security, there is almost no mention of the potential impacts of the prescriptions arising from that analysis on farm households in industrialized or developing countries. It is essential to consider more carefully the inherent flaws in the virtual water and water footprint perspectives, particularly when seeking guidance regarding policy decisions.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Insufficient_consideration_of_regional_water_scarcity">
       Insufficient consideration of regional water scarcity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Insufficient consideration of regional water scarcity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The application and interpretation of water footprints may sometimes be used to promote industrial activities that lead to facile criticism of certain products. For example, the 140 litres required for coffee production for one cup
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterfootprintorg_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-waterfootprintorg-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      might be of no harm to water resources if its cultivation occurs mainly in humid areas, but could be damaging in more arid regions. Other factors such as hydrology, climate, geology, topography, population and demographics should also be taken into account. Nevertheless, high water footprint calculations do suggest that environmental concern may be appropriate.
     </p>
     <p>
      The use of the term "footprint" can also confuse people familiar with the notion of a
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">
       carbon footprint
      </a>
      , because the water footprint concept includes sums of water quantities without necessarily evaluating related impacts. This is in contrast to the carbon footprint, where carbon emissions are not simply summarized but normalized by CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions, which are globally identical, to account for the environmental harm. The difference is due to the somewhat more complex nature of water; while involved in the global hydrological cycle, it is expressed in conditions both local and regional through various forms like river basins, watersheds, on down to groundwater (as part of larger aquifer systems).
     </p>
     <p>
      The water footprint of a business, the 'corporate water footprint', is defined as the total volume of fresh water that is used directly or indirectly to run and support a business. It is the total volume of water use to be associated with the use of the business outputs. The water footprint of a business consists of water used for producing/manufacturing or for supporting activities and the indirect water use in the producer’s supply chain. Water Credit for conserving water: Nagpur based innovator Shripad Vaidya's idea of giving water credit's, much like carbon credits, to those who save and conserve water is gaining ground. These water credits can be marketed or sold to those in need of surplus water for social,agricultural or industrial ventures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_water_use">
       Environmental water use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Environmental water use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although agriculture’s water use includes provision of important terrestrial environmental values (as discussed in the “Water footprint of products” section above), and much “green water’ is used in maintaining forests and wild lands, there is also direct environmental use (e.g. of surface water) that may be allocated by governments. For example, in California, where water use issues are sometimes severe because of drought, about 48 percent of “dedicated water use” in an average water year is for the environment (somewhat more than for agriculture).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Such environmental water use is for keeping streams flowing, maintaining aquatic and riparian habitats, keeping wetlands wet, etc.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sectoral_distributions_of_withdrawn_water_use">
       Sectoral distributions of withdrawn water use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sectoral distributions of withdrawn water use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Several nations estimate sectoral distribution of use of water withdrawn from surface and groundwater sources. For example, in Canada, in 2005, 42 billion cu. m of withdrawn water were used, of which about 38 billion cu. m was freshwater. Distribution of this use among sectors was: thermoelectric power generation 66.2%, manufacturing 13.6%, residential 9.0%, agriculture 4.7%, commercial and institutional 2.7%, water treatment and distribution systems 2.3%, mining 1.1%, and oil and gas extraction 0.5%. The 38 billion cu.m of freshwater withdrawn in that year can be compared with the nation’s annual freshwater yield (estimated as streamflow) of 3,472 billion cu.m.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sectoral distribution is different in many respects in the US, where agriculture accounts for about 39% of fresh water withdrawals, thermoelectric power generation 38%, industrial 4%, residential 1%, and mining (including oil and gas) 1%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maupin2014_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Maupin2014-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Within the agricultural sector, withdrawn water use is for irrigation and for livestock. Whereas all irrigation in the US (including loss in conveyance of irrigation water) is estimated to account for about 38 percent of US withdrawn freshwater use,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maupin2014_39-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Maupin2014-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the irrigation water used for production of livestock feed and forage has been estimated to account for about 9 percent,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and other withdrawn freshwater use for the livestock sector (for drinking, washdown of facilities, etc.) is estimated at about 0.7 percent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maupin2014_39-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Maupin2014-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because agriculture is a major user of withdrawn water, changes in the magnitude and efficiency of its water use are important. In the US, from 1980 (when agriculture’s withdrawn water use peaked) to 2010, there was a 23 percent reduction in agriculture’s use of withdrawn water,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maupin2014_39-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Maupin2014-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
      while US agricultural output increased by 49 percent over that period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        [41]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the US, irrigation water application data are collected in the quintennial Farm and Ranch Irrigation Survey, conducted as part of the Census of Agriculture. Such data indicate great differences in irrigation water use within various agricultural sectors. For example, about 14 percent of corn-for-grain land and 11 percent of soybean land in the US are irrigated, compared with 66 percent of vegetable land, 79 percent of orchard land and 97 percent of rice land.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDA2009_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-USDA2009-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
