   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Negawatt power
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      <b>
       Negawatt power
      </b>
      is a theoretical unit of
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       power
      </a>
      representing an amount of energy (measured in
      <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
       watts
      </a>
      ) saved. The energy saved is a direct result of
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">
       energy conservation
      </a>
      or increased
      <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">
       energy efficiency
      </a>
      . The term was coined by the chief scientist of the
      <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Institute" title="Rocky Mountain Institute">
       Rocky Mountain Institute
      </a>
      and environmentalist
      <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">
       Amory Lovins
      </a>
      in 1985, within the article, "Saving Gigabucks with Negawatts," where he argued that utility customers don’t want
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt-hours" title="Kilowatt-hours">
       kilowatt-hours
      </a>
      of electricity; they want energy services such as hot showers, cold beer, lit rooms, and spinning shafts, which can come more cheaply if electricity is used more efficiently.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abl_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-abl-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lovins felt an international behavioral change was necessary in order to decrease countries' dependence on excessive amounts of energy. The concept of a negawatt could influence a behavioral change in consumers by encouraging them to think about the energy that they spend.
     </p>
     <p>
      A negawatt market can be thought of as a
      <a href="/wiki/Secondary_market" title="Secondary market">
       secondary market
      </a>
      , in which electricity is allocated from one consumer to another consumer within the
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_market" title="Energy market">
       energy market
      </a>
      . In this market, negawatts could be treated as a
      <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">
       commodity
      </a>
      . Commodities have the ability to be traded across time and space, which would allow negawatts to be incorporated in the international trading system. Roughly 10% of all U.S. electrical generating capacity is in place to meet the last 1% of demand and there is where the immediate efficiency opportunity exists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On March 15, 2011, the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Regulatory_Commission" title="Federal Energy Regulatory Commission">
       Federal Energy Regulatory Commission
      </a>
      (FERC), the agency that regulates the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">
       electrical grid
      </a>
      , approved a rule establishing the approach to compensation for
      <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">
       demand response
      </a>
      resources intended to benefit customers and help improve the operation and competitiveness of organized wholesale energy markets. This means that negawatts produced by reducing electrical use can demand the same market prices as real megawatts of generated electricity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The incentives for a negawatt market include receiving money, reduction of national energy dependency, and the local electricity deregulation within certain nations or states. As for the cost incentive, those who produce negawatts or simply conserve energy can earn money by selling the saved energy. The negawatt market could help nations or states obtain a deregulated electricity system by creating another market to purchase electricity from.
     </p>
     <p>
      The negawatt market also has two main drawbacks. Currently, there is no way to precisely measure the amount of energy saved in negawatts, and electricity providers may not want customers to use less energy due to the loss of profit.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Negawatt power is a theoretical unit of power representing an amount of energy (measured in
      <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
       Watts
      </a>
      ) saved. Energy is saved by either increased efficiency or reduced energy consumption; the conserved energy is a "negawatt". The concept of a negawatt is simply a measure of power that is not used. Negawatts are a form of encouragement to motivate consumers to conserve energy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">
       Amory Lovins
      </a>
      considers the concept of conservation "a change in behavior based on the attitude 'Do Less to Use Less.'" He makes a distinction between conservation and efficiency by defining efficiency as "the application of technologies and best practices to eliminate waste based on the attitude, 'Do the same or more with less.'"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cost for negawatt power can be calculated using
      <a href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness_analysis" title="Cost-effectiveness analysis">
       cost-effectiveness analysis
      </a>
      or CEA. For energy efficiency investments a CEA calculation produces the value of saved energy or
      <i>
       negawatts
      </i>
      in $/kWh. Such a valuation allows comparing the price of negawatts with price of energy such as electricity from the grid or the cheapest renewable alternative. Specifically, Tuominen et al. have suggested using the dynamic generation cost type of CEA for energy efficiency investments as it includes the best accounting for the
      <a href="/wiki/Time_value_of_money" title="Time value of money">
       time value
      </a>
      among the various CEA methods available.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_development">
       Origin and development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin and development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negawatt" title="Negawatt">
       negawatt
      </a>
      is a unit in watts of energy saved. It is basically the opposite of a
      <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
       watt
      </a>
      . The term negawatt is derived from megawatt and was created by
      <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">
       Amory Lovins
      </a>
      . Lovins saw a typo — "negawatt" instead of "megawatt" — in a Colorado Public Utilities Commission report in 1989.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      He adopted the term to describe electricity that was not created using energy efficiency and conservation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was concerned with the large inefficiencies of energy use and came up with ways to remedy the problem. Lovins advocated for more efficient light bulbs and reflective metals that increase the intensity of light produced. He wanted consumers to use the energy produced in a smarter way by "wringing more work from the electricity we already have."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lovins felt an international behavioral change was necessary in order to decrease countries' dependence on excessive amounts of energy. The concept of a negawatt could influence a behavioral change in consumers by encouraging them to think about the energy that they spend.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Lovins,
      <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">
       energy efficiency
      </a>
      represents a profitable global market and American companies have at their disposal the technical innovations to lead the way. Not only should they "upgrade their plants and office buildings, but they should encourage the formation of negawatt markets".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lov_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lov-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lovins sees negawatt markets as a win-win solution to many environmental problems. Because it is "now generally cheaper to save fuel than to burn it, global warming, acid rain, and urban smog can be reduced not at a cost but at a profit".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lov_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lov-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Lovins explains that many companies are already enjoying the financial and other rewards that come from saving electricity. Yet progress in converting to electricity saving technologies has been slowed by the indifference or outright opposition of some utilities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abl_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-abl-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A second obstacle to efficiency is that many electricity-using devices are purchased by people who won’t be paying their running costs and thus have little incentive to consider efficiency. Lovins also believes that many customers "don't know what the best efficiency buys are, where to get them, or how to shop for them".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abl_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-abl-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2003 in France under the guide of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thierry_Salomon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thierry Salomon (page does not exist)">
       Thierry Salomon
      </a>
      23 scientists wrote "Le manifeste Négawatt."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Megawatt and negawatt seem to be reminiscent to the larger concept of ecological
      <a href="/wiki/Footprint" title="Footprint">
       footprint
      </a>
      , handprint
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.handsforchange.org/" rel="nofollow">
       handprint
      </a>
      , and by following this line of thought toward compatibility and comparability a second frame of concept seems appropriate:
      <a class="external text" href="http://mindprint.tv" rel="nofollow">
       mindprint
      </a>
      — the impact in another frame or setting where units or numbers can not be compared (see
      <a href="/wiki/Paradigm_shift" title="Paradigm shift">
       paradigm shift
      </a>
      ). See
      <a href="/wiki/Association_n%C3%A9gaWatt" title="Association négaWatt">
       association négaWatt
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Market">
       Market
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Market">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Amory Lovins has advocated a "negawatt revolution", arguing that utility customers don’t want
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt-hours" title="Kilowatt-hours">
       kilowatt-hours
      </a>
      of electricity; they want energy services such as hot showers, cold beer, lit rooms, and spinning shafts, which can come more cheaply if electricity is used more efficiently.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abl_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-abl-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lovins, defines the negawatt market as a way to reduce the gap between the cost of making and saving electricity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The negawatt market can be thought of as a
      <a href="/wiki/Secondary_market" title="Secondary market">
       secondary market
      </a>
      where electricity is allocated from areas of less use to areas of greater use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This would be a secondary market, due to the fact that it would reallocate electricity from one consumer to another within the already existing
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_market" title="Energy market">
       energy market
      </a>
      . Some feel that to establish a viable market, legislation and cooperation between primary producers, distributors, traders and consumers, may be required. This proposal would encourage the market to have legislative regulations, while still allowing the market to work within itself to set prices and allocate resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A negawatt market would allow "demand side resources" to participate in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholesale" title="Wholesale">
       wholesale
      </a>
      energy markets. These markets are commonly referred to as a demand response. Demand response can be defined as "enrolling large users of energy in programs to lower their usage in return for compensation, which helps take pressure off the grid"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This market would help take pressure off the grid because electricity could "be treated as a commodity just like copper or sowbellies," and therefore traded to areas that need it more than others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As any commodity, negawatts would have to be "tradable across time and space" to be an effective market.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Being able to trade negawatts across time and space would allow for an international trading system. To create a market for negawatts, energy companies will need to put more focus toward energy efficiency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This shift in focus would require a new "business structure that will thrive in the 'negawatt market'", which has not yet been developed. Market possibilities are being implemented internationally, implying that one day an official negawatt market will become a reality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">
       Implementation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Implementation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Government_implementation">
       Government implementation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Government implementation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Negawatt power is being implemented in many states in the U.S. and is emerging as an international strategy to reduce energy consumption. "Test negawatt auctions began in 1999 in
      <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">
       Connecticut
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">
       Georgia
      </a>
      and more than a dozen utility exchanges were in existence" in 2000. In an effort to move toward energy efficiency, New York has created programs "supported through Energy $mart, which is run by the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Energy_Research_and_Development_Authority" title="New York State Energy Research and Development Authority">
       New York State Energy Research and Development Authority
      </a>
      (NYSERDA), with money from a small surcharge on utility bills."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Negawatt power is implemented in California as well as Texas. "Some Texas congressmen and energy companies are trying to help California avert blackouts and utility price shocks this summer with [...] 'negawatts'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On January 1, 2009, the states of
      <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">
       South Australia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">
       Victoria (Australia)
      </a>
      became the first in Australia to offer "householders energy efficiency incentives programs delivered via local electricity retailers."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The UK is looking to balance the demand of electricity by proposing a bill in which "the government may pay major users for each “negawatt” of capacity they switch off" during high use. Payment may also be issued for supplying the grid.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Private_implementation">
       Private implementation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Private implementation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The negawatt market is being used by governments and companies. Aluminum manufacturers in the
      <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">
       Pacific Northwest
      </a>
      shut down their power plants and sold the unused energy because selling the negawatts was more profitable for the company than selling the aluminum product. This was possible because "The smelters hold power contracts with the federal Bonneville Power Administration that contain clauses allowing them to market the electricity."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Associated Electric company in rural Missouri is implementing the usage and spreading the knowledge of negawatts by performing energy audits at their customer's homes to show them where they could be saving electricity. Rebates are being given to help cutomers pay for more energy-efficient,
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">
       Energy Star
      </a>
      appliances. Keith Hartner, the CEO of Associated Electric Cooperative Inc., feels that negawatts are generating savings for their customers and for the company as well: “The goal of this program is to save money not only at the generator but also at the meter for the members.”
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Individual households can practice negawatts through using energy-efficient lighting and
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">
       Energy Star
      </a>
      appliances as well as simply reducing
      <a href="/wiki/Standby_power" title="Standby power">
       standby power
      </a>
      . The resulting savings sometimes offset the investment in new high efficient light bulbs and appliances. These efficiencies can offset a portion of the cost of a residential
      <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">
       photovoltaic system
      </a>
      . Negawatts reduces the overall size of the photovoltaic system required for the household, because they are consuming less electricity. This results in a faster payback period for the photovoltaic system. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_San_Diego" title="City of San Diego">
       City of San Diego
      </a>
      has created a negawatts initiative called "Reduce then Produce" to promote this idea.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">
       Advantages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Advantages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cost">
       Cost
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cost">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The most noteworthy advantage in creating a negawatt market is the cost incentive. As many will say, "The cheapest watt is the one that's never created."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartram.2C_Rodgers_2010_P._8-14_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bartram.2C_Rodgers_2010_P._8-14-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this market, the consumers who increase their home efficiency, or decrease their energy consumption, can earn money by selling the saved electricity. This is similar to an
      <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">
       emissions trading
      </a>
      or a cap-and-trade system, in which the energy that is not used can be bought from the consumers who saved the energy and sold to those who need to purchase the extra energy. Providers of electricity can encourage consumers to sell back their unused energy, or negawatts, especially during peak hours. A major question that electric companies need to ask themselves is whether it is less expensive to pay consumers to reduce consumption for a few instances a year or to build and maintain a side-supply resource that would only be used a few times a year. Many argue that the "cost of foregone consumption is less than the cost of increasing the supply of electricity."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      If a consumer conserves a substantial amount of energy then there can even be an allowance for a
      <a href="/wiki/Tax_deduction" title="Tax deduction">
       tax deduction
      </a>
      . According to the Negawatt Power Solutions Group,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a "building that achieved a 50% energy cost reduction may be eligible for tax deduction up to $1.80 per square foot."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Negawatts can help alleviate some of the costs of constructing new, efficient buildings. "The negawatt revolution now provides a way to cut construction costs, capture big returns on capital in renovations, [and] dramatically cut operating expenses."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Existing buildings can be made more efficient by renovating the insulation to cut back on electricity used for heating, installing more efficient light fixtures, and an upgraded
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       HVAC
      </a>
      design. Renovating a building to be more energy efficient will cost less than creating entirely new, more efficient buildings.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_of_national_energy_dependency">
       Reduction of national energy dependency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reduction of national energy dependency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The reduction of the amount of energy that a region emits can slowly separate a nation from a high energy consumption of oil. The desire to become a less energy-dependent country may promote behavioral changes and a different societal outlook on energy usage. These potential societal perspective changes could lead to an overall more sustainable country. The reduced consumption of energy would also produce less
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gases
      </a>
      , which could have positive outcomes on the economy, political parties, and interest groups, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">
       environmentalists
      </a>
      . According to Lovins, improvements in energy efficiency and conservation, due to a change in behavioral attitudes, have a huge potential to reduce a country's "long-term energy needs," such as the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Local_deregulation">
       Local deregulation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Local deregulation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Some conservatives claim that the negawatt market could help nations or states have a deregulated electricity system. This would allow a nation or a state to experiment with "electricity deregulation," in which "demand reductions could be purchased with a minimum of disruption to businesses, workers and the economy."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the United States, for instance, the negawatt market could assist California with
      <a href="/wiki/Rolling_blackout" title="Rolling blackout">
       rolling blackouts
      </a>
      by making more power available from consumers who choose to conserve energy or increase their negawatts. California could achieve the goal of
      <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">
       deregulation
      </a>
      by allowing a deficit area to "purchase an emergency supply from anywhere within with West"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_21-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      in which "the ultimate purpose of deregulation was to allow competition in the electricity market and consumer choice of electricity providers."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Negawatt power would allow the consumers in a country's economy to decide how the energy will be distributed: essentially benefiting regions that hope to have a deregulated electricity system.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Drawbacks">
       Drawbacks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Drawbacks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Difficulty_in_creating_a_negawatt_market">
       Difficulty in creating a negawatt market
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Difficulty in creating a negawatt market">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Currently, there is no way to precisely measure the amount of energy saved in negawatts; it can only be theoretically determined based on the consumer’s history of energy use. Visualizing has a very important role in “enabling residents to understand and manage their energy use,” which serves as a form of encouragement for consumers to conserve energy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartram.2C_Rodgers_2010_P._8-14_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bartram.2C_Rodgers_2010_P._8-14-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Without the visualization of the energy use, it is difficult to conceptualize negawatts because the consumer cannot see a precise value of the amount of saved energy.
      <a href="/wiki/Smart_meter" title="Smart meter">
       Smart meters
      </a>
      are becoming a more developed technology to measure energy usage, but "consumers are calling on state regulators to move cautiously on smart meters, citing complaints in some states that the meters are raising electric bills rather than lowering them."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some municipally owned utilities and cooperatives argue that negawatt power "lets consumers treat electricity as a property right rather than a service [...giving them] legal entitlement to power [that they] don't consume.”
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_21-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This would indicate that consumers would treat electricity as a property, not a service. Some people, including the senior vice president Joe Nipper from the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Public_Power_Association" title="American Public Power Association">
       American Public Power Association
      </a>
      , oppose the idea that people would receive money for power that they did not even spend.
     </p>
     <p>
      Electricity price caps may also need to be implemented in order for the emerging negawatts market to function correctly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      To some, government limitations on markets are unfavorable. There is a current view that negawatts are worth pursuing, but that they are unlikely to satisfy the world's thirst for energy to the extent their advocates assume.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Expense_of_efficiency">
       Expense of efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Negawatt_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Expense of efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Saving energy by the negawatt and creating a negawatt market can present several drawbacks for manufacturers and electricity providers. Manufacturers are less inclined to make energy-efficient devices which meet a specific standard, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_star" title="Energy star">
       Energy star
      </a>
      's standard, because of increased time and cost, while receiving minimal profit. Overall, electricity providers may not want customers to use less energy due to the loss of profit. Some even argue that producing energy-efficient products, such as light bulbs, actually simulate more demand, “resulting in more energy being purchased for conversion into light” of the same strength.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Customers may also be less inclined to buy products that are more energy efficient due to the increase in cost and time spent. "Even when the information is known and, despite the overall long-term cost-saving potential, the price of energy is too low...for individuals to justify the initial cost of energy efficiency measures."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Not only are energy efficient devices more expensive, but "consumers are poorly informed about the savings on offer. Even when they can do the sums, the transaction costs are high: it is a time-consuming chore for someone to identify the best energy-saving equipment, buy it and get it installed."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The technology used to measure the amount of energy that a consumer uses and saves, known as
      <a href="/wiki/Smart_meter" title="Smart meter">
       smart meters
      </a>
      , grid systems, or energy dashboards, require time for the consumer to understand. Some argue
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2016)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      that people need to have access to “simple yet effective information systems to help users understand their energy without having to become technology experts.”
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartram.2C_Rodgers_2010_P._8-14_27-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bartram.2C_Rodgers_2010_P._8-14-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The current visual energy measuring devices could be made simpler and less expensive, which may encourage customers to save more energy, thus increasing their negawatts.
     </p>
     <p>
      The capital cost of energy-efficiency is erroneously argued by some to exceed the energy savings (energy required for raw materials, manufacture and transport to point of sale versus running costs). For instance they argue that the capital cost of buying a new energy efficient car will not be recouped unless the car runs for more than ten years. However increased energy efficiency leads to less usage and longer life.
     </p>
