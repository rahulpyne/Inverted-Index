    Synthetic fuel
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:248px;">

        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="211" data-file-width="246" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/NREL_FT_diesel_vs_conventional_diesel_photo.jpg" width="246"/>

     <p>
      <b>
       Synthetic fuel
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       synfuel
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fuel" title="Liquid fuel">
       liquid fuel
      </a>
      , or sometimes
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_gas" title="Fuel gas">
       gaseous fuel
      </a>
      , obtained from
      <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">
       syngas
      </a>
      , a mixture of
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      , in which the syngas was derived from gasification of solid feedstocks such as
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      or by reforming of
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Common methods for manufacturing synthetic fuels include the
      <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">
       Fischer Tropsch conversion
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquids" title="Gas to liquids">
       methanol to gasoline conversion
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      or direct
      <a href="/wiki/Coal_liquefaction" title="Coal liquefaction">
       coal liquefaction
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As of July 2009, worldwide commercial synthetic fuels production capacity was over 240,000 barrels per day (38,000 m
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      /d), with numerous new projects in construction or development.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_and_principles">
       Classification and principles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification and principles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term 'synthetic fuel' has several different meanings and it may include different types of fuels. More traditional definitions, e.g. definition given by the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">
       International Energy Agency
      </a>
      , define 'synthetic fuel' as any liquid fuel obtained from coal or natural gas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-iea-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration" title="Energy Information Administration">
       Energy Information Administration
      </a>
      defines synthetic fuels in its Annual Energy Outlook 2006, as fuels produced from coal, natural gas, or
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">
       feedstocks
      </a>
      through chemical conversion into
      <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_crude" title="Synthetic crude">
       synthetic crude
      </a>
      and/or synthetic liquid products.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outlook2006_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-outlook2006-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A number of synthetic fuel's definitions include also fuels produced from biomass, and industrial and municipal waste.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-miami-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mti_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mti-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thipse_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-thipse-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The definition of synthetic fuel also allows
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_sands" title="Oil sands">
       oil sands
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">
       oil shale
      </a>
      as synthetic fuel sources, and in addition to liquid fuels, synthesized gaseous fuels are also considered to be synthetic fuels:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-handbook-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook2_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-handbook2-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      in his 'Synthetic fuels handbook' petrochemist James G. Speight included liquid and gaseous fuels as well as clean solid fuels produced by conversion of coal, oil shale or tar sands, and various forms of biomass, although he admits that in the context of substitutes for petroleum-based fuels it has even wider meaning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook2_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-handbook2-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Depending on the context,
      <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">
       methanol
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">
       ethanol
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      may also be included.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee4_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lee4-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapedes_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lapedes-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Synthetic fuels are produced by the chemical process of
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">
       conversion
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook2_10-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-handbook2-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Conversion methods could be direct conversion into liquid transportation fuels, or indirect conversion, in which the source substance is converted initially into
      <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">
       syngas
      </a>
      which then goes through additional conversion process to become liquid fuels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outlook2006_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-outlook2006-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Basic conversion methods include
      <a href="/wiki/Carbonization" title="Carbonization">
       carbonization
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       pyrolysis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">
       hydrogenation
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_dissolution" title="Thermal dissolution">
       thermal dissolution
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luik_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-luik-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Direct conversion of coal to synthetic fuel was originally developed in Germany.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cicero_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cicero-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Bergius_process" title="Bergius process">
       Bergius process
      </a>
      was developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bergius" title="Friedrich Bergius">
       Friedrich Bergius
      </a>
      , yielding a patent in 1913. Karl Goldschmidt invited him to build an industrial plant at his factory the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Th._Goldschmidt_AG&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Th. Goldschmidt AG (page does not exist)">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Processes">
       Processes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Processes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are numerous processes that can be used to produce synthetic fuels.
     </p>
     <p>
      These broadly fall into three categories: Indirect, Direct, and Biofuel processes.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement">
         <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (July 2009)">
          dubious
         </span>
        </a>
        <span class="metadata">
         –
         <a href="/wiki/Talk:Synthetic_fuel#Dubious" title="Talk:Synthetic fuel">
          discuss
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="center">
      <div class="thumb tnone">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:727px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coal_Liquefaction_Technologies_diagram_from_Jan_28_09_technical_support_document_CTL_industry_overview_published_by_the_Office_of_Air_and_Radiation_US_EPA_.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="678" data-file-width="725" height="678" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/b1/Coal_Liquefaction_Technologies_diagram_from_Jan_28_09_technical_support_document_CTL_industry_overview_published_by_the_Office_of_Air_and_Radiation_US_EPA_.jpg" width="725"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         This is a listing of many of the different technologies used for synthetic fuel production. Please note that although this list was compiled for coal to liquids technologies, many of the same processes can also be used with biomass or natural gas feedstocks.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_conversion">
       Indirect conversion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Indirect conversion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Indirect conversion has the widest deployment worldwide, with global production totaling around 260,000 barrels per day (41,000 m
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      /d), and many additional projects under active development.
     </p>
     <p>
      Indirect conversion broadly refers to a process in which biomass, coal, or natural gas is converted to a mix of
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      known as syngas either through
      <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">
       gasification
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_methane_reforming" title="Steam methane reforming">
       steam methane reforming
      </a>
      , and that syngas is processed into a liquid transportation fuel using one of a number of different conversion techniques depending on the desired end product.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indirect_conversion_synthetic_fuels_processes.jpg">
       <img alt="Indirect conversion synthetic fuels processes.jpg" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="848" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/81/Indirect_conversion_synthetic_fuels_processes.jpg" width="848"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      The primary technologies that produce synthetic fuel from syngas are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch" title="Fischer-Tropsch">
       Fischer-Tropsch
      </a>
      synthesis and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobil_process" title="Mobil process">
       Mobil process
      </a>
      (also known as Methanol To Gasoline, or MTG). There are some technologies under development to produce ethanol from syngas, though these have not yet been demonstrated at commercial scale.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Fischer-Tropsch process reacts syngas with typically a cobalt or iron-based catalyst, and transforms the gas into liquid products (primarily
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">
       diesel fuel
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">
       jet fuel
      </a>
      ) and potentially waxes (depending on the FT process employed).
     </p>
     <p>
      The process of producing synfuels through indirect conversion is often referred to as coal-to-liquids (CTL),
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquids" title="Gas to liquids">
       gas-to-liquids
      </a>
      (GTL) or
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass_to_liquid" title="Biomass to liquid">
       biomass-to-liquids
      </a>
      (BTL), depending on the initial feedstock. At least three projects (Ohio River Clean Fuels, Illinois Clean Fuels, and Rentech Natchez) are combining coal and biomass feedstocks, creating hybrid-feedstock synthetic fuels known as Coal and Biomass To Liquids (CBTL).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Indirect conversion process technologies can also be used to produce hydrogen, potentially for use in fuel cell vehicles, either as slipstream co-product, or as a primary output.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_conversion">
       Direct conversion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Direct conversion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Direct conversion refers to processes in which coal or biomass feedstocks are converted directly into intermediate or final products, without going through the intermediate step of conversion to syngas via
      <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">
       gasification
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Direct conversion processes can be broadly broken up into two different methods: Pyrolysis and carbonization, and hydrogenation.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogenation_processes">
       Hydrogenation processes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hydrogenation processes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Bergius_process" title="Bergius process">
       Bergius process
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      One of the main methods of direct conversion of coal to liquids by hydrogenation process is the Bergius process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this process, coal is liquefied by mixing it with hydrogen gas and heating the system (hydrogenation). Dry coal is mixed with heavy oil recycled from the process.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">
       Catalyst
      </a>
      is typically added to the mixture. The reaction occurs at between 400 °C (752 °F) to 500 °C (932 °F) and 20 to 70
      <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">
       MPa
      </a>
      hydrogen pressure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Speight2010_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Speight2010-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The reaction can be summarized as follows:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Speight2010_30-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Speight2010-30">

       </a>
      </sup>

      After
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">

      </a>
      several plants were built in Germany; these plants were extensively used during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      to supply Germany with fuel and lubricants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Kohleoel Process, developed in Germany by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAG_Aktiengesellschaft" title="RAG Aktiengesellschaft">
       Ruhrkohle
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/VEBA" title="VEBA">
       VEBA
      </a>
      , was used in the demonstration plant with the capacity of 200 ton of
      <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">
       lignite
      </a>
      per day, built in
      <a href="/wiki/Bottrop" title="Bottrop">
       Bottrop
      </a>
      , Germany. This plant operated from 1981 to 1987. In this process, coal is mixed with a recycle solvent and iron catalyst. After preheating and pressurizing, H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      is added. The process takes place in tubular reactor at the pressure of 300 bar and at the temperature of 470 °C (880 °F).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dti-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This process was also explored by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SASOL" title="SASOL">
       SASOL
      </a>
      in South Africa.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1970-1980s, Japanese companies
      <a href="/wiki/JFE_Holdings" title="JFE Holdings">
       Nippon Kokan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Metal_Industries" title="Sumitomo Metal Industries">
       Sumitomo Metal Industries
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Heavy_Industries" title="Mitsubishi Heavy Industries">
       Mitsubishi Heavy Industries
      </a>
      developed the NEDOL process. In this process, coal is mixed with a recycled solvent and a synthetic iron-based catalyst; after preheating H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      is added. The reaction takes place in tubular reactor at temperature between 430 °C (810 °F) and 465 °C (870 °F) at the pressure 150-200 bar. The produced oil has low quality and requires intensive upgrading.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_32-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-dti-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      H-Coal process, developed by Hydrocarbon Research, Inc., in 1963, mixes pulverized coal with recycled liquids, hydrogen and catalyst in the ebullated bed reactor. Advantages of this process are that dissolution and oil upgrading are taking place in the single reactor, products have high H:C ratio, and a fast reaction time, while the main disadvantages are high gas yield, high hydrogen consumption, and limitation of oil usage only as a boiler oil because of impurities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee3_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lee3-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The SRC-I and SRC-II (Solvent Refined Coal) processes were developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Oil" title="Gulf Oil">
       Gulf Oil
      </a>
      and implemented as pilot plants in the United States in the 1960s and 1970s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_32-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-dti-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Nuclear Utility Services Corporation developed hydrogenation process which was patented by Wilburn C. Schroeder in 1976. The process involved dried, pulverized coal mixed with roughly 1wt%
      <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">
       molybdenum
      </a>
      catalysts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook2_10-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-handbook2-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hydrogenation occurred by use of high temperature and pressure syngas produced in a separate gasifier. The process ultimately yielded a synthetic crude product,
      <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">
       Naphtha
      </a>
      , a limited amount of C
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      /C
      <sub>
       4
      </sub>
      gas, light-medium weight liquids (C
      <sub>
       5
      </sub>
      -C
      <sub>
       10
      </sub>
      ) suitable for use as fuels, small amounts of NH
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      and significant amounts of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other single-stage hydrogenation processes are the
      <a href="/wiki/Exxon_Donor_Solvent_Process" title="Exxon Donor Solvent Process">
       Exxon Donor Solvent Process
      </a>
      , the Imhausen High-pressure Process, and the Conoco Zinc Chloride Process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_32-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-dti-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is also a number of two-stage direct liquefaction processes; however, after the 1980s only the Catalytic Two-stage Liquefaction Process, modified from the H-Coal Process; the Liquid Solvent Extraction Process by
      <a href="/wiki/British_Coal" title="British Coal">
       British Coal
      </a>
      ; and the Brown Coal Liquefaction Process of Japan have been developed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_32-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-dti-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Chevron Corporation developed a process invented by Joel W. Rosenthal called the Chevron Coal Liquefaction Process (CCLP). It is unique due the close-coupling of the non-catalytic dissolver and the catalytic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroprocessing" title="Hydroprocessing">
       hydroprocessing
      </a>
      unit. The oil produced had properties that were unique when compared to other coal oils; it was lighter and had far fewer heteroatom impurities. The process was scaled-up to the 6 ton per day level, but not proven commercially.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrolysis_and_carbonization_processes">
       Pyrolysis and carbonization processes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pyrolysis and carbonization processes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Karrick_process" title="Karrick process">
       Karrick process
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      There are a number of different carbonization processes. The carbonization conversion occurs through
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       pyrolysis
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Destructive_distillation" title="Destructive distillation">
       destructive distillation
      </a>
      , and it produces condensable
      <a href="/wiki/Coal_tar" title="Coal tar">
       coal tar
      </a>
      , oil and water vapor, non-condensable
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_gas" title="Synthetic gas">
       synthetic gas
      </a>
      , and a solid residue-
      <a href="/wiki/Char" title="Char">
       char
      </a>
      . The condensed coal tar and oil are then further processed by hydrogenation to remove
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
       sulfur
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      species, after which they are processed into fuels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee3_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lee3-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The typical example of carbonization is the
      <a href="/wiki/Karrick_process" title="Karrick process">
       Karrick process
      </a>
      . The process was invented by
      <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Karrick" title="Lewis Karrick">
       Lewis Cass Karrick
      </a>
      in the 1920s. The Karrick process is a low-temperature
      <a href="/wiki/Carbonization" title="Carbonization">
       carbonization
      </a>
      process, where coal is heated at 680 °F (360 °C) to 1,380 °F (750 °C) in the absence of air. These temperatures optimize the production of coal tars richer in lighter hydrocarbons than normal coal tar. However, the produced liquids are mostly a by-product and the main product is semi-coke, a solid and smokeless fuel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hook_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hook-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The COED Process, developed by
      <a href="/wiki/FMC_Corporation" title="FMC Corporation">
       FMC Corporation
      </a>
      , uses a
      <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed" title="Fluidized bed">
       fluidized bed
      </a>
      for processing, in combination with increasing temperature, through four stages of pyrolysis. Heat is transferred by hot gases produced by combustion of part of the produced char. A modification of this process, the COGAS Process, involves the addition of gasification of char.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee3_33-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-lee3-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The TOSCOAL Process, an analogue to the
      <a href="/wiki/TOSCO_II_process" title="TOSCO II process">
       TOSCO II oil shale retorting process
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lurgi-Ruhrgas_process" title="Lurgi-Ruhrgas process">
       Lurgi-Ruhrgas process
      </a>
      , which is also used for the
      <a href="/wiki/Shale_oil_extraction" title="Shale oil extraction">
       shale oil extraction
      </a>
      , uses hot recycled solids for the heat transfer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee3_33-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-lee3-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Liquid yields of pyrolysis and Karrick processes are generally low for practical use for synthetic liquid fuel production.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hook_35-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hook-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Furthermore, the resulting liquids are of low quality and require further treatment before they can be used as motor fuels. In summary, there is little possibility that this process will yield economically viable volumes of liquid fuel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hook_35-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-hook-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuels_processes">
       Biofuels processes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Biofuels processes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      One example of a Biofuel-based synthetic fuel process is Hydrotreated Renewable Jet (HRJ) fuel. There are a number of variants of these processes under development, and the testing and certification process for HRJ aviation fuels is beginning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are two such process under development by
      <a href="/wiki/UOP_LLC" title="UOP LLC">
       UOP
      </a>
      . One using solid biomass feedstocks, and one using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bio-oil" title="Bio-oil">
       bio-oil
      </a>
      and fats. The process using solid second-generation biomass sources such as switchgrass or woody
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      uses pyrolysis to produce a bio-oil, which is then catalytically stabilized and deoxygenated to produce a jet-range fuel. The process using natural oils and fats goes through a deoxygenation process, followed by hydrocracking and isomerization to produce a renewable Synthetic Paraffinic Kerosene jet fuel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">
       Economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The economics of synthetic fuel manufacture vary greatly depending the feedstock used, the precise process employed, site characteristics such as feedstock and transportation costs, and the cost of additional equipment required to control emissions. The examples described below indicate a wide range of production costs between $20/
      <a href="/wiki/Barrel_(unit)" title="Barrel (unit)">
       BBL
      </a>
      for large-scale gas-to-liquids, to as much as $240/BBL for small-scale biomass-to-liquids + Carbon Capture and Sequestration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In order to be economically viable, projects must do much better than just being competitive head-to-head with oil. They must also generate a sufficient return on investment to justify the capital investment in the project.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="GTL_economics">
       GTL economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: GTL economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A synthetic fuel manufactured from natural gas (GTL), without CCS, in a large scale plant in the Middle East (where gas is relatively inexpensive), is expected (as of 2005) to be competitive with oil down to approximately $20 per barrel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Recent advances by the oil company Shell have seen synthetic fuels start to become profitable. The company built a GTL (gas-to-liquid) plant in Qatar, inaugurated in 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was to be capable of producing 300,000 barrels per day (48,000 m
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      /d) of synthetic fuels and other products, using natural gas as a feedstock.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="CTL.2FCBTL.2FBTL_economics">
       CTL/CBTL/BTL economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: CTL/CBTL/BTL economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      According to a December 2007 study, a medium scale (30,000 BPD) coal-to-liquids plant (CTL) sited in the US using bituminous coal, is expected to be competitive with oil down to roughly $52–56/bbl crude-oil equivalent. Adding carbon capture and sequestration to the project was expected to add an additional $10/BBL to the required selling price, though this may be offset by revenues from
      <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">
       enhanced oil recovery
      </a>
      , or by tax credits, or the eventual sale of carbon credits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        [46]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A recent NETL study examined the relative economics of a number of different process configurations for the production of indirect FT fuels using biomass, coal, and CCS.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This study determined a price at which the plant would not only be profitable, but also make a sufficient return to yield a 20% return on the equity investment required to build the plant.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       This chapter details an analysis which derives the Required Selling Price (RSP) of the FT diesel fuels produced in order to determine the economic feasibility and relative competitiveness of the different plant options. A sensitivity analysis was performed to determine how carbon control regulations such as an emissions trading scheme for transportation fuels would affect the price of both petroleum-derived diesel and FT diesel from the different plants. The key findings of these analyses were: (1) CTL plants equipped with CCS are competitive at crude oil prices as low as $86 per barrel and have less life cycle GHG emissions than petroleum-derived diesel. These plants become more economically competitive as carbon prices increase. (2) The incremental cost of adding simple CCS is very low (7 cents per gallon) because CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       capture is an inherent part of the FT process. This becomes the economically preferred option at carbon prices above $5/mtCO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       eq.27 (3) BTL systems are hindered by limited biomass availability which affects the maximum plant size, thereby limiting potential economies of scale. This, combined with relatively high biomass costs results in FT diesel prices which are double that of other configurations: $6.45 to $6.96/gal compared to $2.56 to $2.82/gal for CTL and 15wt% CBTL systems equipped with CCS.
      </p>
      <p>
       The conclusion reached based on these findings was that both the CTL with CCS and the 8wt% to 15wt% CBTL with CCS configurations may offer the most pragmatic solutions to the nation’s energy strategy dilemma: GHG emission reductions which are significant (5% to 33% below the petroleum baseline) at diesel RSPs that are only half as much as the BTL options ($2.56 to $2.82 per gallon compared to $6.45 to $6.96 per gallon for BTL). These options are economically feasible when crude oil prices are $86 to $95 per barrel.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      These economics can change in the event that plentiful low-cost biomass sources can be found, lowing the cost of biomass inputs, and improving economies of scale.
     </p>
     <p>
      Economics for solid feedstock indirect FT process plants are further confused by carbon regulation. Generally, since permitting a CTL plant without CCS will likely be impossible, and CTL+CCS plants have a lower carbon footprint than conventional fuels, carbon regulation is expected to be balance-positive for synthetic fuel production. But it impacts the economics of different process configurations in different ways. The NETL study picked a blended CBTL process using 5-15% biomass alongside coal as the most economical in a range of carbon price and probable future regulation scenarios. Unfortunately, because of scale and cost constraints, pure BTL processes did not score well until very high carbon prices were assumed, though again this may improve with better feedstocks and more efficient larger scale projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_direct_coal_liquefaction_economics">
       Chinese direct coal liquefaction economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Chinese direct coal liquefaction economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Shenhua Group recently reported that their direct coal liquefaction process is competitive with oil prices above $60 per barrel.&lt;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shenhua_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-shenhua-47">
        [47]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Previous reports have indicated an anticipated cost of production of less than $30 per barrel, based on a direct coal liquefaction process, and a coal mining cost of under $10/ton.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In October 2011, actual price of coal in China was as high as $135/ton.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Security_considerations">
       Security considerations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Security considerations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A central consideration for the development of synthetic fuel is the security factor of securing domestic fuel supply from domestic biomass and coal. Nations that are rich in biomass and coal can use synthetic fuel to off-set their use of petroleum derived fuels and foreign oil.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_considerations">
       Environmental considerations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Environmental considerations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The environmental footprint of a given synthetic fuel varies greatly depending on which process is employed, what feedstock is used, what pollution controls are employed, and what the transportation distance and method are for both feedstock procurement and end-product distribution.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In many locations, project development will not be possible due to permitting restrictions if a process design is chosen that does not meet local requirements for clean air, water, and increasingly, lifecycle carbon emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        [52]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lifecycle_greenhouse_gas_emissions">
       Lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Among different indirect FT synthetic fuels production technologies, potential emissions of greenhouse gasses vary greatly.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_to_liquids" title="Coal to liquids">
       Coal to liquids
      </a>
      ("CTL") without
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_sequestration" title="Carbon capture and sequestration">
       carbon capture and sequestration
      </a>
      ("CCS") is expected to result in a significantly higher carbon footprint than conventional petroleum-derived fuels (+147%).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the other hand, biomass-to-liquids with CCS could deliver a 358% reduction in lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Both of these plants fundamentally use
      <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">
       gasification
      </a>
      and FT conversion synthetic fuels technology, but they deliver wildly divergent environmental footprints.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coal_and_biomass_co_converstion_to_transportation_fuels,_Michael_E_Reed,_DOE_NETL_Office_of_Fossil_Energy,_oct_17_2007.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="897" data-file-width="1197" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d9/Coal_and_biomass_co_converstion_to_transportation_fuels%2C_Michael_E_Reed%2C_DOE_NETL_Office_of_Fossil_Energy%2C_oct_17_2007.jpg/300px-Coal_and_biomass_co_converstion_to_transportation_fuels%2C_Michael_E_Reed%2C_DOE_NETL_Office_of_Fossil_Energy%2C_oct_17_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d9/Coal_and_biomass_co_converstion_to_transportation_fuels%2C_Michael_E_Reed%2C_DOE_NETL_Office_of_Fossil_Energy%2C_oct_17_2007.jpg/450px-Coal_and_biomass_co_converstion_to_transportation_fuels%2C_Michael_E_Reed%2C_DOE_NETL_Office_of_Fossil_Energy%2C_oct_17_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d9/Coal_and_biomass_co_converstion_to_transportation_fuels%2C_Michael_E_Reed%2C_DOE_NETL_Office_of_Fossil_Energy%2C_oct_17_2007.jpg/600px-Coal_and_biomass_co_converstion_to_transportation_fuels%2C_Michael_E_Reed%2C_DOE_NETL_Office_of_Fossil_Energy%2C_oct_17_2007.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coal_and_biomass_co_converstion_to_transportation_fuels,_Michael_E_Reed,_DOE_NETL_Office_of_Fossil_Energy,_oct_17_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Lifecycle carbon emissions profiles of various fuels, including many synthetic fuels. Coal and biomass co-conversion to transportation fuels, Michael E. Reed, DOE NETL Office of Fossil Energy, Oct 17 2007
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Generally, CTL without CCS has a higher greenhouse gas footprint. CTL with CCS has a 9-15% reduction in lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions compared to that of petroleum derived diesel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        [53]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      CBTL+CCS plants that blend biomass alongside coal while sequestering carbon do progressively better the more biomass is added. Depending on the type of biomass, the assumptions about root storage, and the transportation logistics, at conservatively 40% biomass alongside coal, CBTL+CCS plants achieve a neutral lifecycle greenhouse gas footprint. At more than 40% biomass, they begin to go lifecycle negative, and effectively store carbon in the ground for every gallon of fuels that they produce.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ultimately BTL plants employing CCS could store massive amounts of carbon while producing transportation fuels from sustainably produced biomass feedstocks, although there are a number of significant economic hurdles, and a few technical hurdles that would have to be overcome to enable the development of such facilities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Serious consideration must also be given to the type and method of feedstock procurement for either the coal or biomass used in such facilities, as reckless development could exacerbate environmental problems caused by
      <a href="/wiki/Mountaintop_removal_mining" title="Mountaintop removal mining">
       mountaintop removal mining
      </a>
      , land use change, fertilizer runoff,
      <a href="/wiki/Food_vs._fuel" title="Food vs. fuel">
       food vs. fuels
      </a>
      concerns, or many other potential factors. Or they could not, depending entirely on project-specific factors on a plant-by-plant basis.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A study from U.S. Department of Energy National Energy Technology Laboratory with much more in-depth information of CBTL life-cycle emissions "Affordable Low Carbon Diesel from Domestic Coal and Biomass".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_27-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-thomas-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Hybrid hydrogen-carbon processes have also been proposed recently
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        [54]
       </a>
      </sup>
      as another closed-carbon cycle alternative, combining
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_electricity" title="Clean electricity">
       'clean' electricity
      </a>
      , recycled CO, H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      and captured CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      with biomass as inputs as a way of reducing the biomass needed.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fuels_emissions">
       Fuels emissions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Fuels emissions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The fuels produced by the various synthetic fuels process also have a wide range of potential environmental performance, though they tend to be very uniform based on the type of synthetic fuels process used (i.e. the tailpipe emissions characteristics of Fischer Tropsch diesel tend to be the same, though their lifecycle greenhouse gas footprint can vary substantially based on which plant produced the fuel, depending on feedstock and plant level sequestration considerations.)
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In particular, Fischer tropsch diesel and jet fuels deliver dramatic across-the-board reductions in all major criteria pollutants such as SOx, NOx, Particulate Matter, and Hydrocarbon emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        [55]
       </a>
      </sup>
      These fuels, because of their high level of purity and lack of contaminants, further enable the use of advanced emissions control equipment that has been shown to virtually eliminate HC, CO, and PM emissions from diesel vehicles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        [56]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In testimony before the Subcommittee on Energy and Environment of the U.S. House of Representatives the following statement was made by a senior scientist from Rentech:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       F-T fuels offer numerous benefits to aviation users. The first is an immediate reduction in particulate emissions. F-T jet fuel has been shown in laboratory combusters and engines to reduce PM emissions by 96% at idle and 78% under cruise operation. Validation of the reduction in other turbine engine emissions is still under way. Concurrent to the PM reductions is an immediate reduction in CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       emissions from F-T fuel. F-T fuels inherently reduce CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       emissions because they have higher energy content per carbon content of the fuel, and the fuel is less dense than conventional jet fuel allowing aircraft to fly further on the same load of fuel.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        [57]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cleanliness of these FT synthetic fuels is further demonstrated by the fact that they are sufficiently non-toxic and environmentally benign as to be considered biodegradable. This owes primarily to the near-absence of sulfur and extremely low level of aromatics present in the fuel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        [58]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional" style="max-width: 886px;_width: 886px;">
      <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 435px">
       <div style="width: 435px">
        <div class="thumb" style="width: 430px;">
         <div style="margin:15.5px auto;">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OSD_Clean_Fuel_Initiative_FT_Diesel_Emissions_Presentation_Slide.jpg">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="895" data-file-width="1197" height="299" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/62/OSD_Clean_Fuel_Initiative_FT_Diesel_Emissions_Presentation_Slide.jpg/400px-OSD_Clean_Fuel_Initiative_FT_Diesel_Emissions_Presentation_Slide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/62/OSD_Clean_Fuel_Initiative_FT_Diesel_Emissions_Presentation_Slide.jpg/600px-OSD_Clean_Fuel_Initiative_FT_Diesel_Emissions_Presentation_Slide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/62/OSD_Clean_Fuel_Initiative_FT_Diesel_Emissions_Presentation_Slide.jpg/800px-OSD_Clean_Fuel_Initiative_FT_Diesel_Emissions_Presentation_Slide.jpg 2x" width="400"/>
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="gallerytext">
         <p>
          Using Fischer Tropsch diesel results in dramatic across the board tailpipe emissions reductions relative to conventional fuels
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
      </li>
      <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 435px">
       <div style="width: 435px">

          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="gallerytext">
         <p>
          Using Fischer Tropsch jet fuels have been proven to dramatically reduce particulate and other aircraft emissions
         </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">
       Sustainability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sustainability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      One concern commonly raised about the development of synthetic fuels plants is sustainability. Fundamentally, transitioning from oil to coal or natural gas for transportation fuels production is a transition from one inherently depletable geologically limited resource to another.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the positive defining characteristics of synthetic fuels production is the ability to use multiple feedstocks (coal, gas, or biomass) to produce the same product from the same plant. In the case of hybrid BCTL plants, some facilities are already planning to use a significant biomass component alongside coal. Ultimately, given the right location with good biomass availability, and sufficiently high oil prices, synthetic fuels plants can be transitioned from coal or gas, over to a 100% biomass feedstock. This provides a path forward towards a renewable fuel source and possibly more sustainable, even if the plant originally produced fuels solely from coal, making the infrastructure forwards-compatible even if the original fossil feedstock runs out.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some synthetic fuels processes can be converted to sustainable production practices more easily than others, depending on the process equipment selected. This is an important design consideration as these facilities are planned and implemented, as additional room must be left in the plant layout to accommodate whatever future plant change requirements in terms of materials handling and gasification might be necessary to accommodate a future change in production profile.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
