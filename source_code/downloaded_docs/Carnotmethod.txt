   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Carnot method
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Carnot method
      </b>
      is an allocation procedure for dividing up fuel input (primary energy, end energy) in joint production processes that generate two or more energy products in one process (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       cogeneration
      </a>
      or trigeneration). It is also suited to allocate other streams such as CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      -emissions or variable costs. The potential to generate mechanical or electrical work (
      <a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">
       exergy
      </a>
      ) is used as the distribution key. For heat this potential can be assessed the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnot_efficiency" title="Carnot efficiency">
       Carnot efficiency
      </a>
      . Thus, the Carnot method is a form of an exergetic allocation method. It uses mean heat grid temperatures at the output of the process as a calculation basis. The main application area of this method is
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       cogeneration
      </a>
      , but it can also be applied to other processes generating a joint products, such as a chiller generating cold and producing waste heat which could be used for low temperature heat demand. The Carnot method's advantage is that no external reference values are required to allocate the input to the different output streams; only endogenous process parameters are needed. Thus, the allocation results remain unbiased of assumptions or external reference values that are open for discussion.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_allocation_factor">
       Fuel allocation factor
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_method&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fuel allocation factor">
        edit

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The fuel share a
      <sub>
       el

      which is needed to generate the combined product electrical energy W (work) and a
      <sub>
       th

      for the thermal energy H (useful heat) respectively, can be calculated accordingly to the first and second
      <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics" title="Laws of thermodynamics">
       laws of thermodynamics
      </a>
      as follows:
     </p>
     <p>
      a
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      = (1 x η
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      ) / (η
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      + η
      <sub>
       c
      </sub>
      × η
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      a
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      = (η
      <sub>
       c
      </sub>
      x η
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      ) / (η
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      + η
      <sub>
       c
      </sub>
      × η
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      Note: a
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      + a
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      = 1
      <br/>
      <br/>
      with
      <br/>
      a
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      : allocation factor for electrical energy, i.e. the share of the fuel input which is allocated to electricity production
      <br/>
      a
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      : allocation factor for thermal energy, i.e. the share of the fuel input which is allocated to heat production
      <br/>
     </p>
     <p>
      η
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      = W/Q
      <sub>
       F
      </sub>
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      = H/Q
      <sub>
       F
      </sub>
      <br/>
      W: electrical work
      <br/>
      H: useful heat
      <br/>
      Q
      <sub>
       F
      </sub>
      : Total heat, fuel or primary energy input
      <br/>
      <br/>
      and
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       c
      </sub>
      : Carnot factor 1-T
      <sub>
       i
      </sub>
      /T
      <sub>
       s
      </sub>
      (Carnot factor for electrical energy is 1)
      <br/>
      T
      <sub>
       i
      </sub>
      : lower temperature, inferior (ambient)
      <br/>
      T
      <sub>
       s
      </sub>
      : upper temperature, superior (useful heat)
      <br/>
     </p>
     <p>
      In heating systems, a good approximation for the upper temperature is the average between forward and return flow on the distribution side of the heat exchager.
      <br/>
      T
      <sub>
       s
      </sub>
      = (T
      <sub>
       FF
      </sub>
      +T
      <sub>
       RF
      </sub>
      ) / 2

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Effective_efficiency">
       Effective efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_method&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effective efficiency">
        edit

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The reciprocal value of the fuel factor (f-intensity) describes the effective efficiency of the assumed sub-process, which in case of CHP is only responsible for electrical or thermal energy generation. This equivalent efficiency corresponds to the effective efficiency of a "virtual boiler" or a "virtual generator" within the CHP plant.
     </p>
     <p>
      η
      <sub>
       el,eff
      </sub>
      = η
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      / a
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      = 1 / f
      <sub>
       F,el
      </sub>
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       th,eff
      </sub>
      = η
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      / a
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      = 1 / f
      <sub>
       F,th
      </sub>
      <br/>
      <br/>
      with
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       el,eff
      </sub>
      : effective efficiency of electricity generation within the CHP process
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       th,eff
      </sub>
      : effective efficiency of heat generation within the CHP process
      <br/>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_factor_of_energy_transformation">
       Performance factor of energy transformation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_method&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Performance factor of energy transformation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Next to the efficiency factor which describes the quantity of usable end energies, the quality of energy transformation according to the
      <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">
       entropy law
      </a>
      is also important. With rising
      <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">
       entropy
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">
       exergy
      </a>
      declines. Exergy does not only consider energy but also energy quality. It can be considered a product of both. Therefore any energy transformation should also be assessed according to its exergetic efficiciency or loss ratios. The quality of the product "thermal energy" is fundamentally determined by the mean temperature level at which this heat is delivered. Hence, the exergetic efficiency η
      <sub>
       x
      </sub>
      describes how much of the fuel's potential to generate physical work remains in the joint energy products. With cogeneration the result is the following relation:
     </p>
     <p>
      η
      <sub>
       x,total
      </sub>
      = η
      <sub>
       el
      </sub>
      + η
      <sub>
       c
      </sub>
      × η
      <sub>
       th
      </sub>
      <br/>
      <br/>
      The allocation with the Carnot method always results in:
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       x,total
      </sub>
      = η
      <sub>
       x,el
      </sub>
      = η
      <sub>
       x,th
      </sub>
      <br/>
      <br/>
      with
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       x,total
      </sub>
      = exergetic efficiency of the combined process
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       x,el
      </sub>
      = exergetic efficiency of the virtual electricity-only process
      <br/>
      η
      <sub>
       x,th
      </sub>
      = exergetic efficiency of the virtual heat-only process
      <br/>
     </p>

      <i>

      <i>
       I
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       O
       <sub>

       </sub>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       O
       <sub>

      </i>

      evaluated with
      <i>
       f
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </i>
      shall be the same as the amount of
      <i>
       O
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
      </i>
      generated from
      <i>
       O
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </i>
      and evaluated with
      <i>
       f
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      f
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      × (η
      <sub>
       21
      </sub>
      × O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) = f
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      × O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
     </p>
     <p>
      If we put this into the first equation, we see the following steps:
     </p>
     <p>
      f
      <sub>
       i
      </sub>
      × I = f
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      × O
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      + f
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      × (η
      <sub>
       21
      </sub>
      × O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      f
      <sub>
       i
      </sub>
      × I = f
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      × (O
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      + η
      <sub>
       21
      </sub>
      × O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      f
      <sub>
       i
      </sub>
      = f
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      × (O
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      /I + η
      <sub>
       21
      </sub>
      × O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      /I)
     </p>
     <p>
      f
      <sub>
       i
      </sub>
      = f
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      × (η
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      + η
      <sub>
       21
      </sub>
      × η
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      f
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      = f
      <sub>
       i
      </sub>
      / (η
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      + η
      <sub>
       21
      </sub>
      × η
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) or respectively f
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      = η
      <sub>
       21
      </sub>
      × f
      <sub>
       i
      </sub>
      / (η
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      + η
      <sub>
       21
      </sub>
      × η
      <sub>
       2

      with
      <i>
       η
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
      </i>
      =
      <i>
       O
       <sub>
        1
       </sub>
       /I

      <i>

      <i>
