   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Zero-energy building
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>

     <p>
      A
      <b>
       zero-energy building
      </b>
      , also known as a
      <b>
       zero net energy
      </b>
      (ZNE) building,
      <b>
       net-zero energy building
      </b>
      (NZEB), or
      <b>
       net zero building
      </b>
      , is a
      <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">
       building
      </a>
      with zero net energy consumption, meaning the total amount of energy used by the building on an annual basis is roughly equal to the amount of
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      created on the site,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      or in other definitions by renewable energy sources elsewhere.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      These buildings consequently contribute less overall greenhouse gas to the atmosphere than similar non-ZNE buildings. They do at times consume non-renewable energy and produce greenhouse gases, but at other times reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_consumption" title="Energy consumption">
       energy consumption
      </a>
      and greenhouse gas production elsewhere by the same amount.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most zero net energy buildings get half or more of their energy from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_grid" title="Power grid">
       grid
      </a>
      , and return the same amount at other times. Buildings that produce a surplus of energy over the year may be called "
      <a href="/wiki/Energy-plus-house" title="Energy-plus-house">
       energy-plus buildings
      </a>
      " and buildings that consume slightly more energy than they produce are called "near-zero energy buildings" or "ultra-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_energy_house" title="Low energy house">
       low energy houses
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      Traditional buildings consume 40% of the total fossil fuel energy in the US and European Union and are significant contributors of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">
       greenhouse gases
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The zero net energy consumption principle is viewed as a means to reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon emissions
      </a>
      and reduce dependence on
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuels
      </a>
      and although zero-energy buildings remain uncommon even in
      <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">
       developed countries
      </a>
      , they are gaining importance and popularity.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most zero-energy buildings use the electrical grid for energy storage but some are independent of the grid. Energy is usually harvested on-site through energy producing technologies like solar and wind, while reducing the overall use of energy with highly efficient
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       HVAC
      </a>
      and lighting technologies. The zero-energy goal is becoming more practical as the costs of alternative energy technologies decrease and the costs of traditional fossil fuels increase.
     </p>
     <p>
      The development of modern zero-energy buildings became possible not only through the progress made in new energy and construction technologies and techniques, but it has also been significantly improved by academic research, which collects precise energy performance data on traditional and experimental buildings and provides performance parameters for advanced computer models to predict the efficacy of engineering designs. Zero-energy buildings can be part of a
      <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">
       smart grid
      </a>
      . Some advantages of these buildings are as follows:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Integration of renewable energy resources
      </li>
      <li>
       Integration of plug-in
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">
        electric vehicles
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Implementation of zero-energy concepts
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The net zero concept is applicable to a wide range of resources due to the many options for producing and
      <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">
       conserving
      </a>
      resources in buildings (e.g. energy, water,
      <a href="/wiki/Zero_waste" title="Zero waste">
       waste
      </a>
      ). Energy is the first resource to be targeted because it is highly managed, expected to continually become more efficient, and the ability to distribute and allocate it will improve disaster resiliency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

      </ul>
     </div>
     <p>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">
       Definitions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-energy_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Despite sharing the name "zero net energy", there are several definitions of what the term means in practice, with a particular difference in usage between North America and Europe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zebdef_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-zebdef-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Zero net site energy use
      </dt>
      <dd>
       In this type of ZNE, the amount of energy provided by on-site
       <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
        renewable energy
       </a>
       sources is equal to the amount of energy used by the building. In the United States, “zero net energy building” generally refers to this type of building.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Zero net source energy use
      </dt>
      <dd>
       This ZNE generates the same amount of energy as is used, including the energy used to transport the energy to the building. This type accounts for losses during
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
        electricity transmission
       </a>
       . These ZNEs must generate more electricity than zero net site energy buildings.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Net zero energy emissions
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Outside the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       , a ZEB is generally defined as one with zero net energy emissions, also known as a
       <i>
        zero carbon building
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        zero emissions building
       </i>
       . Under this definition the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" title="Carbon emissions">
        carbon emissions
       </a>
       generated from on-site or off-site fossil fuel use are balanced by the amount of on-site
       <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
        renewable energy
       </a>
       production. Other definitions include not only the carbon emissions generated by the building in use, but also those generated in the construction of the building and the
       <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">
        embodied energy
       </a>
       of the structure. Others debate whether the carbon emissions of
       <a href="/wiki/Commuting" title="Commuting">
        commuting
       </a>
       to and from the building should also be included in the calculation.Recent work in New Zealand has initiated an approach to include building user transport energy within zero energy building frameworks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Net zero cost
      </dt>
      <dd>
       In this type of building, the cost of purchasing energy is balanced by income from sales of electricity to the grid of electricity generated on-site. Such a status depends on how a utility credits net electricity generation and the utility rate structure the building uses.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Net off-site zero energy use
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A building may be considered a ZEB if 100% of the energy it purchases comes from renewable energy sources, even if the energy is generated off the site.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Off-the-grid
      </dt>
      <dd>
       <a href="/wiki/Off-the-grid" title="Off-the-grid">
        Off-the-grid
       </a>
       buildings are stand-alone ZEBs that are not connected to an off-site energy utility facility. They require distributed renewable energy generation and energy storage capability (for when the sun is not shining, wind is not blowing, etc.). An energy
       <a href="/wiki/Autarky" title="Autarky">
        autarkic
       </a>
       house is a building concept where the balance of the own energy consumption and production can be made on an hourly or even smaller basis. Energy autarkic houses can be taken off-the-grid.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Net zero-energy building
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Based on scientific analysis within the joint research program “Towards Net Zero Energy Solar Buildings”
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Task40_Annex52_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Task40_Annex52-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a methodological framework was set up which allows different definitions, in accordance with country’s political targets, specific (climate) conditions and respectively formulated requirements for indoor conditions: The overall conceptual understanding of a Net ZEB is an energy efficient, grid connected building enabled to generate energy from renewable sources to compensate its own energy demand (see figure 1
       <div class="thumb tright">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Net_ZEB_balance_concept.png">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="736" data-file-width="1422" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Net_ZEB_balance_concept.png/220px-Net_ZEB_balance_concept.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Net_ZEB_balance_concept.png/330px-Net_ZEB_balance_concept.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Net_ZEB_balance_concept.png/440px-Net_ZEB_balance_concept.png 2x" width="220"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Net_ZEB_balance_concept.png" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          Figure 1: The Net ZEB balance concept: balance of weighted energy import respectively energy demand (x-axis) and energy export (feed-in credits) respectively (on-site) generation (y-axis)
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       ).
       <br/>
       The wording “Net” emphasizes the energy exchange between the building and the energy infrastructure. By the building-grid interaction, the Net ZEBs becomes an active part of the renewable energy infrastructure. This connection to energy grids prevents seasonal energy storage and oversized on-site systems for energy generation from renewable sources like in energy autonomous buildings. The similarity of both concepts is a pathway of two actions: 1) reduce energy demand by means of energy efficiency measures and passive energy use; 2) generate energy from renewable sources. However, the Net ZEBs grid interaction and plans to widely increase their numbers
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       evoke considerations on increased flexibility in the shift of energy loads and reduced peak demands.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Within this balance procedure several aspects and explicit choices have to be determined:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The building system boundary is split into a physical boundary which determines which renewable resources are considered (e.g. in buildings footprint, on-site or even off-site, see
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) respectively how many buildings are included in the balance (single building, cluster of buildings) and a balance boundary which determines the included energy uses (e.g. heating, cooling, ventilation, hot water, lighting, appliances, IT, central services, electric vehicles, and embodied energy, etc.). It should be noticed that renewable energy supply options can be prioritized (e.g. by transportation or conversion effort, availability over the lifetime of the building or replication potential for future, etc.) and therefore create a hierarchy. It may be argued that resources within the building footprint or on-site should be given priority over off-site supply options.
      </li>
      <li>
       The weighting system converts the physical units of different energy carriers into a uniform metric (site/final energy, source/primary energy renewable parts included or not, energy cost, equivalent carbon emissions and even energy or environmental credits) and allows their comparison and compensation among each other in one single balance (e.g. exported PV electricity can compensate imported biomass). Politically influenced and therefore possibly asymmetrically or time dependent conversion/weighting factors can affect the relative value of energy carriers and can influence the required energy generation capacity.
      </li>
      <li>
       The balancing period is often assumed to be one year (suitable to cover all operation energy uses). A shorter period (monthly or seasonal) could also be considered as well as a balance over the entire life cycle (including embodied energy, which could also be annualized and counted in addition to operational energy uses).
      </li>
      <li>
       The energy balance can be done in two balance types: 1) Balance of delivered/imported and exported energy (monitoring phase as self-consumption of energy generated on-site can be included); 2) Balance between (weighted) energy demand and (weighted) energy generation (for design phase as normally end users temporal consumption patterns -e.g. for lighting, appliances, etc.- are lacking). Alternatively a balance based on monthly net values in which only residuals per month are summed up to an annual balance is imaginable. This can be seen either as a load/generation balance or as a special case of import/export balance where a “virtual monthly self-consumption” is assumed (see figure 2
       <div class="thumb tright">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Different_types_of_balance.png">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="637" data-file-width="1020" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Different_types_of_balance.png/220px-Different_types_of_balance.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Different_types_of_balance.png/330px-Different_types_of_balance.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Different_types_of_balance.png/440px-Different_types_of_balance.png 2x" width="220"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Different_types_of_balance.png" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          Figure 2: The Net ZEB balance concept: Graphical representation of the different types of balance: import/export balance between weighted exported and delivered energy, load/generation balance between weighted generation and load, and monthly net balance between weighted monthly net values of generation and load
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       and compare
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Consistent_Definition_Framework_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Consistent_Definition_Framework-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ).
      </li>
      <li>
       Beside the energy balance, Net ZEBs can be characterized by their ability to match the building's load by its energy generation (load matching) or to work beneficially with respect to the needs of the local grid infrastructure (grind interaction). Both can be expressed by suitable indicators which are intended as assessment tools only.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The information is based on the publications,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Consistent_Definition_Framework_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Consistent_Definition_Framework-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      in which deeper information could be found.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">
       Design and construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-energy_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design and construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The most cost-effective steps toward a reduction in a building's energy consumption usually occur during the design process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energypyr_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-energypyr-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      To achieve efficient energy use, zero energy design departs significantly from conventional construction practice. Successful zero energy building designers typically combine time tested
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">
       passive solar
      </a>
      , or artificial conditioning, principles that work with the on-site assets. Sunlight and solar heat, prevailing breezes, and the cool of the earth below a building, can provide daylighting and stable indoor temperatures with minimum mechanical means. ZEBs are normally optimized to use
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">
       passive solar
      </a>
      heat gain and shading, combined with
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      to stabilize
      <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">
       diurnal temperature variations
      </a>
      throughout the day, and in most climates are
      <a href="/wiki/Superinsulation" title="Superinsulation">
       superinsulated
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      All the technologies needed to create zero energy buildings are available
      <a href="/wiki/Commercial_off-the-shelf" title="Commercial off-the-shelf">
       off-the-shelf
      </a>
      today.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sophisticated 3-D
      <a href="/wiki/Building_energy_simulation" title="Building energy simulation">
       building energy simulation
      </a>
      tools are available to model how a building will perform with a range of design variables such as building orientation (relative to the daily and seasonal position of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
       sun
      </a>
      ), window and door type and placement, overhang depth, insulation type and values of the building elements, air tightness (
      <a href="/wiki/Weatherization" title="Weatherization">
       weatherization
      </a>
      ), the efficiency of heating, cooling, lighting and other equipment, as well as local climate. These simulations help the designers predict how the building will perform before it is built, and enable them to model the economic and financial implications on building
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost_benefit_analysis" title="Cost benefit analysis">
       cost benefit analysis
      </a>
      , or even more appropriate – life cycle assessment.
     </p>
     <p>
      Zero-energy buildings are built with significant
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy-saving" title="Energy-saving">
       energy-saving
      </a>
      features. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating" title="Heating">
       heating
      </a>
      and cooling loads are lowered by using high-efficiency equipment, added
      <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">
       insulation
      </a>
      , high-efficiency windows, natural
      <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">
       ventilation
      </a>
      , and other techniques. These features vary depending on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_zone" title="Climate zone">
       climate zones
      </a>
      in which the construction occurs. Water heating loads can be lowered by using water conservation fixtures,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_recovery" title="Heat recovery">
       heat recovery
      </a>
      units on waste water, and by using solar water heating, and high-efficiency water heating equipment. In addition,
      <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">
       daylighting
      </a>
      with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skylight_(window)" title="Skylight (window)">
       skylights
      </a>
      or solartubes can provide 100% of daytime illumination within the home. Nighttime illumination is typically done with
      <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">
       fluorescent
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/LED_lamp" title="LED lamp">
       LED
      </a>
      lighting that use 1/3 or less power than incandescent lights, without adding unwanted heat. And
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscellaneous_electric_loads" title="Miscellaneous electric loads">
       miscellaneous electric loads
      </a>
      can be lessened by choosing efficient appliances and minimizing phantom loads or
      <a href="/wiki/Standby_power" title="Standby power">
       standby power
      </a>
      . Other techniques to reach net zero (dependent on climate) are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_sheltered_building" title="Earth sheltered building">
       Earth sheltered building
      </a>
      principles, superinsulation walls using
      <a href="/wiki/Straw-bale_construction" title="Straw-bale construction">
       straw-bale construction
      </a>
      , Vitruvianbuilt pre-fabricated building panels and roof elements plus exterior landscaping for seasonal shading.
     </p>
     <p>
      Zero-energy buildings are often designed to make dual use of energy including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_goods" title="White goods">
       white goods
      </a>
      ; for example, using
      <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">
       refrigerator
      </a>
      exhaust to heat domestic water, ventilation air and shower drain
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchangers
      </a>
      , office machines and computer servers, and body heat to heat the building. These buildings make use of heat energy that conventional buildings may exhaust outside. They may use
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_ventilation" title="Heat recovery ventilation">
       heat recovery ventilation
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_water_heat_recycling" title="Hot water heat recycling">
       hot water heat recycling
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_heat_and_power" title="Combined heat and power">
       combined heat and power
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_chiller" title="Absorption chiller">
       absorption chiller
      </a>
      units.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_harvest">
       Energy harvest
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-energy_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy harvest">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      ZEBs harvest available energy to meet their electricity and heating or cooling needs. In the case of individual houses, various
      <a href="/wiki/Microgeneration" title="Microgeneration">
       microgeneration
      </a>
      technologies may be used to provide heat and electricity to the building, using
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">
       solar cells
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">
       wind turbines
      </a>
      for electricity, and
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
       biofuels
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">
       solar thermal collectors
      </a>
      linked to a
      <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">
       seasonal thermal energy storage
      </a>
      (STES) for space heating. An STES can also be used for summer cooling by storing the cold of winter underground. To cope with fluctuations in demand, zero energy buildings are frequently connected to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electricity_grid" title="Electricity grid">
       electricity grid
      </a>
      , export electricity to the grid when there is a surplus, and drawing electricity when not enough electricity is being produced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zebdef_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-zebdef-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other buildings may be fully
      <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_building" title="Autonomous building">
       autonomous
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Energy harvesting is most often more effective (in cost and resource utilization) when done on a local but combined scale, for example, a group of houses,
      <a href="/wiki/Cohousing" title="Cohousing">
       cohousing
      </a>
      , local district, village, etc. rather than an individual basis. An energy benefit of such localized energy harvesting is the virtual elimination of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_transmission" title="Electrical transmission">
       electrical transmission
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electricity_distribution" title="Electricity distribution">
       electricity distribution
      </a>
      losses. These losses amount to about 7.2%–7.4% of the energy transferred.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Energy harvesting in
      <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">
       commercial
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">
       industrial
      </a>
      applications should benefit from the
      <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">
       topography
      </a>
      of each location. The production of goods under net zero fossil energy consumption requires locations of
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">
       geothermal
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microhydro" title="Microhydro">
       microhydro
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
       solar
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">
       wind
      </a>
      resources to sustain the concept.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Zero-energy neighborhoods, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/BedZED" title="BedZED">
       BedZED
      </a>
      development in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      , and those that are spreading rapidly in
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      , may use
      <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">
       distributed generation
      </a>
      schemes. This may in some cases include
      <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">
       district heating
      </a>
      , community chilled water, shared wind turbines, etc. There are current plans to use ZEB technologies to build entire off-the-grid or net zero energy use cities.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22energy_harvest.22_versus_.22energy_conservation.22_debate">
       The "energy harvest" versus "energy conservation" debate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-energy_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: The "energy harvest" versus "energy conservation" debate'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      One of the key areas of debate in zero energy building design is over the balance between
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">
       energy conservation
      </a>
      and the distributed point-of-use harvesting of
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
       solar energy
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_energy" title="Wind energy">
       wind energy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">
       thermal energy
      </a>
      ). Most zero energy homes use a combination of these strategies.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As a result of significant government subsidies for photovoltaic solar electric systems, wind turbines, etc., there are those who suggest that a ZEB is a conventional house with distributed renewable energy harvesting technologies. Entire additions of such homes have appeared in locations where photovoltaic (PV) subsidies are significant,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      but many so called "Zero Energy Homes" still have utility bills. This type of energy harvesting without added energy conservation may not be cost effective with the current price of electricity generated with photovoltaic equipment (depending on the local price of power company electricity),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zebeconomics_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-zebeconomics-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and may also requires greater embodied energy and greater resources so be thus the less ecological approach.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the 1980s,
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">
       passive solar building design
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_house" title="Passive house">
       passive house
      </a>
      have demonstrated heating energy consumption reductions of 70% to 90% in many locations, without active energy harvesting. For new builds, and with expert design, this can be accomplished with little additional construction cost for materials over a conventional building. Very few industry experts have the skills or experience to fully capture benefits of the passive design.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-spiegel-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Such passive solar designs are much more cost-effective than adding expensive photovoltaic panels on the roof of a conventional inefficient building.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zebeconomics_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-zebeconomics-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A few kilowatt-hours of photovoltaic panels (costing 2 to 3 dollars per annual kWh production, U.S. dollar equivalent) may only reduce external energy requirements by 15% to 30%. A 100,000 BTU (110 MJ) high
      <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_energy_efficiency_ratio" title="Seasonal energy efficiency ratio">
       seasonal energy efficiency ratio
      </a>
      14 conventional air conditioner requires over 7 kW of photovoltaic electricity while it is operating, and that does not include enough for
      <a href="/wiki/Off-the-grid" title="Off-the-grid">
       off-the-grid
      </a>
      night-time operation.
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_cooling" title="Passive cooling">
       Passive cooling
      </a>
      , and superior system engineering techniques, can reduce the air conditioning requirement by 70% to 90%. Photovoltaic-generated electricity becomes more cost-effective when the overall demand for electricity is lower.
     </p>

     <p>

Studies of identical homes in the United States have shown dramatic differences in energy use, with some homes using more than twice the energy of others.

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Over-illumination" title="Over-illumination">
       illumination
      </a>
      and hot water, and the amount of
      <a href="/wiki/Miscellaneous_electric_load" title="Miscellaneous electric load">
       miscellaneous electric devices
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Plug_load" title="Plug load">
       plug loads
      </a>
      used.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_24-0">

     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Utility_Concerns">
       Utility Concerns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-energy_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Utility Concerns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Utility companies are typically legally responsible for maintaining the electrical infrastructure that brings power to our cities, neighborhoods, and individual buildings. Utility companies typically own this infrastructure up to the property line of an individual parcel, and in some cases own electrical infrastructure on private land as well. Utilities have expressed concern that the use of Net Metering for ZNE projects threatens the Utilities base revenue, which in turn impacts their ability to maintain and service the portion of the electrical grid that they are responsible for. Utilities have expressed concern that states that maintain Net Metering laws may saddle non-ZNE homes with higher utility costs, as those homeowners would be responsible for paying for grid maintenance while ZNE home owners would theoretically pay nothing if they do achieve ZNE status. This creates potential equity issues, as currently, the burden would appear to fall on lower-income households. A possible solution to this issue is to create a minimum base charge for all homes connected to the utility grid, which would force ZNE home owners to pay for grid services independently of their electrical use.
     </p>
     <p>
      Additional concerns exist that local distribution as well as larger transmission grids have not been designed to convey electricity in two directions, which may be necessary as higher levels of distributed energy generation come on line. Overcoming this barrier could require extensive upgrades to the electrical grid, however this is not believed to be a major problem until renewable generation reaches much higher levels of penetration than currently realized.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Net_Zero_Energy_Building_Definition">
       Net Zero Energy Building Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-energy_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Net Zero Energy Building Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The US National Renewable Energy Lab (NREL) published a groundbreaking report titled Net-Zero Energy Buildings: A Classification System Based on Renewable Energy Supply Options.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is the first report to lay out a full spectrum classification system for Net Zero/Renewable Energy buildings that includes the full spectrum of Clean Energy sources, both on site and off site. This classification system identifies the following 4 main categories of Net Zero Energy Buildings/Sites/Campuses:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       NZEB:A -- A footprint renewables Net Zero Energy Building
      </li>
      <li>
       NZEB:B -- A site renewables Net Zero Energy Building
      </li>
      <li>
       NZEB:C -- An imported renewables Net Zero Energy Building
      </li>
      <li>
       NZEB:D -- An off-site purchased renewables Net Zero Energy Building
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Applying this U.S. Government Net Zero classification system means that every building "can" become Net Zero with the right combination of the key Net Zero Technologies - PV (solar), GHP (geothermal heating and cooling, thermal batteries), EE (energy efficiency), sometimes Wind, and Electric Batteries. A graphical exposé of the scale of impact of applying these NREL guidelines for Net Zero can be seen in the graphic at Net Zero Foundation titled "Net Zero Effect on U.S. Total Energy Use"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      showing a possible 39% U.S. total fossil fuel use reduction by changing U.S. Residential and Commercial buildings to Net Zero, 37% savings if we still use Nat. Gas for cooking at the same level.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">

      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">

        [33]

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Zero_energy_building_versus_green_building">
       Zero energy building versus green building
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-energy_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Zero energy building versus green building">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The goal of
      <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">
       green building
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">
       sustainable architecture
      </a>
      is to use resources more efficiently and reduce a building's negative impact on the environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Zero energy buildings achieve one key green-building goal of completely or very significantly reducing energy use and
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      emissions for the life of the building. Zero energy buildings may or may not be considered "green" in all areas, such as reducing waste, using
      <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">
       recycled
      </a>
      building materials, etc. However, zero energy, or net-zero buildings do tend to have a much lower ecological impact over the life of the building compared with other "green" buildings that require imported energy and/or fossil fuel to be habitable and meet the needs of occupants.
     </p>
     <p>
      Because of the design challenges and sensitivity to a site that are required to efficiently meet the energy needs of a building and occupants with renewable energy (solar, wind, geothermal, etc.), designers must apply holistic design principles, and take advantage of the free naturally occurring assets available, such as passive solar orientation, natural ventilation, daylighting, thermal mass, and night time cooling.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Certification">
       Certification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

Green Globes involves check lists that are measurement tools, not design tools. Inexperienced designers or architects may cherry-pick points to meet a target certification level, even though those points may not be the best design choices for a specific building or climate.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

      <span class="mw-editsection">

     <p>
      Between 2008 and 2013, researchers from Australia, Austria, Belgium, Canada, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, Republic of Korea, New Zealand, Norway, Portugal, Singapore, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, United Kingdom and USA were working together in the joint research program “Towards Net Zero Energy Solar Buildings” under the umbrella of International Energy Agency (IEA) Solar Heating and Cooling Program (SHC) Task 40 / Energy in Buildings and Communities (EBC, formerly ECBCS) Annex 52

The objective is to develop a common understanding, a harmonized international applicable definition framework (Subtask A, see definitions methodology “Net Zero Energy Building” above), design process tools (Subtask B), advanced building design and technology solutions and industry guidelines for Net ZEBs (Subtask C).      <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_Free_Home_Challenge">
       Energy Free Home Challenge
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-energy_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Energy Free Home Challenge">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In 2007, the philanthropic
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Siebel_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Siebel Foundation (page does not exist)">
       Siebel Foundation
      </a>
      created the Energy Free Home Foundation. The goal was to offer $20 million in global incentive prizes to design and build a 2,000 square foot (186 square meter) three-bedroom, two bathroom home with (1) net-zero annual utility bills that also has (2) high market appeal, and (3) costs no more than a conventional home to construct.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        [71]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The plan included funding to build the top ten entries at $250,000 each, a $10 million first prize, and then a total of 100 such homes to be built and sold to the public.
     </p>
     <p>
      Beginning in 2009,
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Siebel" title="Thomas Siebel">
       Thomas Siebel
      </a>
      made many presentations about his Energy Free Home Challenge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        [72]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Siebel Foundation Report stated that the Energy Free Home Challenge was "Launching in late 2009".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        [73]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">
       Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">
       University of California, Berkeley
      </a>
      participated in writing the "Feasibility of Achieving Zero-Net-Energy, Zero-Net-Cost Homes"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        [74]
       </a>
      </sup>
      for the $20-million Energy Free Home Challenge.
     </p>
     <p>
      If implemented, the Energy Free Home Challenge would have provided increased incentives for improved technology and consumer education about
      <b>
       zero energy buildings
      </b>
      coming in at the same cost as conventional housing.

      <li>
       The State of California has proposed that all new low- and mid-rise residential buildings, and all new commercial buildings, be designed and constructed to ZNE standards beginning in 2020 and 2030, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
        <a href="#cite_note-75">
         [75]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
        <a href="#cite_note-76">
         [76]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The requirements, if implemented, will be promulgated via the California Building Code, which is updated on a three-year cycle and which currently mandates some of the highest energy efficiency standards in the United States. California is anticipated to further increase efficiency requirements by 2020, thus avoiding the trends discussed above of building standard housing and achieving ZNE by adding large amounts of renewables. The California Energy Commission is required to perform a cost-benefit analysis to prove that new regulations create a net benefit for residents of the state. There has yet to be publicly released analysis of the impact that ZNE standards may have to construction, real estate, and energy prices in the state. Debate exists as to if the state's stringent efficiency requirements are directly responsible for the apparent "flatlining" of residential electricity use in the state since 1975.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
        <a href="#cite_note-77">
         [77]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This debate is relevant to ZNE codes, as it remains to be seen via models or in practice what overall effect the proliferation of ZNE buildings will have on overall electricity use in the state, and at what cost.
      </li>
      <li>
       West Village, located on the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Davis" title="University of California, Davis">
        University of California campus in Davis, California
       </a>
       , was the largest ZNE-planned community in North America at the time of its opening in 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Home_.7B.7B.21.7D.7D_UC_Davis_West_Village_78-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Home_.7B.7B.21.7D.7D_UC_Davis_West_Village-78">
         [78]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The development contains student housing for approximately 1,980 UC Davis students as well as leasable office space and community amenities including a community center, pool, gym, restaurant and convenience store. Office spaces in the development are currently leased by energy and transportation-related University programs. The project was a public-private partnership between the university and West Village Community Partnership LLC, led by Carmel Partners of San Francisco, a private developer, who entered into a 60-year ground lease with the university and was responsible for the design, construction, and implementation of the $300 million project, which is intended to be market-rate housing for Davis. This is unique as the developer designed the project to achieve ZNE at no added cost to themselves or to the residents. Designed and modeled to achieve ZNE, the project uses a mixture of passive elements (roof overhangs, well-insulated walls, radiant heat barriers, ducts in insulated spaces, etc.) as well as active approaches (occupancy sensors on lights, high-efficiency appliances and lighting, etc.). Designed to out-perform California's 2008 Title 24 energy codes by 50%, the project produced 87% of the energy it consumed during its first year in operation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Home_.7B.7B.21.7D.7D_UC_Davis_West_Village_78-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Home_.7B.7B.21.7D.7D_UC_Davis_West_Village-78">
         [78]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The shortcoming in ZNE status is attributed to several factors, including improperly functioning heat pump water heaters, which have since been fixed. Occupant behavior is significantly different than anticipated, with the all-student population using more energy on a per-capita basis than typical inhabitants of single-family homes in the area. One of the primary factors driving increased energy use appears to be the increased miscellaneous electrical loads (MEL, or plug loads) in the form of mini-refrigerators, lights, computers, gaming consoles, televisions, and other electronic equipment. The university continues to work with the developer to identify strategies for achieving ZNE status. These approaches include incentivizing occupant behavior and increasing the site's renewable energy capacity, which is a 4 MW photovoltaic array per the original design. The West Village site is also home to the Honda Smart Home US,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
        <a href="#cite_note-79">
         [79]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a beyond-ZNE single-family home that incorporates cutting-edge technologies in energy management, lighting, construction, and water efficiency.
      </li>
      <li>
       The IDeAs Z2 Design Facility
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
        <a href="#cite_note-80">
         [80]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a net zero energy, zero carbon retrofit project occupied since 2007. It uses less than one fourth the energy of a typical U.S. office
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81">
        <a href="#cite_note-81">
         [81]
        </a>

The remaining energy demand is met with renewable energy from its building-integrated photovoltaic array. In 2009, building owner and occupant Integrated Design Associates (IDeAs) recorded actual measured energy use intensity of 21.17 kbtu/sf-year, with 21.72 kbtu/sf-year produced, for a net of −0.55 kbtu/sf-yr. The building is also carbon neutral, with no gas connection, and with

       Richardsville Elementary School, part of the Warren County Public School District in south central Kentucky, is the first Net Zero energy school in the United States. To reach Net Zero, innovative energy reduction strategies were used by CMTA Consulting Engineers and Sherman Carter Barnhart Architects including dedicated outdoor air systems (DOAS) with dynamic reset, new IT systems, alternative methods to prepare lunches, and the use of solar photovoltaics. The project has an efficient thermal envelope constructed with insulated concrete form (ICF) walls, geothermal water source heat pumps, low-flow fixtures, and features daylighting extensively throughout. It is also the first truly wireless school in Kentucky.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91">
        <a href="#cite_note-91">
         [91]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Locust Trace AgriScience Center, an agricultural-based vocational school serving Fayette County Public Schools and surrounding districts, features a Net Zero Academic Building engineered by CMTA Consulting Engineers and designed by Tate Hill Jacobs Architects. The facility, located in Lexington, Kentucky, also has a greenhouse, riding arena with stalls, and a barn. To reach Net Zero in the Academic Building the project utilizes an air-tight envelope, expanded indoor temperature setpoints in specified areas to more closely model real-world conditions, a solar thermal system, and geothermal water source heat pumps. The school has further reduced its site impact by minimizing municipal water use through the use of a dual system consisting of a standard leach field system and a constructed wetlands system and using pervious surfaces to collect, drain, and use rainwater for crop irrigation and animal watering.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92">
        <a href="#cite_note-92">
         [92]
        </a>
       </sup>

     <ul>
      <li>
       Net Zero Energy Building Certification launched in 2011, with an international following. The first project, Painters Hall,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110">
        <a href="#cite_note-110">
         [110]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is Pringle Creek’s Community Center, café, office, art gallery, and event venue. Originally built in the 1930s, Painters Hall was renovated to LEED Platinum Net Zero energy building standards in 2010, demonstrating the potential of converting existing building stock into high‐performance, sustainable building sites. Painters Hall features simple low-‐cost solutions for energy reduction, such as natural daylighting and passive cooling lighting, that save money and increase comfort. A district ground-source geothermal loop serves the building’s GSHP for highly efficient heating and air conditioning. Excess generation from the 20.2 kW rooftop solar array offsets pumping for the neighborhoods geo loop system. Open to the public, Painters Hall is a hub for gatherings of friends, neighbors, and visitors at the heart of a neighborhood designed around nature and community.
