     <p>
      A
      <b>
       carbon diet
      </b>
      refers to reducing the impact on
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
       climate change
      </a>
      by reducing
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      (principally
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      ) production.
     </p>
     <p>
      Individuals and businesses produce
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      from daily activities such as driving, heating, and the consumption of products and services. To reduce the effects of climate change, we could reduce our carbon output by going on a carbon diet.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are references to the use of the term carbon diet in several publications.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term "carbon diet" is used in the book
      <i>
       Gore: A Political Life
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-one-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "And many scientists and economists maintain that the costs of adapting to any change are both easier to achieve politically and more efficacious scientifically than trying to avoid the problem through a crash carbon diet."
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Deborah Jones from
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.theglobeandmail.com" rel="nofollow">
       The Globe &amp; Mail
      </a>
      newspaper writes "It's week two of my family's "carbon diet" -- a Globe and Mail assignment to see how my family of four adults can cut its greenhouse-gas emissions to match the provincial goal of a 33-per-cent reduction..."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-two-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This differs from a
      <a href="/wiki/Low_carbon_diet" title="Low carbon diet">
       low carbon diet
      </a>
      which refers to making choices about eating that reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      emissions.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Key_components_of_a_carbon_diet">
       Key components of a carbon diet
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_diet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Key components of a carbon diet">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A carbon diet is similar to a food diet. It starts with assessing weight (measured in tonnes of carbon dioxide) and then determining where the ideal weight should be. The following outlines the steps of a carbon diet:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Calculate a
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">
        carbon footprint
       </a>
       to understand the amount of carbon dioxide emissions
      </li>
      <li>
       Measure the carbon footprint against peers (e.g., similar company size or for individuals, a national average)
      </li>
      <li>
       Determine the ideal carbon footprint
      </li>
      <li>
       Identify the source of the most significant carbon dioxide emissions
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Reduce carbon dioxide emissions by starting with the most significant sources first
      </li>
     </ul>
