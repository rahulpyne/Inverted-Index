     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Royal Society of Chemistry
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       RSC
      </b>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Learned_society" title="Learned society">
       learned society
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Professional_association" title="Professional association">
       professional association
      </a>
      ) in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      with the goal of "advancing the
      <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
       chemical
      </a>
      sciences". It was formed in 1980 from the merger of the
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Society" title="Chemical Society">
       Chemical Society
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Institute of Chemistry">
       Royal Institute of Chemistry
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Faraday_Society" title="Faraday Society">
       Faraday Society
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Analytical_Chemistry" title="Society for Analytical Chemistry">
       Society for Analytical Chemistry
      </a>
      with a new Royal Charter and the dual role of learned society and professional body. At its inception, the Society had a combined membership of 34,000 in the UK and a further 8,000 abroad.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The headquarters of the Society are at
      <a href="/wiki/Burlington_House" title="Burlington House">
       Burlington House
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Piccadilly" title="Piccadilly">
       Piccadilly
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      . It also has offices in Thomas Graham House in
      <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">
       Cambridge
      </a>
      (named for
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Graham_(chemist)" title="Thomas Graham (chemist)">
       Thomas Graham
      </a>
      , the first president of the
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Society" title="Chemical Society">
       Chemical Society
      </a>
      ) where
      <i>
       RSC Publishing
      </i>
      is based. The Society has offices in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_City_Science_Center" title="University City Science Center">
       University City Science Center
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">
       Philadelphia
      </a>
      , in both
      <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">
       Beijing
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">
       Shanghai
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">
       China
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">
       Bangalore
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The organisation carries out research, publishes
      <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">
       journals
      </a>
      , books and databases, as well as hosting conferences, seminars and workshops. It is the professional body for chemistry in the UK, with the ability to award the status of
      <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Chemist" title="Chartered Chemist">
       Chartered Chemist
      </a>
      (CChem) and, through the Science Council the awards of Chartered Scientist (CSci), Registered Scientist (RSci) and Registered Science Technician (RScTech) to suitably qualified candidates. The designation FRSC is given to a group of elected
      <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Chemistry">
       Fellows of the society
      </a>
      who have made major contributions to chemistry and other interface disciplines such as biological chemistry. The names of Fellows are published each year in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
        The Times
       </a>
      </i>
      (London).
      <a href="/wiki/Honorary_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Honorary Fellows of the Royal Society of Chemistry">
       Honorary Fellowship of the Society
      </a>
      ("HonFRSC") is awarded for distinguished service in the field of chemistry.
     </p>

     <p>
      The president is elected biennially and wears a badge in the form of a spoked wheel, with the standing figure of Joseph Priestley depicted in enamel, mainly in red and blue, on a hexagonal medallion in the centre. The rim of the wheel is gold, and the twelve spokes are of non-tarnishable metals.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Membership_grades_and_post-nominals">
       Membership grades and post-nominals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Society_of_Chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Membership grades and post-nominals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The following are membership grades with
      <a href="/wiki/Post-nominal_letters" title="Post-nominal letters">
       post-nominals
      </a>
      (designatory letters):
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Affiliate
       </i>
       : (no post-nominal) The grade for students and those involved in chemistry who do not meet the requirements for the following grades.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        AMRSC
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        Associate Member, Royal Society of Chemistry
       </i>
       The entry level for RSC membership, AMRSC is awarded to graduates (or equivalent) in the chemical sciences.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        MRSC
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        Member, Royal Society of Chemistry
       </i>
       Awarded to graduates (or equivalent) with at least 3 years' experience, who have acquired key skills through professional activity
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        FRSC
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Chemistry">
         Fellow of the Royal Society of Chemistry
        </a>
       </i>
       Fellowship may be awarded to nominees who have made an outstanding contribution to chemistry (see
       <a href="/wiki/Category:Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Category:Fellows of the Royal Society of Chemistry">
        Category:Fellows of the Royal Society of Chemistry
       </a>
       ).
       <ul>
        <li>
         <i>
          HonFRSC
         </i>
         :
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Honorary_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Honorary Fellows of the Royal Society of Chemistry">
           Honorary Fellow of the Society
          </a>
         </i>
         Honorary Fellowship is awarded for distinguished service in the field of chemistry.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haughey_16-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Haughey-16">
           [16]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        CChem
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Chemist" title="Chartered Chemist">
         Chartered Chemist
        </a>
       </i>
       The award of CChem is considered separately from admission to a category of RSC membership. Candidates need to be MRSC or FRSC and demonstrate development of specific professional attributes and be in a job which requires their chemical knowledge and skills.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        CSci
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Scientist" title="Chartered Scientist">
         Chartered Scientist
        </a>
       </i>
       The RSC is a licensed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Science_Council" title="Science Council">
        Science Council
       </a>
       for the registration of
       <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Scientist" title="Chartered Scientist">
        Chartered Scientists
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        EurChem
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/European_Chemist" title="European Chemist">
         European Chemist
        </a>
       </i>
       The RSC is a member of the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=European_Communities_Chemistry_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="European Communities Chemistry Council (page does not exist)">
        European Communities Chemistry Council
       </a>
       (ECCC), and can award this designation to Chartered Chemists.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        MChemA
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        Mastership in Chemical Analysis
       </i>
       The RSC awards this postgraduate qualification which is the UK statutory qualification for practice as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Analyst" title="Public Analyst">
        Public Analyst
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It requires candidates to submit a portfolio of suitable experience and to take theory papers and a one-day laboratory practical examination.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="GRSC">
       GRSC
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Society_of_Chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: GRSC">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The qualification
      <i>
       GRSC
      </i>
      (Graduate of the Royal Society of Chemistry) was awarded from 1981 to 1995 for completion of college courses equivalent to a chemistry degree and overseen by the RSC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRSC_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GRSC-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It replaced the GRIC offered by the Royal Institute of Chemistry.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">
       Divisions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Society_of_Chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Divisions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The society is organised around 9 divisions, based on subject areas, and local sections, both in the United Kingdom and overseas. Divisions cover broad areas of chemistry but also contain many special interest groups for more specific areas.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Analytical Division
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">
        analytical chemistry
       </a>
       and promoting the original aims of the
       <i>
        Society for Analytical Chemistry
       </i>
       . 12 Subject Groups.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Dalton Division
       </i>
       , named after
       <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">
        John Dalton
       </a>
       , for
       <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">
        inorganic chemistry
       </a>
       . 6 Subject Groups.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Education Division
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Chemistry_education" title="Chemistry education">
        chemical education
       </a>
       . 4 Subject Groups.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Faraday Division
       </i>
       , named after
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">
        Michael Faraday
       </a>
       , for
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">
        physical chemistry
       </a>
       and promoting the original aims of the
       <i>
        Faraday Society
       </i>
       . 14 Subject Groups.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Organic Division
       </i>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">
        organic chemistry
       </a>
       . 6 Subject Groups.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Chemical Biology Interface Division
       </i>
       . 2 Subject Groups.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Environment, Sustainability and Energy Division
       </i>
       . 3 Subject Groups.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Materials Chemistry Division
       </i>
       . 4 Subject Groups.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Industry and Technology Division
       </i>
       . 13 Subject Groups.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      There are 12 subjects groups not attached to a division.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Local_sections">
       Local sections
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

      </a>

     </p>

      publisher: surplus made by its publishing business is invested to support its aim of advancing the chemical sciences.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_journals" title="Scientific journals">
       scientific journals
      </a>
      , including its flagship journals
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Communications" title="Chemical Communications">
        Chemical Communications
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Science_(journal)" title="Chemical Science (journal)">
       <i>
        Chemical Science
       </i>

      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Society_Reviews" title="Chemical Society Reviews">

       </a>
      </i>
      , the society publishes:

      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Chemistry" title="Education in Chemistry">
         Education in Chemistry
        </a>
       </i>
       for teachers.
      </li>
      <li>
       A free online journal for chemistry educators,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chemistry_Education_Research_and_Practice" title="Chemistry Education Research and Practice">
         Chemistry Education Research and Practice
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       A general chemistry magazine
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chemistry_World" title="Chemistry World">
         Chemistry World
        </a>
       </i>
       , sent monthly to all members of the Society throughout the world. The editorial board consists of 10 academic and industrial chemists. It was first published in January 2004. It replaced
       <i>
        Chemistry in Britain
       </i>
       , first published in 1965. Its contents include news, articles of a general chemical nature, such as the history of chemistry and technological developments, book reviews and letters from readers. Its ISSN is 1473-7604.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chemistry_World" title="Chemistry World">
         Chemistry World
        </a>
       </i>
       is supported by the
       <i>
        Chemistry World Podcast
       </i>
       , which is presented by Cambridge University scientist
       <a href="/wiki/Chris_Smith_(doctor)" title="Chris Smith (doctor)">
        Dr Chris Smith
       </a>
       , who also edits
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Naked_Scientists" title="The Naked Scientists">
         The Naked Scientists
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Professional reference books across the chemical sciences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Books for students, including the
       <i>
        Tutorial Chemistry Texts
       </i>
       series of 23 books, edited by E. W. Abel, and the 8 books in the
       <i>
        Molecular World
       </i>
       series, whose coordinating editor is L. E. Smart.
      </li>
      <li>
       Books on the history of chemistry, such as a history of the
       <a href="/wiki/Faraday_Society" title="Faraday Society">
        Faraday Society
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Subscriptions to the journals are available individually, or "all-in", under a provision called "RSC Gold".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gold_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gold-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Society_of_Chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although opened as the Chemistry Centre in 2010, the building has been occupied by the Royal Society of Chemistry since 1857 (at which time it was known as the Chemical Society) – the heart of the Chemistry Centre is the RSC's Library and Information Centre which itself dates back to 1842. Over the years, the library for the RSC has received many gifts from notable fellows including
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">
       Michael Faraday
      </a>
      . The library became a centre for information on the chemical sciences during the 1st and 2nd world wars when extensive use was made on the chemical reference material available. In November 2014, the society announced that the centre would be rebranded as simply "Royal Society of Chemistry at Burlington House" and the name "Chemistry Centre" dropped.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Library_and_information_centre">
       Library and information centre
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Society_of_Chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Library and information centre">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The society has a large library covering mainly chemistry-based subjects, including online access for members, housed at the Chemistry Centre at Burlington House. It is part of the Chemistry Centre and is a resource for RSC members, although the public are also welcome.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Benevolent_fund">
       Benevolent fund
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Society_of_Chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Benevolent fund">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RSC_Staircase.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1836" data-file-width="3264" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/RSC_Staircase.jpg/300px-RSC_Staircase.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/RSC_Staircase.jpg/450px-RSC_Staircase.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/RSC_Staircase.jpg/600px-RSC_Staircase.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RSC_Staircase.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The staircase at the Royal Society of Chemistry, Burlington House, designed by the partnership of Robert Richardson Banks and Charles Barry, Jr.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Royal Society of Chemistry's benevolent fund supports the members and their families during difficult times, through advice and guidance, financial support and volunteer support.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It dates back to 1920 when the Institute of Chemistry (later the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Institute of Chemistry">
       Royal Institute of Chemistry
      </a>
      ) established it as a memorial to its members who died in the first world war. It had an aim "to help necessitous persons who are, or have been, Fellows or Associates of the Institute, their wives or children, and the widows and dependent relatives of deceased Fellows and Associates" through voluntary contributions from members. The benevolent fund's ethos of "members helping members" stems from the Institute of Chemistry's Council raising money for members in need from other members prior to the fund being formed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1960, the benevolent fund introduced a volunteer visitor system, where members were asked to visit applicants, beneficiaries, elderly and housebound members in their area. Each local section was also appointed a fund representative.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A major review of the fund to modernize it took place in 2003-2004, forming a new strategy document that is updated every three years. It underwent another review in 2014 with the aim to widen its reach and provide a more holistic support wider than financial assistance to Royal Society of Chemistry members and their families.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Benevolent Fund Committee, formed of volunteer members of the Royal Society of Chemistry, oversees the benevolent fund and meet every three months.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prizes_and_awards">
       Prizes and awards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Society_of_Chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Prizes and awards">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Royal_Society_of_Chemistry_medals_and_awards" title="List of Royal Society of Chemistry medals and awards">
       List of Royal Society of Chemistry medals and awards
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The RSC awards a variety of prizes and awards each year that include awards for excellence in any area of chemistry, in specialist areas or for achievement at particular stages of a chemist's career.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Medals are awarded centrally by the RSC and by the divisions of the organisation. There are also awards that are administered by RSC interest groups.
     </p>
     <p>
      The centrally awarded medals include the
      <a href="/wiki/Harrison-Meldola_Memorial_Prizes" title="Harrison-Meldola Memorial Prizes">
       Harrison-Meldola Memorial Prizes
      </a>
      which are awarded to a British chemist who is under 32 years of age for promising original investigations in chemistry
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corday-Morgan_medal" title="Corday-Morgan medal">
       Corday-Morgan medals
      </a>
      which consist of three separate awards made for the most meritorious contributions to experimental chemistry (including computer simulation).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Tilden Prize, previously known as the Tilden Lecture, consists of three awards annually to scientists in mid-career for advances in chemistry.
     </p>
     <p>
      Previous winners of the Harrison-Meldola Prize (known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Meldola_Medal_and_Prize" title="Meldola Medal and Prize">
       Meldola Medal and Prize
      </a>
      prior to its merger in 2008 with the Edward Harrison prize) include
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Kelk_Ingold" title="Christopher Kelk Ingold">
       Christopher Kelk Ingold
      </a>
      (1921, 1922),
      <a href="/wiki/Cyril_Norman_Hinshelwood" title="Cyril Norman Hinshelwood">
       Cyril Norman Hinshelwood
      </a>
      (1923), R.H. Stokes (1946), D.H. Williams (1966), and J. Evans (1978).
     </p>
     <p>
      Corday-Morgan medal recipients include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derek_Harold_Richard_Barton" title="Derek Harold Richard Barton">
       Derek Barton
      </a>
      (1949),
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Sydney_Nyholm" title="Ronald Sydney Nyholm">
       Ronald Sydney Nyholm
      </a>
      (1950),
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Sanger" title="Frederick Sanger">
       Frederick Sanger
      </a>
      (1951),
      <a href="/wiki/John_Cornforth" title="John Cornforth">
       John Cornforth
      </a>
      (1953),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rex_Richards_(scientist)" title="Rex Richards (scientist)">
       Rex Richards
      </a>
      (1954), and
      <a href="/wiki/George_Porter" title="George Porter">
       George Porter
      </a>
      (1955).
     </p>
     <p>
      The Faraday Division annually awards the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marlow_Award&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marlow Award (page does not exist)">
       Marlow Award
      </a>
      for contributions to
      <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">
       physical chemistry
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_physics" title="Chemical physics">
       chemical physics
      </a>
      by members of the Faraday Division under the age of 32.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Recent recipients include
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Orr-Ewing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andrew Orr-Ewing (page does not exist)">
       Andrew Orr-Ewing
      </a>
      , (1999),
      <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_A._Jones" title="Jonathan A. Jones">
       Jonathan A. Jones
      </a>
      , (2000),
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Helen_Fielding_(Chemist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Helen Fielding (Chemist) (page does not exist)">
       Helen Fielding
      </a>
      (2001),
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Essex&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jonathan Essex (page does not exist)">
       Jonathan Essex
      </a>
      (2002),
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daren_Caruana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daren Caruana (page does not exist)">

      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Julie_Macpherson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Julie Macpherson (page does not exist)">
       Julie Macpherson
      </a>

      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_Manby&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fred Manby (page does not exist)">

     <p>
      The Interactive Lab Primer is a site developed to provide tips to a variety of chemical experimentation skills.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The site covers basic lab safety tips, demonstrates primary lab techniques, introduces a couple common lab apparatus in lab, and provides other references.

      <a href="/wiki/ChemSpider" title="ChemSpider">
       ChemSpider
      </a>
      is a database of chemicals and chemical properties.

      <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">

      scheme, "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landmarks_of_Chemistry" title="Landmarks of Chemistry">
       Landmarks of Chemistry
      </a>
