    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Sustainable advertising
      </b>
      addresses the
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">
       carbon footprint
      </a>
      and other negative environmental and social impacts associated with the production and distribution of advertising materials. A growing number of companies are making a commitment to the reduction of their environmental impact associated with advertising production and distribution.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising.27s_environmental_impact">
       Advertising's environmental impact
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_advertising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Advertising's environmental impact">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Print advertising impacts the environment due to the carbon dioxide emitted into the atmosphere as a result of the production and distribution of print media. Factors include the sourcing and production of paper, petroleum-based ink, solvents, plastics and adhesives used and the fossil fuels burned in the manufacturing and distribution of newspapers and magazines. Digital media has impacts due to the greenhouse gas emissions associated with the manufacturing and operation of servers and datacenter devices, networking devices and client computers as well as the e-waste impacts of these devices at the end of their useful lives.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004, over 7 billion metrics tons of
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      equivalent greenhouse gases associated with print media advertising were emitted into the atmosphere by the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2005 U.S. advertisers spent over $65 billion on print media advertising and created over 250,000 ad pages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A single ad page run in a popular consumer magazine can represent as much as seven tons of carbon dioxide emissions when supply chain factors associated with papermaking, printing, logistics and landfill disposal or incineration of post-consumer and unsold media are taken into consideration. According to a recent
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">
        New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      article quoting David J. Refkin, director of sustainable development for Time Inc., a single copy of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
        Time
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine results in the emission of .29 pounds of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      equivalent greenhouse gasses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the USA, about 25 million sq.meters or about 10,000 tons of non-biodegradeable PVC is directly attributed to outdoor billboards every single year. To put this into context this would cover the equivalent of 16 square miles or the centre of most major urban cities.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_involvement">
       Corporate involvement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_advertising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Corporate involvement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Corporations such as
      <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">
       General Electric
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Timberland_Company" title="The Timberland Company">
       Timberland
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">
       Wal-Mart
      </a>
      are making substantial commitments to developing and marketing sustainable products and business practices.
      <a href="/wiki/Victoria%27s_Secret" title="Victoria's Secret">
       Victoria's Secret
      </a>
      has recently agreed to reduce the impact of its catalogues by using recycled papers and stopping using paper from endangered forests.
      <a href="/wiki/Moxie_Sozo" title="Moxie Sozo">
       Moxie Sozo
      </a>
      is the first
      <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">
       graphic design
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Advertising_agency" title="Advertising agency">
       advertising agency
      </a>
      to be carbon-neutral,
      <a href="/wiki/Zero_waste" title="Zero waste">
       zero waste
      </a>
      and powered by 100 percent renewable energy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, many corporations take advantage of the sustainable issue by "greenwashing" their products to build a facade that the products are indeed sustainable, when in reality it is only a marketing strategy to gain better public relations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2006, jewelry company
      <a href="/wiki/John_Hardy_(jewelry)" title="John Hardy (jewelry)">
       John Hardy
      </a>
      began a pilot bamboo reforestation project on
      <a href="/wiki/Nusa_Penida" title="Nusa Penida">
       Nusa Penida
      </a>
      , a small island off the coast of
      <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">
       Bali
      </a>
      where the company's workshops are located. The primary object is to sequester
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      by planting
      <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">
       bamboo
      </a>
      , a long-lived, rapidly growing woody perennial grass. In order to offset the company's advertising footprint of 451 metric tons of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      , an area equivalent to four football fields will be planted.
     </p>
