     <p>
      By year end 2015
      <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      was the world's fifth biggest producer of
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      with 23,031 MW installed capacity (including 11 MW of wind-hydro capacity), providing 48,118 GWh of power and 19% of the country's total electricity production in that year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In earlier periods wind energy covered 16% of the demand in 2010, 13.8% in 2009 and 11.5% in 2008.
     </p>
     <p>
      Following the fallout from the financial crisis in 2008 and the dire straits of the Spanish economy in the subsequent period new installations of new wind turbines all but stagnated between 2012 and 2015 remaining at close to 23,000 MW installed capacity for the entire period. Whilst Spain put expansion on ice the other world leading countries continued to expand or accelerated new wind turbine installations and by 2015 India had moved ahead of Spain in total installations. As of end 2015 the countries when ranked by total installations were: 1.
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_China" title="Wind power in China">
       China
      </a>
      , 2.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_USA" title="Wind power in USA">
       Usa
      </a>
      . 3.
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Germany" title="Wind power in Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      4.
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_India" title="Wind power in India">
       India
      </a>
      . 5. Spain and 6.
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Wind power in the United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2009, for the first time in the annual calculation, wind overtook coal that produced 33,844 GWh. In 2010, 43,692 GWh of wind electricity was produced in Spain, with an end of year installed capacity of over 20 GW, representing 20% of capacity, and 16% of production.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Wind was the third biggest energy source in the country, exceeded by thermal gas combined cycle at 68,828 GWh and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_Power" title="Nuclear Power">
       nuclear
      </a>
      , that reached 61,944 GWh.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2009, the largest producer of wind power in Spain was
      <a href="/wiki/Iberdrola" title="Iberdrola">
       Iberdrola
      </a>
      , with 25.5% of capacity, followed by
      <a href="/wiki/Acciona" title="Acciona">
       Acciona
      </a>
      with 20.9% and NEO Energia (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDP_Renewables" title="EDP Renewables">
       EDP Renewables
      </a>
      ) with 8.3%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On windy days, wind power generation has surpassed all other electricity sources in Spain; On 21 November 2015 at 4:50 am, 70.4% of the electricity consumed on the Spanish Peninsula was covered with wind power energy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 6th February 2013, wind power achieved an earlier record in electricity production, reaching an instantaneous peak of 17,056 MW and an hourly production of 16,918 MWh.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In November 2011 a capacity peak of 59% of power demand being generated by wind power was reached.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Power peaks of 14.960 GW were reached in November 2010,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and in November 2009, a wind storm caused wind farms to produce a peak of 53% of total electricity demand (11.546 GW).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ie_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ie-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Navarra">
       Navarra
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Navarra">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The US rating agency
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poors" title="Standard &amp; Poors">
       Standard &amp; Poors
      </a>
      , in a current investigation of standard of living in Europe, ranked Navarre, whose primary source of renewable energy is wind power, uppermost among the 17 autonomous regions of Spain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stewart_2006_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stewart_2006-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Navarre, Europe’s sixth largest producer of wind power, currently sustains approximately 70 percent of its electricity needs from renewable energy sources, wind farms being used most extensively, and has a 900-megawatt capacity of installed wind power.
     </p>
     <p>
      Navarre lacks thermal, nuclear, coal, oil, gas fields, or hefty hydro-electric power stations, but does possess considerable wind renewable resources, which the Government of Navarre pursued to drop its external energy dependence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberto2000_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Alberto2000-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Navarre was entirely reliant on imported energy until wind-power development and utilization began progress in 1996.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stewart_2006_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stewart_2006-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Galicia">
       Galicia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Galicia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">
       Galicia
      </a>
      currently leads in wind power development in the autonomous regions for the third consecutive year with an increase in wind power of 264 MW, succeeding Castilla La Mancha, which exceeded the development goal of 1000 MW, and followed by Aragon, Navarre, and Castile-León, and the remaining autonomous regions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montes2007_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Montes2007-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Castilla Leon and La Rioja have initiated wind energy production, and the north-eastern area of Soria also holds the capacity to be an efficient producer; the possession of workable resources for wind power development is also represented in the Cantabrian, eastern and south-eastern coasts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montes2007_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Montes2007-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Research">
       Research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Largely concerned with advancing energy efficiency use in Spain, the Institute for Energy Saving and Diversification (IDAE) also seeks to expand renewable energy sources and energies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montes2007_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Montes2007-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Research concerning the production of
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      from electrolysis of water by a wind farm began in 2004 at a newly installed laboratory in the Universidad Pública de Navarra under an agreement between Energía Hidroeléctrica de Navarra, Stuart Energy Systems of Canada, and Statkraft of Norway. The lab replicated the power generation environment of a wind farm and examined the effects of an electrolyzer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FCB2004_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FCB2004-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Concentrated research is occurring concerning wind measurement in the
      <a href="/wiki/Albacete" title="Albacete">
       Albacete
      </a>
      region at
      <a href="/wiki/Higueruela" title="Higueruela">
       Higueruela
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montes2007_14-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Montes2007-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power_industry">
       Wind power industry
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wind power industry">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Spanish wind energy sector now hosts the involvement of over 500 companies, with approximately 150 wind turbine production plants and their machinery across the Spanish regions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-REA2005_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-REA2005-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The assets of the Spanish industry are being noticed and acted upon by financial analysts, as United States Ernst and Young in 2005 ranked the wind market in Spain among the uppermost in its index of “long-term country attractiveness”.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graber2005_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Graber2005-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Including those indirectly employed in supplying components and services, the total number of jobs supported by Spain’s wind industry has reached more than 30 000, and is estimated to double to 60 000 by 2010” (2005).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-REA2005_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-REA2005-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gamesa_E.C3.B3lica">
       Gamesa Eólica
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gamesa Eólica">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Gamesa_Corporaci%C3%B3n_Tecnol%C3%B3gica" title="Gamesa Corporación Tecnológica">
       Gamesa Corporación Tecnológica
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Gamesa, based in the Basque Country is a large global wind turbine manufacturer with 13% share by installed power in 2005.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s1a_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-s1a-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The company values the distinctive geographical setting of Spain as a benefit to Spanish companies competing in the global arena.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graber2005_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Graber2005-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gamesa Eólica currently operates plants in Spain, The US and China. It has projects in many other parts of the world including Egypt, Germany, Ireland, Italy, Japan, Korea, and Portugal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graber2005_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Graber2005-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gamesa opened a manufacturing plant for wind turbine generator blades in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania in 2005, creating 500 part-time building and operations jobs and 236 permanent manufacturing jobs; the building, operation, and upkeep of Gamesa’s wind farms, in conjunction with its two Philadelphia offices and production plant, formed about 1,000 jobs in the state over a five-year period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curry2005_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Curry2005-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The company seeks expansion into Greece, Taiwan, and the United Kingdom.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graber2005_19-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Graber2005-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
       Portugal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
       Turkey
      </a>
      ,

      </a>

      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montes2007_14-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Montes2007-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The wind energy capacity for major companies in Spain was the following as of 2007: Gamesa Eólica, 3281 MW; Made, 803 MW; Neg Micon, 715 MW; Ecotècnia, 446 MW; G. Electric, 343 MW; Izar-Bonus, 317 MW; Desa &amp; AWP, 121 MW; Enercon, 58 MW; Lagerwey, 38 MW; and Others, 113 MW (2007).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montes2007_14-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Montes2007-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Future_development">
       Future development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Future development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Three factors will control the further progress of wind power development in Spain: the capability of the wind farms network to hold all the electricity harnessed by wind power, predominantly in off-peak times, the cost of energy, and the environmental effect that the abundance of wind farm development in Spain could turn out.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberto2000_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Alberto2000-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Spanish wind power industry will be confronted with the following issues in the immediate future:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberto2000_16-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Alberto2000-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       formulating its development to be congruent with required supply agreements by the national electricity supply operator
      </li>
      <li>
       guaranteeing that the installation of wind farms occurs with recognition of the environment
      </li>
      <li>
       synchronizing wind power development of the 17 autonomous regions
      </li>
      <li>
       trimming down the investment costs to acquire sufficient returns with declining energy prices in the upcoming years.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      It is also noteworthy that the supportive Spanish policies for wind power development have resulted in severe competition for construction sites among major companies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberto2000_16-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Alberto2000-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Political leaders in the autonomous communities have been frazzled by the numerous applications for wind farm construction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberto2000_16-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Alberto2000-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Local possession of wind power is not present in Spain, but does not appear to take away from further development of wind power in Spain since a much smaller and weaker quantity of local anti-wind farm grid population inhabits the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toke2007_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Toke2007-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, a further obstacle concerning wind power development needs to be tackled before Spain can achieve these ambitious objectives: construction of a central control center for all the Spanish wind farms, analogous to the control center used for traditional power plants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graber2005_19-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Graber2005-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">
       Opposition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Opposition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      With some exceptions, there has been little opposition to the installation of inland wind parks. However, the projects to build offshore parks have been more controversial. According to Cohn et al.,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The proposal of building the biggest offshore wind power production facility in the world in southwestern Spain on the spot of the 1805
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">
       Battle of Trafalgar
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engineer2003_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Engineer2003-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      has been met with strong oppostition from the towns in the coast of Cádiz, who fear for tourism and fisheries in the area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      There have also been complaints by the British, who claim that the area is a war grave and that any development of the area could destroy archaeological evidence of the historic battle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engineer2003_31-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Engineer2003-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
