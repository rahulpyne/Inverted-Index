     <p>
      A
      <b>
       boiler
      </b>
      is a closed
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">
       vessel
      </a>
      in which
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      or other
      <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">
       fluid
      </a>
      is heated. The fluid does not necessarily
      <a href="/wiki/Boiling" title="Boiling">
       boil
      </a>
      . (In North America, the term "
      <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">
       furnace
      </a>
      " is normally used if the purpose is not to actually boil the fluid.) The heated or vaporized fluid exits the boiler for use in various processes or heating applications,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      including
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler_(water_heating)" title="Boiler (water heating)">
       water heating
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">
       central heating
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler_(power_generation)" title="Boiler (power generation)">
       boiler-based power generation
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">
       cooking
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">
       sanitation
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">
       Materials
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Materials">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">
       pressure vessel
      </a>
      of a boiler is usually made of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mild_steel" title="Mild steel">
       steel
      </a>
      (or
      <a href="/wiki/Alloy_steel" title="Alloy steel">
       alloy steel
      </a>
      ), or historically of
      <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">
       wrought iron
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">
       Stainless steel
      </a>
      , especially of the
      <a href="/wiki/Austenitic_stainless_steel" title="Austenitic stainless steel">
       austenitic
      </a>
      types, is not used in wetted parts of boilers due to corrosion and stress corrosion cracking.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, ferritic stainless steel is often used in superheater sections that will not be exposed to
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler_water" title="Boiler water">
       boiling water
      </a>
      , and electrically-heated stainless steel shell boilers are allowed under the European "Pressure Equipment Directive" for production of steam for sterilizers and disinfectors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Live_steam" title="Live steam">
       live steam
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Scale_model" title="Scale model">
       models
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
       copper
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">
       brass
      </a>
      is often used because it is more easily fabricated in smaller size boilers. Historically, copper was often used for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firebox_(locomotive)" title="Firebox (locomotive)">
       fireboxes
      </a>
      (particularly for
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">
       steam locomotives
      </a>
      ), because of its better formability and higher thermal conductivity; however, in more recent times, the high price of copper often makes this an uneconomic choice and cheaper substitutes (such as steel) are used instead.
     </p>
     <p>
      For much of the Victorian "age of steam", the only material used for boilermaking was the highest grade of wrought iron, with assembly by
      <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">
       rivetting
      </a>
      . This iron was often obtained from specialist
      <a href="/wiki/Ironworks" title="Ironworks">
       ironworks
      </a>
      , such as at
      <a href="/wiki/Cleator_Moor" title="Cleator Moor">
       Cleator Moor
      </a>
      (UK), noted for the high quality of their
      <a href="/wiki/Rolling_(metalworking)" title="Rolling (metalworking)">
       rolled plate
      </a>
      and its suitability for high-reliability use in critical applications, such as high-pressure boilers. In the 20th century, design practice instead moved towards the use of steel, which is stronger and cheaper, with
      <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">
       welded
      </a>
      construction, which is quicker and requires less labour. It should be noted, however, that wrought iron boilers corrode far slower than their modern-day steel counterparts, and are less susceptible to localized pitting and stress-corrosion. This makes the longevity of older wrought-iron boilers far superior to those of welded steel boilers.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">
       Cast iron
      </a>
      may be used for the heating vessel of domestic water heaters. Although such heaters are usually termed "boilers" in some countries, their purpose is usually to produce hot water, not steam, and so they run at low pressure and try to avoid actual boiling. The brittleness of cast iron makes it impractical for high-pressure steam boilers.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">
       Energy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Energy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The source of heat for a boiler is
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      of any of several
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">
       fuels
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">
       wood
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">
       oil
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_steam_boiler" title="Electric steam boiler">
       Electric steam boilers
      </a>
      use
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" title="Electrical resistance">
       resistance-
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immersion_heater" title="Immersion heater">
       immersion-type
      </a>
      heating elements.
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">
       Nuclear fission
      </a>
      is also used as a heat source for generating
      <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">
       steam
      </a>
      , either directly (BWR) or, in most cases, in specialised
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchangers
      </a>
      called "steam generators" (PWR).
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_steam_generator" title="Heat recovery steam generator">
       Heat recovery steam generators
      </a>
      (HRSGs) use the heat rejected from other processes such as
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">
       gas turbine
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Boiler_efficiency">
       Boiler efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Boiler efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      there are two methods to measure the boiler efficiency 1) direct method 2) indirect method
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Direct method
      </b>
      -direct method of boiler efficiency test is more usable or more common
     </p>
     <p>
      boiler efficiency =Q*(Hg-Hf)/q*GCV *100 Q =Total steam flow Hg= Enthalpy of saturated steam in k cal/kg Hf =Enthalpy of feed water in kcal/kg q= quantity of fuel use in kg/hr GCV =gross calorific value in Kcal/kg like pet coke (8200 KCAL/KG)
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       indirect method
      </b>
      -to measure the boiler efficiency in indirect method we need a following parameter like 1)Ultimate analysis of fuel (H2,S2,S,C moisture constraint,ash constraint) 2)percentage of O2 or CO2 at flue gas 3)flue gas temperature at outlet 4)ambient temperature in deg c and humidity of air in kg/kg 5)GCV of fuel in Kcal/kg 6)ash percentage in combustible fuel 7)GCV of ash in Kcal/kg
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Configurations">
       Configurations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Configurations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Boilers can be classified into the following configurations:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Pot boiler
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Haycock boiler
       </i>
       /
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haystack_boiler" title="Haystack boiler">
        Haystack boiler
       </a>
       : a primitive "kettle" where a fire heats a partially filled water container from below. 18th century Haycock boilers generally produced and stored large volumes of very low-pressure steam, often hardly above that of the atmosphere. These could burn wood or most often, coal. Efficiency was very low.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Flued_boiler" title="Flued boiler">
        Flued boiler
       </a>
       with one or two large flues—an early type or forerunner of
       <a href="/wiki/Fire-tube_boiler" title="Fire-tube boiler">
        fire-tube boiler
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <div class="thumb tright">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steam_Boiler_2_English_version.png">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="532" data-file-width="450" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Steam_Boiler_2_English_version.png/225px-Steam_Boiler_2_English_version.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Steam_Boiler_2_English_version.png/338px-Steam_Boiler_2_English_version.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/18/Steam_Boiler_2_English_version.png 2x" width="225"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Steam_Boiler_2_English_version.png" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          Diagram of a fire-tube boiler
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <a href="/wiki/Fire-tube_boiler" title="Fire-tube boiler">
        Fire-tube boiler
       </a>
       : Here, water partially fills a boiler barrel with a small volume left above to accommodate the steam (
       <i>
        steam space
       </i>
       ). This is the type of boiler used in nearly all steam locomotives. The heat source is inside a furnace or
       <i>
        firebox
       </i>
       that has to be kept permanently surrounded by the water in order to maintain the temperature of the
       <i>
        heating surface
       </i>
       below the
       <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">
        boiling point
       </a>
       . The furnace can be situated at one end of a fire-tube which lengthens the path of the hot gases, thus augmenting the heating surface which can be further increased by making the gases reverse direction through a second parallel tube or a bundle of multiple tubes (two-pass or return flue boiler); alternatively the gases may be taken along the sides and then beneath the boiler through flues (3-pass boiler). In case of a locomotive-type boiler, a boiler barrel extends from the firebox and the hot gases pass through a bundle of fire tubes inside the barrel which greatly increases the heating surface compared to a single tube and further improves heat transfer. Fire-tube boilers usually have a comparatively low rate of steam production, but high steam storage capacity. Fire-tube boilers mostly burn solid fuels, but are readily adaptable to those of the liquid or gas variety.
      </li>
      <li>
       <div class="thumb tright">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steam_Boiler_3_english.png">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="450" height="338" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Steam_Boiler_3_english.png/225px-Steam_Boiler_3_english.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Steam_Boiler_3_english.png/338px-Steam_Boiler_3_english.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/Steam_Boiler_3_english.png 2x" width="225"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Steam_Boiler_3_english.png" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          Diagram of a water-tube boiler.
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <a href="/wiki/Water-tube_boiler" title="Water-tube boiler">
        Water-tube boiler
       </a>
       : In this type, tubes filled with water are arranged inside a furnace in a number of possible configurations. Often the water tubes connect large drums, the lower ones containing water and the upper ones steam and water; in other cases, such as a mono-tube boiler, water is circulated by a pump through a succession of coils. This type generally gives high steam production rates, but less storage capacity than the above. Water tube boilers can be designed to exploit any heat source and are generally preferred in high-pressure applications since the high-pressure water/steam is contained within small diameter pipes which can withstand the pressure with a thinner wall.
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="/wiki/Flash_boiler" title="Flash boiler">
          Flash boiler
         </a>
         : A flash boiler is a specialized type of water-tube boiler in which tubes are close together and water is pumped through them. A flash boiler differs from the type of mono-tube steam generator in which the tube is permanently filled with water. In a flash boiler, the tube is kept so hot that the water feed is quickly flashed into steam and superheated. Flash boilers had some use in automobiles in the 19th century and this use continued into the early 20th century. .
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

     <ul>
      <li>
       Fire-tube boiler with Water-tube firebox. Sometimes the two above types have been combined in the following manner: the firebox contains an assembly of water tubes, called
       <a href="/wiki/Thermic_siphon" title="Thermic siphon">
        thermic siphons

       . The gases then pass through a conventional firetube boiler. Water-tube fireboxes were installed in many
       <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">
       Safety
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Safety">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler_explosion" title="Boiler explosion">
       Boiler explosion
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      To define and secure boilers safely, some professional specialized organizations such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Mechanical_Engineers" title="American Society of Mechanical Engineers">
       American Society of Mechanical Engineers
      </a>
      (ASME) develop standards and regulation codes. For instance, the ASME
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiler_and_Pressure_Vessel_Code" title="Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code">
       Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code
      </a>
      is a standard providing a wide range of rules and directives to ensure compliance of the boilers and other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_vessels" title="Pressure vessels">
       pressure vessels
      </a>
      with safety, security and design standards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Historically, boilers were a source of many serious injuries and property destruction due to poorly understood engineering principles. Thin and brittle metal shells can rupture, while poorly welded or riveted seams could open up, leading to a violent eruption of the pressurized steam. When water is converted to steam it expands to over 1,000 times its original volume and travels down steam pipes at over 100 kilometres per hour. Because of this, steam is a great way of moving energy and heat around a site from a central boiler house to where it is needed, but without the right boiler feed water treatment, a steam-raising plant will suffer from scale formation and corrosion. At best, this increases energy costs and can lead to poor quality steam, reduced efficiency, shorter plant life and unreliable operation. At worst, it can lead to catastrophic failure and loss of life. Collapsed or dislodged boiler tubes can also spray scalding-hot steam and smoke out of the air intake and firing chute, injuring the firemen who load the coal into the fire chamber. Extremely large boilers providing hundreds of horsepower to operate factories can potentially demolish entire buildings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A boiler that has a loss of feed water and is permitted to boil dry can be extremely dangerous. If feed water is then sent into the empty boiler, the small cascade of incoming water instantly boils on contact with the superheated metal shell and leads to a violent explosion that cannot be controlled even by safety steam valves. Draining of the boiler can also happen if a leak occurs in the steam supply lines that is larger than the make-up water supply could replace. The
      <i>
       Hartford Loop
      </i>
      was invented in 1919 by the Hartford Steam Boiler and Insurance Company as a method to help prevent this condition from occurring, and thereby reduce their insurance claims.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Superheated_steam_boiler">
       Superheated steam boiler
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Superheated steam boiler">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Superheater.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1531" data-file-width="2300" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Superheater.jpg/300px-Superheater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Superheater.jpg/450px-Superheater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Superheater.jpg/600px-Superheater.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Superheater.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A superheated boiler on a steam locomotive.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Superheater" title="Superheater">
       Superheater
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Most boilers produce steam to be used at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturation_temperature" title="Saturation temperature">
       saturation temperature
      </a>
      ; that is, saturated steam. Superheated steam boilers vaporize the water and then further heat the steam in a
      <i>
       superheater
      </i>
      . This provides steam at much higher temperature, but can decrease the overall thermal efficiency of the steam generating plant because the higher steam temperature requires a higher flue gas exhaust temperature.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      There are several ways to circumvent this problem, typically by providing an
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Economizer" title="Economizer">
        economizer
       </a>
      </i>
      that heats the feed water, a combustion air heater in the hot flue gas exhaust path, or both. There are advantages to superheated steam that may, and often will, increase overall efficiency of both steam generation and its utilization: gains in input temperature to a turbine should outweigh any cost in additional boiler complication and expense. There may also be practical limitations in using
      <i>
       wet
      </i>
      steam, as entrained condensation droplets will damage turbine blades.
     </p>
     <p>
      Superheated steam presents unique safety concerns because, if any system component fails and allows steam to escape, the high pressure and temperature can cause serious, instantaneous harm to anyone in its path. Since the escaping steam will initially be completely superheated vapor, detection can be difficult, although the intense heat and sound from such a leak clearly indicates its presence.
     </p>
     <p>
      Superheater operation is similar to that of the coils on an air conditioning unit, although for a different purpose. The steam piping is directed through the flue gas path in the boiler furnace. The temperature in this area is typically between 1,300 and 1,600
      <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">
       °C
      </a>
      (2,372 and 2,912
      <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">
       °F
      </a>
      ). Some superheaters are radiant type; that is, they absorb heat by radiation. Others are convection type, absorbing heat from a fluid. Some are a combination of the two types. Through either method, the extreme heat in the flue gas path will also heat the superheater steam piping and the steam within. While the temperature of the steam in the superheater rises, the pressure of the steam does not and the pressure remains the same as that of the boiler.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Almost all steam superheater system designs remove droplets entrained in the steam to prevent damage to the turbine blading and associated piping.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_steam_generator">
       Supercritical steam generator
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Supercritical steam generator">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turmkessel02.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="465" data-file-width="484" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Turmkessel02.png/250px-Turmkessel02.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Turmkessel02.png/375px-Turmkessel02.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/Turmkessel02.png 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turmkessel02.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Boiler for a power plant.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_steam_generator" title="Supercritical steam generator">
       Supercritical steam generator
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Supercritical steam generators are frequently used for the production of
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">
       electric power
      </a>
      . They operate
      <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">
       at supercritical
      </a>
      pressure. In contrast to a "subcritical boiler", a supercritical steam generator operates at such a high pressure (over 3,200 psi or 22 MPa) that the physical turbulence that characterizes boiling ceases to occur; the fluid is neither liquid nor gas but a super-critical fluid. There is no generation of steam bubbles within the water, because the pressure is above the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_temperature_and_pressure" title="Critical temperature and pressure">
       critical pressure
      </a>
      point at which steam bubbles can form. As the fluid expands through the turbine stages, its thermodynamic state drops below the critical point as it does work turning the turbine which turns the electrical generator from which power is ultimately extracted. The fluid at that point may be a mix of steam and liquid droplets as it passes into the
      <a href="/wiki/Condenser_(heat_transfer)" title="Condenser (heat transfer)">
       condenser
      </a>
      . This results in slightly less fuel use and therefore less
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      production. The term "boiler" should not be used for a supercritical pressure steam generator, as no "boiling" actually occurs in this device.
     </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Boiler_fittings_and_accessories">
       Boiler fittings and accessories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Boiler fittings and accessories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Pressuretrol" title="Pressuretrol">
         Pressuretrols
        </a>
       </b>
       to control the steam pressure in the boiler. Boilers generally have 2 or 3 pressuretrols: a manual-reset pressuretrol, which functions as a safety by setting the upper limit of steam pressure, the operating pressuretrol, which controls when the boiler fires to maintain pressure, and for boilers equipped with a modulating burner, a modulating pressuretrol which controls the amount of fire.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Relief_valve" title="Relief valve">
         Safety valve
        </a>
        :
       </b>
       It is used to relieve pressure and prevent possible
       <a href="/wiki/Boiler_explosion" title="Boiler explosion">
        explosion of a boiler
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Water level indicators:
       </b>
       They show the operator the level of fluid in the boiler, also known as a
       <a href="/wiki/Sight_glass" title="Sight glass">
        sight glass
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gauge" title="Water gauge">
        water gauge
       </a>
       or water column.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Bottom
        <a href="/wiki/Boiler_blowdown" title="Boiler blowdown">
         blowdown
        </a>
        valves:
       </b>
       They provide a means for removing solid particulates that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condense" title="Condense">
        condense
       </a>
       and lie on the bottom of a boiler. As the name implies, this valve is usually located directly on the bottom of the boiler, and is occasionally opened to use the pressure in the boiler to push these particulates out.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Continuous blowdown valve:
       </b>
       This allows a small quantity of water to escape continuously. Its purpose is to prevent the water in the boiler becoming saturated with dissolved salts. Saturation would lead to foaming and cause water droplets to be carried over with the steam – a condition known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priming_(steam_engine)" title="Priming (steam engine)">
        priming
       </a>
       . Blowdown is also often used to monitor the chemistry of the boiler water.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Trycock:
       </b>
       a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Valve" title="Valve">
        valve
       </a>
       that is often use to manually check a liquid level in a tank. Most commonly found on a water boiler.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Flash tank:
       </b>
       High-pressure blowdown enters this vessel where the steam can 'flash' safely and be used in a low-pressure system or be vented to atmosphere while the ambient pressure blowdown flows to drain.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Automatic blowdown/continuous heat recovery system:
       </b>
       This system allows the boiler to blowdown only when makeup water is flowing to the boiler, thereby transferring the maximum amount of heat possible from the blowdown to the makeup water. No flash tank is generally needed as the blowdown discharged is close to the temperature of the makeup water.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Hand holes:
       </b>
       They are steel plates installed in openings in "header" to allow for inspections &amp; installation of tubes and inspection of internal surfaces.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Steam drum internals
       </b>
       , a series of screen, scrubber &amp; cans (cyclone separators).
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Low-water cutoff:
       </b>
       It is a mechanical means (usually a float switch) that is used to turn off the burner or shut off fuel to the boiler to prevent it from running once the water goes below a certain point. If a boiler is "dry-fired" (burned without water in it) it can cause rupture or catastrophic failure.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Surface blowdown line:
       </b>
       It provides a means for removing foam or other lightweight non-condensible substances that tend to float on top of the water inside the boiler.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Circulating
        <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">
         pump
        </a>
        :
       </b>
       It is designed to circulate water back to the boiler after it has expelled some of its heat.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Feedwater
        <a href="/wiki/Check_valve" title="Check valve">
         check valve
        </a>
        or clack valve
       </b>
       : A non-return stop valve in the
       <a href="/wiki/Boiler_feedwater" title="Boiler feedwater">
        feedwater
       </a>
       line. This may be fitted to the side of the boiler, just below the water level, or to the top of the boiler.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Draught">
       Draught
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Draught">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">
       fuel
      </a>
      -heated boiler must provide air to oxidize its fuel. Early boilers provided this stream of air, or
      <i>
       draught
      </i>
      , through the natural action of
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      in a
      <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_stack" title="Flue-gas stack">
       chimney
      </a>
      connected to the exhaust of the combustion chamber. Since the heated flue gas is less dense than the ambient air surrounding the boiler, the flue gas rises in the chimney, pulling denser, fresh air into the combustion chamber.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most modern boilers depend on mechanical draught rather than natural draught. This is because natural draught is subject to outside air conditions and temperature of flue gases leaving the furnace, as well as the chimney height. All these factors make proper draught hard to attain and therefore make mechanical draught equipment much more reliable and economical.
     </p>
     <p>
      Types of draught can also be divided into
      <i>
       induced draught
      </i>
      , where exhaust gases are pulled out of the boiler;
      <i>
       forced draught
      </i>
      , where fresh air is pushed into the boiler; and
      <i>
       balanced draught
      </i>
      , where both effects are employed. Natural draught through the use of a chimney is a type of induced draught; mechanical draught can be induced, forced or balanced.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are two types of mechanical induced draught. The first is through use of a steam jet. The steam jet oriented in the direction of flue gas flow induces flue gases into the stack and allows for a greater flue gas velocity increasing the overall draught in the furnace. This method was common on steam driven locomotives which could not have tall chimneys. The second method is by simply using an induced draught fan (ID fan) which removes flue gases from the furnace and forces the exhaust gas up the stack. Almost all induced draught furnaces operate with a slightly negative pressure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mechanical forced draught is provided by means of a fan forcing air into the combustion chamber. Air is often passed through an air heater; which, as the name suggests, heats the air going into the furnace in order to increase the overall efficiency of the boiler. Dampers are used to control the quantity of air admitted to the furnace. Forced draught furnaces usually have a positive pressure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Balanced draught is obtained through use of both induced and forced draught. This is more common with larger boilers where the flue gases have to travel a long distance through many boiler passes. The induced draught fan works in conjunction with the forced draught fan allowing the furnace pressure to be maintained slightly below atmospheric.
     </p>
