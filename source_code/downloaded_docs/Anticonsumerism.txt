     <p>
      <b>
       Anti-consumerism
      </b>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociopolitical" title="Sociopolitical">
       sociopolitical
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">
       ideology
      </a>
      that is opposed to
      <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">
       consumerism
      </a>
      , the continual buying and consuming of material possessions. Anti-consumerism is concerned with the private actions of business corporations in pursuit of financial and economic goals at the expense of the public welfare, especially in matters of environmental protection,
      <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">
       social stratification
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">
       ethics
      </a>
      in the governing of a society. In politics, anti-consumerism overlaps with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_activism" title="Environmental activism">
       environmental activism
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-globalization" title="Anti-globalization">
       anti-globalization
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal-rights" title="Animal-rights">
       animal-rights
      </a>
      activism; moreover, a conceptual variation of anti-consumerism is
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Post-consumerism" title="Post-consumerism">
        post-consumerism
       </a>
      </i>
      , living in a material way that transcends consumerism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Anti-consumerism arose in response to the problems caused by the long-term mistreatment of human consumers and of the animals consumed, and from the incorporation of
      <a href="/wiki/Consumer_education" title="Consumer education">
       consumer education
      </a>
      to school
      <a href="/wiki/Curriculum" title="Curriculum">
       curricula
      </a>
      ; examples of anti-consumerism are the book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/No_Logo" title="No Logo">
        No Logo
       </a>
      </i>
      (2000) by
      <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Klein" title="Naomi Klein">
       Naomi Klein
      </a>
      , and documentary films such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Corporation_(film)" title="The Corporation (film)">
        The Corporation
       </a>
      </i>
      (2003), by Mark Achbar and Jennifer Abbott, and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surplus_(film)" title="Surplus (film)">
        Surplus: Terrorized into Being Consumers
       </a>
      </i>
      (2003), by Erik Gandini; each made
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-corporate_activism" title="Anti-corporate activism">
       anti-corporate activism
      </a>
      popular as an ideologically accessible form of civil and political action.
     </p>
     <p>
      The criticism of
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">
       economic materialism
      </a>
      as a dehumanizing behaviour that is destructive of the Earth, as
      <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">
       human habitat
      </a>
      , comes from
      <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">
       religion
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_activism" title="Social activism">
       social activism
      </a>
      . The religious criticism asserts that materialist consumerism interferes with the connection between the individual man and woman and God, and so is an inherently
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immoral" title="Immoral">
       immoral
      </a>
      style of life; thus the German historian
      <a href="/wiki/Oswald_Spengler" title="Oswald Spengler">
       Oswald Spengler
      </a>
      (1880–1936) said that "Life in America is exclusively economic in structure, and lacks depth."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      From the Roman Catholic perspective,
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">
       Thomas Aquinas
      </a>
      said that "Greed is a sin against God, just as all mortal sins, in as much as man condemns things eternal for the sake of temporal things"; in that vein,
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_of_Assisi" title="Francis of Assisi">
       Francis of Assisi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ammon_Hennacy" title="Ammon Hennacy">
       Ammon Hennacy
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohandas_Gandhi" title="Mohandas Gandhi">
       Mohandas Gandhi
      </a>
      said that spiritual inspiration guided them towards
      <a href="/wiki/Simple_living" title="Simple living">
       simple living
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      From the secular perspective, social activism indicates that from consumerist materialism derive
      <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">
       crime
      </a>
      (which originates from the
      <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">
       poverty
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">
       economic inequality
      </a>
      ), industrial
      <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">
       pollution
      </a>
      and the consequent
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">
       environmental degradation
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">
       war
      </a>
      as a business. About the societal discontent born of
      <a href="/wiki/Malaise" title="Malaise">
       malaise
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hedonism" title="Hedonism">
       hedonism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">
       Pope Benedict XVI
      </a>
      said that the philosophy of materialism offers no
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/raison_d%27%C3%AAtre" title="wikt:raison d'être">
        raison d'être
       </a>
      </i>
      for human existence;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      likewise, the writer
      <a href="/wiki/Georges_Duhamel" title="Georges Duhamel">
       Georges Duhamel
      </a>
      said that "American materialism [is] a beacon of mediocrity that threatened to eclipse French civilization".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Anti-consumerism originated from criticism of consumption, starting with
      <a href="/wiki/Thorstein_Veblen" title="Thorstein Veblen">
       Thorstein Veblen
      </a>
      , who, in the book
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_the_Leisure_Class" title="Theory of the Leisure Class">
        The Theory of the Leisure Class: An Economic Study of Institutions
       </a>
      </i>
      (1899), indicated that
      <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">
       consumerism
      </a>
      dates from the
      <a href="/wiki/Cradle_of_civilization" title="Cradle of civilization">
       cradle of civilization
      </a>
      . The term
      <i>
       consumerism
      </i>
      also denotes economic policies associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">
       Keynesian economics
      </a>
      , and the belief that the free choice of consumers should dictate the economic structure of a society. (cf.
      <a href="/wiki/Producerism" title="Producerism">
       producerism
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_society">
       Politics and society
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Politics and society">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anticonsumismo.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Anticonsumismo.JPG/250px-Anticonsumismo.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Anticonsumismo.JPG/375px-Anticonsumismo.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Anticonsumismo.JPG/500px-Anticonsumismo.JPG 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anticonsumismo.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An anti-consumerist
        <a href="/wiki/Stencil_graffiti" title="Stencil graffiti">
         stencil graffiti
        </a>
        saying "Consuming consumes you"
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-corporate_activist" title="Anti-corporate activist">
       anti-corporate activists
      </a>
      believe the rise of large-business corporations poses a threat to the legitimate authority of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nation_states" title="Nation states">
       nation states
      </a>
      and the public sphere.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      They feel corporations are invading people's
      <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">
       privacy
      </a>
      , manipulating politics and governments, and creating false needs in consumers. They state evidence such as invasive advertising
      <a href="/wiki/Adware" title="Adware">
       adware
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" title="Spam (electronic)">
       spam
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Telemarketing" title="Telemarketing">
       telemarketing
      </a>
      , child-targeted advertising, aggressive
      <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_marketing" title="Guerrilla marketing">
       guerrilla marketing
      </a>
      , massive corporate campaign contributions in political elections, interference in the policies of sovereign nation states (
      <a href="/wiki/Ken_Saro-Wiwa" title="Ken Saro-Wiwa">
       Ken Saro-Wiwa
      </a>
      ), and news stories about
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate_corruption" title="Corporate corruption">
       corporate corruption
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">
       Enron
      </a>
      , for example).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Anti-consumerism protesters point out that the main responsibility of a corporation is to answer only to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shareholders" title="Shareholders">
       shareholders
      </a>
      , giving
      <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">
       human rights
      </a>
      and other issues almost no consideration.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The management does have a primary responsibility to their shareholders, since any
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philanthropic" title="Philanthropic">
       philanthropic
      </a>
      activities that do not directly serve the business could be deemed to be a
      <a href="/wiki/Position_of_trust" title="Position of trust">
       breach of trust
      </a>
      . This sort of financial responsibility means that multi-national corporations will pursue strategies to intensify labor and reduce costs. For example, they will attempt to find low wage economies with laws which are conveniently lenient on human rights, the
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">
       natural environment
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">
       trade union
      </a>
      organization and so on (see, for example,
      <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc.#Human_rights_concerns" title="Nike, Inc.">
       Nike
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      An important contribution to the critique of consumerism has been made by French philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Stiegler" title="Bernard Stiegler">
       Bernard Stiegler
      </a>
      , arguing modern capitalism is governed by consumption rather than production, and the advertising techniques used to create
      <a href="/wiki/Consumer_behaviour" title="Consumer behaviour">
       consumer behaviour
      </a>
      amount to the destruction of psychic and collective
      <a href="/wiki/Individuation" title="Individuation">
       individuation
      </a>
      . The diversion of libidinal energy toward the consumption of consumer products, he argues, results in an addictive cycle of consumption, leading to hyper consumption, the exhaustion of desire, and the reign of symbolic misery.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conspicuous_consumption">
       Conspicuous consumption
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conspicuous consumption">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">
       Conspicuous consumption
      </a>
     </div>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       It is preoccupation with possessions, more than anything else, that prevents us from living freely and nobly.
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       <cite>
        —
        <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">
         Bertrand Russell
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </cite>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Trying to reduce environmental pollution without reducing consumerism is like combating drug trafficking without reducing the drug addiction.
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       <cite>
        —
        <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Majfud" title="Jorge Majfud">
         Jorge Majfud
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </cite>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In many critical contexts,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      the term describes the tendency of people to identify strongly with products or services they consume, especially with commercial
      <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">
       brand
      </a>
      names and obvious
      <a href="/wiki/Status_symbol" title="Status symbol">
       status-enhancing
      </a>
      appeal, such as a brand of expensive automobiles or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewelry" title="Jewelry">
       jewelry
      </a>
      . It is a pejorative term which most people deny, having some more specific excuse or
      <a href="/wiki/Rationalization_(psychology)" title="Rationalization (psychology)">
       rationalization
      </a>
      for consumption other than the idea that they are "compelled to consume". A culture that has a high amount of consumerism is referred to as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_culture" title="Consumer culture">
       consumer culture
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      To those who embrace the idea of consumerism, these products are not seen as valuable in themselves, but rather as social signals that allow them to identify like-minded people through consumption and display of similar products. Few would yet go so far, though, as to admit that their relationships with a product or brand name could be substitutes for healthy human relationships that sometimes lack in a dysfunctional modern
      <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">
       society
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The older term
      <i>
       conspicuous consumption
      </i>
      described the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      in the 1960s, but was soon linked to larger debates about
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_influence" title="Media influence">
       media influence
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Culture_jamming" title="Culture jamming">
       culture jamming
      </a>
      , and its corollary
      <a href="/wiki/Productivism" title="Productivism">
       productivism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BarsStencil.jpg">

     <p>
      The term and concept of
      <i>
       conspicuous consumption
      </i>
      originated at the turn of the 20th century in the writing of economist
      <a href="/wiki/Thorstein_Veblen" title="Thorstein Veblen">
       Thorstein Veblen
      </a>
      . The term describes an apparently irrational and confounding form of economic behaviour. Veblen's scathing proposal that this unnecessary consumption is a form of status display is made in darkly humorous observations like the following:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       It is true of dress in even a higher degree than of most other items of consumption, that people will undergo a very considerable degree of privation in the comforts or the necessaries of life in order to afford what is considered a decent amount of wasteful consumption; so that it is by no means an uncommon occurrence, in an inclement climate, for people to go ill clad in order to appear well dressed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In 1955, economist
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Victor_Lebow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Victor Lebow (page does not exist)">
       Victor Lebow
      </a>
      stated (as quoted by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_E._Rees_(academic)" title="William E. Rees (academic)">
       William Rees
      </a>
      , 2009):
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       "Our enormously productive economy demands that we make consumption our way of life, that we convert the buying and use of goods into rituals, that we seek our spiritual satisfaction and our ego satisfaction in consumption. We need things consumed, burned up, worn out, replaced and discarded at an ever-increasing rate".
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      According to archaeologists, evidence of conspicuous consumption up to several millennia ago has been found, suggesting that such behavior is inherent to humans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Consumerism_and_advertising">
       Consumerism and advertising
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Consumerism and advertising">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Anti-consumerists believe
      <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">
       advertising
      </a>
      plays a huge role in human life by informing values and assumptions of the cultural system, deeming what is acceptable and determining social standards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World._Dir_1997_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-World._Dir_1997-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      They declare that ads create a hyper-real world where commodities appear as the key to securing happiness. Anti-consumerists cite studies that find that individuals believe their quality of life improves in relation to social values that lie outside the capability of the market place. Therefore, advertising attempts to equate the social with the material by utilizing images and slogans to link commodities with the real sources of human happiness, such as meaningful relationships. Ads are then a detriment to society because they tell consumers that accumulating more and more possessions will bring them closer to self-actualization, or the concept of a complete and secure being. "The underlying message is that owning these products will enhance our image and ensure our popularity with others".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      And while advertising promises that a product will make the consumer happy, advertising simultaneously depends upon the consumer never being
      <i>
       truly
      </i>
      happy, as then the consumer would no longer feel the need to consume needless products.
     </p>
     <p>
      Anti-consumerists claim that in a consumerist society, advertisement images disempower and objectify the consumer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rumbo_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rumbo-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      By stressing individual power, choice and desire, advertising falsely implies the control lies with the consumer. Because anti-consumerists believe commodities only supply short-term gratification, they detract from a sustainably happy society. Further, advertisers have resorted to new techniques of capturing attention, such as the increased speed of ads and
      <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">
       product placements
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World._Dir_1997_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-World._Dir_1997-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this way, commercials infiltrate the consumerist society and become an inextricable part of culture. Anti-consumerists condemn advertising because it constructs a simulated world that offers fantastical
      <a href="/wiki/Escapism" title="Escapism">
       escapism
      </a>
      to consumers, rather than reflecting actual reality. They further argue that ads depict the interests and lifestyles of the elite as natural; cultivating a deep sense of inadequacy among viewers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rumbo_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rumbo-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      They denounce use of beautiful models because they glamorize the commodity beyond reach of the average individual.
     </p>
     <p>
      In an opinion segment of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">
        New Scientist
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine published in August 2009, reporter Andy Coghlan cited
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_E._Rees_(academic)" title="William E. Rees (academic)">
       William Rees
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">
       University of British Columbia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">
       epidemiologist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Warren_Hern" title="Warren Hern">
       Warren Hern
      </a>
      of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">
       University of Colorado at Boulder
      </a>
      , saying that human beings, despite considering themselves civilized thinkers, are "subconsciously still driven by an impulse for survival, domination and expansion... an impulse which now finds expression in the idea that inexorable economic growth is the answer to everything, and, given time, will redress all the world's existing inequalities." According to figures presented by Rees at the annual meeting of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ecological_Society_of_America" title="Ecological Society of America">
       Ecological Society of America
      </a>
      , human society is in a "global overshoot", consuming 30% more material than is sustainable from the world's resources. Rees went on to state that at present, 85 countries are exceeding their domestic "bio-capacities", and compensate for their lack of local material by depleting the stocks of other countries.
     </p>

     <p>
      Such anti consumerist, anti capitalist notions are not without their detractors. New thought and theory has spurred movements to alter world economic climate. Green movements and some other thinkers are opposed to the focus put on economics. The need for terminology has created familiar ideas such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrying-capacity" title="Carrying-capacity">
       carrying-capacity
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">
       ecological footprint
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">
       David Ricardo
      </a>
      , an early economist, had ideas that state the finitude of growth, rather than the opposite; his ideas were similar to those of
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">
       Mark Twain
      </a>
      , when he said "Buy land, they don't make it anymore." To Ricardian logic, land was a limiting factor.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anti-consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2015)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      have accused anti-consumerists of opposing modernity or
      <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">
       utilitarianism
      </a>
      . Right-wing critics see anti-consumerism as rooted in socialism. In 1999, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libertarian" title="Libertarian">
       libertarian
      </a>
      magazine
      <i>
       Reason
      </i>
      attacked anti-consumerism, claiming
      <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">
       Marxist
      </a>
      academics are repackaging themselves as anti-consumerists. James Twitchell, a professor at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">
       University of Florida
      </a>
      and popular writer, referred to anti-consumerism arguments as "Marxism Lite."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There have also been socialist critics of anti-consumerism who see it as a form of anti-modern "reactionary socialism", and state that anti-consumerism has also been adopted by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ultra-conservatism (page does not exist)">
       ultra-conservatives
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">
       fascists
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
