     <p>
      <b>
       Hydropower
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       water power
      </b>
      (from the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
       ύδωρ
      </span>
      , "water") is
      <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">
       power
      </a>
      derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       energy
      </a>
      of falling water or fast running water, which may be harnessed for useful purposes. Since ancient times, hydropower from many kinds of
      <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">
       watermills
      </a>
      has been used as a
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      source for
      <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">
       irrigation
      </a>
      and the operation of various mechanical devices, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">
       gristmills
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">
       sawmills
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">
       textile
      </a>
      mills,
      <a href="/wiki/Trip_hammer" title="Trip hammer">
       trip hammers
      </a>
      , dock
      <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">
       cranes
      </a>
      , domestic
      <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">
       lifts
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">
       ore
      </a>
      mills. A
      <a href="/wiki/Trompe" title="Trompe">
       trompe
      </a>
      , which produces compressed air from falling water, is sometimes used to power other machinery at a distance.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the late 19th century, hydropower became a source for generating
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Cragside" title="Cragside">
       Cragside
      </a>
      in Northumberland was the first house powered by hydroelectricity in 1878
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the first commercial hydroelectric power plant was built at
      <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">
       Niagara Falls
      </a>
      in 1879. In 1881, street lamps in the city of Niagara Falls were powered by hydropower.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the early 20th century, the term has been used almost exclusively in conjunction with the modern development of
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectric power
      </a>
      . International institutions such as the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      view hydropower as a means for
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">
       economic development
      </a>
      without adding substantial amounts of carbon to the atmosphere,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP5813_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WP5813-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      but
      <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">
       dams
      </a>
      can have significant negative
      <a href="/wiki/Social_impact_assessment" title="Social impact assessment">
       social
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">
       environmental impacts
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-per_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-per-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">
       India
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">
       water wheels
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">
       watermills
      </a>
      were built
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2015)">
          when?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      ; in
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
       Imperial Rome
      </a>
      , water powered mills produced flour from grain, and were also used for sawing timber and stone; in China, watermills were widely used since the
      <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">
       Han dynasty
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      and the rest of the Far East, hydraulically operated "pot wheel" pumps raised water into crop or irrigation canals.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2015)">
          when?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The power of a wave of water released from a tank was used for extraction of metal ores in a method known as
      <a href="/wiki/Hushing" title="Hushing">
       hushing
      </a>
      . The method was first used at the
      <a href="/wiki/Dolaucothi_Gold_Mines" title="Dolaucothi Gold Mines">
       Dolaucothi Gold Mines
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">
       Wales
      </a>
      from 75 AD onwards, but had been developed in
      <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      at such mines as
      <a href="/wiki/Las_M%C3%A9dulas" title="Las Médulas">
       Las Médulas
      </a>
      . Hushing was also widely used in
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
       Britain
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Medieval
      </a>
      and later periods to extract
      <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">
       lead
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">
       tin
      </a>
      ores.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It later evolved into
      <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_mining" title="Hydraulic mining">
       hydraulic mining
      </a>
      when used during the
      <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">
       California Gold Rush
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </a>
      , Islamic mechanical engineer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Jazari" title="Al-Jazari">
       Al-Jazari
      </a>
      described designs for 50 devices, many of them water powered, in his book,
      <i>
       The Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices
      </i>
      , including clocks, a device to serve wine, and five devices to lift water from rivers or pools, though three are animal-powered and one can be powered by animal or water. These include
      <a href="/wiki/Scoop_wheel" title="Scoop wheel">
       an endless belt with jugs attached
      </a>
      , a cow-powered
      <a href="/wiki/Shadoof" title="Shadoof">
       shadoof
      </a>
      , and a reciprocating device with hinged valves.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS">
         <span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (November 2015)">
          better source needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1753, French engineer
      <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Forest_de_B%C3%A9lidor" title="Bernard Forest de Bélidor">
       Bernard Forest de Bélidor
      </a>
      published
      <i>
       Architecture Hydraulique
      </i>
      which described vertical- and horizontal-axis hydraulic machines. By the late nineteenth century, the
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">
       electric generator
      </a>
      was developed by a team led by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_managers" title="Project managers">
       project managers
      </a>
      and prominent pioneers of renewable energy
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jacob_S._Gibbs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jacob S. Gibbs (page does not exist)">
       Jacob S. Gibbs
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brinsley_Coleberd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brinsley Coleberd (page does not exist)">
       Brinsley Coleberd
      </a>
      and could now be coupled with hydraulics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doehis_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-doehis-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The growing demand for the
      <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">
       Industrial Revolution
      </a>
      would drive development as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watenc_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-watenc-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the beginning of the Industrial Revolution in Britain, water was the main source of power for new inventions such as
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Arkwright" title="Richard Arkwright">
       Richard Arkwright
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Water_frame" title="Water frame">
       water frame
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kreis_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kreis-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the use of water power gave way to steam power in many of the larger mills and factories, it was still used during the 18th and 19th centuries for many smaller operations, such as driving the bellows in small
      <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">
       blast furnaces
      </a>
      (e.g. the
      <a href="/wiki/Dyfi_Furnace" title="Dyfi Furnace">
       Dyfi Furnace
      </a>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">
       gristmills
      </a>
      , such as those built at
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anthony_Falls" title="Saint Anthony Falls">
       Saint Anthony Falls
      </a>
      , which uses the 50-foot (15 m) drop in the
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">
       Mississippi River
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1830s, at the early peak in US
      <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">
       canal
      </a>
      -building, hydropower provided the energy to transport
      <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">
       barge
      </a>
      traffic up and down steep hills using
      <a href="/wiki/Cable_railway" title="Cable railway">
       inclined plane railroads
      </a>
      . As railroads overtook canals for transportation, canal systems were modified and developed into hydropower systems; the
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_Lowell,_Massachusetts" title="History of Lowell, Massachusetts">
       history of Lowell, Massachusetts
      </a>
      is a classic example of commercial development and industrialization, built upon the availability of water power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Technological advances had moved the open water wheel into an enclosed
      <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">
       turbine
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Water_engine" title="Water engine">
       water motor
      </a>
      . In 1848
      <a href="/wiki/James_B._Francis" title="James B. Francis">
       James B. Francis
      </a>
      , while working as head engineer of Lowell's Locks and Canals company, improved on these designs to create a turbine with 90% efficiency.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      He applied scientific principles and testing methods to the problem of turbine design. His mathematical and graphical calculation methods allowed confident design of high efficiency turbines to exactly match a site's specific flow conditions. The
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_turbine" title="Francis turbine">
       Francis reaction turbine
      </a>
      is still in wide use today. In the 1870s, deriving from uses in the California mining industry,
      <a href="/wiki/Lester_Allan_Pelton" title="Lester Allan Pelton">
       Lester Allan Pelton
      </a>
      developed the high efficiency
      <a href="/wiki/Pelton_wheel" title="Pelton wheel">
       Pelton wheel impulse turbine
      </a>
      , which utilized hydropower from the high head streams characteristic of the mountainous California interior.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydraulic_power-pipe_networks">
       Hydraulic power-pipe networks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hydraulic power-pipe networks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_power_network" title="Hydraulic power network">
       Hydraulic power networks
      </a>
      used pipes to carrying pressurized water and transmit mechanical power from the source to end users. The power source was normally a head of water, which could also be assisted by a pump. These were extensive in
      <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">
       Victorian
      </a>
      cities in the United Kingdom. A hydraulic power network was also developed in
      <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">
       Geneva
      </a>
      , Switzerland. The world-famous
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_d%27Eau" title="Jet d'Eau">
       Jet d'Eau
      </a>
      was originally designed as the over-pressure relief valve for the network.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geneva_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-geneva-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Compressed_air_hydro">
       Compressed air hydro
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Compressed air hydro">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Trompe" title="Trompe">
       Trompe
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Where there is a plentiful head of water it can be made to generate
      <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">
       compressed air
      </a>
      directly without moving parts. In these designs, a falling column of water is purposely mixed with air bubbles generated through turbulence or a venturi pressure reducer at the high level intake. This is allowed to fall down a shaft into a subterranean, high-roofed chamber where the now-compressed air separates from the water and becomes trapped. The height of the falling water column maintains compression of the air in the top of the chamber, while an outlet, submerged below the water level in the chamber allows water to flow back to the surface at a lower level than the intake. A separate outlet in the roof of the chamber supplies the compressed air. A facility on this principle was built on the
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal_River_(Timiskaming_District)" title="Montreal River (Timiskaming District)">
       Montreal River
      </a>
      at Ragged Shutes near
      <a href="/wiki/Cobalt,_Ontario" title="Cobalt, Ontario">
       Cobalt, Ontario
      </a>
      in 1910 and supplied 5,000 horsepower to nearby mines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydropower_types">
       Hydropower types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hydropower types">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       Hydroelectricity
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Hydropower is used primarily to generate
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>
      . Broad categories include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity#Generating_methods" title="Hydroelectricity">
        Conventional hydroelectric
       </a>
       , referring to hydroelectric dams.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">
        Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity
       </a>
       , which captures the kinetic energy in rivers or streams, without a large reservoir and sometimes without the use of dams.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Small_hydro" title="Small hydro">
        Small hydro
       </a>
       projects are 10 megawatts or less and often have no artificial reservoirs.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Micro_hydro" title="Micro hydro">
        Micro hydro
       </a>
       projects provide a few kilowatts to a few hundred kilowatts to isolated homes, villages, or small industries.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Conduit_hydroelectricity" title="Conduit hydroelectricity">
        Conduit hydroelectricity
       </a>
       projects utilize water which has already been diverted for use elsewhere; in a municipal water system, for example.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">
        Pumped-storage hydroelectricity
       </a>
       stores water pumped uphill into reservoirs during periods of low demand to be released for generation when demand is high or system generation is low.
      </li>
     </ul>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_the_amount_of_available_power">
       Calculating the amount of available power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Calculating the amount of available power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A hydropower resource can be evaluated by its available
      <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">
       power
      </a>
      . Power is a function of the
      <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_head" title="Hydraulic head">
       hydraulic head
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rate_of_fluid_flow" title="Rate of fluid flow">
       rate of fluid flow
      </a>
      . The head is the energy per unit weight (or unit mass) of water. The static head is proportional to the difference in height through which the water falls. Dynamic head is related to the velocity of moving water. Each unit of water can do an amount of work equal to its weight times the head.
     </p>
     <p>
      The power available from falling water can be calculated from the flow rate and density of water, the height of fall, and the local acceleration due to gravity. In SI units, the power is:
     </p>
     <p>
      <span>
       <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

             <!-- η -->

          </annotation>
         </semantics>
        </math>
       </span>
       <img alt="P=\eta \rho \,Qgh\!" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aec701e4cf348b928ae2ff23d3913a4b8570e916" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; margin-right: -0.338ex; width:11.92ex; height:2.676ex;"/>
      </span>
     </p>
     <p>
      where
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       P is power in watts
      </li>
      <li>
       η is the dimensionless efficiency of the turbine
      </li>
      <li>
       ρ is the density of water in kilograms per cubic metre
      </li>
      <li>
       Q is the flow in cubic metres per second
      </li>
      <li>
       g is the acceleration due to gravity
      </li>
      <li>
       h is the height difference between inlet and outlet in metres
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      To illustrate, power is calculated for a turbine that is 85% efficient, with water at 1000 kg/cubic metre (62.5 pounds/cubic foot) and a flow rate of 80 cubic-meters/second (2800 cubic-feet/second), gravity of 9.81 metres per second squared and with a net head of 145 m (480 ft).
     </p>

     <p>
      Operators of hydroelectric stations will compare the total electrical energy produced with the theoretical potential energy of the water passing through the turbine to calculate efficiency. Procedures and definitions for calculation of efficiency are given in test codes such as
      <a href="/wiki/ASME" title="ASME">
       ASME
      </a>
      PTC 18 and
      <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">
       IEC
      </a>
      60041. Field testing of turbines is used to validate the manufacturer's guaranteed efficiency. Detailed calculation of the efficiency of a hydropower turbine will account for the head lost due to flow friction in the power canal or penstock, rise in tail water level due to flow, the location of the station and effect of varying gravity, the temperature and barometric pressure of the air, the density of the water at ambient temperature, and the altitudes above sea level of the forebay and tailbay. For precise calculations, errors due to rounding and the number of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Significant_digit" title="Significant digit">
       significant digits
      </a>
      of constants must be considered.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some hydropower systems such as
      <a href="/wiki/Water_wheel" title="Water wheel">
       water wheels
      </a>
      can draw power from the flow of a body of water without necessarily changing its height. In this case, the available power is the
      <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
       kinetic energy
      </a>
      of the flowing water. Over-shot water wheels can efficiently capture both types of energy.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The water flow in a stream can vary widely from season to season. Development of a hydropower site requires analysis of
      <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">
       flow records
      </a>
      , sometimes spanning decades, to assess the reliable annual energy supply. Dams and reservoirs provide a more dependable source of power by smoothing seasonal changes in water flow. However reservoirs have significant
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs" title="Environmental impact of reservoirs">
       environmental impact
      </a>
      , as does alteration of naturally occurring stream flow. The design of dams must also account for the worst-case, "probable maximum flood" that can be expected at the site; a
      <a href="/wiki/Spillway" title="Spillway">
       spillway
      </a>
      is often included to bypass flood flows around the dam. A computer
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrological_modelling" title="Hydrological modelling">
       model of the hydraulic basin
      </a>
      and rainfall and snowfall records are used to predict the maximum flood.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydropower_sustainability">
       Hydropower sustainability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydropower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hydropower sustainability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As with other forms of economic activity, hydropower projects can have both a positive and a negative environmental and social impact, because the construction of a dam and power plant, along with the impounding of a reservoir, creates certain social and physical changes.
     </p>
     <p>
      A number of tools have been developed to assist projects.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most new hydropower project must undergo an
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">
       Environmental and Social Impact Assessment
      </a>
      . This provides a base line understand of the pre project conditions, estimates potential impacts and puts in place management plans to avoid, mitigate, or compensate for impacts.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Hydropower_Sustainability_Assessment_Protocol" title="Hydropower Sustainability Assessment Protocol">
       Hydropower Sustainability Assessment Protocol
      </a>
      is another tool which can be used to promote and guide more sustainable hydropower projects. It is a methodology used to audit the performance of a hydropower project across more than twenty environmental, social, technical and economic topics. A Protocol assessment provides a rapid sustainability health check. It does not replace an environmental and social impact assessment (ESIA), which takes place over a much longer period of time, usually as a mandatory regulatory requirement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/World_Commission_on_Dams" title="World Commission on Dams">
       World Commission on Dams
      </a>
      final report describes a framework for planning water and energy projects that is intended to protect dam-affected people and the environment, and ensure that the benefits from dams are more equitably distributed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/International_Finance_Corporation" title="International Finance Corporation">
       IFC
      </a>
      's Environmental and Social Performance Standards define IFC clients' responsibilities for managing their environmental and social risks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      ’s safeguard policies are used by the Bank to help identify, avoid, and minimize harms to people and the environment caused by investment projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Equator_Principles" title="Equator Principles">
       Equator Principles
      </a>
      is a risk management framework, adopted by financial institutions, for determining, assessing and managing environmental and social risk in projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Reservoirs accumulate plant material, which then decomposes, emitting
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      in uneven bursts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
