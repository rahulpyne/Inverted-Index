     <p>
      A
      <b>
       battery electric vehicle
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       BEV
      </b>
      ),
      <b>
       battery-only electric vehicle
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       BOEV
      </b>
      ) or
      <b>
       all-electric vehicle
      </b>
      is a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">
       electric vehicle
      </a>
      (EV) that uses
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">
       chemical energy
      </a>
      stored in
      <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">
       rechargeable battery
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Battery_pack" title="Battery pack">
       packs
      </a>
      . BEVs use
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">
       electric motors
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Motor_controller" title="Motor controller">
       motor controllers
      </a>
      instead of
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       internal combustion engines
      </a>
      (ICEs) for propulsion. They derive all power from battery packs and thus have no internal combustion engine,
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">
       fuel cell
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">
       fuel tank
      </a>
      . BEVs include bicycles, scooters, skateboards, rail cars, watercraft, forklifts, buses, trucks and cars. Since the introduction of the all-electric Nissan Leaf in December 2010, about 1 million highway legal
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">
       plug-in electric vehicles
      </a>
      have been sold worldwide by mid-September 2015, of which about 620,000 are all-electric passenger cars and light-duty trucks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global1mi_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global1mi-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of June 2016
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      , the best-selling
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-electric_car" title="All-electric car">
       all-electric car
      </a>
      in history is the
      <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Leaf" title="Nissan Leaf">
       Nissan Leaf
      </a>
      , with global sales of more than 228,000 units, followed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Model_S" title="Tesla Model S">
       Tesla Model S
      </a>
      , with global sales of almost 130,000 units.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Top10Global062016_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Top10Global062016-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
       Terminology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">
       Hybrid electric vehicle
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">
       Plug-in hybrid
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">
       Plug-in electric vehicle
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Vehicles using both electric motors and internal combustion engines are examples of
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">
       hybrid electric vehicles
      </a>
      , and are not considered pure or all-electric vehicles because they cannot be externally charged (operate in charge-sustaining mode) and instead they are continually recharged with power from the
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       internal combustion engine
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regenerative_braking" title="Regenerative braking">
       regenerative braking
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEVs_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PEVs-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle" title="Hybrid vehicle">
       Hybrid vehicles
      </a>
      with batteries that can be charged externally to displace some or all of their internal combustion engine power and
      <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">
       gasoline
      </a>
      fuel are called
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">
       plug-in hybrid
      </a>
      electric vehicles (PHEV), and run as BEVs during their
      <a href="/wiki/Charge-depleting" title="Charge-depleting">
       charge-depleting
      </a>
      mode. PHEVs with a
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle_drivetrain#Series_hybrid" title="Hybrid vehicle drivetrain">
       series
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">
       powertrain
      </a>
      are also called range-extended electric vehicles (REEVs), such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Volt" title="Chevrolet Volt">
       Chevrolet Volt
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Fisker_Karma" title="Fisker Karma">
       Fisker Karma
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">
       Plug-in electric vehicles
      </a>
      (PEVs) are a subcategory of
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">
       electric vehicles
      </a>
      that includes battery electric vehicles (BEVs), plug-in hybrid vehicles, (PHEVs), and
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle_conversion" title="Electric vehicle conversion">
       electric vehicle conversions
      </a>
      of hybrid electric vehicles and conventional
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       internal combustion engine
      </a>
      vehicles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PEVs_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-PEVs-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSE_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CSE-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In China, plug-in electric vehicles, together with hybrid electric vehicles are called new energy vehicles (NEVs).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEVs_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NEVs-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, in the United States,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">
       neighborhood electric vehicles
      </a>
      (NEVs) are battery electric vehicles that are legally limited to roads with posted speed limits no higher than 45 miles per hour (72 km/h), are usually built to have a top speed of 30 miles per hour (48 km/h), and have a maximum loaded weight of 3,000 lbs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicles_by_type">
       Vehicles by type
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vehicles by type">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The concept of battery electric vehicles is to use charged
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle_battery" title="Electric vehicle battery">
       batteries
      </a>
      on board vehicles for propulsion. Battery electric cars are becoming more and more attractive with the advancement of new battery technology (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium_Ion" title="Lithium Ion">
       Lithium Ion
      </a>
      ) that have higher power and
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">
       energy density
      </a>
      (i.e., greater possible acceleration and more range with fewer batteries) and higher oil prices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      BEVs include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">
       automobiles
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Truck#Light_trucks" title="Truck">
       light trucks
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">
       neighborhood electric vehicles
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>

     <ul>
      <li>
       Battery electric railcars:

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">
       Electric car
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_production_battery_electric_vehicles" title="List of production battery electric vehicles">
       List of production battery electric vehicles
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_car_use_by_country" title="Electric car use by country">
       electric car use by country
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      An
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">
       electric car
      </a>
      is a plug-in battery powered
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">
       automobile
      </a>
      which is
      <a href="/wiki/Ground_propulsion" title="Ground propulsion">
       propelled
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">
       electric motor
      </a>
      (s). Although electric cars often give good acceleration and have generally acceptable top speed, the lower
      <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">
       specific energy
      </a>
      of production batteries available in 2015 compared with
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon-based_fuel" title="Carbon-based fuel">
       carbon-based fuels
      </a>
      means that electric cars need batteries that are fairly large fraction of the vehicle mass but still often give relatively low range between charges. Recharging can also take significant lengths of time. For journeys within a single battery charge, rather than long journeys, electric cars are practical forms of transportation and can be recharged overnight.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Zero-emissions_vehicle" title="Zero-emissions vehicle">

     <p>
      Electric cars are expected to have a major impact in the auto industry
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
      given advantages in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle_emissions" title="Motor vehicle emissions">
       city pollution
      </a>
      , less dependence on oil, and expected rise in gasoline prices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        [46]
       </a>
      </sup>
      World governments are pledging billions to fund development of electric vehicles and their components. The US has pledged
      <span style="white-space: nowrap">
       US$2.4 billion
      </span>
      in federal grants for electric cars and batteries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        [47]
       </a>
      </sup>
      China has announced it will provide
      <span style="white-space: nowrap">
       US$15 billion
      </span>
      to initiate an electric car industry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since 2008, about 620,000 highway-capable all-electric passenger cars and light utility vehicles have been sold worldwide by mid-September 2015, out of total global sales of about 1 million light-duty plug-in electric vehicles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global1mi_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global1mi-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renault-Nissan_Alliance" title="Renault-Nissan Alliance">
       Renault-Nissan Alliance
      </a>
      is the leading all-electric vehicle manufacturer. The Alliance achieved the sales milestone of 350,000 all-electric vehicles delivered globally in August 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RN350K_49-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-RN350K-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ranking second is
      <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Motors" title="Tesla Motors">
       Tesla Motors
      </a>
      with over 139,000 electric cars sold between 2008 and June 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesla125K_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tesla125K-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tesla2Q2016_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tesla2Q2016-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Model_S_driving.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3744" data-file-width="5616" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Model_S_driving.jpg/220px-Model_S_driving.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Model_S_driving.jpg/330px-Model_S_driving.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Model_S_driving.jpg/440px-Model_S_driving.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

        The
        <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Model_S" title="Tesla Model S">
         Tesla Model S
        </a>
        is the world's second best selling all-electric car, with global sales of almost 130,000 units up until June 2016.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Top10Global062016_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Top10Global062016-3">

     <p>
      As of June 2016
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      , the world's all-time top selling highway legal electric car is the Nissan Leaf, released in December 2010, with global sales of over 228,000 units, followed by the Tesla Model S (about 129,400), the Renault Zoe (51,193), the BMW i3 (around 49,500), and the Mitsubishi i-MiEV family (about 37,600).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Top10Global062016_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Top10Global062016-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Renault Kangoo Z.E. utility van is the leader of the light-duty all-electric segment with global sales of 23,219 units through June 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renault2011_16_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Renault2011_16-52">
        [52]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_E" title="Formula E">
       Formula E
      </a>
      is a fully electric international single seater championship. The series was conceived in 2012, and the inaugural championship started in Beijing on 13 September 2014. The series is sanctioned by the FIA. Alejandro Agag is the current CEO of Formula E.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Formula E championship is currently contested by ten teams with two drivers each (after the withdrawal of Team Trulli, there are temporarily only nine teams competing). Racing generally takes place on temporary city-center street circuits which are approximately 2 to 3.4 km (1.2 to 2.1 mi) long. Currently, only the Mexico City ePrix takes place on a road course, a modified version of the Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Two-_and_three-wheeled_vehicles">
       Two- and three-wheeled vehicles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Two- and three-wheeled vehicles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      An increasing variety of two-wheeled electric vehicles is being made for transportation and entertainment. These include devices for sitting on such as
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_motorcycles_and_scooters" title="Electric motorcycles and scooters">
       electric motorcycles and scooters
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_bicycles" title="Electric bicycles">
       electric bicycles
      </a>
      , as well as devices for standing on such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Segway_PT" title="Segway PT">
       Segway PT
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-balancing_two-wheeled_board" title="Self-balancing two-wheeled board">
       self-balancing two-wheeled boards
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Three-wheeled vehicles include
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_rickshaw" title="Electric rickshaw">
       electric rickshaws
      </a>
      , a powered variant of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cycle_rickshaw" title="Cycle rickshaw">
       cycle rickshaw
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Motors">
       Motors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Motors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Traction_motor" title="Traction motor">
       Traction motor
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Electric cars have traditionally used series wound DC motors, a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Brushed_DC_electric_motor" title="Brushed DC electric motor">
       brushed DC electric motor
      </a>
      . Separately excited and permanent magnet are just two of the types of DC motors available. More recent electric vehicles have made use of a variety of
      <a href="/wiki/AC_motor" title="AC motor">
       AC motor
      </a>
      types, as these are simpler to build and have no brushes that can wear out. These are usually
      <a href="/wiki/Induction_motor" title="Induction motor">
       induction motors
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brushless_AC_electric_motor" title="Brushless AC electric motor">
       brushless AC electric motors
      </a>
      which use permanent magnets. There are several variations of the permanent magnet motor which offer simpler drive schemes and/or lower cost including the
      <a href="/wiki/Brushless_DC_electric_motor" title="Brushless DC electric motor">
       brushless DC electric motor
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Motor_controllers">
       Motor controllers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Motor controllers">
        edit

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Motor_controller" title="Motor controller">
       Motor controller
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The motor controller regulates the power to the motor, supplying either variable pulse width DC or variable frequency variable amplitude AC, depending on the motor type, DC or AC.
     </p>
