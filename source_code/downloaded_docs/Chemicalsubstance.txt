     <p>
      A
      <b>
       chemical substance
      </b>
      is a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
       matter
      </a>
      that has constant
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">
       chemical composition
      </a>
      and characteristic properties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It cannot be separated into components by physical separation methods, i.e., without breaking chemical bonds. Chemical substances can be
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
       chemical elements
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">
       chemical compounds
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
       ions
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">
       alloys
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Chemical substances are often called 'pure' to set them apart from
      <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">
       mixtures
      </a>
      . A common example of a chemical substance is pure
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">
       water
      </a>
      ; it has the same properties and the same
      <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">
       ratio
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      whether it is isolated from a river or made in a
      <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">
       laboratory
      </a>
      . Other chemical substances commonly encountered in pure form are
      <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">
       diamond
      </a>
      (carbon),
      <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
       gold
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edible_salt" title="Edible salt">
       table salt
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">
       sodium chloride
      </a>
      ) and refined
      <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">
       sugar
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">
       sucrose
      </a>
      ). However, in practice, no substance is entirely pure, and chemical purity is specified according to the intended use of the chemical.
     </p>
     <p>
      Chemical substances exist as
      <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">
       solids
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">
       liquids
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
       gases
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
       plasma
      </a>
      , and may change between these
      <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">
       phases of matter
      </a>
      with changes in
      <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
       temperature
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      . Chemical substances may be combined or converted to others by means of
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">
       chemical reactions
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Forms of
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       energy
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
       light
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">
       heat
      </a>
      , are not considered to be matter, and are thus not "substances" in this regard.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nile_red_01.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1913" data-file-width="2900" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Nile_red_01.jpg/220px-Nile_red_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Nile_red_01.jpg/330px-Nile_red_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Nile_red_01.jpg/440px-Nile_red_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nile_red_01.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Colors of a single chemical (
        <a href="/wiki/Nile_red" title="Nile red">
         Nile red
        </a>
        ) in different solvents, under visible and UV light, showing how the chemical interacts dynamically with its solvent environment.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A chemical substance (also called a pure substance) may well be defined as "any material with a definite chemical composition" in an introductory general chemistry textbook.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HillPetrucciP5_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HillPetrucciP5-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to this definition a chemical substance can either be a pure
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
       chemical element
      </a>
      or a pure chemical compound. But, there are exceptions to this definition; a pure substance can also be defined as a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
       matter
      </a>
      that has both definite composition and distinct properties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The chemical substance index published by
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Abstracts_Service" title="Chemical Abstracts Service">
       CAS
      </a>
      also includes several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alloys" title="Alloys">
       alloys
      </a>
      of uncertain composition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">
       Non-stoichiometric compounds
      </a>
      are a special case (in inorganic chemistry) that violates the law of constant composition, and for them, it is sometimes difficult to draw the line between a mixture and a compound, as in the case of
      <a href="/wiki/Palladium_hydride" title="Palladium hydride">
       palladium hydride
      </a>
      . Broader definitions of chemicals or chemical substances can be found, for example: "the term 'chemical substance' means any organic or inorganic substance of a particular molecular identity, including – (i) any combination of such substances occurring in whole or in part as a result of a chemical reaction or occurring in nature"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
       geology
      </a>
      , substances of uniform composition are called
      <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">
       minerals
      </a>
      , while physical mixtures (aggregates) of several minerals (different substances) are defined as
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
       rocks
      </a>
      . Many minerals, however, mutually dissolve into
      <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">
       solid solutions
      </a>
      , such that a single rock is a uniform substance despite being a mixture in stoichiometric terms.
      <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">
       Feldspars
      </a>
      are a common example:
      <a href="/wiki/Anorthoclase" title="Anorthoclase">
       anorthoclase
      </a>
      is an alkali aluminium silicate, where the alkali metal is interchangeably either sodium or potassium.
     </p>
     <p>
      In law, "chemical substances" may include both pure substances and mixtures with a defined composition or manufacturing process. For example, the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       EU
      </a>
      regulation
      <a href="/wiki/Registration,_Evaluation,_Authorisation_and_Restriction_of_Chemicals" title="Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals">
       REACH
      </a>
      defines "monoconstituent substances", "multiconstituent substances" and "substances of unknown or variable composition". The latter two consist of multiple chemical substances; however, their identity can be established either by direct chemical analysis or reference to a single manufacturing process. For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">
       charcoal
      </a>
      is an extremely complex, partially polymeric mixture that can be defined by its manufacturing process. Therefore, although the exact chemical identity is unknown, identification can be made to a sufficient accuracy. The CAS index also includes mixtures.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">
       Polymers
      </a>
      almost always appear as mixtures of molecules of multiple molar masses, each of which could be considered a separate chemical substance. However, the polymer may be defined by a known precursor or reaction(s) and the
      <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass_distribution" title="Molar mass distribution">
       molar mass distribution
      </a>
      . For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">
       polyethylene
      </a>
      is a mixture of very long chains of -CH
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      - repeating units, and is generally sold in several molar mass distributions,
      <a href="/wiki/Low-density_polyethylene" title="Low-density polyethylene">
       LDPE
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Medium-density_polyethylene" title="Medium-density polyethylene">
       MDPE
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/High-density_polyethylene" title="High-density polyethylene">
       HDPE
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-molecular-weight_polyethylene" title="Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene">
       UHMWPE
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">
       concept
      </a>
      of a "chemical substance" became firmly established in the late eighteenth century after work by the chemist
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Proust" title="Joseph Proust">
       Joseph Proust
      </a>
      on the composition of some pure chemical compounds such as
      <a href="/wiki/Basic_copper_carbonate" title="Basic copper carbonate">
       basic copper carbonate
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hill_et_al_p37_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hill_et_al_p37-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      He deduced that, "All samples of a compound have the same composition; that is, all samples have the same proportions, by mass, of the elements present in the compound." This is now known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_of_constant_composition" title="Law of constant composition">
       law of constant composition
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later with the advancement of methods for
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">
       chemical synthesis
      </a>
      particularly in the realm of
      <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">
       organic chemistry
      </a>
      ; the discovery of many more chemical elements and new techniques in the realm of
      <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">
       analytical chemistry
      </a>
      used for isolation and purification of elements and compounds from chemicals that led to the establishment of modern
      <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
       chemistry
      </a>
      , the concept was defined as is found in most chemistry textbooks. However, there are some controversies regarding this definition mainly because the large number of chemical substances reported in chemistry literature need to be indexed.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomerism" title="Isomerism">
       Isomerism
      </a>
      caused much consternation to early researchers, since
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomers" title="Isomers">
       isomers
      </a>
      have exactly the same composition, but differ in configuration (arrangement) of the atoms. For example, there was much speculation for the chemical identity of
      <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">
       benzene
      </a>
      , until the correct structure was described by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_August_Kekul%C3%A9" title="Friedrich August Kekulé">
       Friedrich August Kekulé
      </a>
      . Likewise, the idea of
      <a href="/wiki/Stereoisomerism" title="Stereoisomerism">
       stereoisomerism
      </a>
      - that atoms have rigid three-dimensional structure and can thus form isomers that differ only in their three-dimensional arrangement - was another crucial step in understanding the concept of distinct chemical substances. For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Tartaric_acid" title="Tartaric acid">
       tartaric acid
      </a>
      has three distinct isomers, a pair of
      <a href="/wiki/Diastereomer" title="Diastereomer">
       diastereomers
      </a>
      with one diastereomer forming two
      <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">
       enantiomers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_elements">
       Chemical elements
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

        edit
       </a>

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Schwefel_01.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schwefel_01.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Native sulfur crystals. Sulfur occurs naturally as elemental sulfur, in
        <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">
         sulfide
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">
         sulfate
        </a>
        minerals and in
        <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">
         hydrogen sulfide
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
       Chemical element
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_elements" title="List of elements">
       List of elements
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      An
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
       element
      </a>
      is a chemical substance that is made up of a particular kind of atoms and hence cannot be broken down or transformed by a chemical reaction into a different element, though it can be transmutated into another element through a
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">
       nuclear reaction
      </a>
      . This is so, because all of the atoms in a sample of an element have the same number of protons, though they may be different
      <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
       isotopes
      </a>
      , with differing numbers of neutrons.
     </p>
     <p>
      As of 2012, there are 118 known elements, about 80 of which are stable – that is, they do not change by
      <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">
       radioactive decay
      </a>
      into other elements. Some elements can occur as more than a single chemical substance (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allotropes" title="Allotropes">
       allotropes
      </a>
      ). For instance, oxygen exists as both diatomic oxygen (O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">
       ozone
      </a>
      (O
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      ). The majority of elements are classified as
      <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">
       metals
      </a>
      . These are elements with a characteristic
      <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">
       lustre
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
       iron
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
       copper
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
       gold
      </a>
      . Metals typically conduct electricity and heat well, and they are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleability" title="Malleability">
       malleable
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">
       ductile
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hill_et_al_pp44-45_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hill_et_al_pp44-45-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Around a dozen elements,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
       carbon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      , are classified as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-metal" title="Non-metal">
       non-metals
      </a>
      . Non-metals lack the metallic properties described above, they also have a high
      <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">
       electronegativity
      </a>
      and a tendency to form
      <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
       negative ions
      </a>
      . Certain elements such as
      <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">
       silicon
      </a>
      sometimes resemble metals and sometimes resemble non-metals, and are known as
      <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">
       metalloids
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_compounds">
       Chemical compounds
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemical compounds">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Potassium-ferricyanide-sample.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="665" data-file-width="1000" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Potassium-ferricyanide-sample.jpg/220px-Potassium-ferricyanide-sample.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Potassium-ferricyanide-sample.jpg/330px-Potassium-ferricyanide-sample.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Potassium-ferricyanide-sample.jpg/440px-Potassium-ferricyanide-sample.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Potassium-ferricyanide-sample.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Potassium_ferricyanide" title="Potassium ferricyanide">
         Potassium ferricyanide
        </a>
        is a compound of potassium, iron, carbon and nitrogen; although it contains cyanide anions, it does not release them and is nontoxic.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">
       Chemical compound
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_organic_compounds" title="List of organic compounds">
       List of organic compounds
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_inorganic_compounds" title="List of inorganic compounds">
       List of inorganic compounds
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A pure chemical compound is a chemical substance that is composed of a particular set of
      <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
       molecules
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
       ions
      </a>
      . Two or more elements combined into one substance through a
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">
       chemical reaction
      </a>
      form a
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">
       chemical compound
      </a>
      . All compounds are substances, but not all substances are compounds.
     </p>
     <p>
      A chemical compound can be either atoms
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">
       bonded
      </a>
      together in
      <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
       molecules
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">
       crystals
      </a>
      in which atoms, molecules or ions form a
      <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">
       crystalline lattice
      </a>
      . Compounds based primarily on carbon and hydrogen atoms are called
      <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">
       organic compounds
      </a>
      , and all others are called
      <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">
       inorganic compounds
      </a>
      . Compounds containing bonds between carbon and a metal are called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic_compound" title="Organometallic compound">
       organometallic compounds
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Compounds in which components share electrons are known as
      <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">
       covalent
      </a>
      compounds. Compounds consisting of oppositely charged
      <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
       ions
      </a>
      are known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">
       ionic
      </a>
      compounds, or
      <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">
       salts
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In organic chemistry, there can be more than one chemical compound with the same composition and molecular weight. Generally, these are called
      <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">
       isomers
      </a>
      . Isomers usually have substantially different chemical properties, may be isolated and do not spontaneously convert to each other. A common example is
      <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">
       glucose
      </a>
      vs.
      <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">
       fructose
      </a>
      . The former is an
      <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">
       aldehyde
      </a>
      , the latter is a
      <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">
       ketone
      </a>
      . Their interconversion requires either
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glucose_isomerase" title="Glucose isomerase">
       enzymatic
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lobry%E2%80%93de_Bruyn%E2%80%93van_Ekenstein_transformation" title="Lobry–de Bruyn–van Ekenstein transformation">
       acid-base catalysis
      </a>
      . However, there are also
      <a href="/wiki/Tautomer" title="Tautomer">
       tautomers
      </a>
      , where isomerization occurs spontaneously, such that a pure substance cannot be isolated into its tautomers. A common example is
      <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">
       glucose
      </a>
      , which has open-chain and ring forms. One cannot manufacture pure open-chain glucose because glucose spontaneously cyclizes to the
      <a href="/wiki/Hemiacetal" title="Hemiacetal">
       hemiacetal
      </a>
      form. Materials may also comprise other entities such as polymers. These may be inorganic or organic and sometimes a combination of inorganic and organic.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Substances_versus_mixtures">
       Substances versus mixtures
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Substances versus mixtures">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vintage_cranberry_glass.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="600" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Vintage_cranberry_glass.jpg/220px-Vintage_cranberry_glass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Vintage_cranberry_glass.jpg/330px-Vintage_cranberry_glass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Vintage_cranberry_glass.jpg/440px-Vintage_cranberry_glass.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vintage_cranberry_glass.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Cranberry glass, while it looks homogeneous, is a
        <i>
         mixture
        </i>
        consisting of glass and
        <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
         gold
        </a>
        colloidal particles of ca. 40 nm diameter, which give it a red color.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">
       Mixture
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      All matter consists of various elements and chemical compounds, but these are often intimately mixed together. Mixtures contain more than one chemical substance, and they do not have a fixed composition. In principle, they can be separated into the component substances by purely
      <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">
       mechanical
      </a>
      processes.
      <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">
       Butter
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">
       soil
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">
       wood
      </a>
      are common examples of mixtures.
     </p>
     <p>
      Grey iron metal and yellow
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
       sulfur
      </a>
      are both chemical elements, and they can be mixed together in any ratio to form a yellow-grey mixture. No chemical process occurs, and the material can be identified as a mixture by the fact that the sulfur and the iron can be separated by a mechanical process, such as using a
      <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">
       magnet
      </a>
      to attract the iron away from the sulfur.
     </p>
     <p>
      In contrast, if iron and sulfur are heated together in a certain ratio (1 atom of iron for each atom of sulfur, or by weight, 56
      <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">
       grams
      </a>
      (1
      <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">
       mol
      </a>
      ) of iron to 32 grams (1 mol) of sulfur), a chemical reaction takes place and a new substance is formed, the compound
      <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_sulfide" title="Iron(II) sulfide">
       iron(II) sulfide
      </a>
      , with chemical formula FeS. The resulting compound has all the properties of a chemical substance and is not a mixture. Iron(II) sulfide has its own distinct properties such as
      <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">
       melting point
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">
       solubility
      </a>
      , and the two elements cannot be separated using normal mechanical processes; a magnet will be unable to recover the iron, since there is no metallic iron present in the compound.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chemicals_versus_chemical_substances">
       Chemicals versus chemical substances
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemicals versus chemical substances">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While the term
      <i>
       chemical substance
      </i>
      is a precise technical term that is synonymous with "chemical" for professional chemists, the meaning of the word
      <i>
       chemical
      </i>
      varies for non-chemists within the English speaking world or those using English. For industries, government and society in general in some countries,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the word
      <i>
       chemical
      </i>
      includes a wider class of substances that contain many mixtures of such chemical substances, often finding application in many vocations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In countries that require a list of ingredients in products, the "chemicals" listed would be equated with "chemical substances".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Within the chemical industry, manufactured "chemicals" are chemical substances, which can be classified by production volume into bulk chemicals,
      <a href="/wiki/Fine_chemical" title="Fine chemical">
       fine chemicals
      </a>
      and chemicals found in research only:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Bulk chemicals are produced in very large quantities, usually with highly optimized continuous processes and to a relatively low price.
      </li>
      <li>
       Fine chemicals are produced at a high cost in small quantities for special low-volume applications such as biocides, pharmaceuticals and speciality chemicals for technical applications.
      </li>
      <li>
       Research chemicals are produced individually for research, such as when searching for synthetic routes or screening substances for pharmaceutical activity. In effect, their price per gram is very high, although they are not sold.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The cause of the difference in production volume is the complexity of the molecular structure of the chemical. Bulk chemicals are usually much less complex. While fine chemicals may be more complex, many of them are simple enough to be sold as "building blocks" in the synthesis of more complex molecules targeted for single use, as named above. The
      <i>
       production
      </i>
      of a chemical includes not only its synthesis but also its purification to eliminate by-products and impurities involved in the synthesis. The last step in production should be the analysis of batch lots of chemicals in order to identify and quantify the percentages of impurities for the buyer of the chemicals. The required purity and analysis depends on the application, but higher tolerance of impurities is usually expected in the production of bulk chemicals. Thus, the user of the chemical in the US might choose between the bulk or "technical grade" with higher amounts of impurities or a much purer "pharmaceutical grade" (labeled "USP",
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Pharmacopeia" title="United States Pharmacopeia">
       United States Pharmacopeia
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_and_indexing">
       Naming and indexing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Naming and indexing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Every chemical substance has one or more
      <a href="/wiki/Systematic_name" title="Systematic name">
       systematic names
      </a>
      , usually named according to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature" title="IUPAC nomenclature">
       IUPAC rules for naming
      </a>
      . An alternative system is used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Abstracts_Service" title="Chemical Abstracts Service">
       Chemical Abstracts Service
      </a>
      (CAS).
     </p>
     <p>
      Many compounds are also known by their more common, simpler names, many of which predate the systematic name. For example, the long-known
      <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">
       sugar
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">
       glucose
      </a>
      is now systematically named 6-(hydroxymethyl)oxane-2,3,4,5-tetrol.
      <a href="/wiki/Secondary_metabolite" title="Secondary metabolite">
       Natural products
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">
       pharmaceuticals
      </a>
      are also given simpler names, for example the mild pain-killer
      <a href="/wiki/Naproxen" title="Naproxen">
       Naproxen
      </a>
      is the more common name for the chemical compound (S)-6-methoxy-α-methyl-2-naphthaleneacetic acid.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">
       Chemists
      </a>
      frequently refer to
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">
       chemical compounds
      </a>
      using
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">
       chemical formulae
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_structure" title="Molecular structure">
       molecular structure
      </a>
      of the compound. There has been a phenomenal growth in the number of chemical compounds being synthesized (or isolated), and then reported in the
      <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">
       scientific literature
      </a>
      by professional chemists around the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      An enormous number of chemical compounds are possible through the chemical combination of the known chemical elements. As of May 2011, about sixty million chemical compounds are known.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The names of many of these compounds are often nontrivial and hence not very easy to remember or cite accurately. Also it is difficult to keep the track of them in the literature. Several international organizations like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">
       IUPAC
      </a>
      and CAS have initiated steps to make such tasks easier. CAS provides the abstracting services of the chemical literature, and provides a numerical identifier, known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CAS_registry_number" title="CAS registry number">
       CAS registry number
      </a>
      to each chemical substance that has been reported in the chemical literature (such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemistry_journal" title="Chemistry journal">
       chemistry journals
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">
       patents
      </a>
      ). This information is compiled as a
      <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">
       database
      </a>
      and is popularly known as the Chemical substances index. Other computer-friendly systems that have been developed for substance information, are:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simplified_molecular_input_line_entry_specification" title="Simplified molecular input line entry specification">
       SMILES
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Chemical_Identifier" title="International Chemical Identifier">
       International Chemical Identifier
      </a>
      or InChI.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Isolation.2C_purification.2C_characterization.2C_and_identification">
       Isolation, purification, characterization, and identification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Isolation, purification, characterization, and identification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Often a pure substance needs to be isolated from a
      <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">
       mixture
      </a>
      , for example from a
      <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">
       natural source
      </a>
      (where a sample often contains numerous chemical substances) or after a
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">
       chemical reaction
      </a>
      (which often give mixtures of chemical substances).
     </p>
