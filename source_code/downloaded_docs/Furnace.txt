     <p>
      A
      <b>
       furnace
      </b>
      is a device used for high-temperature heating. The name derives from
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       fornax
      </i>
      , which means
      <a href="/wiki/Oven" title="Oven">
       oven
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
       American English
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">
       Canadian English
      </a>
      usage, the term
      <i>
       furnace
      </i>
      on its own refers to the
      <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">
       household
      </a>
      heating systems based on a central furnace (known either as a
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">
       boiler
      </a>
      , or a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heater" title="Heater">
       heater
      </a>
      in British English), and sometimes as a synonym for
      <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">
       kiln
      </a>
      , a device used in the production of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramic_materials" title="Ceramic materials">
       ceramics
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
       British English
      </a>
      , a
      <i>
       furnace
      </i>
      is an
      <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">
       industrial
      </a>
      furnace used for many things, such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_extraction" title="Resource extraction">
       extraction
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">
       metal
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">
       ore
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">
       smelting
      </a>
      ) or in
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">
       oil refineries
      </a>
      and other chemical plants, for example as the heat source for
      <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">
       fractional distillation
      </a>
      columns.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       furnace
      </i>
      can also refer to a direct fired heater, used in boiler applications in chemical industries or for providing heat to
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">
       chemical reactions
      </a>
      for processes like cracking, and is part of the standard
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      names for many metallurgical furnaces worldwide.
     </p>
     <p>
      The heat energy to fuel a furnace may be supplied directly by fuel
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      , by electricity such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">
       electric arc furnace
      </a>
      , or through
      <a href="/wiki/Induction_heating" title="Induction heating">
       induction heating
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Induction_furnace" title="Induction furnace">
       induction furnaces
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      A large furnace is a
      <a href="/wiki/Major_appliance" title="Major appliance">
       major appliance
      </a>
      that is permanently installed to provide heat to an interior space through intermediary fluid movement, which may be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">
       air
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">
       steam
      </a>
      , or hot
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      . Heating appliances that use steam or hot water as the fluid are normally referred to as a residential steam boiler or residential hot water boiler. The most common
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">
       fuel
      </a>
      source for modern furnaces in the United States is
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      ; other common fuel sources include
      <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">
       LPG
      </a>
      (liquefied petroleum gas),
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">
       fuel oil
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">
       wood
      </a>
      . In some cases
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" title="Electrical resistance">
       electrical resistance
      </a>
      heating is used as the source of heat, especially where the cost of electricity is low.
     </p>
     <p>
      Modern high-efficiency furnaces can be 98%
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">
       efficient
      </a>
      and operate without a chimney. The small amount of waste gas and heat are mechanically ventilated through PVC pipes that can be vented through the side or roof of the house. Fuel efficiency in a gas furnace is measured in
      <a href="/wiki/Annual_fuel_utilization_efficiency" title="Annual fuel utilization efficiency">
       AFUE
      </a>
      (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">
       Categories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Categories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Residential furnaces can be classified into four general categories, based on efficiency and design.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_draft">
       Natural draft
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural draft">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lamneck-central-heating-gas-furnace-cutaway-diagram.png">
        <img alt="A cutaway diagram of a Lamneck central heating gas furnace." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="665" data-file-width="509" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Lamneck-central-heating-gas-furnace-cutaway-diagram.png/140px-Lamneck-central-heating-gas-furnace-cutaway-diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Lamneck-central-heating-gas-furnace-cutaway-diagram.png/210px-Lamneck-central-heating-gas-furnace-cutaway-diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Lamneck-central-heating-gas-furnace-cutaway-diagram.png/280px-Lamneck-central-heating-gas-furnace-cutaway-diagram.png 2x" width="140"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lamneck-central-heating-gas-furnace-cutaway-diagram.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Diagram of natural draft gas furnace, early 20th century.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first category would be natural draft, atmospheric burner furnaces. These furnaces consisted of cast-iron or riveted-steel heat exchangers built within an outer shell of brick, masonry, or steel. The heat exchangers were vented through brick or masonry chimneys. Air circulation depended on large, upwardly pitched pipes constructed of wood or metal The pipes would channel the warm air into floor or wall vents inside the home. This method of heating worked because
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_convection" title="Natural convection">
       warm air rises
      </a>
      . The system was simple, had few controls, a single automatic gas valve, and no blower. These furnaces could be made to work with any fuel simply by adapting the burner area. They have been operated with wood, coke, coal, trash, paper, natural gas, and fuel oil. Furnaces that used solid fuels required daily maintenance to remove ash and
      <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(waste)" title="Clinker (waste)">
       "clinkers"
      </a>
      that accumulated in the bottom of the burner area. In later years, these furnaces were adapted with electric blowers to aid air distribution and speed moving heat into the home. Gas and oil-fired systems were usually controlled by a thermostat inside the home, while most wood and coal-fired furnaces were controlled by the amount of fuel in the burner and position of the fresh-air damper on the burner access door.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Forced-air">
       Forced-air
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forced-air">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The second category of residential furnace is the forced-air, atmospheric burner style with a cast-iron or sectional steel heat exchanger. This style furnace was used to replace the big, natural draft systems, and was sometimes installed on the existing gravity duct work. The heated air was moved by blowers which were belt driven and designed for a wide range of speeds. These furnaces were still big and bulky compared to modern furnaces, and had heavy-steel exteriors with bolt-on removable panels. Energy efficiency would range anywhere from just over 50% to upward of 65%
      <a href="/wiki/Annual_fuel_utilization_efficiency" title="Annual fuel utilization efficiency">
       AFUE
      </a>
      . This style furnace still used large, masonry or brick chimneys for flues and was eventually designed to accommodate air-conditioning systems.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Forced_draft">
       Forced draft
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Forced draft">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The third category of furnace is the forced draft, mid-efficiency furnace with a steel
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchanger
      </a>
      and multi-speed blower. These furnaces were physically much more compact than the previous styles. They were equipped with combustion air blowers that would pull air through the heat exchanger which greatly increased fuel efficiency while allowing the heat exchangers to become smaller. These furnace may have multi-speed blowers and were designed to work with central air-conditioning systems.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Condensing">
       Condensing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Condensing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The fourth category of furnace is the high-efficiency, or condensing furnace. High-efficiency furnaces can achieve from 89% to 98% fuel efficiency. This style of furnace includes a sealed combustion area, combustion draft inducer and a secondary
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
       heat exchanger
      </a>
      . Because the heat exchanger removes most of the heat from the exhaust gas, it actually condenses water vapor and other chemicals (which form a mild acid) as it operates. The vent pipes are normally installed with PVC pipe versus metal vent pipe to prevent corrosion. The draft inducer allows for the exhaust piping to be routed vertically or horizontally as it exits the structure. The most efficient arrangement for high-efficiency furnaces include PVC piping that brings fresh combustion air from the outside of the home directly to the furnace. Normally the combustion air (fresh air) PVC is routed alongside the exhaust PVC during installation and the pipes exit through a sidewall of the home in the same location. High efficiency furnaces typically deliver a 25% to 35% fuel savings over a 60%
      <a href="/wiki/Annual_fuel_utilization_efficiency" title="Annual fuel utilization efficiency">
       AFUE
      </a>
      furnace.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_distribution">
       Heat distribution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heat distribution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The furnace transfers heat to the living space of the building through an intermediary distribution system. If the distribution is through hot water (or other fluid) or through steam, then the furnace is more commonly called a
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler#Hydronic_boilers" title="Boiler">
       boiler
      </a>
      . One advantage of a boiler is that the furnace can provide hot water for bathing and washing dishes, rather than requiring a separate
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_heater" title="Water heater">
       water heater
      </a>
      . One disadvantage to this type of application is when the boiler breaks down, neither heating nor domestic hot water are available.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Octopus-style-heating-furnace-Snow-Hill-Maryland-USA.jpg">
        <img alt='A photo of an "octopus"-type hot-air furnace in a basement.' class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3309" data-file-width="4705" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Octopus-style-heating-furnace-Snow-Hill-Maryland-USA.jpg/220px-Octopus-style-heating-furnace-Snow-Hill-Maryland-USA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Octopus-style-heating-furnace-Snow-Hill-Maryland-USA.jpg/330px-Octopus-style-heating-furnace-Snow-Hill-Maryland-USA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Octopus-style-heating-furnace-Snow-Hill-Maryland-USA.jpg/440px-Octopus-style-heating-furnace-Snow-Hill-Maryland-USA.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Octopus-style-heating-furnace-Snow-Hill-Maryland-USA.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        "Octopus" furnace with oil burner.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Air convection heating systems have been in use for over a century, but the older systems relied on a passive air circulation system where the greater density of cooler air caused it to sink into the furnace, and the lesser density of the warmed air caused it to rise in the ductwork, the two forces acting together to drive air circulation in a system termed "gravity-feed" the layout of the ducts and furnace was optimized for short, large ducts. This caused the furnace to be referred to as an "octopus" furnace.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:137px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gasfurnace.jpg">
        <img alt="A photo of a modern forced-air gas furnace with associated ductwork nearby." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="598" data-file-width="427" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Gasfurnace.jpg/135px-Gasfurnace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Gasfurnace.jpg/203px-Gasfurnace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Gasfurnace.jpg/270px-Gasfurnace.jpg 2x" width="135"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gasfurnace.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Forced-air gas furnace, design circa 1991.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      By comparison, most modern "warm air" furnaces typically use a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fan_(mechanical)" title="Fan (mechanical)">
       fan
      </a>
      to circulate air to the rooms of house and pull cooler air back to the furnace for reheating; this is called
      <a href="/wiki/Forced-air" title="Forced-air">
       forced-air
      </a>
      heat. Because the fan easily overcomes the
      <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">
       resistance
      </a>
      of the ductwork, the arrangement of ducts can be far more flexible than the octopus of old. In American practice, separate ducts collect cool air to be returned to the furnace. At the furnace, cool air passes into the furnace, usually through an air filter, through the blower, then through the heat exchanger of the furnace, whence it is blown throughout the
      <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">
       building
      </a>
      . One major advantage of this type of system is that it also enables easy installation of central
      <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">
       air conditioning
      </a>
      , simply by adding a cooling coil at the outlet of the furnace.
     </p>
     <p>
      Air is circulated through
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duct_(HVAC)" title="Duct (HVAC)">
       ductwork
      </a>
      , which may be made of sheet metal or plastic "flex" duct, and is insulated or uninsulated. Unless the ducts and
      <a href="/wiki/Plenum_space" title="Plenum space">
       plenum
      </a>
      have been sealed using mastic or foil duct tape, the ductwork is likely to have a high leakage of conditioned air, possibly into unconditioned spaces. Another cause of wasted energy is the installation of ductwork in unheated areas, such as attics and crawl spaces; or ductwork of air conditioning systems in attics in warm climates.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Furnace_types">
       Furnace types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Furnace types">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Single-stage">
       Single-stage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Single-stage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      A single-stage furnace has only one stage of operation, it is either on or off. This means that it is relatively noisy, always running at the highest speed, and always pumping out the hottest air at the highest velocity.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the benefits to a single-stage furnace is typically the cost for installation. Single-stage furnaces are relatively inexpensive since the technology is rather simple.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Two-stage">
       Two-stage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Two-stage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      This type has two stages of operation, full speed and half (or reduced) speed. Depending on the demanded heat, they can run at a lower speed most of the time. They can be quieter, move the air at less velocity, and will better keep the desired temperature in the house.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modulating">
       Modulating
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modulating">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      This type of furnace can modulate the heat output and air velocity nearly continuously, depending on the demanded heat and outside temperature. This means that it only works as much as necessary and therefore saves energy.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_failures">
       Possible failures
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Possible failures">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The following rare but difficult-to-diagnose failure can occur. If the temperature inside the furnace exceeds a maximum threshold, a safety mechanism with a
      <a href="/wiki/Thermostat" title="Thermostat">
       thermostat
      </a>
      will shut the furnace down. A symptom of this failure is that the furnace repeatedly shuts down before the house reaches the desired temperature; this is commonly referred to as the furnace "riding the high limit switch". This condition commonly occurs if the temperature setting of the high limit thermostat is set too close to the normal
      <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">
       operating temperature
      </a>
      of the furnace. Another situation may occur if a humidifier is incorrectly installed on the furnace and the duct which directs a portion of the humidified air back into the furnace is too large. The solution is to reduce the diameter of the cross-feed tube, or install a baffle that reduces the volume of re-fed air.
     </p>

     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">
       metallurgy
      </a>
      , several specialized furnaces are used. These include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Furnaces used in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smelter" title="Smelter">
        smelters
       </a>
       , including:
       <ul>
        <li>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">
          blast furnace
         </a>
         , used to
         <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">
          reduce
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">
          iron ore
         </a>
         to
         <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">
          pig iron
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">
          Steelmaking
         </a>
         furnaces, including:
         <ul>
          <li>
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puddling_furnace" title="Puddling furnace">
            Puddling furnace
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a href="/wiki/Reverberatory_furnace" title="Reverberatory furnace">
            Reverberatory furnace

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_process_furnaces">
       Industrial process furnaces
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Industrial process furnaces">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Furnace2-en.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="731" data-file-width="593" height="431" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Furnace2-en.svg/350px-Furnace2-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Furnace2-en.svg/525px-Furnace2-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Furnace2-en.svg/700px-Furnace2-en.svg.png 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Furnace2-en.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Schematic diagram of an industrial process furnace
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An industrial furnace or direct fired heater, is an equipment used to provide heat for a process or can serve as
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reactor" title="Chemical reactor">
       reactor
      </a>
      which provides heats of reaction. Furnace designs vary as to its function, heating duty, type of fuel and method of introducing combustion air. However, most process furnaces have some common features.
     </p>
     <p>
      Fuel flows into the
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_burner" title="Gas burner">
       burner
      </a>
      and is burnt with air provided from an air blower. There can be more than one burner in a particular furnace which can be arranged in cells which heat a particular set of tubes. Burners can also be floor mounted, wall mounted or roof mounted depending on design. The flames heat up the tubes, which in turn heat the fluid inside in the first part of the furnace known as the radiant section or
      <a href="/wiki/Firebox_(steam_engine)" title="Firebox (steam engine)">
       firebox
      </a>
      . In this chamber where combustion takes place, the heat is transferred mainly by
      <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">
       radiation
      </a>
      to tubes around the
      <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">
       fire
      </a>
      in the chamber. The
      <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">
       heating fluid
      </a>
      passes through the tubes and is thus heated to the desired temperature. The gases from the combustion are known as
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gas
      </a>
      . After the flue gas leaves the firebox, most furnace designs include a
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      section where more heat is recovered before venting to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
       atmosphere
      </a>
      through the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gas_stack" title="Flue gas stack">
       flue gas stack
      </a>
      . (HTF=Heat Transfer Fluid. Industries commonly use their furnaces to heat a secondary fluid with special additives like anti-
      <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">
       rust
      </a>
      and high heat transfer efficiency. This heated fluid is then circulated round the whole plant to heat exchangers to be used wherever heat is needed instead of directly heating the product line as the product or material may be volatile or prone to
      <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">
       cracking
      </a>
      at the furnace temperature.)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Radiant_section">
       Radiant section
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Radiant section">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Middle_rad_sect.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="500" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e7/Middle_rad_sect.jpg/350px-Middle_rad_sect.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e7/Middle_rad_sect.jpg 1.5x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Middle_rad_sect.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Middle of radiant section
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The radiant section is where the tubes receive almost all its heat by
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
       radiation
      </a>
      from the flame. In a vertical, cylindrical furnace, the tubes are vertical. Tubes can be vertical or horizontal, placed along the
      <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">
       refractory
      </a>
      wall, in the middle, etc., or arranged in cells. Studs are used to hold the
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">
       insulation
      </a>
      together and on the wall of the furnace. They are placed about 1 ft (300 mm) apart in this picture of the inside of a furnace. The tubes, shown below, which are reddish brown from
      <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">
       corrosion
      </a>
      , are
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">
       carbon steel
      </a>
      tubes and run the height of the radiant section. The tubes are a distance away from the insulation so radiation can be reflected to the back of the tubes to maintain a uniform tube wall temperature. Tube guides at the top, middle and bottom hold the tubes in place.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Convection_section">
       Convection section
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Convection section">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Conv_sect.gif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="376" data-file-width="500" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Conv_sect.gif/350px-Conv_sect.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/61/Conv_sect.gif 1.5x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Conv_sect.gif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Convection section
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The convection section is located above the radiant section where it is cooler to recover additional heat.
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
       Heat transfer
      </a>
      takes place by
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      here, and the tubes are finned to increase heat transfer. The first two tube rows in the bottom of the convection section and at the top of the radiant section is an area of bare tubes (without fins) and are known as the shield section ("shock tubes"), so named because they are still exposed to plenty of radiation from the firebox and they also act to shield the convection section tubes, which are normally of less resistant material from the high temperatures in the firebox. The area of the radiant section just before flue gas enters the shield section and into the convection section called the bridgezone. A crossover is the tube that connects from the convection section outlet to the radiant section inlet. The crossover piping is normally located outside so that the temperature can be monitored and the efficiency of the convection section can be calculated. The sightglass at the top allows personnel to see the flame shape and pattern from above and visually inspect if flame impingement is occurring. Flame impingement happens when the flame touches the tubes and causes small isolated spots of very high temperature.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Radiant_coil">
       Radiant coil
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Radiant coil">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This is a series of tubes horizontal/ vertical hairpin type connected at ends (with 180° bends) or helical in construction. The radiant coil absorbs heat through radiation. They can be single pass or multi pass depending upon the process-side pressure drop allowed. The radiant coils and bends are housed in the radiant box. Radiant coil materials vary from carbon steel for low temperature services to high alloy steels for high temperature services. These are supported from the radiant side walls or hanging from the radiant roof. Material of these supports is generally high alloy steel. While designing the radiant coil, care is taken so that provision for expansion (in hot conditions) is kept.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Burner">
       Burner
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Burner">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burner1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="500" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/27/Burner1.jpg/350px-Burner1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/27/Burner1.jpg 1.5x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burner1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Furnace burner
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The burner in the vertical, cylindrical furnace as above, is located in the floor and fires upward. Some furnaces have side fired burners, such as in train
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locomotives" title="Locomotives">
       locomotives
      </a>
      . The burner
      <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">
       tile
      </a>
      is made of high temperature refractory and is where the flame is contained. Air registers located below the burner and at the outlet of the air blower are devices with movable flaps or vanes that control the shape and pattern of the flame, whether it spreads out or even swirls around. Flames should not spread out too much, as this will cause flame impingement. Air registers can be classified as primary, secondary and if applicable, tertiary, depending on when their air is introduced. The primary air register supplies primary air, which is the first to be introduced in the burner. Secondary air is added to supplement primary air. Burners may include a pre-mixer to mix the air and fuel for better combustion before introducing into the burner. Some burners even use steam as premix to preheat the air and create better mixing of the fuel and heated air. The floor of the furnace is mostly made of a different material from that of the wall, typically hard castable refractory to allow technicians to walk on its floor during maintenance.
     </p>
     <p>
      A furnace can be lit by a small
      <a href="/wiki/Pilot_light" title="Pilot light">
       pilot flame
      </a>
      or in some older models, by hand. Most pilot flames nowadays are lit by an ignition transformer (much like a car's spark plugs). The pilot flame in turn lights up the main flame. The pilot flame uses natural gas while the main flame can use both
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">
       diesel
      </a>
      and natural gas. When using liquid fuels, an atomizer is used, otherwise, the liquid fuel will simply pour onto the furnace floor and become a hazard. Using a pilot flame for lighting the furnace increases safety and ease compared to using a manual ignition method (like a match).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sootblower">
       Sootblower
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sootblower">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Sootblowers are found in the convection section. As this section is above the radiant section and air movement is slower because of the fins,
      <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">
       soot
      </a>
      tends to accumulate here. Sootblowing is normally done when the efficiency of the convection section is decreased. This can be calculated by looking at the temperature change from the crossover piping and at the convection section exit.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sootblowers utilize flowing media such as water, air or steam to remove deposits from the tubes. This is typically done during maintenance with the air blower turned on. There are several different types of sootblowers used. Wall blowers of the rotary type are mounted on furnace walls protruding between the convection tubes. The
      <a href="/wiki/Lance" title="Lance">
       lances
      </a>
      are connected to a steam source with holes drilled into it at intervals along its length. When it is turned on, it rotates and blows the soot off the tubes and out through the stack.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stack">
       Stack
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Stack">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stack_damper_mechanism.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="899" data-file-width="917" height="343" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Stack_damper_mechanism.svg/350px-Stack_damper_mechanism.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Stack_damper_mechanism.svg/525px-Stack_damper_mechanism.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Stack_damper_mechanism.svg/700px-Stack_damper_mechanism.svg.png 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stack_damper_mechanism.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Stack damper
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gas_stacks" title="Flue gas stacks">
       flue gas stack
      </a>
      is a cylindrical structure at the top of all the heat transfer chambers. The
      <a href="/wiki/Flue" title="Flue">
       breeching
      </a>
      directly below it collects the flue gas and brings it up high into the atmosphere where it will not endanger personnel.
     </p>
     <p>
      The stack
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damper_(architecture)" title="Damper (architecture)">
       damper
      </a>
      contained within works like a
      <a href="/wiki/Butterfly_valve" title="Butterfly valve">
       butterfly valve
      </a>
      and regulates
      <a href="/wiki/Stack_effect" title="Stack effect">
       draft
      </a>
      (pressure difference between air intake and air exit) in the furnace, which is what pulls the flue gas through the convection section. The stack damper also regulates the heat lost through the stack. As the damper closes, the amount of heat escaping the furnace through the stack decreases, but the
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      or draft in the furnace increases which poses risks to those working around it if there are air leakages in the furnace, the flames can then escape out of the firebox or even explode if the pressure is too great.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Insulation">
       Insulation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Insulation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Insulation is an important part of the furnace because it improves efficiency by minimizing heat escape from the heated chamber. Refractory materials such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firebrick" title="Firebrick">
       firebrick
      </a>
      , castable refractories and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_temperature_insulation_wool" title="High temperature insulation wool">
       ceramic fibre
      </a>
      , are used for insulation. The floor of the furnace are normally castable type refractories while those on the walls are nailed or glued in place. Ceramic fibre is commonly used for the roof and wall of the furnace and is graded by its
      <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
       density
      </a>
      and then its maximum temperature rating. For example, 8# 2,300 °F means 8 lb/ft
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      density with a maximum temperature rating of 2,300 °F. The actual service temperature rating for ceramic fiber is a bit lower than the maximum rated temperature. (i.e. 2300 °F is only good to 2145 °F before permanent linear shrinkage).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Foundations">
       Foundations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Foundations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Concrete pillars are foundation on which the heater is mounted. They can be four nos. for smaller heaters and may be up to 24 nos. for large size heaters. Design of pillars and entire foundation is done based on the load bearing capacity of soil and seismic conditions prevailing in the area. Foundation bolts are grouted in foundation after installation of the heater.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Access_doors">
       Access doors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Access doors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The heater body is provided with access doors at various locations. Access doors are to be used only during shutdown of heater. The normal size of the access door is 600x400 mm, which is sufficient for movement of men/ material into and out of the heater. During operation the access doors are properly bolted using leak proof high temperature gaskets.
     </p>
