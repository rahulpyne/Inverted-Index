     <p>
      <b>
       Sustainable urban infrastructure
      </b>
      , also called
      <b>
       sustainable municipal infrastructure
      </b>
      in Canada, is an
      <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">
       infrastructure
      </a>
      that facilitates a place or
      <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">
       regions
      </a>
      progress towards the goal of
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">
       sustainable living
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Attention is paid to
      <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">
       technological
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">
       government
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Policy" title="Policy">
       policy
      </a>
      which enables
      <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">
       urban planning
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">
       sustainable architecture
      </a>
      and initiatives that promote
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">
       sustainable agriculture
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the College of Engineering and Applied Science of the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_Denver" title="University of Colorado Denver">
       University of Colorado Denver
      </a>
      , urban infrastructure refers to the engineered systems (water, energy, transport, sanitation, information) that make up a city. Challenges resulting from increasing population growth generated a need for sustainable infrastructure that is high performing, cost-effective, resource-efficient and environmentally-friendly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Swiss
      <a href="/wiki/Global_Infrastructure_Basel_Foundation" title="Global Infrastructure Basel Foundation">
       Global Infrastructure Basel Foundation
      </a>
      (GIB) supports various stakeholders, such as governments, banks and cities, in the designing, implementing and financing sustainable urban infrastructure projects at all stages of the project cycle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Currently, GIB develops, in cooperation with the French bank
      <a href="/wiki/Natixis" title="Natixis">
       Natixis
      </a>
      , the the SuRe® Standard – The Standard for Sustainable and Resilient Infrastructure:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a global voluntary ISEAL standard.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Roadside and urban infrastructures such as signposts, bollards and street furniture are prone to impact from vehicles and damage resulting in ever increasing costs associated with maintenance. "
      <i>
       We are in the midst of global infrastructure crisis.
      </i>
      Infrastructure worldwide is deteriorating and needs to be replaced or enhanced. Existing public funding sources are inadequate to meet the need, but the problem is more than money — What we believe is required is a broad, robust infrastructure life cycle perspective
      <i>
       ." Deloitte Research. Closing the Infrastructure Gap. Washington, D.C., Deloitte Research.
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      New self-healing technology protects surrounding paving and foundations from damage when items of infrastructure are impacted, reducing maintenance and improving the sustainability of urban developments. Self-healing developments result in Zero waste and Zero landfill from maintenance on items of urban infrastructure for the life of the development.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">
       United States Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      , the planning process of sustainable design can lead to the development of a community that is ecologically, economically, and socially sustainable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">
       design
      </a>
      emphasis for a sustainable urban infrastructure is on localization and
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">
       sustainable living
      </a>
      . The aim is to reduce individual's
      <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">
       ecological footprint
      </a>
      according to the principles of
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">
       sustainable development
      </a>
      in areas with a high
      <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">
       population density
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The criteria for what can be included in this kind of urban environment varies from place to place, given differences in existing infrastructure and built form,
      <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
       climate
      </a>
      and availability of local resources or talents.
     </p>
     <p>
      Generally speaking the following could be considered sustainable urban infrastructure:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">
        public transport
       </a>
       networks
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">
        Distributed generation
       </a>
       and integrated
       <a href="/wiki/Energy_demand_management" title="Energy demand management">
        energy demand management
       </a>
       initiatives and programs
      </li>
      <li>
       high efficiency buildings and other development constraints such as only permitting the construction of
       <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">
        green buildings
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_habitat" title="Sustainable habitat">
        sustainable habitats
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Energy-efficient_landscaping" title="Energy-efficient landscaping">
        energy-efficient landscaping
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       connected
       <a href="/wiki/Protected_area" title="Protected area">
        green spaces
       </a>
       and

      </li>

       practices to protect
       <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">
        water resources

       .
      </li>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Federation_of_Canadian_Municipalities">
       Federation of Canadian Municipalities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_urban_infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Federation of Canadian Municipalities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Several organizations in Canada related to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FCM_InfraGuide" title="FCM InfraGuide">
       FCM InfraGuide
      </a>
      project, including the
      <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Canadian_Municipalities" title="Federation of Canadian Municipalities">
       Federation of Canadian Municipalities
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure_Canada" title="Infrastructure Canada">
       Infrastructure Canada
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Research_Council_of_Canada" title="National Research Council of Canada">
       National Research Council of Canada
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_Public_Works_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canadian Public Works Association (page does not exist)">
       Canadian Public Works Association
      </a>
      , seek to achieve
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
       sustainability
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_infrastructure" title="Municipal infrastructure">
       municipal infrastructure
      </a>
      especially large scale
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_infrastructure" title="Urban infrastructure">
       urban infrastructure
      </a>
      , they advocate
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protocol" title="Environmental protocol">
       environmental protocols
      </a>
      and inclusion of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_and_social_indicator" title="Ecological and social indicator">
       ecological and social indicators
      </a>
      and factors in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_making" title="Decision making">
       decision making
      </a>
      at the earliest possible stage. There is little focus yet on
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_rural_infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sustainable rural infrastructure (page does not exist)">
       sustainable rural infrastructure
      </a>
      though this is a stated goal of the project, as is spreading it to achieve
      <a href="/wiki/Rural_development" title="Rural development">
       rural development
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_nation" title="Developing nation">
       developing nations
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In their view,
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
       sustainability
      </a>
      concerns apply to all of "maintaining, repairing and upgrading the infrastructure that sustains our
      <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">
       quality of life
      </a>
      " including at least:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       municipal
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_making" title="Decision making">
        decision making
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Investment_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Investment planning (page does not exist)">
        investment planning
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potable_water" title="Potable water">
        potable water
       </a>
       supply
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">
        stormwater
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_water" title="Waste water">
        waste water
       </a>
       especially minimizing the distance that such water travels to be treated and reused
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">
        roads
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">
        sidewalks
       </a>
       and their integration with
       <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">
        transit systems
       </a>
       to achieve smoother flow of people
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protocol" title="Environmental protocol">
        environmental protocols
       </a>
       and multi-discipline practices to ensure they are respected, e.g.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_procurement" title="Green procurement">
        green procurement
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      These and other Canadian official entities including the
      <a href="/wiki/Auditor_General_of_Canada" title="Auditor General of Canada">
       Auditor General of Canada
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Service_Canada" title="Service Canada">
       Service Canada
      </a>
      are focused on related efforts such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_performance_audit" title="Municipal performance audit">
       municipal performance audits
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">
       information technology
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communications_technology" title="Communications technology">
       communications technology
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_purchasing" title="Moral purchasing">
       moral purchasing
      </a>
      and sharing of "data, information, common infrastructure, technology" and the need to "integrate their business processes" to further reduce duplication and waste, especially
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-waste" title="E-waste">
       e-waste
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      emissions
      <i>
       a concern under
       <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">
        Kyoto Protocol
       </a>
       targets that Canada has committed to achieve.
      </i>
      In 2011, Canada withdrew from the Kyoto Protocol due to economic concerns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Communities of Tomorrow is a non-profit enterprise in Saskatchewan, Canada that fosters the development and commercialization of innovative sustainable infrastructure solutions for the global marketplace. Sustainable infrastructure is the development of water, sewer, roads, and other infrastructure systems that meet the needs of current and future generations in a socially, economically and environmentally sustainable manner. Communities of Tomorrow brings industry firms together with researchers to collaboratively develop new infrastructure solutions to existing or future problems with the ultimate goal to commercialize them. It’s about building greener and longer lasting infrastructure, such as roads and water systems, to offer the global marketplace.
     </p>
