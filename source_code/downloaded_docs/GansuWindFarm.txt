     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Gansu Wind Farm Project
      </b>
      (also called
      <b>
       Jiuquan Wind Power Base
      </b>
      ) is a group of large
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">
       wind farms
      </a>
      under the construction in western
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gansu_province" title="Gansu province">
       Gansu province
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      (
      <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">

      <a href="/wiki/Jiuquan" title="Jiuquan">
       Jiuquan
      </a>
      in two localities of
      <a href="/wiki/Guazhou_County" title="Guazhou County">
       Guazhou County
      </a>
      and also near
      <a href="/wiki/Yumen_City" title="Yumen City">
       Yumen City
      </a>
      , in the northwest province of
      <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">
       Gansu
      </a>
      , which has an abundance of wind resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CD-2010.11.04_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CD-2010.11.04-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 8 GW of initial planned capacity of this wind farm is similar to that of the
      <a href="/wiki/Kashiwazaki-Kariwa_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Kashiwazaki-Kariwa Nuclear Power Plant">
       Kashiwazaki-Kariwa Nuclear Power Plant
      </a>
      in Japan, which was the largest nuclear power plant in the world until its closure in 2012.
     </p>
     <p>
      When fully completed the entire project may become the world's biggest collective windfarm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian-2012.03.19_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2012.03.19-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      The project is one of six national
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      megaprojects approved by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Government of the People's Republic of China">
       Chinese government
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CD-2010.11.04_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CD-2010.11.04-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is expected to grow to 20,000
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatts" title="Megawatts">
       megawatts
      </a>
      by 2020, at an estimated cost of 120 billion Chinese yuan ($17.5 billion). The project is being built by more than 20 developers in two localities in
      <a href="/wiki/Guazhou_County" title="Guazhou County">
       Guazhou County
      </a>
      and also near
      <a href="/wiki/Yumen_City" title="Yumen City">
       Yumen City
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reu_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-reu-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guazhou.champs_%C3%A9oliennes.2.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3272" data-file-width="4940" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Guazhou.champs_%C3%A9oliennes.2.jpg/220px-Guazhou.champs_%C3%A9oliennes.2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Guazhou.champs_%C3%A9oliennes.2.jpg/330px-Guazhou.champs_%C3%A9oliennes.2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Guazhou.champs_%C3%A9oliennes.2.jpg/440px-Guazhou.champs_%C3%A9oliennes.2.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guazhou.champs_%C3%A9oliennes.2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Gansu Wind Farm
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The project is divided to multiple phases. The first 3,800 MW phase consisted of eighteen 200 MW wind farms and two 100 MW wind farms. The second 8,000 MW phase consists of forty 200 MW wind farms. The planned capacity is 5,160 MW by 2010, 12,710 MW by 2015 and 20,000 MW in 2020.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reu_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-reu-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008, construction began on a 750 kV AC power line to carry electricity from the wind farm,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and construction of the wind farms themselves started in August 2009.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reu_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-reu-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Power is being purchased for 0.54 yuan per kWh, even though electricity from coal fired powerplants would be about half that price.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian-2012.03.19_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2012.03.19-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since operations began, some 6.26 billion kWh has been generated as of October 31, 2011 with 5.96 billion kWh of that produced in 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 2010 officials announced the completion of the project's first phase, involving the installation of over 3,500 wind turbines with an installed capacity of approximately 5,160 MW according to Wang Jianxin, director of the Jiuquan Development and Reform Commission.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CD-2010.11.04_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-CD-2010.11.04-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Total installed capacity rose to approximately 6,000 MW in March 2012—roughly equivalent to the United Kingdom's entire wind power capacity at that time—with new wind turbines being erected at the rate of 36 per day.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Coordinated_control_centre">
       Coordinated control centre
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gansu_Wind_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coordinated control centre">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On March 1, 2012, a 'wind power coordinated control system' was implemented to adjust the output of the 18 wind farms of the Gansu Wind Farm Project, which total 10 million kW, to meet the needs of the transmission grid, which is limited to 1.5 million kW. This permitted the production of 1 million kWh more per day than previously, and greatly improves the system's stability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Curtailment of wind turbine operations is a first order method for dealing with the intermittency of wind, but normally loses available output when the power grid's transmission capacity has been reached. Other methods involve either added local industrial usage or added local storage capacity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
