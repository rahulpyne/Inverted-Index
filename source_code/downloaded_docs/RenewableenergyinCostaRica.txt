   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Renewable energy in Costa Rica
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       Renewable energy
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
       Costa Rica
      </a>
      accounts for an average of 90% of the energy output for the entire nation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 2014, 99% of its electrical energy was derived from
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      sources, about 80% of which from hydroelectric power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a country, Costa Rica has a geographic advantage over others in that its high concentration per capita of rivers, dams, and volcanoes allow for a high renewable energy output. In addition, Costa Rica is the fourth highest nation in terms of rainfall per capita: it receives an average of 2,926mm of precipitation per year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a smaller nation with a population of only 5 million and no major industry, the need for strong energy infrastructure is less than for larger countries of higher population density. The 1948 elimination of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_Costa_Rica" title="Military of Costa Rica">
       military of Costa Rica
      </a>
      also allows that the government does not need to spend millions of dollars on the defense budget and instead invests in social programs and renewable energy generation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As president of Costa Rica in 1948,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Figueres" title="José Figueres">
       José Figueres
      </a>
      announced that the nation's former military budget would be refocused specifically in healthcare, education, and environmental protection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While Costa Rica's largest source of energy is
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectricity
      </a>
      , other sources include
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">
       geothermal energy
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
       solar power
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:1-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">
       Sources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity">
       Hydroelectricity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Costa Rica receives about 80% of its energy from hydroelectric plants alone due to its extreme amounts of rainfall and multiple rivers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:3-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As the largest source of energy,
      <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">
       hydropower
      </a>
      alone can generate nearly enough energy to power the whole country.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Dams
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Costa Rica’s largest dams include The
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Arenal" title="Lake Arenal">
       Lake Arenal
      </a>
      Dam,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Cachi" title="Lake Cachi">
       Lake Cachi
      </a>
      Dam, the Rio Macho Dam, the Birris Dam, and the proposed
      <a href="/wiki/El_Diqu%C3%ADs_Hydroelectric_Project" title="El Diquís Hydroelectric Project">
       El Diquís Hydroelectric Project
      </a>
      . The Lake Arenal Dam was built in 1979 as the first dam intended solely for hydroelectric power usage. It is located on Lake Arenal next to the famous
      <a href="/wiki/Arenal_Volcano" title="Arenal Volcano">
       Arenal Volcano
      </a>
      , one of the major tourist attractions in Costa Rica. At about 33 square miles wide, it provides enough electricity to power 12% of the entire country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Lake Cachi, Rio Macho, and Birris Dams are all powered by the Rio Reventazon (
      <a href="/wiki/Reventaz%C3%B3n_River" title="Reventazón River">
       Reventazón River
      </a>
      ) and its tributaries. The Cachi dam is situated on an artificial lake that was built in the 1970's and produces enough energy to power about 330,000 homes in Costa Rica. It also controls floods and provides recreational activities in the lake. Upstream of Cachi, the Rio Macho hydroelectric power plant has been operating since 1963 to provide hydroelectric power for central Costa Rica.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:6-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the downstream, the Birris dam is one of the largest dams in Costa Rica and has the potential to power 160,000 homes. It also employes 3,000 Costa Ricans and provides drinking water to 40% of the metropolitan city area. The "El Disquís Hydroelectric Project" is a proposed hydroelectric system that Costa Rica plans to build by 2018 as the largest Central American hydroelectric plant. This plant will also be located on the Reventazón River and will provide the nation with 631MW, enough energy to reach at least one million consumers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal_power">
       Geothermal power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geothermal power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Geothermal power is a natural energy source that provides subterranean heat and power as a byproduct of volcanic energy. Costa Rica has six currently active volcanoes and dozens of inactive volcanoes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unlike many other forms of renewable energy, geothermal can be continuously generated and is not dependent on weather. Geothermal energy contributes to about 15 percent of the energy in the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Plants
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The North Volcanic Mountain Ridge in
      <a href="/wiki/Guanacaste_Province" title="Guanacaste Province">
       Guanacaste
      </a>
      is the region of Costa Rica with the most potential for geothermal power generation. Volcanoes in the region include Miravalles, Rincón de la Vieja, and Tenorio.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Miravalles Geothermal Field, opened in 1994, contains five plants and results in the production of 14% of the National Electric System's power capacity, or about 163 MW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:7-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Pailas Geothermal Power Plant, established in July 2011, produces a total of 55MW
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is located just outside Rincón de la Vieja National Park.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rinc%C3%B3n_de_la_Vieja" title="Rincón de la Vieja">
       Rincón de la Vieja
      </a>
      is a 600,000 year old volcano and the largest in the northwestern region of Costa Rica. In 2014, Costa Rica's government approved legislation for a $958 million geothermal project in the region to offset the country's reliance on hydropower.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">
       Wind power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wind power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      While Costa Rica is not known specifically for its wind power, there have been a few different wind energy projects developed in the past decade. In March 2015, The Costa Rican Institute of Electricity stated that they plan on doubling the installed wind capacity in the nation by 2017.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:2-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 2011, only 2.1% of energy produced in the nation came from wind power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:5-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Wind Power is primarily used in Costa Rica during the months of December to March, or the dry season. During this period, there is a general decreased rainfall in the nation and hydropower output decreases.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Farms
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Currently, there are nine large wind farms operating in Costa Rica. The Tejona Wind Power Project (TWPP) is a 19.8 MW project, fully operational since 2003, that consists of thirty wind turbines. Most recently, Tila Wind, an even larger 20-MW farm, opened in 2015. Three of the remaining eight are owned by ICE and Compania Nacional de Fuerza y Luz SA (CNFL) and CoopeSantos RL. The remaining five are privately owned, and nearly all of the wind power plants are in the province of Guanacaste.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:2-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">
       Solar power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solar power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Like wind power,
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
       solar power
      </a>
      is another newer energy source in the country. Costa Rica's proximity to the equator and high frequency of sunny days provides high solar power potential. The first solar power projects in the country were established in 1978 by just a few researchers from public universities at the Solar Power Laboratory at the National University. Though still on a smaller scale and mostly privately owned, plans for larger more commercial projects are beginning to break through.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass">
       Biomass
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biomass">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As of 2011, only .25% of energy produced in Costa Rica came from biomass. The Jorge Manuel Dengo Obregon National Development Plan proposes the development of sustainable biomass for energy. Currently, biomass is primarily used for cooking and heating kitchen appliances to reduce the reliance on petroleum in the household.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:5-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_organizations">
       Energy organizations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Energy organizations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="ICE">
       ICE
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: ICE">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although the first large-scale hydroelectric station began to function at the beginning of the 1900s, large-scale hydroelectric power and electricity did not begin to be developed until the formation of
      <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Costarricense_de_Electricidad" title="Instituto Costarricense de Electricidad">
       Instituto Costarricense de Electricidad
      </a>
      (Costa Rica Electricity Institute) in 1949. The Costa Rica Electricity Institute (ICE) is one of the largest companies in Costa Rica. This company has had a monopoly over all of the electricity generation in the nation since its birth. ICE owns nearly all of the large dams in the nation and generates most of the Costa Rican’s hydroelectric power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:4-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="ACOPE">
       ACOPE
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: ACOPE">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Asociación Costarricense de Productores de Energía (ACOPE) was founded in 1989 and represents more than 40 private hydroelectric, wind, and biomass units generating in the country. Though there are a few large private energy companies in Costa Rica, most primarily generate power to sell to ICE.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Consorcio_Nacional_de_Empresas_de_Electrificacion_de_Costa_Rica">
       Consorcio Nacional de Empresas de Electrificacion de Costa Rica
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Consorcio Nacional de Empresas de Electrificacion de Costa Rica">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Consorcio Nacional de Empresas de Electrificacion de Costa Rica (Conelectricas), formed in 1989, is a union that aims to develop hydroelectric projects. The union consists of four smaller agencies: Coopeguanacaste, R.L, Coope Alfaro Ruiz, R.L, Coopelesca, R.L, and Coopesantos, R.L. Together, these four cooperatives provide electricity to around 500,000 people, or 22% of the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-:5-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="2015:_75_days_of_renewable_energy">
       2015: 75 days of renewable energy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2015: 75 days of renewable energy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      At the start of January 2015, Costa Rica’s 4.9 million person population ran entirely on renewable energy power for 75 days straight. This was a record-breaking achievement in that Costa Rica was the first nation to power itself completely clean of fossil fuels for this long a timeframe. This summer was an exceptionally rainy one for Costa Rica: while usually the dry season, the sheer amount of power provided from an unusually high amount of precipitation led to a higher than usual amount of hydroelectricity generated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:3-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_neutrality">
       Carbon neutrality
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Carbon neutrality">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Costa Rica aims to be 100%
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_neutral" title="Carbon neutral">
       carbon neutral
      </a>
      by 2021.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:4-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Apart from relying 99% on renewable resources, carbon neutrality is achieved by adopting electric and hybrid transportation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, the Costa Rican government is also beginning to offset the country's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="Carbon dioxide emissions">
       carbon dioxide emissions
      </a>
      with new budgeting, laws, and incentives, including measures to promote
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biofuels" title="Biofuels">
       biofuels
      </a>
      , hybrid vehicles, and clean energy. Another way of offsetting emissions will be a "C-Neutral" tax which will certify that ecotourism and other industrial practices are offsetting their carbon emissions. At $10 per one ton of carbon, the money will fund conservation, reforestation, and research.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">
       Legislation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legislation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As of November 2015, the office of governing Citizen Action Party legislator is working on a bill allowing geothermal electricity development in national parks. In turn, the ICE would compensate land used for the projects with land outside the parks. The bill addresses concerns over geothermal electricity generation in Rincón de la Vieja, Tenorio, and Arenal Volcano national parks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conflicts">
       Conflicts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Conflicts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">
       Transportation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Transportation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicles_in_Costa_Rica" title="Plug-in electric vehicles in Costa Rica">
       Plug-in electric vehicles in Costa Rica
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The biggest obstacle to carbon neutrality is the transportation sector. Vehicles consume 70% of the petroleum consumed in the nation, which is 40% of the total carbon emissions. The primary mode of public transportation are diesel-powered buses. More than 50% of Costa Rica's personal passenger cars are over 20 years old with high polluting engines, though the government is beginning to propose new hybrid and
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">
       plug-in electric vehicle
      </a>
      initiatives. Other proposed solutions include trains, gondolas, and trolleys in the metropolitan
      <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rican_Central_Valley" title="Costa Rican Central Valley">
       Costa Rican Central Valley
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">
       Reliability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Reliability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Though hydroelectricity from dams is a high-density and high-power energy source, it is also highly dependent on weather conditions. Hydropower is reliant on rainfall and is therefore not the most reliable form of energy during seasons of drought. After an extreme season of drought in 2014, Costa Rica was forced to resort to diesel generators since sufficient rainfall was unavailable to power any of the hydroelectric dams.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Wind and solar are both energy sources that are highly dependent on weather conditions and have therefore been used on a much smaller scale than both hydroelectric and geothermal energy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-:3-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_and_Social_Impact">
       Environmental and Social Impact
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Costa_Rica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Environmental and Social Impact">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      One main barrier to extensive geothermal power is the abundance of national parks and protected areas that prevent excavation near many of Costa Rican’s volcanoes. For example, though Rincon de la Vieja has the potential to fuel a high density of geothermal energy, Costa Rican law currently prohibits the extraction of resources inside national parks in order to preserve the land.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:7-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hydroelectric dams have also been criticized for their effects on the surrounding ecosystems. Controversy around hydroelectric dam usage stems from both environmental impact on a surrounding region and from human displacement of land. Hydroelectric dams have been equated to interference in fish migration patterns, ecosystem disrupt, interference in water quality, and land degradation from flooding the land.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Birris dam especially caused much controversy regarding the displacement of people who lived in the area, especially in relation to the
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_Costa_Rica" title="Indigenous peoples of Costa Rica">
       indigenous peoples of Costa Rica
      </a>
      . Though dams provide job opportunities for locals, they also displace large populations of humans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:6-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
