     <p>
      This is a
      <b>
       list of renewable energy topics by country
      </b>
      . These links can be used to compare developments in renewable energy in different countries and to help and encourage new writers to participate in writing about developments in their own countries or countries of interest.
     </p>
     <p>
      The list refers to
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      in general, as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
       solar power
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">
       geothermal energy
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
       biofuel
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydro-electricity" title="Hydro-electricity">
       hydro-electricity
      </a>
      . As of 2013, China, Germany, and Japan, three of the world's four largest economies, as well as India, generate more electricity from renewables than from nuclear power.
     </p>
     <p>
      Based on
      <a href="/wiki/REN21" title="REN21">
       REN21
      </a>
      's 2014 report, renewables contributed 19 percent to humans'
      <a href="/wiki/World_energy_consumption" title="World energy consumption">
       global energy consumption
      </a>
      . This energy consumption is divided as 9% coming from traditional biomass, 4.2% as heat energy (non-biomass), 3.8% hydro electricity and 2% is electricity from wind, solar, geothermal, and biomass.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">
       China
      </a>
      is the world's largest producer of
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectricity
      </a>
      , followed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power_in_Canada" title="Hydroelectric power in Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       Wind power
      </a>
      capacity is growing at the rate of 26% annually, and is widely used in
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_the_European_Union" title="Wind power in the European Union">
       Europe
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_China" title="Wind power in China">
       Asia
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_the_United_States" title="Wind power in the United States">
       United States
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glob_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Glob-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Wind power accounts for approximately 30% of electricity use in
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Denmark" title="Wind power in Denmark">
       Denmark
      </a>
      , 20% in
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Portugal" title="Wind power in Portugal">
       Portugal
      </a>
      , and 18% in
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Spain" title="Wind power in Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_power_stations" title="Photovoltaic power stations">
       PV power stations
      </a>
      are popular in
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Japan" title="Solar power in Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_China" title="Solar power in China">
       China
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_the_United_States" title="Solar power in the United States">
       United States
      </a>
      . The world's largest
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">
       geothermal power
      </a>
      installation is
      <a href="/wiki/The_Geysers" title="The Geysers">
       The Geysers
      </a>
      in California, with a rated capacity of 750 MW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calpine_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-calpine-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brazil has one of the largest renewable energy programs in the world, involving production of
      <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">
       ethanol fuel
      </a>
      from sugar cane, and ethanol now provides 18 percent of the country's automotive fuel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ethanol fuel is also widely available in the USA.
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicles_in_Norway" title="Plug-in electric vehicles in Norway">
       Plug-in electric vehicles in Norway
      </a>
      reached a market share of 22.4% in 2015.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TopSix2015_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TopSix2015-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the highest in the world.
     </p>
     <p>
      While many renewable energy projects are large-scale, renewable technologies are also suited to developing countries, where energy is often crucial in
      <a href="/wiki/Human_development_(humanity)" title="Human development (humanity)">
       human development
      </a>
      . Small solar PV systems provide electricity to a few million households, and micro-hydro configured into mini-grids serves many more.
     </p>
