    Low-impact development (UK)
   </h1>

     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      This article is about the UK meaning of LID. For the Canadian/US usage, see

     <p>
      <b>
       Low-impact development
      </b>
      (LID) has been defined as "development which through its low negative environmental impact either enhances or does not significantly diminish environmental quality".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Future_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Future-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewinsohn_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lewinsohn-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The interplay between would-be developers and the UK planning authorities since the 1980s has led to a diversity of unique, locally adapted developments, often making use of natural, local and reclaimed materials in delivering highly affordable, low or
      <a href="/wiki/Zero_carbon_housing" title="Zero carbon housing">
       zero carbon housing
      </a>
      . These LIDs often strive to be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-sufficient" title="Self-sufficient">
       self-sufficient
      </a>
      in terms of waste management, energy, water and other needs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are numerous examples of LIDs throughout the UK, and local and national authorities have come to recognise the need for the concept to be incorporated into planning strategies.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-impact_development_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Low-impact development (LID), in the UK sense of the term, was described by Simon Fairlie, a former editor of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Ecologist" title="The Ecologist">
       The Ecologist
      </a>
      magazine, in 1996 as: "development that through its low impact either enhances or does not significantly diminish environmental quality."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fairlie later wrote:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       "Neither the term nor the concept was new. People have been living low impact lifestyles in low impact buildings for centuries; indeed until very recently the majority of people in the world lived that way."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickerill_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pickerill-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In 2009 Fairlie revised his definition of a LID as: "development which, by virtue of its low or benign environmental impact, may be allowed in locations where conventional development is not permitted."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickerill_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pickerill-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      He explained:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       "I prefer this revised definition because wrapped up in it is the main argument; that low impact buildings need not be bound by the restrictions necessary to protect the countryside from 'conventional' high impact development – a.k.a. suburban sprawl. There are two other principle arguments in favour of LID: (i) that some form of exception policy is necessary because conventional housing in a countryside protected from sprawl becomes too expensive for the people who work there; and (ii) soon we will all have to live more sustainable low impact lifestyles, so pioneers should be encouraged."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickerill_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pickerill-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Others have expanded on the definition. A study by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_West_England" title="University of West England">
       University of West England
      </a>
      acknowledged that: "LID is usually integrally connected with land management and as much as describing physical development, LID also describes a form of livelihood."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West_England_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-West_England-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, it also states that as LID is a "multi featured and intrinsically integrated form of development," a simple definition cannot capture the meaning of LID and goes on to develop "a detailed themed definition with detailed criteria."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West_England_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-West_England-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dr Larch Maxey
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      in 2013 held the main features of LID to be:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       locally adapted, diverse and unique
      </li>
      <li>
       based on renewable resources
      </li>
      <li>
       of an appropriate scale
      </li>
      <li>
       visually unobtrusive
      </li>
      <li>
       enhances biodiversity
      </li>
      <li>
       increases public access to open space
      </li>
      <li>
       generates little traffic
      </li>
      <li>
       linked to sustainable livelihoods
      </li>
      <li>
       co-ordinated by a management plan
      </li>
     </ul>

       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">
       Wales
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-impact_development_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wales">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/House_of_the_Future,_Cardiff" title="House of the Future, Cardiff">
       House of the Future, Cardiff
      </a>
      , completed in 2000, was originally a showcase of the latest green building technologies, and later transformed into an education centre.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-backtothefuture_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-backtothefuture-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In West Wales,
      <a href="/wiki/Lammas_Ecovillage" title="Lammas Ecovillage">
       Lammas Ecovillage
      </a>
      (near
      <a href="/wiki/Crymych" title="Crymych">
       Crymych
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">
       Pembrokeshire
      </a>
      ) is a community of independent, off-grid households begun in 2009.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nearby
      <i>
       Pwll Broga
      </i>
      roundhouse is a development that was built without planning permission in 2012, refused retrospective planning permission in 2014,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glandwr_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Glandwr-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      but granted permission in July 2015, having met the requirements of the Welsh government's One Planet Development policy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/That_Roundhouse" title="That Roundhouse">
       That Roundhouse
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Brithdir_Mawr" title="Brithdir Mawr">
       Brithdir Mawr
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Pembrokeshire" title="Newport, Pembrokeshire">
       Newport, Pembrokeshire
      </a>
      ) was granted planning permission in 2008 with a review in 3 years.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">
       Benefits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-impact_development_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Benefits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Substantial research has concluded that LID represents some of the most innovative and
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">
       sustainable development
      </a>
      in the UK.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West_England_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-West_England-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      LIDs have innovated and demonstrated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">
       sustainable
      </a>
      solutions including
      <a href="/wiki/Zero_carbon_housing" title="Zero carbon housing">
       low/zero carbon housing design
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">
       rainwater harvesting
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      generation,
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_minimisation" title="Waste minimisation">
       waste minimisation
      </a>
      and innovative forms of
      <a href="/wiki/Land_management" title="Land management">
       land management
      </a>
      , including
      <a href="/wiki/No-till_farming" title="No-till farming">
       No/low-till farming
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">
       permaculture
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">
       agroforestry
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      LID has also shown a capacity to enhance local
      <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">
       biodiversity
      </a>
      and public access to local space, and to produce traffic movements far below the national average. This has been attributed to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lift-sharing" title="Lift-sharing">
       lift-sharing
      </a>
      , to residents' greater use of public transport, walking and cycling and to the integration of local land based employment with other household activities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Government" title="Welsh Assembly Government">
       Welsh Assembly Government
      </a>
      has noted, such "...Development therefore is not just describing a physical development. It is describing a way of living differently where there is a symbiotic relationship between people and land, making a reduction in environmental impacts possible".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Constraints">
       Constraints
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-impact_development_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Constraints">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Over the years, there have been various struggles with planning authorities over LID in the UK. Tony Wrench spent over a decade fighting the planning authorities until he was granted planning permission for
      <a href="/wiki/That_Roundhouse" title="That Roundhouse">
       That Roundhouse
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As Lisa Lewinsohn points out in her MSc thesis on LID, Tony Wrench and his partner Jane Faith have been "enforced against, fined, refused planning permission several times"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewinsohn_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lewinsohn-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      while
      <a href="/wiki/Lammas_Ecovillage" title="Lammas Ecovillage">
       Lammas
      </a>
      has "probably spent about £50,000 on the application process."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewinsohn_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lewinsohn-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly, since 1986 Tir Penrhos Isaf has tried several times to get planning permission
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planning_History_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Planning_History-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and only succeeded in December 2006, twenty years after their first planning application was submitted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The residents of Tir Penrhos Isaf consider:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       "that current planning and building legislation represent some of the greatest obstacles to developing sustainable systems in Britain. The legislation favours those who already have land and property, actively encourages the squandering of resources and environmental degradation and actively discourages movements towards low impact, sustainable development."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planning_History_36-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Planning_History-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Government_policy">
       Government policy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-impact_development_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Government policy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The extensive research interest in LID, backed up by the practical examples of the existing LIDs, has led to a growing number of planning policies in the UK designed to allow for LIDs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      These include the Dartmoor National Park Authority's emerging plan on low impact dwellings in the countryside,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the Pembrokeshire Low Impact Development Policy (Policy 52)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Government" title="Welsh Assembly Government">
       Welsh Assembly Government
      </a>
      ’s One Planet Development policy (OPD)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        [41]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Future_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Future-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which is supported by the independent One Planet Council.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first development to receive permanent planning permission under the One Planet scheme was Nant-y-Cwm, near
      <a href="/wiki/Caerphilly" title="Caerphilly">
       Caerphilly
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The criteria for OPD in Wales include the requirement that 65% of all subsistence, or 30% of food and 35% of livelihood, come from the land.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
