    Gorlov helical turbine
   </h1>

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Gorlov helical turbine
      </b>
      (GHT) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">
       water turbine
      </a>
      evolved from the
      <a href="/wiki/Darrieus_wind_turbine" title="Darrieus wind turbine">
       Darrieus turbine
      </a>
      design by altering it to have
      <a href="/wiki/Helix" title="Helix">
       helical
      </a>
      blades/foils. It was patented in a series of patents from September 19, 1995
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      to July 3, 2001
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and won 2001
      <a href="/wiki/ASME" title="ASME">
       ASME
      </a>
      Thomas A.
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">
       Edison
      </a>
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.asme.org/about-asme/honors-awards/achievement-awards/thomas-a--edison-patent-award" rel="nofollow">
       Patent Award
      </a>
      . GHT was invented by Professor
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gorlov" title="Alexander Gorlov">
       Alexander M. Gorlov
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_University" title="Northeastern University">
       Northeastern University
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alexander_Gorlov_inside_Helical_Turbine.gif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="309" data-file-width="234" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Alexander_Gorlov_inside_Helical_Turbine.gif/170px-Alexander_Gorlov_inside_Helical_Turbine.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/23/Alexander_Gorlov_inside_Helical_Turbine.gif 1.5x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alexander_Gorlov_inside_Helical_Turbine.gif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A.M.Gorlov with his turbine.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The physical principles of the GHT work
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      are the same as for its main prototype, the Darrieus turbine, and for the family of similar
      <a href="/wiki/Vertical_axis_wind_turbine" title="Vertical axis wind turbine">
       Vertical axis wind turbines
      </a>
      which includes also
      <a href="/wiki/Turby_wind_turbine" title="Turby wind turbine">
       Turby wind turbine
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Quietrevolution_wind_turbine" title="Quietrevolution wind turbine">
       Quietrevolution wind turbine
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_Green_Energy" title="Urban Green Energy">
       Urban Green Energy
      </a>
      . GHT, turby and quietrevolution solved pulsatory
      <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
       torque
      </a>
      issues by using the helical twist of the blades.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fluid_performance">
       Fluid performance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorlov_helical_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fluid performance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term "foil" is used to describe the shape of the blade cross-section at a given point, with no distinction for the type of fluid, (thus referring to either an "
      <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">
       airfoil
      </a>
      " or "
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrofoil" title="Hydrofoil">
       hydrofoil
      </a>
      "). In the helical design, the blades curve around the axis, which has the effect of evenly distributing the foil sections throughout the rotation cycle, so there is always a foil section at every possible
      <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">
       angle of attack
      </a>
      . In this way, the sum of the lift and drag forces on each blade do not change abruptly with rotation angle. The turbine generates a smoother torque curve, so there is much less vibration and noise than in the Darrieus design. It also minimizes peak stresses in the structure and materials, and facilitates self-starting of the turbine. In testing environments the GHT has been observed to have up to 35% efficiency in energy capture reported by several groups.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Among the other vertical-axis turbine systems, the Davis Hydro turbine, the EnCurrent turbine, and the Gorlov Helical turbine have all undergone scale testing at laboratory or sea. Overall, these technologies represent the current norm of tidal current development."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Turbine_axis-orientation">
       Turbine axis-orientation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorlov_helical_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Turbine axis-orientation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The main difference between the Gorlov helical turbine and conventional turbines is the orientation of the axis in relation to current flow. The GHT is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical-axis_wind_turbine" title="Vertical-axis wind turbine">
       vertical-axis turbine
      </a>
      which means the axis is positioned perpendicular to current flow, whereas traditional turbines are
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine#Horizontal_axis" title="Wind turbine">
       horizontal-axis turbines
      </a>
      which means the axis is positioned parallel to the flow of the current. Fluid flows, such as wind, will naturally change direction, however they will still remain parallel to the ground. So in all vertical-axis turbines, the flow remains perpendicular to the axis, regardless of the flow direction, and the turbines always rotate in the same direction. This is one of the main advantages of vertical-axis turbines.
     </p>
     <p>
      If the direction of the water flow is fixed, then the Gorlov turbine axis could be vertical or horizontal, the only requirement is orthogonality to the flow.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GorlovReport1998_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GorlovReport1998-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Airfoil_.2F_hydrofoil">
       Airfoil / hydrofoil
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorlov_helical_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Airfoil / hydrofoil">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The GHT operates under a
      <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">
       lift
      </a>
      -based concept (see
      <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">
       airfoil
      </a>
      ). The foil sections on the GHT are symmetrical, both top-to-bottom and also from the leading-to-trailing edge. The GHT can actually spin equally well in either direction. The GHT works under the same principle as the Darrieus turbine; that is, it relies upon the movement of the foils in order to change the apparent direction of the flow relative to the foils, and thus change the (apparent) "angle of attack" of the foil.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">
       Environmental issues
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorlov_helical_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A GHT is proposed
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GorlovReport1998_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GorlovReport1998-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Low_head_hydro_power" title="Low head hydro power">
       low-head
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Micro_hydro" title="Micro hydro">
       micro hydro
      </a>
      installations, when construction of a
      <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">
       dam
      </a>
      is undesirable. The GHT is an example of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damless_hydro" title="Damless hydro">
       damless hydro
      </a>
      technology. The technology may potentially offer cost and environmental benefits over dam-based micro-hydro systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some advantages of damless hydro are that it eliminates the potential for failure of a dam, which improves public safety. It also eliminates the initial cost of dam engineering, construction and maintenance, reduces the environmental and ecological complications, and potentially simplifies the regulatory issues put into law specifically to mitigate the problems with dams.
     </p>
     <p>
      In general, a major ecological issue with hydropower installations is their actual and perceived risk to aquatic life. It is claimed that a GHT spins slowly enough that fish can see it soon enough to swim around it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      From preliminary tests in 2001, it was claimed that if a fish swims between the slowly moving turbine blades, the fish will not be harmed. Also it would be difficult for a fish to become lodged or stuck in the turbine, because the open spaces between the blades are larger than even the largest fish living in a small river. A fish also would not be tumbled around in a
      <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">
       vortex
      </a>
      , because the GHT does not create a lot of turbulence, so small objects would be harmlessly swept through with the current.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="How_it_works">
       How it works
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorlov_helical_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: How it works">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <table style="float:left">
      <tr>
       <td>
        <div class="thumb tright">

     <div style="clear:both;">
     </div>
     <p>
      In this example the direction of the fluid flow is to the left.
      <br/>
      As the turbine rotates, in this case in a clockwise direction, the motion of the foil through the fluid changes the
      <a href="/wiki/Apparent_wind" title="Apparent wind">
       apparent velocity
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">
       angle of attack
      </a>
      (speed and direction) of the fluid with respect to the
      <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">
       frame of reference
      </a>
      of the foil. The combined effect of these two flow components (i.e. the
      <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">
       vector
      </a>
      sum), yields the net total "Apparent flow velocity" as shown in the next figure.
     </p>

      <tr>

          </a>

          </div>

       </td>

          </a>

          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
       </td>

     <div style="clear:both;">
     </div>
     <p>
      The action of this apparent flow on each foil section generates both a
      <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">
       lift
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">
       drag
      </a>
      force, the sum of which is shown in the figure above titled "Net force vectors". Each of these net force vectors can be split into two orthogonal vectors: a radial component and a tangential component, shown here as "Normal force" and "Axial force" respectively. The normal forces are opposed by the rigidity of the turbine structure and do not impart any rotational force or energy to the turbine. The remaining force component propels the turbine in the clockwise direction, and it is from this
      <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
       torque
      </a>
      that energy can be harvested.
     </p>
     <p>
      [With regards to the figure above left "Apparent flow velocity...", Lucid Energy Technologies, rights holder to the patent to the Gorlov Helical Turbine, notes that this diagram, with no apparent velocity at an azimuth angle of 180 degrees (blade at its point in rotation where it is instantaneously moving in downstream direction), may be subject to misinterpretation. This is because a zero apparently flow velocity could occur only at a tip speed ratio of unity (i.e. TSR=1, where the current flow induced by rotation equals the current flow). The GHT generally operates at a TSR substantially greater than unity.]
     </p>
     <p>
      (The diagrams "Net Force Vectors" and "Normal Force Vectors" are partially incorrect. The downwind segments should show the vectors outside the circles. Otherwise there would be no net sideways loading on the turbine.) M Koester 2015.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">
       Commercial use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorlov_helical_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commercial use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Helical turbines in water stream generate mechanical power independent on direction of the water flow. Then electric generators assembled upon the common shaft transfer the power into electricity for the commercial use.
     </p>

     </p>
