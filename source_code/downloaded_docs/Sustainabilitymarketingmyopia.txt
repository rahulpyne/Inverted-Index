     <p>
      <b>
       Sustainability marketing myopia
      </b>
      is a term used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainability_marketing" title="Sustainability marketing">
       sustainability marketing
      </a>
      referring to a distortion stemming from the overlooking of socio-environmental attributes of a
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_product_development" title="Sustainable product development">
       sustainable product
      </a>
      or service at the expenses of customer benefits and values.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz2010_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz2010-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The idea of sustainability marketing myopia is rooted into conventional
      <a href="/wiki/Marketing_myopia" title="Marketing myopia">
       marketing myopia theory
      </a>
      , as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Green_marketing" title="Green marketing">
       green marketing
      </a>
      myopia.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_myopia">
       Marketing myopia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marketing myopia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The marketing myopia theory was originally proposed in 1960 by American economist
      <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Levitt" title="Theodore Levitt">
       Theodore Levitt
      </a>
      . According to Levitt marketers should not overlook the importance of company potential and product attributes at the expenses of market needs; catering for market needs should receive first priority.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevitt1960_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevitt1960-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A company, besides being technically sound and product oriented, also needs to be customer oriented in order to successfully cater for a market. Knowledge of customer needs and of innovations that can be implemented to maintain customer interest, as well as of how to adapt to the changing business market is crucial.
     </p>
     <p>
      Marketing myopia has been highly influential in the formation of modern marketing theory, and was heeded by marketers to such an extent that some authors now speak of a “new marketing myopia” stemming from too narrow a focus on customer to the exclusion of other stakeholders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith_et_al.2009_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith_et_al.2009-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Green_marketing_myopia">
       Green marketing myopia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Green marketing myopia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Green marketing is the marketing of products that are presumed to be environmentally safe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGMI_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGMI-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In order to be successful, green marketing must fulfill two objectives: improved environmental quality and customer satisfaction. Misjudging either or overemphasizing the former at the expense of the latter can be defined as green marketing myopia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200624_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200624-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The marketing discipline has long argued that innovation must consider an intimate understanding of the customer
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevitt1960_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevitt1960-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a close look at green marketing practices over time reveals that green products must be positioned on a consumer value sought by targeted consumers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200624_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200624-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As such, successful green products are able to appeal to mainstream consumers or lucrative market niches and frequently command price premiums by offering “non-green” consumer value (such as convenience and performance). When consumers are convinced of the desirable “non-green” benefits of environmental products, they are more inclined to adopt them.
     </p>
     <p>
      Aside from offering environmental benefits that do not meet consumer preferences, green marketing myopia can also occur when green products fail to provide credible, substantive environmental benefits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200625_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200625-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Towards_sustainability">
       Towards sustainability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Towards sustainability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability_marketing_myopia">
       Sustainability marketing myopia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sustainability marketing myopia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Sustainability marketing aims at marketing sustainable products and services which “satisfy customer needs and significantly improve the social and environmental performance along the whole life cycle”,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009154_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009154-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      while increasing customer value and achieving the company's objectives. In turns, sustainability marketing myopia is an exaggerated focus on the socio-ecological attributes of the product over the core consumer values, a distortion of the marketing process which is likely to lead to the product failing on the market or remaining confined in a small alternative niche.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009153_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009153-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Just as an excessive focus on product attributes generates marketing myopia, and just as a single-minded focus on customers results in “new marketing myopia”, in both green and sustainability marketing an unbalanced strategy neglecting one aspect (namely, product attributes) is detrimental to the effectiveness of the marketing process.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, it is important to note that sustainability marketing myopia differs from green marketing myopia in that the former follows a broader approach to the marketing myopia issue, taking into account the social attributes of a product, as well as the environmental ones. At the same time, sustainability marketing myopia encompasses sustainable services and product-related services,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009159_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009159-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      not products alone.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Avoiding_sustainability_marketing_myopia">
       Avoiding sustainability marketing myopia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Avoiding sustainability marketing myopia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Generally, sustainability marketing myopia can be avoided in two ways:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       by identifying and stressing the inherent consumer’s values (efficiency and cost-effectiveness, health and safety, convenience, symbolism and status) of the socio-ecological features of the product. In other words, companies should highlight the personal customer benefits stemming from the socio-ecological features of the product;
      </li>
      <li>
       by aligning socio-ecological attributes with core benefits of the product to create “motive alliances”.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009164-167_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009164-167-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Core benefits of the product can comprise functionality, performance, design, durability, taste, freshness, uniqueness, aesthetics, fashion. Motive alliances are the connections of such core benefits with the relevant socio-ecological attributes embedded in the product, an operation also known as
       <a href="/wiki/Product_bundling" title="Product bundling">
        bundling
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200630_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200630-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jacquelyn_Ottman" title="Jacquelyn Ottman">
       Ottman
      </a>
      et al.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200632_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200632-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      provides examples of successful marketing communications based on the highlighting of inherent consumer’s values of the socio-ecological features of products, and on bundling:
     </p>
     <table class="wikitable">
      <tr>
       <th>
        Value
       </th>
       <th>
        Message and business product
       </th>
      </tr>

       </td>
       <td>

         <li>
          “The only thing our washer will shrink is your water bill.” -
          <i>
           ASKO
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>

          <i>
           Tide Coldwater Laundry Detergent
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          "mpg:” -
          <i>
           Toyota Prius
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </td>
      </tr>

       <td>
        Health and safety
       </td>
       <td>
        <ul>
         <li>
          “20 years of refusing to farm with toxic pesticides. Stubborn, perhaps. Healthy, most definitely.” -
          <i>
           Earthbound Farm Organic
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          “Safer for You and the Environment.” -

     <p>
      Besides striking a right balance of focus between product attributes and consumers’ benefits, sustainability marketers should also avoid employing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green-washing" title="Green-washing">
       unsubstantive claims
      </a>
      about the socio-environmental benefits of their products.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200631_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200631-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This particular form of marketing myopia is best avoided by building an image of credibility for the
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_brand" title="Sustainability brand">
       brand
      </a>
      through effective sustainability communication, so that consumers can easily associate their products with sound socio-environmental performances.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Myopia_of_sustainable_services">
       Myopia of sustainable services
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Myopia of sustainable services">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrolux_washing_machines">
       Electrolux washing machines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electrolux washing machines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      According to the authors of Natural Capitalism “product dematerialisation” is one of the prerequisites for a more sustainable business model. In order to become more sustainable the future economy will transform from “sale of goods” to the “sale of services”.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200635_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200635-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Belz and Peattie also stress the role of services as a part of sustainable solution, talking of product-related, use-oriented and result-oriented services. Product-related services are offered additionally and they optimize the product use, e.g. automobile companies offering courses on a more ecological and safe driving manner. In the case of use-oriented services, a service and not a product is sold to the consumer e.g. car-sharing. As for result-oriented services, consumers neither own nor operate a product e. g. public transportation, with consumers only enjoying the outcome of the product, the transportation services.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009159-161_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009159-161-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      “Product dematerialisation” is, too, at risk of incurring in sustainability marketing myopia. In 1999 Electrolux piloted an use-oriented sustainable “pay-per-wash” service in Sweden, failing to succeed because of a myopic marketing approach. New energy and water efficient washing machines were distributed by Electrolux among consumers. Electrolux supplied customers with maintenance services through a central database via the Internet and took care of the disposal and upgrading of the machines. Consumers did not pay for the machines themselves; they were only charged with a small installation fee at the beginning, and through a pay-per-wash charging system (10 Swedish kronor (about 1 $) per wash). However, an excessive emphasis on the green component in the marketing of this service, achieved at the expenses of other consumer benefits and values, failed to convince the consumers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMakower2005_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakower2005-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to A. Ottmann et al. not only environmental but also other inherent consumer values should have been better marketed by Electrolux: convenience of pay-per-wash, including easy trade-ins for upgrades, free service and saved money of a new washing machine. In addition, the sustainable service might have been successful if pay-per-wash convenience would have been bundled with more desirable features.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200635_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttman_et_al.200635-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Myopia of sustainable products">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fairtrade_coffee">
       Fairtrade coffee
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fairtrade coffee">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The initial idea of introducing the
      <a href="/wiki/Fairtrade_certification" title="Fairtrade certification">
       Fairtrade
      </a>
      coffee certification was to abolish poverty among coffee farmers and their workers by supporting better working conditions and enabling growers to compete in the global market.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009155_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009155-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      For this reason, Fairtrade coffee started to be acquired directly from the farmers for a higher price than standard coffee. A minimum price for coffee is guaranteed and if the market prices outreach the minimum, growers receive a premium.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHulm2006_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHulm2006-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In compliance with rising consumers´ demand for environmentally and social beneficial products the Fairtrade certification appears to be an evident indicator. Products marked with the label have to meet standards ensuring that strict social and environmental measures are taken.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009155_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009155-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first generation of Fairtrade coffee suffered from sustainability marketing myopia. First, due to the exaggerated focus on the socio-ecological aspects the quality and the taste of the coffee were put into the background. Most coffee farmers used to sell their coffee to both Fairtrade and open markets. As the price in the open market is entirely defined by quality, farmers sold their better quality beans in that market and then squandered their poorer beans into the Fairtrade market, where they were nonetheless ensured a good price. Furthermore, the certainty that Fairtrade buyers would mix supplies together discouraged farmers to improve the quality of their own beans without having any financial reward for that.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingleton2008_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingleton2008-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Furthermore, Fairtrade coffee failed to guarantee the social and environmental benefits to the consumer as covenant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009164_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009164-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Moreover, Fairtrade has been accused of misleading consumers about its ability to monitor production practices, with particular regards to salary conditions of temporary workers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeitzman2006_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeitzman2006-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first generation Fairtrade coffee with a worse quality and bitter taste failed to satisfy all customers´ needs and supposedly to improve the social performance along the whole life cycle of the product.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Green_buildings">
       Green buildings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_marketing_myopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Green buildings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      As well as Electrolux and Fairtrade coffee suppliers, the producers of green buildings in Western Europe suffered from sustainability marketing myopia in the experimental stages of energy-efficient houses. Companies focussed on the energy-efficient attributes of the houses at the expenses of cost-effectiveness and affordability, marketing their products on the basis of
      <a href="/wiki/Intergenerational_equity" title="Intergenerational equity">
       intergenerational equity
      </a>
      , ecology and energy-savings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2010_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2010-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, further inherent consumer values as comfort and design were also ignored at these early stages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009166_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009166-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nevertheless, since the beginning of the twenty-first century marketers from construction companies have improved their strategies. Successful sustainability marketing concepts have been progressively adopted, including motive alliances and the use of a new marketing mix based on so-called 4 Cs: consumer solution, consumer cost, communication and convenience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie200933_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie200933-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2010_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2010-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This new approach has fostered a rise in the market share of energy-efficient houses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009166_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2009166-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      81Fünf Holzbau is an example of such a company applying successful marketing strategies. It is a fast expanding German company which focuses on energy-efficient wooden houses. Conventional criteria as high-quality levels and comfort are aligned with sustainable components in the company
      <a href="/wiki/Marketing_communications" title="Marketing communications">
       marketing communication
      </a>
      : healthy indoor environment created by using natural woods, energy savings and security from future oil price rises are among the arguments used.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2010_23-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2010-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their interactive online toolkit “
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.energiecomforthaus.de/" rel="nofollow">
       Energy Comfort House
      </a>
      ” allows to easily calculate the life cycle cost and savings compared to conventional houses and acts, therefore, as a credibility tool.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2010_23-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelz.2C_Pettie2010-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In order to offer the broadest choice in terms of consumer solutions, different house models are provided to various customer target groups according to their environmental consciousness and affordability.
     </p>
