     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Public Utility Regulatory Policies Act
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       PURPA
      </b>
      ,
      <a class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/pl-95-617" rel="nofollow">
       Pub.L. 95â617
      </a>
      , 92
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large" title="United States Statutes at Large">
       Stat.
      </a>
      <a class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/stat-92-3117" rel="nofollow">
       3117
      </a>
      , enacted NovemberÂ 9, 1978) is a
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Congress" title="Act of Congress">
       Act
      </a>
      passed as part of the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Act" title="National Energy Act">
       National Energy Act
      </a>
      . It was meant to promote energy conservation (reduce demand) and promote greater use of domestic
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       energy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      (increase supply). The law was created in response to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1973_energy_crisis" title="1973 energy crisis">
       1973 energy crisis
      </a>
      , and one year in advance of a
      <a href="/wiki/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">
       second energy crisis
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Upon entering the White House, President Jimmy Carter made energy policy a top priority. The law started the energy industry on the road to restructuring.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Law">
       Law
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Law">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Public Utility Regulatory Policies Act of 1978 (PURPA) encouraged
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       creating a market for power from non-utility power producers
      </li>
      <li>
       increased efficiency by making use of cogeneration
      </li>
      <li>
       ending promotional rate structures
      </li>
      <li>
       encouraging the development of hydroelectric power
      </li>
      <li>
       the conservation of electric energy and natural gas
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-utility_power_producers">
       Non-utility power producers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Non-utility power producers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Energy companies were classified as
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_monopoly" title="Natural monopoly">
       natural monopolies
      </a>
      , and for this reason, most were established with vertically integrated structures (that is, they undertook all the functions of generating, transmitting, and distributing electricity to the customer). Utilities became protected as regulated monopolies because it was thought that a company could produce power more efficiently and economically as one company than as several.
     </p>
     <p>
      PURPA started the industry on the road to restructuring and is one of the first laws that began the
      <a href="/wiki/NERC_Tag#Electric_Deregulation_in_North_America" title="NERC Tag">
       deregulation
      </a>
      of energy companies. The provision which enabled non-utility generators ("NUGs") to produce power for use by customers attached to a utility's grid broke the previous monopoly in the generation function.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smithpow_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-smithpow-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ending_Promotional_Rate_Structure">
       Ending Promotional Rate Structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ending Promotional Rate Structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Utilities offered customers a "rate structure" that decreased the cost per kWh price of electricity with increasing usage, with subsequent increments costing less per unit. PURPA eliminated promotional rate structures except when they could be justified by the cost structure of utility companies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smithpow_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-smithpow-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cogeneration">
       Cogeneration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cogeneration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      One provision of PURPA is the requirement for increased use of energy
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       cogeneration
      </a>
      . The law forced
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">
       electric utilities
      </a>
      to buy power from other more efficient producers, such as cogeneration plants, if that cost was less than the utility's own "avoided cost" rate to the consumer; the avoided cost rate was the additional costs that the electric utility would incur if it generated the required power itself, or if available, could purchase its demand requirements from another source. At the time generally, where demand was growing, this "avoided cost" was considered to be the construction and
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuel
      </a>
      costs incurred in the operation of another
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_power_plant" title="Thermal power plant">
       thermal power plant
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As an effect, the number of cogeneration plants, which produce electric power and steam, increased. These plants are encouraged by the law, on the basis that they harness thermal energy (in the form of usable steam) that would be otherwise wasted if electricity alone was produced. PURPA also became the basic legislation that enabled renewable energy providers to gain a toehold in the market, particularly in California, where state authorities were more aggressive in their interpretation of the statute. The portion of the act dealing with cogeneration and small power production appears in US code in
      <a class="external text" href="http://uscode.house.gov/download/pls/16C12.txt" rel="nofollow">
       Title 16 â Conservation
      </a>
      , Chapter 12 â Federal Regulation and Development of Power, Subchapter II â Regulation of Electric Utility Companies Engaged in Interstate Commerce, Sec 824a-3 â Cogeneration and Small Power Production.
     </p>
     <p>
      This led to the establishment of a new class of generating facilities, which would receive special rate and regulatory treatment. Generating facilities in this group are known as
      <i>
       <b>
        qualifying facilities (QFs)
       </b>
      </i>
      , and fall into two categories: qualifying small power production facilities and qualifying cogeneration facilities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-test-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_Energy_and_Hydropower">
       Renewable Energy and Hydropower
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Renewable Energy and Hydropower">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      PURPA provided favorable terms to companies that produced electricity from renewable (non-fossil-fuel) resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smithpow_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-smithpow-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">
       Implementation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Implementation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although a Federal law, PURPA's implementation was left to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States_Rights" title="States Rights">
       individual states
      </a>
      , because needs varied; a variety of regulatory regimes developed in states where renewable power resources were needed, available for development, or the generated power could be
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
       transmitted
      </a>
      . Little was done in many states where such resources were unavailable, where the demand growth was slower or previously accommodated in planning.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      PURPA is becoming less important, as many of the contracts made under it during the 1980s are expiring.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Another reason for PURPA's reduced significance is that
      <a href="/wiki/NERC_Tag#Electric_Deregulation_in_North_America" title="NERC Tag">
       electric deregulation
      </a>
      and open access to electricity transportation by utilities has created a vast market for the purchase of energy and State regulatory agencies have therefore stopped forcing utilities to give contracts to developers of non-utility power projects. However, it is still an important piece of legislation promoting renewable energy because it exempts the developers of such projects from numerous State and Federal regulatory regimes.
     </p>
     <p>
      This
      <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">
       free market
      </a>
      approach presented investment opportunity and government encouragement for more development of environment-friendly, renewable energy projects and technologies; the law created a market in which non-utility
      <a href="/wiki/Independent_Power_Producer" title="Independent Power Producer">
       Independent Power Producers
      </a>
      developed, and some energy market players
      <a href="/wiki/Enron_scandal" title="Enron scandal">
       failed
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Critics of PURPA cited that power producers signed multi-year cost of electricity contracts at a time when energy prices were high. When oil prices went down, utilities had to honor the rates of those contracts, leading to high power prices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conc_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-conc-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      PURPA was the only existing federal law that requires competition in the utility industry and the only law that encourages renewables, if it is cost competitive with conventional polluting resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conc_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-conc-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Amendment_proposals_and_new_legislation">
       Amendment proposals and new legislation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Utility_Regulatory_Policies_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Amendment proposals and new legislation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In February 2005, Senator
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jeffords" title="Jim Jeffords">
       Jim Jeffords
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">
       Vermont
      </a>
      introduced an
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.govtrack.us/congress/bill.xpd?bill=s109-427" rel="nofollow">
       amendment
      </a>
      to PURPA calling for a
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_portfolio_standard" title="Renewable portfolio standard">
       Renewable portfolio standard
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      PURPA was amended in 2005 by the
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_2005" title="Energy Policy Act of 2005">
       Energy Policy Act of 2005
      </a>
      by sections 1251 through 1254. There is pending legislation
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2009)">
          when?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      in the US Senate that would amend PURPA to require FERC to develop standards for interconnection of
      <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">
       distributed generation
      </a>
      facilities, and that would require âelectric utilitiesâ meeting the PURPA size requirement (retail sales of more than 500 million kw hrs) to implement those standards.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One proposed law that would amend PURPA is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydropower_Regulatory_Efficiency_Act_of_2013_(H.R._267;_113th_Congress)" title="Hydropower Regulatory Efficiency Act of 2013 (H.R. 267; 113th Congress)">
       Hydropower Regulatory Efficiency Act of 2013 (H.R. 267)
      </a>
      . The bill was introduced into the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">
       United States House of Representatives
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/113th_United_States_Congress" title="113th United States Congress">
       113th United States Congress
      </a>
      on January 15, 2013, and it passed the House on February 13, 2013 by a vote of 422-0. If enacted, the bill would change some of the regulations in the United States surrounding
      <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">
       hydropower
      </a>
      by making it easier for smaller hydropower stations to be created. According to the bill's proponents, current regulations are unwieldy and represent a significant hurdle to creating more hydropower plants. H.R. 267 would alter those regulations to make it easier for smaller plants to get approval quickly. Section 3 of H.R. 267 amends the Public Utility Regulatory Policies Act of 1978 (PURPA) to increase from 5,000 to 10,000
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt#Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">
       kilowatts
      </a>
      the size of small
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectric power
      </a>
      projects which the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Regulatory_Commission" title="Federal Energy Regulatory Commission">
       Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC)
      </a>
      may exempt from its license requirements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-267text_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-267text-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      See related energy policy contained in
      <a class="external text" href="http://uscode.house.gov/download/pls/42C134.txt" rel="nofollow">
       42 USC Chapter 134
      </a>
      â Energy Policy.
     </p>
