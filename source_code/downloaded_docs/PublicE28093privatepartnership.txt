   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Public–private partnership
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       public–private partnership
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       PPP
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       3P
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       P3
      </b>
      ) is a cooperative arrangement between one or more public and private sector actors, typically of a long term nature.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Governments have used such a mix of public and private endeavors throughout history.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the past few decades has seen a clear trend towards governments across the globe making greater use of various PPP arrangements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is no consensus about how to define a PPP.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      PPPs can be understood of both as a governance mechanism and a language game.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      When understood as a language game, or brand, the PPP phrase can cover hundreds of different types of long term contracts with a wide range of risk allocations, funding arrangements and transparency requirements. And as a brand, the PPP concept is also closely related to concepts such as privatization and the contracting out of government services.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      When understood as a governance mechanism the PPP concept encompasses at least five families of potential arrangements, one of which is the long term infrastructure contract in the model of the UK's Private Finance Initiative (PFI).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Particular types of arrangements have been favored in different countries at different times.
     </p>
     <p>
      Infrastructure PPPs as a phenomenon can be understood at five different levels: as a particular project or activity, as a form of project delivery, as a statement of government policy, as a tool of government or as a wider cultural phenomena.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Different disciplines commonly emphasize different aspects of the PPP phenomena.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The engineering and economics professions primarily take a utilitarian, functional focus emphasising concerns such as project delivery and relative value- for-money (VfM) compared to the traditional ways of delivering large infrastructure projects. In contrast, public administrators and political scientists tend to view PPPs more as a policy brand, and as a useful tool for governments to achieve their objectives.
     </p>
     <p>
      Common themes of PPPs are the sharing of risk and the development of innovative, long term relationships between the public and private sectors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The use of private finance is another key dimension of many PPPs, particularly those influenced by the UK PFI model, although this has aspect has waned since the global financial crisis of 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The PPP phenomenon has been controversial. The lack of a shared understanding of what a PPP is makes the process of evaluating whether PPPs have been successful complex.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Evidence of PPP performance in terms of VfM and efficiency, for example, is mixed and often unavailable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <p>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Long_term_infrastructure_contracts">
       Long term infrastructure contracts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Long term infrastructure contracts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      PPPs often involve a contract between a
      <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">
       public sector
      </a>
      authority and a private party, in which the private party provides a public service or project and assumes substantial financial, technical and operational risk in the project. In some types of PPP, the cost of using the service is borne exclusively by the users of the service and not by the taxpayer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In other types (notably the PFI), capital investment is made by the private sector on the basis of a contract with government to provide agreed services and the cost of providing the service is borne wholly or in part by the government. Government contributions to a PPP may also be in kind (notably the transfer of existing assets). In projects that are aimed at creating
      <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">
       public goods
      </a>
      like in the
      <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">
       infrastructure
      </a>
      sector, the government may provide a capital subsidy in the form of a one-time
      <a href="/wiki/Grant_(money)" title="Grant (money)">
       grant
      </a>
      , so as to make the project economically viable. In some other cases, the government may support the project by providing revenue subsidies, including
      <a href="/wiki/Tax_break" title="Tax break">
       tax breaks
      </a>
      or by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annuity_(finance_theory)" title="Annuity (finance theory)">
       guaranteed annual revenues
      </a>
      for a fixed time period. In all cases, the partnerships include a transfer of significant risks to the private sector, generally in an integrated and holistic way, minimizing interfaces for the public entity. An optimal risk allocation is the main value generator for this model of delivering public service.
     </p>
     <p>
      There many drivers for PPPs. One common driver involves the claim that PPPs enable the public sector to harness the expertise and efficiencies that the private sector can bring to the delivery of certain facilities and services traditionally procured and delivered by the public sector. Another common driver is that PPPs may be structured so that the public sector body seeking to make a capital investment does not incur any borrowing. Rather, the PPP borrowing is incurred by the private sector vehicle implementing the project. On PPP projects where the cost of using the service is intended to be borne exclusively by the end user, the PPP is, from the public sector's perspective, an "off-balance sheet" method of financing the delivery of new or refurbished public sector assets. On PPP projects where the public sector intends to compensate the private sector through availability payments once the facility is established or renewed, the financing is, from the public sector's perspective, "on-balance sheet"; however, the public sector will regularly benefit from significantly deferred cash flows. Generally, financing costs will be higher for a PPP than for a traditional public financing, because of the private sector higher cost of capital. However, extra financing costs can be offset by private sector efficiency, savings resulting from a holistic approach to delivering the project or service, and from the better risk allocation in the long run.
     </p>
     <p>
      Typically, a private sector consortium forms a special company called a "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_purpose_vehicle" title="Special purpose vehicle">
       special purpose vehicle
      </a>
      " (SPV) to develop, build, maintain and operate the asset for the contracted period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In cases where the government has invested in the project, it is typically (but not always) allotted an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ownership_equity" title="Ownership equity">
       equity
      </a>
      share in the SPV.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The consortium is usually made up of a building contractor, a maintenance company and equity investor(s). It is the SPV that signs the contract with the government and with subcontractors to build the facility and then maintain it. In the
      <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">
       infrastructure
      </a>
      sector, complex arrangements and contracts that guarantee and secure the
      <a href="/wiki/Cash_flow" title="Cash flow">
       cash flows
      </a>
      make PPP projects prime candidates for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Finance" title="Project Finance">
       project financing
      </a>
      . A typical PPP example would be a hospital building financed and constructed by a private developer and then leased to the hospital authority. The private developer then acts as landlord, providing housekeeping and other non-medical services while the hospital itself provides medical services.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
       Origins
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Pressure to change the standard model of public procurement arose initially from concerns about the level of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_debt" title="Public debt">
       public debt
      </a>
      , which grew rapidly during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" title="Macroeconomic">
       macroeconomic
      </a>
      dislocation of the 1970s and 1980s. Governments sought to encourage private
      <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">
       investment
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">
       infrastructure
      </a>
      , initially on the basis of
      <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">
       accounting
      </a>
      fallacies arising from the fact that public accounts did not distinguish between recurrent and capital expenditures.
     </p>
     <p>
      The idea that private provision of infrastructure represented a way of providing infrastructure at no cost to the public has now been generally abandoned; however, interest in alternatives to the standard model of public procurement persisted. In particular, it has been argued that models involving an enhanced role for the private sector, with a single private-sector organization taking responsibility for most aspects of service provisions for a given project, could yield an improved allocation of
      <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">
       risk
      </a>
      , while maintaining public
      <a href="/wiki/Accountability" title="Accountability">
       accountability
      </a>
      for essential aspects of service provision.
     </p>
     <p>
      Initially, most public–private partnerships were negotiated individually, as one-off deals, and much of this activity began in the early 1990s in the UK.
     </p>
     <p>
      PPPs are organized along a continuum between public and private nodes and needs as they integrate normative, albeit separate and distinct, functions of society—the market and the commons. A common challenge for PPPs is allowing for these fluctuations and reinforcing the intended partnership without diminishing either sector. Multisectoral, or collaborative, partnering is experienced on a continuum of private to public in varying degrees of implementation according to the need, time restraints, and the issue at hand. Even though these partnerships are now common, it is normal for both private and public sectors to be critical of the other's approach and methods. It is at the merger of these sectors that we see how a unified partnership has immediate impact in the development of communities and the provision of public services.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_and_decline">
       Growth and decline
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Growth and decline">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      From 1990 to 2009 nearly 1,400 PPP deals were signed in the European Union, representing a capital value of approximately €260 billion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since the onset of the financial crisis in 2008, estimates suggest that the number of PPP deals closed has fallen more than 40 percent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barlow.2C_J._Roehrich_2010_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Barlow.2C_J._Roehrich_2010-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Investments in public sector infrastructure are seen as an important means of maintaining economic activity, as was highlighted in a European Commission communication on PPPs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result of the significant role that PPPs have adopted in the development of public sector infrastructure, in addition to the complexity of such transactions, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_PPP_Expertise_Centre_(EPEC)" title="European PPP Expertise Centre (EPEC)">
       European PPP Expertise Centre (EPEC)
      </a>
      was established to support public-sector capacity to implement PPPs and share timely solutions to problems common across Europe in PPPs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        [41]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      PPPs provide a unique perspective on the collaborative and network aspects of public management. The advancement of PPPs, as a concept and a practice, is a product of the
      <a href="/wiki/New_public_management" title="New public management">
       new public management
      </a>
      of the late 20th century and globalization pressures. The term "public–private partnership" is prey to thinking in parts rather than the whole of the partnership, which makes it difficult to pin down a universally accepted definition of PPPs.
     </p>
     <p>
      U.S. city managers' motivations for exploring public–private service delivery vary. According to a 2007 survey, two primary reasons were expressed: cost reduction (86.7%) and external fiscal pressures, including tax restrictions (50.3%). No other motivations expressed exceeded 16%. In the 2012 survey, however, interest had shifted to the need for better processes (69%), relationship building (77%), better outcomes (81%), leveraging resources (84%), and belief that collaborative service delivery is "the right thing to do" (86%). Among those surveyed, the provision of public services through contracts with private firms peaked in 1977 at 18% and has declined since. The most common form of shared service delivery now involves contracts between governments, growing from 17% in 2002 to 20% in 2007. "At the same time, approximately 22% of the local governments in the survey indicated that they had brought back in-house at least one service that they had previously provided through some alternative private arrangement."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JR-study_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JR-study-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">
       Controversy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Controversy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A common problem with PPP projects is that private investors obtained a
      <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_return" title="Rate of return">
       rate of return
      </a>
      that was higher than the government's
      <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">
       bond rate
      </a>
      , even though most or all of the income risk associated with the project was borne by the public sector.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barlow.2C_J._Roehrich_2010_39-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Barlow.2C_J._Roehrich_2010-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2008 report by PriceWaterhouseCoopers argued that the comparison between public and private borrowing rates is not fair, because there are "constraints on public borrowing", which may imply that public borrowing is too high, and so PFI projects can be beneficial by not putting debt directly on government books.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A number of Australian studies of early initiatives to promote private investment in infrastructure concluded that, in most cases, the schemes being proposed were inferior to the standard model of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_procurement" title="Public procurement">
       public procurement
      </a>
      based on competitively tendered construction of publicly owned assets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2009, the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Treasury" title="New Zealand Treasury">
       New Zealand Treasury
      </a>
      , in response to inquiries by the new
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party" title="New Zealand National Party">
       National Party
      </a>
      government, released a report on PPP schemes that concluded that "there is little reliable empirical evidence about the costs and benefits of PPPs" and that there "are other ways of obtaining private sector finance", as well as that "the advantages of PPPs must be weighed against the contractual complexities and rigidities they entail".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MISGUIDED_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MISGUIDED-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One response to these negative findings was the development of formal procedures for the assessment of PPPs in which the focus was on "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_for_money" title="Value for money">
       value for money
      </a>
      " rather than
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debt_reduction" title="Debt reduction">
       reductions in debt
      </a>
      . The underlying framework was one in which value for money was achieved by an appropriate allocation of risk. These assessment procedures were incorporated in the private finance initiative and its Australian counterparts from the late 1990s onwards.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Another model being discussed is the public–private community partnership (PPCP), in which both the government and private players work together for social welfare, eliminating the prime focus of private players on profit.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      This model is being applied more in developing nations such as India.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Privatisation_of_water">
       Privatisation of water
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Privatisation of water">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After a wave of
      <a href="/wiki/Water_privatization" title="Water privatization">
       privatisation of many water services
      </a>
      in the 1990s, mostly in developing countries, experiences show that global
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_corporation" title="Water corporation">
       water corporations
      </a>
      have not brought the promised improvements in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_water_supply" title="Public water supply">
       public water
      </a>
      utilities. Instead of lower prices, large volumes of investment and improvements in the connection of the poor to water and sanitation,
      <a href="/wiki/Water_tariff" title="Water tariff">
       water tariffs
      </a>
      have increased out of reach of poor households. Water multinationals are withdrawing from developing countries and the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      is reluctant to provide support.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        [46]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The privatisation of the water services of the city of Paris was proven to be unwanted and at the end of 2009 the city did not renew its contract with two of the French water corporations,
      <a href="/wiki/Suez_(company)" title="Suez (company)">
       Suez
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Veolia_Water" title="Veolia Water">
       Veolia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        [47]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
      After one year of being controlled by the public, it is projected that the water tariff will be cut by between 5% and 10%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Contract management is a crucial factor in shared service delivery, and services that are more challenging to monitor or fully capture in contractual language often remain in municipal control. In the 2007 survey of U.S. city managers, the most difficult was judged to be the operation and management of hospitals, and the least difficult the cleaning of streets and parking lots. The study revealed that communities often fail to sufficiently monitor collaborative agreements or other forms of service delivery: "For instance, in 2002, only 47.3% of managers involved with private firms as delivery partners reported that they evaluate that service delivery. By 2007, that was down to 45.4%.
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_monitoring&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Performance monitoring (page does not exist)">
       Performance monitoring
      </a>
      is a general concern from these surveys and in the scholarly criticisms of these arrangements."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JR-study_42-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-JR-study-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JR-post_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JR-post-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Health_services">
       Health services
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Health services">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_service" title="Health service">
       health services
      </a>
      PPP can be described as a long-term contract (typically 15–30 years) between a public-sector authority and one or more private sector companies operating as a legal entity. The government provides the strength of its purchasing power, outlines goals for an optimal health system, and empowers private enterprise to innovate, build, maintain and/or manage delivery of agreed-upon services over the term of the contract. The private sector receives payment for its services and assumes substantial financial, technical and operational risk while benefitting from the upside potential of shared cost savings.
     </p>
     <p>
      The private entity is made up of any combination of participants who have a vested interested in working together to provide core competencies in operations, technology, funding and technical expertise. The opportunity for multi-sector market participants includes hospital providers and physician groups, technology companies,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_company" title="Pharmaceutical company">
       pharmaceutical
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Medical_device" title="Medical device">
       medical device
      </a>
      companies,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Private_health_insurance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Private health insurance (page does not exist)">
       private health insurers
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facilities_manager" title="Facilities manager">
       facilities managers
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Construction_firm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Construction firm (page does not exist)">
       construction firms
      </a>
      . Funding sources could include banks,
      <a href="/wiki/Private_equity_firm" title="Private equity firm">
       private equity firms
      </a>
      , philanthropists and
      <a href="/wiki/Pension_fund" title="Pension fund">
       pension fund
      </a>
      managers.
     </p>
     <p>
      For more than two decades public–private partnerships have been used to finance
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Health_infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Health infrastructure (page does not exist)">
       health infrastructure
      </a>
      . Governments are increasingly looking to the PPP-model to solve larger problems in healthcare delivery. There is not a country in the world where healthcare is financed entirely by the government.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      While the provision of health is widely recognized as the responsibility of government, private capital and expertise are increasingly viewed as sources to induce
      <a href="/wiki/Efficiency" title="Efficiency">
       efficiency
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">
       innovation
      </a>
      . As PPPs move from financing infrastructure to managing care delivery, there is an opportunity to
      <a href="/wiki/Cost_reduction" title="Cost reduction">
       reduce overall
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Healthcare_costs" title="Healthcare costs">
       cost of healthcare
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The larger scope of health PPPs to manage and finance care delivery and infrastructure means a larger potential market for private organizations. PricewaterhouseCoopers projects that spending on healthcare among the
      <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">
       Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development
      </a>
      (OECD) and
      <a href="/wiki/BRIC" title="BRIC">
       BRIC
      </a>
      nations of Brazil, Russia, India and China will grow by 51 percent between 2010 and 2020, amounting to a cumulative total of more than $71 trillion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pwc.com_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pwc.com-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Of this, $3.6 trillion is projected to be spent on health infrastructure and $68.1 trillion will be spent on non-infrastructure health spending cumulatively over the next decade. Annually, spending on health infrastructure among the OECD and BRIC nations is projected to increase to $397 billion by 2020, up from $263 billion in 2010. The larger market for health PPPs is projected to be in non-infrastructure spending, estimated to be more than $7.5 trillion annually, up from $5 trillion in 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pwc.com_51-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-pwc.com-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Health spending in the United States accounts for approximately half of all health spending among OECD nations, but the biggest growth will be outside of the U.S. According to PwC projections, the countries that are expected to have the highest health spending growth between 2010 and 2020 are China, where health spending is expected to increase by 166 percent, and India, which will see a 140 percent increase. As health spending increases it is putting pressure on governments and spurring them to look for private capital and expertise.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pwc.com_51-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-pwc.com-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Product_development_partnerships">
       Product development partnerships
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Product development partnerships">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Product development partnerships (PDPs) are a class of public–private partnerships that focus on pharmaceutical product development for diseases of the developing world. These include preventive medicines such as vaccines and microbicides, as well as treatments for otherwise neglected diseases. PDPs were first created in the 1990s to unite the public sector's commitment to international public goods for health with industry's intellectual property, expertise in product development, and marketing.
     </p>
     <p>
      International PDPs work to accelerate research and development of pharmaceutical products for underserved populations that are not profitable for private companies. They may also be involved in helping plan for access and availability of the products they develop to those in need in their target populations. Publicly financed, with intellectual property rights granted by pharmaceutical industry partners for specific markets, PDPs are able to focus on their missions rather than concerns about recouping development costs through the profitability of the products being developed. These not-for-profit organizations bridge public- and private-sector interests, with a view toward resolving the specific incentive and financial barriers to increased industry involvement in the development of safe and effective pharmaceutical products.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Financing">
       Financing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Financing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A key motivation for governments considering public-private partnerships is the possibility of bringing in new sources of financing for funding public infrastructure and service needs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barlow.2C_J._Roehrich_2010_39-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Barlow.2C_J._Roehrich_2010-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is important to understand the main mechanisms for infrastructure projects, the principal investors in developing countries, sources of finance (limited recourse, debt, equity, etc.), the typical project finance structure, and key issues arising from developing project financed transactions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        [54]
       </a>
      </sup>
      An interesting aspect, quite ubiquitous in project finance, is the dual nature of the parties involved, being both service providers to and contracted with the newly created entity (
      <a href="/wiki/Special_purpose_entity" title="Special purpose entity">
       Special purpose entity
      </a>
      ), and shareholders of that entity. For example, it is quite common for the construction contractor or operations and maintenance service provider to hold an equity stake in a project finance's capital structure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        [55]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some governments utilize a
      <a href="/wiki/Public_sector_comparator" title="Public sector comparator">
       public sector comparator
      </a>
      for assessing the financial benefit of a public-private partnership and the optimal level of private sector involvement.
     </p>
     <p>
      A number of key risks need to be taken into consideration as well. These risks will need to be allocated and managed to ensure the successful financing of the project. The party that is best placed to manage these risks in a cost-effective way may not necessarily always be the private sector. However, there are a number of mechanisms products available in the market for project sponsors, lenders and governments to mitigate some of the project risks, such as: Hedging and futures contracts; insurance; and risk mitigation products provided by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_finance_institutions" title="International finance institutions">
       international finance institutions
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        [56]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Centralised_units">
       Centralised units
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Centralised units">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      (2007)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        [57]
       </a>
      </sup>
      states that governments tend to create Centralised PPP Units as a response to weaknesses in the central government's ability to effectively manage PPP programmes. Different governments suffer from different institutional failures in the PPP procurement process, hence these Centralised PPP units need to address these different issues by shaping their functions to suit the individual government needs. The function, location (within government) and jurisdiction (i.e. who controls it) of dedicated PPP Units may differ amongst countries, but generally these include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Policy_advocacy" title="Policy advocacy">
        Policy guidance
       </a>
       and advice on the content of national legislation. Guidance also includes defining which sectors are eligible for PPPs as well as which PPP methods and schemes can be carried out.
      </li>
      <li>
       Approving or rejecting proposed PPP projects i.e. playing a gatekeeper role that can occur at any stage of the process i.e. at the initial planning stage or at the final approval stage.
      </li>
      <li>
       Providing technical support to government organisations at the project identification, evaluation,
       <a href="/wiki/Procurement" title="Procurement">
        procurement
       </a>
       or contract management phase.
      </li>
      <li>
       Capacity building i.e. training of public sector officials that are involved in PPP programmes or are interested in the PPP process.
      </li>
      <li>
       Promote PPPs within the private sector i.e. PPP
       <a href="/wiki/Market_development" title="Market development">
        market development
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A 2013 review
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Literature_Review:_Evaluating_the_Costs_and_Benefits_of_Centralised_PPP_Units_58-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Literature_Review:_Evaluating_the_Costs_and_Benefits_of_Centralised_PPP_Units-58">
        [58]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which targeted research based on the value of centralised PPP Units (and does not look at the value of PPPs in general or any other type of PPP arrangement as the review was aimed at providing evidence needed to decide whether or not to set up a Centralised PPP Unit) found-
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       No quantitative evidence: There is very little quantitative evidence of the value of centralised PPP coordination units against ministries or government agencies individually procuring PPP projects. Most of the studies available on PPP Units tend to focus on their role and only carry out brief descriptive analyses of their value.
      </li>
      <li>
       Limited Authority: The majority of the PPP Units reviewed in the literature do not play a particularly important role in approving or rejecting PPP programmes or projects. Whilst their advice is used in the decision making process by other government bodies, the majority do not actually have any executive power to make such decisions themselves. Hence, when they have more authority their value is seen to be higher.
      </li>
      <li>
       PPP Units differ by country and sector: Government failures, in regards to PPP units, vary by government. The requirements for PPPs also vary by country and sector and so do the risks involved (i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Financial_risk" title="Financial risk">
        financial
       </a>
       , social etc.) for the country government. Hence, PPP Units need to be tailored to solve these failures, properly assess risks and be located in the correct government departments where it can command the most power. PPP Units can play a number of important roles in the PPP process; however, not all PPP Units will play the same role as their functions have been tailored to individual country needs. In some cases, limits to their authority have curtailed their effectiveness.
      </li>
      <li>
       Implicit value: The lack of rigorous evidence does not prove that PPP Units are not an important contributor to the success of a country's PPP programme. The literature review does show that whilst there is no quantitative data, there are widespread perceptions on the importance of a well-functioning PPP unit for the success of a country's PPP programme.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The author of the 2013 review
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Literature_Review:_Evaluating_the_Costs_and_Benefits_of_Centralised_PPP_Units_58-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Literature_Review:_Evaluating_the_Costs_and_Benefits_of_Centralised_PPP_Units-58">
        [58]
       </a>
      </sup>
      found no literature that rigorously evaluates the usefulness of PPP Units. The literature does show that PPP Units should be individually tailored to different government functions, address different government failures and be appropriately positioned to support the country's PPP Programme. Where these conditions seem to have been met, there is consensus that PPP Units have played a positive role in national PPP Programmes.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_cases">
       Specific cases
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Specific cases">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While some PPP projects have proceeded smoothly, others have been highly controversial. Australian examples include the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airport_railway_line,_Sydney" title="Airport railway line, Sydney">
       Airport Link
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Cross_City_Tunnel" title="Cross City Tunnel">
       Cross City Tunnel
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        [59]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Harbour_Tunnel" title="Sydney Harbour Tunnel">
       Sydney Harbour Tunnel
      </a>
      , all in
      <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">
       Sydney
      </a>
      ; the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Cross_Station" title="Southern Cross Station">
       Southern Cross Station
      </a>
      redevelopment in
      <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">
       Melbourne
      </a>
      ; and the
      <a href="/wiki/Robina,_Queensland" title="Robina, Queensland">
       Robina
      </a>
      hospital in
      <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">
       Queensland
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      , public–private partnerships have been extremely successful in developing infrastructure, particularly road assets under the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Highways_Authority_of_India" title="National Highways Authority of India">
       National Highways Authority of India
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Midday_Meal_Scheme" title="Midday Meal Scheme">
       Midday Meal Scheme
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Akshaya_Patra_Foundation" title="Akshaya Patra Foundation">
       Akshaya Patra Foundation
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      , public–private partnerships have become significant in both social and infrastructure development. PPP Canada Inc. was created as a Crown corporation with an independent Board of Directors reporting through the Minister of Finance to Parliament. Its mandate is to improve the delivery of public infrastructure by achieving better value, timeliness and accountability to taxpayers, through P3s. The Corporation became operational in February 2009 with the appointments of a chair of the board of directors and a chief executive officer.
     </p>
     <p>
      PPPs exist in a variety of forms in
      <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">
       British Columbia
      </a>
      through the focused efforts of Partnerships BC, a company registered under the Business Corporations Act, that is wholly owned by the Province of British Columbia and reports to its shareholder the Minister of Finance. Projects include the
      <a href="/wiki/Canada_Line" title="Canada Line">
       Canada Line
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">
       rapid transit
      </a>
      line, the
      <a href="/wiki/Abbotsford,_British_Columbia" title="Abbotsford, British Columbia">
       Abbotsford
      </a>
      Hospital and Cancer Centre and the Sea-to-Sky Highway project.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        [60]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
       Quebec
      </a>
      , a number of notable PPPs include the
      <a href="/wiki/McGill_University_Health_Centre" title="McGill University Health Centre">
       McGill University Health Centre
      </a>
      , the new western extension of
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Autoroute_30" title="Quebec Autoroute 30">
       Autoroute 30
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Université de Montréal">
       Université de Montréal
      </a>
      's Hospital Research Center.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the UK, two-thirds of the
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_London_Underground#Public-Private_Partnership" title="History of the London Underground">
       London Underground PPP
      </a>
      was taken back into public control in July 2007 after only four and a half years at an estimated cost of £2 billion and the remaining one-third was taken back into public control in May 2010 after seven and a half years for a purchase price of £310m.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        [61]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Government had paid advisers £180m for structuring, negotiating and implementing the PPP and had reimbursed £275m of bid costs to the winning bidders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 30-year PPP contract for the refurbishment of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)#Property_portfolio" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">
       MOD Main Building
      </a>
      in London was estimated to give a saving of only £100,000 as compared to the £746.2m cost of public procurement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The refinancing of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altcourse_(HM_Prison)" title="Altcourse (HM Prison)">
       Fazakerley Prison
      </a>
      PFI contract following the completion of construction delivered an 81% gain to the private sector operator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        [64]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Air_Traffic_Services#Ownership_structure_and_management" title="National Air Traffic Services">
       NATS PPP
      </a>
      saw 51% of the UK's air traffic control service transferred to the private sector; however, following the decline in air traffic after the
      <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">
       September 11 attacks
      </a>
      , the Government and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BAA_Limited" title="BAA Limited">
       BAA Limited
      </a>
      each invested £65m in the private sector operator in 2003.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        [65]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Newfoundland
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gillespie_Reid" title="Robert Gillespie Reid">
       Robert Gillespie Reid
      </a>
      contracted to operate the railways for fifty years from 1898, though originally they were to become his property at the end of the period.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Studies">
       Studies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Studies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A 2013 study published in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/State_and_Local_Government_Review" title="State and Local Government Review">
        State and Local Government Review
       </a>
      </i>
      found that definitions of public–private partnerships vary widely between municipalities: "Many public and private officials tout public-private partnerships for any number of activities, when in truth the relationship is contractual, a franchise, or the load shedding of some previously public service to a private or nonprofit entity." A more general term for such agreements is "shared service delivery" — municipalities joining together, with private firms, or with nonprofits to provide services to citizens.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JR-study_42-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-JR-study-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JR-post_50-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-JR-post-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_theory">
       Economic theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Economic theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In economic theory, public–private partnerships have been studied through the lens of
      <a href="/wiki/Contract_theory" title="Contract theory">
       contract theory
      </a>
      . The first theoretical study on PPPs has been conducted by
      <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Hart_(economist)" title="Oliver Hart (economist)">
       Oliver Hart
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        [66]
       </a>
      </sup>
      From an economic theory perspective, what distinguishes a PPP from traditional public procurement of infrastructure services is the fact that the building and operating stages are bundled in the case of PPPs. Hence, the private firm has strong incentives in the building stage to make investments with regard to the operating stage. These investments can be desirable but may also be undesirable (e.g., when the investments not only reduce operating costs, but also reduce service quality). Hence, there is a trade-off and it depends on the particular situation whether a PPP or traditional procurement is to be preferred. Hart's model has been extended in several directions. For instance, authors have studied various externalities between the building and operating stages,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        [67]
       </a>
      </sup>
      insurance when firms are risk-averse,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        [68]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and implications of PPPs for incentives to innovate and gather information.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        [69]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_and_barriers">
       Challenges and barriers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public%E2%80%93private_partnership&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Challenges and barriers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Public–private partnerships have seen a large increase over the years in part because local and state governments rely heavily on the growing number of non-profits to provide many public services that they cannot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2008_70-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Smith_2008-70">
        [70]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Entering into a public-private partnership can be rewarding as well as destructive if not done with caution and education. Partnerships need balance from both parties as well as continuous maintenance. If entered into lightly, one can find its organization falling in various areas proving to be one of many partnership failures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
        [71]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <b>
        Flexibility
       </b>
       between the two partners as the contract and staff involved throughout the process: If one party feels they are losing some of the control they may work on adopting more rules and regulations throughout the process instead of working together to be flexible and mediate an issue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
         [71]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Timeline
       </b>
       : Non-profits are working on a long-term timeline. Many of their goals can only be achieved with long-term commitment; this is where their focus will lie. For-profit organizations are more short-term oriented because of short-term goals focusing primarily on profitability. Finally, government agencies' timeline depends a lot on election timelines and therefore can change regularly.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
        <a href="#cite_note-72">
         [72]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Focus of the project
       </b>
       : Partners may not have the same focus when entering into a partnership even though they think they might.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Funding priorities
       </b>
       : When parties cannot agree on where funding should go this can sometimes lead to losses in time, resources, and the overall funding for the project.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
         [71]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Funding priorities for government bodies looks typically at where the public's funds were spent in relation to the contract made. This then typically is looked at as in how many hours of participations, forms filled out, meals served, etc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2008_70-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_2008-70">
         [70]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Neighborhood organizations or small and local non-profits saw a broad source of funding during the early years but there has been a shift in funding more recently reducing the overall funding and seeing more of it go to larger agencies focusing on large grants.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Accountability" title="Accountability">
         Accountability
        </a>
       </b>
       : With the rise in public-private partnerships there is also a rise in the responsibility that the non-profits tend to hold. With the government relying on many more of these organizations to provide the public services they cannot it is also proving difficult for the government to hold these non-profits responsible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2008_70-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_2008-70">
         [70]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When responsibilities are not set to the letter this can cause some in managerial positions to take the back seat, seeing their counterparts taking the initiative to get tasks done. This leaves an unbalance of work and sometimes those with the most stills are not doing the job. This can also be brought on by undermanagement causing more problems such as a lack of focus for the projects, mismanaged funding, and miscommunication.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
         [71]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Too many projects and partnerships can also lead to a lack of accountability. When there are too many tasks they seem to all fall short of the hoped perfection.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
         [71]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some partners may be taking over roles of others because accountability has not been well defined. This can also lead to some taking advantage of others when they note the any weakness. This can cause a distrustful partnership.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eschenfelder_2011_73-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Eschenfelder_2011-73">
         [73]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Communication or understanding
       </b>
       : One of the largest issues that can be discussed, communication can be a huge downfall and can contribute to many of the other risks within partnerships. It can be said that when entering into a cross-sector partnership it is difficult to understand and collaborate due to the diversity and differing languages spoken amongst the sectors. Items like performance measures, goal measurements, government regulations, and the nature of funding can all be interpreted differently thus causing blurred lines of communication.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
         [71]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Autonomy within the partnership
       </b>
       : While working together is important it is still a strength to be able to work on parts of the project alone, take initiative when needed, and keep some individualism throughout the process. This is beginning to happen more with the privatization of public-private partnerships where the private organization may own the partnership itself and the government then keeps full responsibility for it. This keeps parts of the partnership separate for focus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
        <a href="#cite_note-74">
         [74]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Conflicts
       </b>
       : These can arise from any of the above topics but even outside issues or forces may bring a partnership to a halt. Even though these partnerships are entered into with the best of intentions even the most trivial issues can snowball into greater conflict halting a partnership dead in its tracks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eschenfelder_2011_73-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Eschenfelder_2011-73">
         [73]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Having no understanding and communication between parties can cause conflicts with use of language, stereotyping, negative assumptions, and prejudice about the other organization. These conflicts can be related to territorialism or protectionism, and a lack of commitment to working within the partnership.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cairns_.26_Harris_2011_75-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cairns_.26_Harris_2011-75">
         [75]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Possible solutions
       </b>
       : Partnerships might not be natural for business and managers do not want to depend on others but it is possible with careful solutions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
         [71]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <ol>
        <li>
         Creating an ongoing narrative about partnerships and how will these be developed, maintained, terminated. This is especially prevalent to the local and state governments who rely heavily on the non-profits for the public services.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2008_70-3">
          <a href="#cite_note-Smith_2008-70">
           [70]
          </a>
         </sup>
         A business partnership model would not be accurate or appropriate for a P3.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomfield_2006_76-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Bloomfield_2006-76">
           [76]
          </a>
         </sup>
         Many partnerships can be terminated early due to issues with trust and cooperation during the contract implementation process. These issues can be avoided when the organization has initial guidelines for dos and don'ts.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomfield_2006_76-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-Bloomfield_2006-76">
           [76]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </li>
        <li>
         Creating a formal control mechanism for the partnership.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-7">
          <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
           [71]
          </a>
         </sup>
         <ol>
          <li>
           Ensure that there is a continuous commitment with negotiations in any time of trouble and even an outline for termination procedures if necessary.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babiak_.26_Thibault_71-8">
            <a href="#cite_note-Babiak_.26_Thibault-71">
             [71]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </li>
          <li>
           Conflict resolution, outreach and organizational development are items that managers can work on and even assign specialists to each task. Creating a timeline to be followed throughout the partnership assists in mutual understanding and communication as well.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2008_70-4">
            <a href="#cite_note-Smith_2008-70">
             [70]
            </a>
           </sup>
           Assigning specialists to work with skills in communication, conflict resolution, negotiation and policy analysis cross-sector partnerships have also been able to flourish.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cairns_.26_Harris_2011_75-1">
            <a href="#cite_note-Cairns_.26_Harris_2011-75">
             [75]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </li>
         </ol>
        </li>
       </ol>
      </li>
     </ol>
