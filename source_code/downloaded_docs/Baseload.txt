   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Base load
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       base load
      </b>
      on a grid is the minimum level of demand on an
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">
       electrical grid
      </a>
      over 24 hours. Base load power sources are
      <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">
       power stations
      </a>
      which can consistently generate the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_power" title="Electrical power">
       electrical power
      </a>
      needed to satisfy this minimum demand.
     </p>
     <p>
      Historically, large
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_grid" title="Power grid">
       power grids
      </a>
      have used base load power plants exclusively. However, there is no specific technical requirement for this to be so. The base load requirement can equally well be met by the appropriate mix of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermittent_power_source" title="Intermittent power source">
       intermittent power sources
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">
       peaking power plants
      </a>
      , hydroelectric power and other sources.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Base_load&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1173" data-file-width="1564" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/220px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/330px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/440px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A base load power station in
        <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">
         Taiwan
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Baseload plant
      </b>
      , (also
      <b>
       baseload power plant
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       base load power station
      </b>
      ) is an energy station devoted to the production of base load supply. Baseload plants are the production facilities used to meet some or all of a given region's continuous energy demand, and produce energy at a constant rate, usually at a low cost relative to other production facilities available to the system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Examples of baseload plants using nonrenewable fuels include
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">
       nuclear
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-fired_power_station" title="Coal-fired power station">
       coal-fired
      </a>
      plants. Baseload plants typically run at all times through the year except in the case of repairs or maintenance. These plants are often designed for relatively high efficiency, and may be
      <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">
       combined cycle
      </a>
      plants, but may take several days to start up and shut down.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Each baseload power plant on a grid is allotted a specific amount of the baseload power demand to handle. The base load power is determined by the
      <a href="/wiki/Load_duration_curve" title="Load duration curve">
       load duration curve
      </a>
      of the system. For a typical power system, the rule of thumb is that the base load power is usually 35-40% of the maximum load during the year.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Peaks or spikes in customer power demand are frequently handled by smaller and more responsive, but perhaps somewhat less efficient types of power plants called
      <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">
       peaking power plants
      </a>
      , often powered with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_turbine_power_plant" title="Gas turbine power plant">
       gas turbines
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While historically large power grids have had base load power plant to exclusively meet the base load, there is no specific technical requirement for this to be so. The base load can equally well be met by the appropriate quantity of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermittent_power_source" title="Intermittent power source">
       intermittent power sources
      </a>
      and peaking power plant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Among the renewable energy sources,
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectric
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">
       geothermal
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">
       biogas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_thermal#Heat_storage" title="Solar thermal">
       solar thermal with storage
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ocean_thermal_energy_conversion" title="Ocean thermal energy conversion">
       ocean thermal energy conversion
      </a>
      can provide base load power. A significant fraction of the average
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      production is available with 95% or greater probability, and so may be used for baseload power.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hydroelectric power also has the desirable attribute of
      <a href="/wiki/Dispatchable_generation" title="Dispatchable generation">
       dispatchability
      </a>
      , but conversely a hydroelectric plant may run low on its "fuel" (water at the reservoir elevation) if a long
      <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">
       drought
      </a>
      occurs over its
      <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">
       drainage basin
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">
       Economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Base_load&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Power plants are designated
      <i>
       baseload
      </i>
      based on their low cost generation, efficiency and safety at rated output power levels. Baseload power plants do not change production to match power consumption demands since it is more economical to operate them at constant production levels. Use of higher cost combined-cycle plants or combustion turbines is thus minimized, and these plants can be cycled up and down to match more rapid fluctuations in consumption. Baseload generators, such as nuclear and coal, often have very high
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_costs" title="Fixed costs">
       fixed costs
      </a>
      , high
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_load_factor" title="Plant load factor">
       plant load factor
      </a>
      but very low
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marginal_costs" title="Marginal costs">
       marginal costs
      </a>
      . On the other hand, peak load generators, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      , have low fixed costs, low plant load factor and high marginal costs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Typically baseload plants are large and provide a majority of the power used by a
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
       grid
      </a>
      . Thus, they are more effective when used continuously to cover the power baseload required by the grid.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Base_load_power_plant_usage">
       Base load power plant usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Base_load&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Base load power plant usage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">
       Nuclear
      </a>
      power plants may take many hours, if not days, to change their power output,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      although modern stations, and those in
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_France" title="Nuclear power in France">
       France
      </a>
      , can and do operate as
      <a href="/wiki/Load_following_power_plant" title="Load following power plant">
       load following power plants
      </a>
      and alter their output to meet varying demands.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because they require a long period of time to heat up to
      <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">
       operating temperature
      </a>
      , these plants typically handle large amounts of baseload demand. Different plants and technologies may have differing capacities to increase or decrease output on demand: nuclear plants are generally run at close to maximum output continuously (apart from maintenance,
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">
       refueling
      </a>
      and periodic refurbishment), while coal-fired plants may be cycled over the course of a day to meet demand.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Plants with multiple generating units may be used as a group to improve the "fit" with demand, by operating each unit as close to peak
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">
       efficiency
      </a>
      as possible.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_plc" title="National Grid plc">
       National Grid plc
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">
       chief executive officer
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Holliday" title="Steve Holliday">
       Steve Holliday
      </a>
      in 2015, baseload is "outdated", as microgrids would become the primary means of production, and large powerplants relegated to supply the remainder.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2016,
      <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Evans-Pritchard" title="Ambrose Evans-Pritchard">
       Ambrose Evans-Pritchard
      </a>
      of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_Telegraph" title="Daily Telegraph">
       Daily Telegraph
      </a>
      wrote that, with advances in energy storage, 'there ceases to be much point in building costly "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseload" title="Baseload">
       baseload
      </a>
      " power plants' and goes on to argue 'Nuclear reactors cannot be switched on and off as need demands - unlike gas plants. They are useless as a back-up for the decentralized grid of the future, when wind, solar, hydro, and other renewables will dominate the power supply'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
