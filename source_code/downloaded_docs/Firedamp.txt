    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Firedamp
      </b>
      is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammable" title="Flammable">
       flammable
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
       gas
      </a>
      found in
      <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">
       coal mines
      </a>
      . It is the name given to a number of flammable gases, especially
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      . It is particularly found in areas where the coal is
      <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">
       bituminous
      </a>
      . The gas accumulates in pockets in the coal and adjacent strata, and when they are penetrated, the release can trigger explosions. Historically, if such a pocket was highly pressurized, it was termed a "bag of foulness".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
       Name
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Firedamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Damp_(mining)" title="Damp (mining)">
       Damps
      </a>
      is the collective name given to all gases (other than air) found in coal mines in Great Britain. The word corresponds to German
      <i>
       Dampf
      </i>
      , the name for "vapour".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Alongside firedamp, other damps include
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Blackdamp" title="Blackdamp">
        blackdamp
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      and other gases), poisonous, explosive
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stinkdamp" title="Stinkdamp">
        stinkdamp
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">
       hydrogen sulfide
      </a>
      ), with its characteristic "rotten egg" odour, and the insidiously lethal
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Afterdamp" title="Afterdamp">
        afterdamp
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      and other gases) produced following explosions of firedamp or coal dust.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contribution_to_mine_deaths">
       Contribution to mine deaths
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Firedamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contribution to mine deaths">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Firedamp is explosive at concentrations between 4% and 16%, with most explosions occurring at around 10%. It caused much loss of life in coal mines before the invention of the
      <a href="/wiki/Geordie_lamp" title="Geordie lamp">
       Geordie lamp
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Davy_lamp" title="Davy lamp">
       Davy lamp
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Holland-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The invention was prompted by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felling_mine_disaster" title="Felling mine disaster">
       Felling mine disaster
      </a>
      near
      <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">
       Newcastle upon Tyne
      </a>
      claiming 92 lives on 25 May 1812.
      <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">
       Davy
      </a>
      experimented with
      <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
       iron
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Gauze" title="Gauze">
       gauze
      </a>
      , determining the maximum size of the gaps and the optimum
      <a href="/wiki/Wire" title="Wire">
       wire
      </a>
      thickness to prevent a
      <a href="/wiki/Flame" title="Flame">
       flame
      </a>
      passing through the gauze. If a naked flame was thus enclosed totally by such a gauze, then methane could pass into the lamp and burn safely above the flame. He did not patent his invention.
     </p>
     <p>
      Even after the safety lamps were brought into common use, firedamp explosions could still occur from sparks produced when coal contaminated with
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">
       pyrites
      </a>
      was struck with metal tools. The presence of
      <a href="/wiki/Coal_dust" title="Coal dust">
       coal dust
      </a>
      in the air increased the risk of explosion with firedamp, and indeed could cause explosions itself.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Davy_lamp.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="491" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Davy_lamp.png/220px-Davy_lamp.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Davy_lamp.png/330px-Davy_lamp.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Davy_lamp.png/440px-Davy_lamp.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Davy_lamp.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Davy lamp (drawing)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Tyneside" title="Tyneside">
       Tyneside
      </a>
      coal mines in England had the deadly combination of bituminous coal contaminated with pyrites, and a great number of lives were lost in accidents due to firedamp explosions, including 102 dead at
      <a href="/wiki/Wallsend" title="Wallsend">
       Wallsend
      </a>
      in 1835.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Holland-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A continuous flame was produced at
      <a href="/wiki/Whitehaven" title="Whitehaven">
       Whitehaven
      </a>
      some time before 1733, described as being "a yard wide and two yards long." The miners dealt with it by piping it to the outside.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Rather than the Davy lamp, Tyneside miners used the Geordie lamp, a similar safety lamp designed by
      <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">
       George Stephenson
      </a>
      . After the widespread introduction of the safety lamp, explosions continued because the early Davy lamps were fragile and easily damaged. The iron gauze for example only needed to lose one wire to become unsafe. The light was also very poor, and there were continuous attempts to improve the basic design. The height of the cone of burning methane in a flame safety lamp can be used to estimate the concentration of the gas in the local atmosphere. It was not until the 1890s that safe and reliable electric lamps became available in collieries.
     </p>
