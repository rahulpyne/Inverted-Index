   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Window insulation film
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Window insulation film
      </b>
      is a plastic film which can be applied to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_window" title="Glass window">
       glass windows
      </a>
      to reduce heat transfer. There are two types in common use designed to reduce heat flow via
      <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
       radiation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      respectively.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_control_film">
       Solar control film
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_insulation_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Solar control film">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      This works by reflecting the infra-red component of
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
       solar energy
      </a>
      (often 700W/sq M) and absorbing the UV component. Some films are also silvered or tinted to reduce visible light.
     </p>
     <p>
      Typical absorption for a silvered film is 65% for visible and infra red with 99% for UV. This type of film sticks directly onto the glass.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Convection_control_film">
       Convection control film
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_insulation_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Convection control film">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A film is attached to the window frame to trap a pocket of air between the glass and the film, creating a double-glazed system with an insulating gap of still air.
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
       Thermal conductivity
      </a>
      of still air is 0.024 W/m/K and much lower than that of glass (0.96 W/m/K).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Factors which limit the performance of a double glazed window are gap width, convection within the cavity and radiative heat transfer across the gap which is largely independent of its width.
     </p>
     <p>
      Optimal gap width depends on the temperature difference imposed across the gap. A European standard (EN 673) uses 20 °C difference between the inside and outside temperature which results in an optimal simulated gap width of about 17 mm for a standard double glazed window. A US standard (NFRC) uses a 39 °C difference which yields a smaller optimal gap width of about 13 mm. Using the European standard a window with an ideal gap of 17 mm has a simulated
      <a href="/wiki/R-value_(insulation)" title="R-value (insulation)">
       U-Value
      </a>
      of about 2.8 W/m²/K, a window with a much smaller gap of 6 mm has a U-value of about 3.3 W/m²/K, while a single glazed window has a U-value of about 5.5 W/m²/K.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (see also
      <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">
       insulated glazing
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Window-insulation-kit.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="449" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Window-insulation-kit.jpg/300px-Window-insulation-kit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Window-insulation-kit.jpg 1.5x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Window-insulation-kit.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <b>
         Typical window insulation film kit
        </b>
        of plastic shrink film (folded-up) and a roll of double-sided tape
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      One commonly used film is a heat-shrink plastic which is attached to the window frame using double-sided pressure-sensitive tape. A hair dryer is used to remove creases and improve optical clarity.
     </p>
     <p>
      Reduced heat flow also helps prevent
      <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">
       condensation
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which is triggered when the temperature of the inside surface falls below the
      <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">
       dew point
      </a>
      . Assuming an outside temperature of 0 °C with wind velocity 15 mph and inside temperature 20 °C condensation occurs at only 30%RH
      <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">
       relative humidity
      </a>
      with a single-glazed system compared with 60%RH for a double-glazed system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Condensation also transfers additional heat, which can be significant.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
       Applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Window_insulation_film&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "Low-E" glass was introduced to the North American residential building market in the 1980s by a collaboration between Rob Hammon, now with BIRAenergy, and a windshield glass manufacturer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Solar control film is an effective way to control excessive sunshine during the summer months.
     </p>
     <p>
      Convection control film is often used in winter when the outgoing heat flow is more important, especially for single-glazed windows where the temperature difference is often sufficient to cause condensation.
     </p>
