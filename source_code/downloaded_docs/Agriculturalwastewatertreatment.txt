     <p>
      <b>
       Agricultural wastewater treatment
      </b>
      is a farm management agenda for controlling pollution from surface runoff that may be contaminated by chemicals in fertiliser, pesticides, animal slurry, crop residues or irrigation water.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nonpoint_source_pollution">
       Nonpoint source pollution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nonpoint source pollution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source_pollution" title="Nonpoint source pollution">
       Nonpoint source pollution
      </a>
      from farms is caused by
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">
       surface runoff
      </a>
      from fields during rain storms. Agricultural runoff is a major source of
      <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">
       pollution
      </a>
      , in some cases the only source, in many
      <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">
       watersheds
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-fact_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA-fact-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sediment_runoff">
       Sediment runoff
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sediment runoff">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eroded_soils_in_Iowa.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2100" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Eroded_soils_in_Iowa.png/220px-Eroded_soils_in_Iowa.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Eroded_soils_in_Iowa.png/330px-Eroded_soils_in_Iowa.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Eroded_soils_in_Iowa.png/440px-Eroded_soils_in_Iowa.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eroded_soils_in_Iowa.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Highly erodible soils on a farm in
        <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">
         Iowa
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">
       Soil
      </a>
      washed off fields is the largest source of agricultural pollution in the United States. Excess sediment causes high levels of
      <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">
       turbidity
      </a>
      in water bodies, which can inhibit growth of
      <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_plant" title="Aquatic plant">
       aquatic plants
      </a>
      , clog
      <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">
       fish
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">
       gills
      </a>
      and smother animal
      <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">
       larvae
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-fact_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA-fact-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Farmers may utilize
      <a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">
       erosion controls
      </a>
      to reduce runoff flows and retain soil on their fields. Common techniques include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Contour_plowing" title="Contour plowing">
        contour ploughing
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       crop
       <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">
        mulching
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">
        crop rotation
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       planting
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perennial" title="Perennial">
        perennial
       </a>
       crops
      </li>
      <li>
       installing
       <a href="/wiki/Riparian_buffer" title="Riparian buffer">
        riparian buffers
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :pp. 4-95–4-96
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_runoff">
       Nutrient runoff
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nutrient runoff">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epandeur_de_Fumier_Rolland.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Epandeur_de_Fumier_Rolland.JPG/220px-Epandeur_de_Fumier_Rolland.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Epandeur_de_Fumier_Rolland.JPG/330px-Epandeur_de_Fumier_Rolland.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Epandeur_de_Fumier_Rolland.JPG/440px-Epandeur_de_Fumier_Rolland.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Epandeur_de_Fumier_Rolland.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Manure spreader
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       Nitrogen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">
       phosphorus
      </a>
      are key pollutants found in runoff, and they are applied to farmland in several ways, such as in the form of commercial
      <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">
       fertilizer
      </a>
      , animal
      <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">
       manure
      </a>
      , or municipal or industrial wastewater (effluent) or sludge. These chemicals may also enter runoff from
      <a href="/wiki/Crop_residue" title="Crop residue">
       crop residues
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">
       irrigation
      </a>
      water,
      <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">
       wildlife
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(Aerosol_physics)" title="Deposition (Aerosol physics)">
       atmospheric deposition
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :p. 2–9
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Farmers can develop and implement
      <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_management" title="Nutrient management">
       nutrient management
      </a>
      plans to mitigate impacts on water quality by:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       mapping and documenting fields, crop types,
       <a href="/wiki/Soil_type" title="Soil type">
        soil types
       </a>
       , water bodies
      </li>
      <li>
       developing realistic
       <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">
        crop yield
       </a>
       projections
      </li>
      <li>
       conducting
       <a href="/wiki/Soil_test" title="Soil test">
        soil tests
       </a>
       and nutrient analyses of manures and/or sludges applied
      </li>
      <li>
       identifying other significant nutrient sources (e.g., irrigation water)
      </li>
      <li>
       evaluating significant field features such as highly erodible soils, subsurface drains, and shallow aquifers
      </li>
      <li>
       applying fertilizers, manures, and/or sludges based on realistic yield goals and using
       <a href="/wiki/Precision_agriculture" title="Precision agriculture">
        precision agriculture
       </a>
       techniques.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :pp. 4-37–4-38
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>

     </div>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesticides" title="Pesticides">
       Pesticides
      </a>
      are widely used by farmers to control plant
      <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">
       pests
      </a>
      and enhance
      <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_productivity" title="Agricultural productivity">
       production
      </a>
      , but chemical pesticides can also cause
      <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">
       water quality
      </a>
      problems. Pesticides may appear in
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">
       surface water
      </a>
      due to:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       direct application (e.g. aerial spraying or
       <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_spreader" title="Broadcast spreader">
        broadcasting
       </a>
       over water bodies)
      </li>
      <li>
       runoff during rain storms
      </li>
      <li>
       aerial drift (from adjacent fields).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :p.2–22
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Some pesticides have also been detected in
      <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">
       groundwater
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :p.2–24
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Farmers may use
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Pest_Management" title="Integrated Pest Management">
       Integrated Pest Management
      </a>
      (IPM) techniques (which can include
      <a href="/wiki/Biological_pest_control" title="Biological pest control">
       biological pest control
      </a>
      ) to maintain control over pests, reduce reliance on chemical pesticides, and protect water quality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are few safe ways of disposing of pesticide surpluses other than through containment in well managed landfills or by
      <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">
       incineration
      </a>
      . In some parts of the world, spraying on land is a permitted method of disposal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Point_source_pollution">
       Point source pollution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Point source pollution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Farms with large
      <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">
       livestock
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">
       poultry
      </a>
      operations, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factory_farm" title="Factory farm">
       factory farms
      </a>
      , can be a major source of
      <a href="/wiki/Point_source_pollution" title="Point source pollution">
       point source
      </a>
      wastewater. In the United States, these facilities are called
      <i>
       concentrated animal feeding operations
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       confined animal feeding operations
      </i>
      and are being subject to increasing government
      <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">
       regulation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_wastes">
       Animal wastes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Animal wastes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="350" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg/220px-Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg/330px-Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Confined Animal Feeding Operation in the
        <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
         United States
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The constituents of animal wastewater typically contain
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Strong organic content — much stronger than human sewage
      </li>
      <li>
       High
       <a href="/wiki/Total_suspended_solids" title="Total suspended solids">
        solids
       </a>
       concentration
      </li>
      <li>
       High
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">
        nitrate
       </a>
       and phosphorus content
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">
        Antibiotics
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Synthetic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormones" title="Hormones">
        hormones
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Often high concentrations of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">
        parasites
       </a>
       and their eggs
      </li>
      <li>
       Spores of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidiosis" title="Cryptosporidiosis">
         Cryptosporidium
        </a>
       </i>
       (a protozoan) resistant to drinking water treatment processes
      </li>
      <li>
       Spores of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Giardia" title="Giardia">
         Giardia
        </a>
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       Human pathogenic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">
        bacteria
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">
         Brucella
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">
         Salmonella
        </a>
       </i>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Animal wastes from cattle can be produced as solid or semisolid manure or as a liquid
      <a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">
       slurry
      </a>
      . The production of slurry is especially common in housed dairy cattle.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Treatment
      </b>
     </p>
     <p>
      Whilst solid manure heaps outdoors can give rise to polluting wastewaters from runoff, this type of waste is usually relatively easy to treat by containment and/or covering of the heap.
     </p>
     <p>
      Animal slurries require special handling and are usually treated by containment in lagoons before disposal by spray or trickle application to grassland.
      <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">
       Constructed wetlands
      </a>
      are sometimes used to facilitate treatment of animal wastes, as are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobic_lagoons" title="Anaerobic lagoons">
       anaerobic lagoons
      </a>
      . Excessive application or application to sodden land or insufficient land area can result in direct runoff to watercourses, with the potential for causing severe
      <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">
       pollution
      </a>
      . Application of slurries to land overlying
      <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">
       aquifers
      </a>
      can result in direct contamination or, more commonly, elevation of nitrogen levels as
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">
       nitrite
      </a>
      or nitrate.
     </p>
     <p>
      The disposal of any wastewater containing animal waste upstream of a drinking water intake can pose serious health problems to those drinking the water because of the highly resistant spores present in many animals that are capable of causing disabling disease in humans. This risk exists even for very low-level seepage via shallow surface drains or from rainfall run-off.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some animal slurries are treated by mixing with straws and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composting" title="Composting">
       composted
      </a>
      at high temperature to produce a bacteriologically sterile and friable manure for soil improvement.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Piggery_waste">
       Piggery waste
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Piggery waste">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hog_confinement_barn_interior.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="771" data-file-width="1079" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Hog_confinement_barn_interior.jpg/220px-Hog_confinement_barn_interior.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Hog_confinement_barn_interior.jpg/330px-Hog_confinement_barn_interior.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Hog_confinement_barn_interior.jpg/440px-Hog_confinement_barn_interior.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hog_confinement_barn_interior.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">
         Hog
        </a>
        confinement barn or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piggery" title="Piggery">
         piggery
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Piggery waste is comparable to other animal wastes and is processed as for general animal waste, except that many piggery wastes contain elevated levels of
      <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
       copper
      </a>
      that can be toxic in the natural environment. The liquid fraction of the waste is frequently separated off and re-used in the piggery to avoid the prohibitively expensive costs of disposing of copper-rich liquid.
      <a href="/wiki/Ascariasis" title="Ascariasis">
       Ascarid
      </a>
      worms and their eggs are also common in piggery waste and can infect humans if wastewater treatment is ineffective.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Silage_liquor">
       Silage liquor
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Silage liquor">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Fresh or wilted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">
       grass
      </a>
      or other green crops can be made into a semi-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">
       fermented
      </a>
      product called
      <a href="/wiki/Silage" title="Silage">
       silage
      </a>
      which can be stored and used as winter forage for cattle and sheep. The production of silage often involves the use of an acid conditioner such as
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">
       sulfuric acid
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">
       formic acid
      </a>
      . The process of silage making frequently produces a yellow-brown strongly smelling liquid which is very rich in simple
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugars" title="Sugars">
       sugars
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">
       alcohol
      </a>
      , short-chain organic acids and silage conditioner. This liquor is one of the most polluting organic substances known. The volume of silage liquor produced is generally in proportion to the moisture content of the ensiled material.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Treatment
      </b>
      <br/>
      Silage liquor is best treated through prevention by wilting crops well before silage making. Any silage liquor that is produced can be used as part of the food for pigs. The most effective treatment is by containment in a slurry lagoon and by subsequent spreading on land following substantial dilution with slurry. Containment of silage liquor on its own can cause structural problems in concrete pits because of the acidic nature of silage liquor.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Milking_parlour_.28dairy_farming.29_wastes">
       Milking parlour (dairy farming) wastes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Milking parlour (dairy farming) wastes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although milk has a deserved reputation as an important and valuable food product, its presence in wastewaters is highly polluting because of its organic strength, which can lead to very rapid
      <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">
       de-oxygenation
      </a>
      of receiving waters.
      <a href="/wiki/Dairy_farming#Milking_parlors" title="Dairy farming">
       Milking parlour
      </a>
      wastes also contain large volumes of wash-down water, some animal waste together with cleaning and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disinfection" title="Disinfection">
       disinfection
      </a>
      chemicals.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Treatment
      </b>
      <br/>
      Milking parlour wastes are often treated in
      <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">
       admixture
      </a>
      with human sewage in a local
      <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">
       sewage treatment
      </a>
      plant. This ensures that disinfectants and cleaning agents are sufficiently diluted and amenable to treatment. Running milking wastewaters into a farm slurry lagoon is a possible option although this tends to consume lagoon capacity very quickly. Land spreading is also a treatment option.
      <i>
       See also
       <a href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater_treatment" title="Industrial wastewater treatment">
        Industrial wastewater treatment
       </a>
       .
      </i>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Slaughtering_waste">
       Slaughtering waste
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Slaughtering waste">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Wastewater from slaughtering activities is similar to milking parlour waste (see above) although considerably stronger in its organic composition and therefore potentially much more polluting.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Treatment
      </b>
      <br/>
      As for milking parlour waste (see above).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetable_washing_water">
       Vegetable washing water
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Vegetable washing water">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Washing of
      <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">
       vegetables
      </a>
      produces large volumes of water contaminated by soil and vegetable pieces. Low levels of pesticides used to treat the vegetables may also be present together with moderate levels of disinfectants such as
      <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
       chlorine
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Treatment
      </b>
      <br/>
      Most vegetable washing waters are extensively recycled with the solids removed by settlement and filtration. The recovered soil can be returned to the land.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Firewater">
       Firewater
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agricultural_wastewater_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Firewater">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although few farms plan for fires, fires are nevertheless more common on farms than on many other industrial premises. Stores of pesticides, herbicides, fuel oil for farm machinery and fertilizers can all help promote fire and can all be present in environmentally lethal quantities in
      <a href="/wiki/Firewater_(fire_fighting)" title="Firewater (fire fighting)">
       firewater
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Firefighting" title="Firefighting">
       fire fighting
      </a>
      at farms.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Treatment
      </b>
      <br/>
      All farm environmental management plans should allow for containment of substantial quantities of firewater and for its subsequent recovery and disposal by specialist disposal companies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The concentration and mixture of contaminants in firewater make them unsuited to any treatment method available on the farm. Even land spreading has produced severe taste and odour problems for downstream water supply companies in the past.
     </p>
