   </h1>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Energy Storage as a Service
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       ESaaS
      </b>
      ; pronounced /
      <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
       i’sæs/
      </a>
      ) allows a facility to benefit from the advantages of an
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">
       energy storage system
      </a>
      by entering into a service agreement without having to purchasing the system. Energy storage systems provide a range of services to generate
      <a href="/wiki/Revenue" title="Revenue">
       revenue
      </a>
      , create
      <a href="/wiki/Cost_reduction" title="Cost reduction">
       savings
      </a>
      , and improve
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_quality" title="Electric power quality">
       electricity resiliency
      </a>
      . The operation of the ESaaS system is a unique combination of an advanced battery storage system, an
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_management_system" title="Energy management system">
       energy management system
      </a>
      , and a service contract which can deliver value to a business by providing reliable power more economically.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term ESaaS was developed and trademarked by Constant Power Inc.,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a
      <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">
       Toronto
      </a>
      -based company, in 2016. The service has been designed to work in the
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North American
      </a>
      open electricity markets.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Components">
       Components
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      ESaaS is the combination of an
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">
       energy storage
      </a>
      system, a
      <a href="/wiki/Remote_operation" title="Remote operation">
       control and monitoring system
      </a>
      , and a
      <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">
       service contract
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The most common energy storage systems used for ESaaS are
      <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">
       lithium-ion
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Flow_battery" title="Flow battery">
       flow
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Battery" title="Battery">
       batteries
      </a>
      due to their compact size, non-invasive installation, high efficiencies, and fast reaction times but other storage mediums may be used such as
      <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air_energy_storage" title="Compressed air energy storage">
       compressed air
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">
       flywheels
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">
       pumped hydro
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Battery" title="Battery">
       batteries
      </a>
      are sized based on the facility's needs and is paired with a
      <a href="/wiki/Power_inverter" title="Power inverter">
       power inverter
      </a>
      to convert the DC power to AC power in order to connect directly to the facility’s electricity supply.
     </p>
     <p>
      ESaaS systems are
      <a href="/wiki/Remote_operation" title="Remote operation">
       remotely monitored and controlled
      </a>
      by the ESaaS operator using a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supervisory_Control_and_Data_Acquisition" title="Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition">
       Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA)
      </a>
      system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supervisory_Control_and_Data_Acquisition" title="Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition">
       SCADA
      </a>
      communicates with the facility's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_Management_System" title="Energy Management System">
       Energy Management System (EMS)
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_conversion" title="Electric power conversion">
       Power Conversion System (PCS)
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Management_System" title="Battery Management System">
       Battery Management System (BMS)
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The ESaaS operator is responsible for ensuring the ESaaS system is monitoring and responding to the facility’s needs as well as overriding commands to participate in regional incentive programs such as
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_demand_management" title="Energy demand management">
       coincident peak management
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_demand_management" title="Energy demand management">
       demand response programs
      </a>
      in real time.
     </p>
     <p>
      The facility benefiting from the ESaaS system is linked to the ESaaS system operator through a
      <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">
       service contract
      </a>
      . The contract specifies the length of the service term, payment structure, and list of services the facility wishes to participate in.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Services">
       Services
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Services">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      ESaaS is used to perform a variety of services including:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Coincident Peak Management
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       During times of high regional demand,
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_transmission_organization_(North_America)" title="Regional transmission organization (North America)">
        Independent Service Operators (ISOs)/Regional Transmission Organizations (RTOs)
       </a>
       offer incentives for facilities to reduce or curtail their load. ESaaS allows a facility to isolate or offset their load during these high regional demand periods to decrease demand from the electricity grid to benefit from the incentives. The system is designed to work in conjunction or independent of facility curtailment.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">
         Demand Response
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_transmission_organization_(North_America)" title="Regional transmission organization (North America)">
        ISOs/RTOs
       </a>
       offer facilities payment for curtailing their energy demand when dispatched by the grid operator. ESaaS allows facilities to participate in these programs by off-setting all or a portion of a facility load during a
       <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">
        demand response
       </a>
       occurrence. A facility can benefit from the incentive without interrupting their facility operation.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Power_factor" title="Power factor">
         Power Factor Correction
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       During charging and discharging, active and reactive power may be balanced prior to supplying a facility. By balancing the amount of
       <a href="/wiki/AC_power" title="AC power">
        active and reactive power
       </a>
       to a facility, the power factor and resulting facility electrical efficiency may be improved. This improvement may reduce a facility's monthly
       <a href="/wiki/Peak_demand" title="Peak demand">
        peak demand
       </a>
       charge.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_quality" title="Electric power quality">
         Power Quality
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       ESaaS actively monitors electricity supply to a facility. In times of
       <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source" title="Intermittent energy source">
        intermittent power supply
       </a>
       , ESaaS acts as an
       <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Uninterruptible power supply">
        uninterruptible power supply
       </a>
       (UPS) to ensure uninterrupted, reliable power supply to eliminate unexpected fluctuations. Fluctuating and intermittent power affects equipment operation which may cause costly delays and defects in production.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_Station" title="Power Station">
         Back-up Power
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       If the electricity grid experiences a
       <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">
        power outage
       </a>
       , ESaaS offers a
       <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">
        back-up power
       </a>
       service to continue powering all or a portion of a facility's electricity demand. Depending on the size of the ESaaS installation, ESaaS may maintain facility operation for the duration of a
       <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">
        grid failure
       </a>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Load_management" title="Load management">
         Peak Shaving
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       ESaaS actively monitors a facility’s energy profile to normalize the electricity draw from the
       <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">
        electricity grid
       </a>
       . The ESaaS system stores energy when the facility demand is lower than average and discharges the stored energy when the facility demand is higher than average. The result is a steady draw of electricity from the electricity grid and a lower monthly
       <a href="/wiki/Peak_demand" title="Peak demand">
        peak demand
       </a>
       charge.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage" title="Arbitrage">
         Energy Arbitrage
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
         <a href="#cite_note-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       ESaaS actively monitors local electricity
       <a href="/wiki/Spot_contract" title="Spot contract">
        spot prices
       </a>
       to store energy when the price is low to be utilized when electricity prices are high. This is commonly referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage" title="Arbitrage">
        arbitrage
       </a>
       . The net different in price results in cost savings.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Ancillary_services_(electric_power)" title="Ancillary services (electric power)">
         Market Ancillary Services
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
         <a href="#cite_note-19">
          [19]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       ESaaS enables facilities to participate in the local
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_transmission_organization_(North_America)" title="Regional transmission organization (North America)">
        ISO/RTO
       </a>
       markets to provide services such as
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency_response" title="Frequency response">
        frequency regulation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Operating_reserve" title="Operating reserve">
        operating reserve
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Dispatchable_generation" title="Dispatchable generation">
        dispatchable generation
       </a>
       . By participating in the local market, facilities can generate revenue through the ESaaS contract.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Electricity_market" title="Electricity market">
         Transmission Support
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
         <a href="#cite_note-20">
          [20]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       ESaaS may provide services to ease
       <a href="/wiki/Electricity_market" title="Electricity market">
        congestion and constraint on electricity transmission networks
       </a>
       by storing energy during heavy transmission periods to be released during less congested periods. The use of this service can prolong the life of infrastructure and defers system upgrades.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Markets_served">
       Markets served
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Markets served">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      ESaaS primarily benefits large energy consumers with an average demand of over 500 kW,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Current early adopters of ESaaS are
      <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">
       manufacturers
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_plant" title="Chemical plant">
       chemical
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Electronic_component" title="Electronic component">
       electrical
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">
       lighting
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">
       metal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">
       petrochemical
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">
       plastics
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Commercial_building" title="Commercial building">
       commercial
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">
       retail
      </a>
      , large offices, medium offices, multi-residential,
      <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">
       supermarkets
      </a>
      ), public facilities (
      <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">
       colleges
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">
       universities
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hotel" title="Hotel">
       hotels
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hospitality" title="Hospitality">
       hospitality
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">
       schools
      </a>
      ), and resources (
      <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">

      <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">

      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">
       Benefits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Benefits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="System_benefactor_does_not_require_installation_capital">
       System benefactor does not require installation capital
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: System benefactor does not require installation capital">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      To participate in an ESaaS service, the installation system benefactor does not require any
      <a href="/wiki/Capital_requirement" title="Capital requirement">
       capital outlay
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Upon installing an ESaaS service, the facility sees immediate savings and/or revenue generation. Initial capital is often a hurdle for facilities to adopt an energy storage system since in most cases, the
      <a href="/wiki/Payback_period" title="Payback period">
       payback
      </a>
      period
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      of an
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">
       energy storage system
      </a>
      is 5–10 years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="System_operated_by_a_third-party_system_operator.5B26.5D">
       System operated by a third-party system operator
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: System operated by a third-party system operator[26]">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      ESaaS is a
      <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">
       contracted
      </a>
      service
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      that is automatically controlled by a third party. This eliminates responsibility for the facility
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      to allocate resources to manage their energy profile allowing a facility to operate their core business. The system operators have knowledge of local electricity sectors that continually
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supervisory_Control_and_Data_Acquisition" title="Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition">
       monitor and update
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      system protocols as regional markets change. The information is used to optimize the value realized by the ESaaS system while still meeting facility requirements.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental">
       Environmental
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For most ESaaS services, energy is stored during night time, off-peak hours when energy production is created from
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">
       non-carbon emitting sources
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The energy is then used to offset the required
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       carbon emitting
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      production during peak-times. The load shifting capability provided by ESaaS displaces heavy emitting generation requirements.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pricing">
       Pricing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pricing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      ESaaS contracts have a
      <a href="/wiki/Fixed_cost" title="Fixed cost">
       fixed monthly price
      </a>
      over a contracted
      <a href="/wiki/Term_(time)" title="Term (time)">
       term
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fixed price is based on potential economic benefit and applicable programs in the region of deployment. The ESaaS contract price is always less than the economic value provided by the service to ensure the client retains a net positive value through the service.
     </p>
