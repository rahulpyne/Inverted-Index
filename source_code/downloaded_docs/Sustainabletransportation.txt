     <p>
      <b>
       Sustainable transport
      </b>
      refers to the broad subject of
      <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">
       transport
      </a>
      that is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">
       sustainable
      </a>
      in the senses of social, environmental and climate impacts and the ability to, in the global scope, supply the source energy indefinitely. Components for evaluating sustainability include the particular
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vehicles" title="Vehicles">
       vehicles
      </a>
      used for road, water or air transport; the source of
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       energy
      </a>
      ; and the
      <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">
       infrastructure
      </a>
      used to accommodate the transport (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roads" title="Roads">
       roads
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railways" title="Railways">
       railways
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Airway_(aviation)" title="Airway (aviation)">
       airways
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterways" title="Waterways">
       waterways
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canals" title="Canals">
       canals
      </a>
      and terminals). Another component for evaluation is
      <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">
       pipelines
      </a>
      for transporting liquid or gas materials. Transport operations and
      <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">
       logistics
      </a>
      as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Transit-oriented_development" title="Transit-oriented development">
       transit-oriented development
      </a>
      are also involved in evaluation. Transportation sustainability is largely being measured by transportation system effectiveness and efficiency as well as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">
       environmental
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
       climate
      </a>
      impacts of the system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Short-term activity often promotes incremental improvement in
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">
       fuel efficiency
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_emissions_control" title="Vehicle emissions control">
       vehicle emissions controls
      </a>
      while long-term goals include migrating transportation from fossil-based energy to other alternatives such as
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      and use of other
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">
       renewable resources
      </a>
      . The entire life cycle of transport systems is subject to
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_measurement" title="Sustainability measurement">
       sustainability measurement
      </a>
      and optimization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sustainable transport systems make a positive contribution to the environmental, social and economic
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
       sustainability
      </a>
      of the communities they serve. Transport systems exist to provide social and economic connections, and people quickly take up the opportunities offered by increased mobility,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      with poor households benefiting greatly from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_carbon" title="Low carbon">
       low carbon
      </a>
      transport options.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The advantages of increased mobility need to be weighed against the environmental, social and economic costs that transport systems pose.
     </p>
     <p>
      Transport systems have significant impacts on the environment, accounting for between 20% and 25% of world energy consumption and
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-world_energy_council_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-world_energy_council-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The majority of the emissions, almost 97%, came from direct burning of fossil fuels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Greenhouse gas emissions from transport are increasing at a faster rate than any other energy using sector.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IPCC-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Road transport is also a major contributor to local air pollution and
      <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">
       smog
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme_(UNEP)" title="United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP)">
       United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP)
      </a>
      estimates that each year 2.4 million premature deaths from outdoor air pollution could be avoided.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Particularly hazardous for health are emissions of black carbon, a component of particulate matter, which is a known cause of respiratory and carcinogenic diseases and a significant contributor to global climate change.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LEDS_in_Practice:_Breathe_clean_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LEDS_in_Practice:_Breathe_clean-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The links between
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">
       greenhouse gas emissions
      </a>
      and particulate matter make
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_carbon" title="Low carbon">
       low carbon
      </a>
      transport an increasingly sustainable investment at local level—both by reducing emission levels and thus mitigating climate change; and by improving public health through better
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">
       air quality
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LEDS_in_Practice:_Breathe_clean_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-LEDS_in_Practice:_Breathe_clean-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The social costs of transport include road crashes, air pollution, physical inactivity,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHO-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      time taken away from the
      <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">
       family
      </a>
      while
      <a href="/wiki/Commuting" title="Commuting">
       commuting
      </a>
      and vulnerability to
      <a href="/wiki/2000s_energy_crisis" title="2000s energy crisis">
       fuel price increases
      </a>
      . Many of these negative impacts fall disproportionately on those social groups who are also least likely to own and drive cars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">
       Traffic congestion
      </a>
      imposes economic costs by wasting people's time and by slowing the delivery of goods and services.
     </p>
     <p>
      Traditional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transport_planning" title="Transport planning">
       transport planning
      </a>
      aims to improve mobility, especially for vehicles, and may fail to adequately consider wider impacts. But the real purpose of transport is access - to work, education, goods and services, friends and family - and there are proven techniques to improve access while simultaneously reducing environmental and social impacts, and managing traffic congestion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Communities which are successfully improving the sustainability of their transport networks are doing so as part of a wider programme of creating more vibrant, livable,
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_city" title="Sustainable city">
       sustainable cities
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term
      <b>
       sustainable transport
      </b>
      came into use as a logical follow-on from
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">
       sustainable development
      </a>
      , and is used to describe modes of transport, and systems of transport planning, which are consistent with wider concerns of
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
       sustainability
      </a>
      . There are many definitions of the sustainable transport, and of the related terms
      <b>
       sustainable transportation
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       sustainable mobility
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      One such definition, from the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       European Union
      </a>
      Council of Ministers of Transport, defines a sustainable transportation system as one that:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Allows the basic access and development needs of individuals, companies and society to be met safely and in a manner consistent with human and
       <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_health" title="Ecosystem health">
        ecosystem health
       </a>
       , and promotes equity within and between successive generations.
      </li>
      <li>
       Is Affordable, operates fairly and efficiently, offers a choice of transport mode, and supports a competitive economy, as well as balanced regional development.
      </li>
      <li>
       Limits emissions and waste within the planet’s ability to absorb them, uses renewable resources at or below their rates of generation, and uses non-renewable resources at or below the rates of development of renewable substitutes, while minimizing the impact on the use of land and the generation of noise.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Sustainability extends beyond just the operating efficiency and emissions. A
      <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">
       Life-cycle assessment
      </a>
      involves production, use and post-use considerations. A
      <a href="/wiki/Cradle-to-cradle_design" title="Cradle-to-cradle design">
       cradle-to-cradle design
      </a>
      is more important than a focus on a single factor such as energy efficiency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kuruma_zukushi.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="822" data-file-width="528" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Kuruma_zukushi.jpg/200px-Kuruma_zukushi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Kuruma_zukushi.jpg/300px-Kuruma_zukushi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Kuruma_zukushi.jpg/400px-Kuruma_zukushi.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kuruma_zukushi.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An 1889 Japanese print shows various forms of transportation
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Most of the tools and concepts of sustainable transport were developed before the phrase was coined. Walking, the first mode of transport, is also the most sustainable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Public transport dates back at least as far as the invention of the public bus by
      <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">
       Blaise Pascal
      </a>
      in 1662.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_trams" title="History of trams">
       passenger tram
      </a>
      began operation in 1807 and the
      <a href="/wiki/Stockton_and_Darlington_Railway" title="Stockton and Darlington Railway">
       first passenger rail service
      </a>
      in 1825.
      <a href="/wiki/Bicycle#History" title="Bicycle">
       Pedal bicycles
      </a>
      date from the 1860s. These were the only personal transport choices available to most people in Western countries prior to
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      , and remain the only options for most people in the developing world. Freight was moved by human power, animal power or rail.
     </p>
     <p>
      The post-war years brought increased wealth and a demand for much greater mobility for people and goods. The number of road vehicles in Britain increased fivefold between 1950 and 1979,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dft.gov.uk_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dft.gov.uk-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      with similar trends in other Western nations. Most affluent countries and cities invested heavily in bigger and better-designed roads and motorways, which were considered essential to underpin growth and prosperity. Transport planning became a branch of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_Planning" title="Urban Planning">
       Urban Planning
      </a>
      and identified
      <a href="/wiki/Induced_demand" title="Induced demand">
       induced demand
      </a>
      as a pivotal change from "predict and provide" toward a sustainable approach incorporating
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" title="Land use planning">
       land use planning
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transit" title="Public transit">
       public transit
      </a>
      . Public investment in transit, walking and cycling declined dramatically in the United States, Great Britain and Australia, although this did not occur to the same extent in Canada or mainland Europe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRB_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TRB-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Concerns about the sustainability of this approach became widespread during the
      <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">
       1973 oil crisis
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">
       1979 energy crisis
      </a>
      . The high cost and limited availability of fuel led to a resurgence of interest in alternatives to single occupancy vehicle travel.
     </p>
     <p>
      Transport innovations dating from this period include
      <a href="/wiki/High-occupancy_vehicle_lane" title="High-occupancy vehicle lane">
       high-occupancy vehicle lanes
      </a>
      , citywide
      <a href="/wiki/Carpool" title="Carpool">
       carpool
      </a>
      systems and
      <a href="/wiki/Transportation_demand_management" title="Transportation demand management">
       transportation demand management
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
       Singapore
      </a>
      implemented
      <a href="/wiki/Congestion_pricing" title="Congestion pricing">
       congestion pricing
      </a>
      in the late 1970s, and
      <a href="/wiki/Curitiba" title="Curitiba">
       Curitiba
      </a>
      began implementing its
      <a href="/wiki/Rede_Integrada_de_Transporte" title="Rede Integrada de Transporte">
       Bus Rapid Transit
      </a>
      system in the early 1980s.
     </p>
     <p>
      Relatively low and stable oil prices during the 1980s and 1990s led to significant increases in vehicle travel from 1980–2000, both directly because people chose to travel by car more often and for greater distances, and indirectly because cities developed tracts of suburban housing, distant from shops and from workplaces, now referred to as
      <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">
       urban sprawl
      </a>
      . Trends in freight logistics, including a movement from rail and coastal shipping to road freight and a requirement for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Just-in-time_(business)" title="Just-in-time (business)">
       just in time
      </a>
      deliveries, meant that freight traffic grew faster than general vehicle traffic.
     </p>
     <p>
      At the same time, the academic foundations of the "predict and provide" approach to transport were being questioned, notably by
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Newman_(environmental_scientist)" title="Peter Newman (environmental scientist)">
       Peter Newman
      </a>
      in a set of comparative studies of cities and their transport systems dating from the mid-1980s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The British Government's White Paper on Transport
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      marked a change in direction for transport planning in the UK. In the introduction to the White Paper, Prime Minister
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">
       Tony Blair
      </a>
      stated that
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       We recognise that we cannot simply build our way out of the problems we face. It would be environmentally irresponsible - and would not work.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      A companion document to the White Paper called "Smarter Choices" researched the potential to scale up the small and scattered sustainable transport initiatives then occurring across Britain, and concluded that the comprehensive application of these techniques could reduce peak period car travel in urban areas by over 20%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A similar study
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      by the United States Federal Highway Administration,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      was also released in 2004 and also concluded that a more proactive approach to transportation demand was an important component of overall national transport strategy.
     </p>

     <p>
      Transport systems are major emitters of greenhouse gases, responsible for 23% of world energy-related GHG emissions in 2004, with about three quarters coming from road vehicles. Currently 95% of transport energy comes from petroleum.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-IPCC-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Energy is consumed in the manufacture as well as the use of vehicles, and is
      <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">
       embodied
      </a>
      in transport infrastructure including roads, bridges and railways.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first historical attempts of evaluating the Life Cycle environmental impact of vehicle is due to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodore_Von_Karman" title="Theodore Von Karman">
       Theodore Von Karman
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      After decades in which all the analysis has been focused on emending the Von Karman model, Dewulf and Van Langenhove have introduced an model based on the second law of thermodynamics and exergy analysis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Chester and Orwath,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
      have developed a similar model based on the first law that accounts the necessary costs for the infrastructure. Recently Trancossi has started a long term research activity for energy optimization of transport vehicles by
      <a href="/wiki/Constructal_law" title="Constructal law">
       constructal law
      </a>
      . This activity has allowed producing a preliminary design method that applies to road vehicles,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and has improved the
      <a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">
       EMIPS
      </a>
      method by De Wulf with a more detailed evaluation of the losses and has produced today largest database of calculated data of energy efficiency and emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      He has also introduced a method for evaluating the energy lost for moving the vehicle and the payload. The robustness of the data have been verified by Mittal and Singh,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Centre for Public Policy of Indian Institute of Management Bangalore.
     </p>
     <p>
      The environmental impacts of transport can be reduced by reducing the weight of vehicles,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_35-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      sustainable styles of driving, reducing the friction of tires, encouraging electric and hybrid vehicles, improving the walking and cycling environment in cities, and by enhancing the role of public transport, especially electric rail.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-IPCC-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Green_vehicle" title="Green vehicle">
       Green vehicles
      </a>
      are intended to have less environmental impact than equivalent standard vehicles, although when the environmental impact of a vehicle is assessed over the whole of its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" title="Life cycle assessment">
       life cycle
      </a>
      this may not be the case.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_vehicles" title="Electric vehicles">
       Electric vehicle
      </a>
      technology has the potential to reduce transport CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions, depending on the embodied energy of the vehicle and the source of the electricity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VDI_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VDI-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The primary sources of electricity currently used in most countries (coal, gas, oil) mean that until world electricity production changes substantially, private electric cars will result in the same or higher production of CO2 than petrol equivalent vehicles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Online Electric Vehicle (OLEV), developed by the Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST), is an electric vehicle that can be charged while stationary or driving, thus removing the need to stop at a charging station. The City of Gumi in South Korea runs a 24 km roundtrip along which the bus will receive 100 kW (136 horsepower) electricity at an 85% maximum power transmission efficiency rate while maintaining a 17 cm air gap between the underbody of the vehicle and the road surface. At that power, only a few sections of the road need embedded cables.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle" title="Hybrid vehicle">
       Hybrid vehicles
      </a>
      , which use an
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       internal combustion engine
      </a>
      combined with an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_engine" title="Electric engine">
       electric engine
      </a>
      to achieve better
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">
       fuel efficiency
      </a>
      than a regular combustion engine, are already common.
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas#Transportation_Fuel" title="Natural gas">
       Natural gas
      </a>
      is also used as a transport fuel.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biofuels" title="Biofuels">
       Biofuels
      </a>
      are a less common, and less promising, technology; Brazil met 17% of its transport fuel needs from
      <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel_in_Brazil" title="Ethanol fuel in Brazil">
       bioethanol
      </a>
      in 2007, but the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">
       OECD
      </a>
      has warned that the success of biofuels in Brazil is due to specific local circumstances; internationally, biofuels are forecast to have little or no impact on greenhouse emissions, at significantly higher cost than energy efficiency measures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        [41]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In practice there is a sliding scale of
      <b>
       green transport
      </b>
      depending on the sustainability of the option. Green vehicles are more
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel-efficient" title="Fuel-efficient">
       fuel-efficient
      </a>
      , but only in comparison with standard vehicles, and they still contribute to traffic congestion and road crashes. Well-patronised
      <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">
       public transport
      </a>
      networks based on traditional diesel buses use less fuel per passenger than private vehicles, and are generally safer and use less road space than private vehicles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRB_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-TRB-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Green public transport vehicles including electric trains, trams and
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_bus" title="Electric bus">
       electric buses
      </a>
      combine the advantages of green vehicles with those of sustainable transport choices. Other transport choices with very low environmental impact are
      <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">
       cycling
      </a>
      and other
      <a href="/wiki/Human-powered_transport" title="Human-powered transport">
       human-powered vehicles
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_powered_transport" title="Animal powered transport">
       animal powered transport
      </a>
      . The most common green transport choice, with the least environmental impact is
      <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">
       walking
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Transport on rails boasts an excellent efficiency (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency_in_transportation" title="Fuel efficiency in transportation">
       fuel efficiency in transportation
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_and_social_sustainability">
       Transport and social sustainability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transport and social sustainability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:C_Class_Tram,_Melbourne_-_Jan_2008.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2071" data-file-width="4214" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/C_Class_Tram%2C_Melbourne_-_Jan_2008.jpg/220px-C_Class_Tram%2C_Melbourne_-_Jan_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/C_Class_Tram%2C_Melbourne_-_Jan_2008.jpg/330px-C_Class_Tram%2C_Melbourne_-_Jan_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/C_Class_Tram%2C_Melbourne_-_Jan_2008.jpg/440px-C_Class_Tram%2C_Melbourne_-_Jan_2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:C_Class_Tram,_Melbourne_-_Jan_2008.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A tram in Melbourne, Australia
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Cities with overbuilt roadways have experienced unintended consequences, linked to radical drops in
      <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">
       public transport
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">
       walking
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">
       cycling
      </a>
      . In many cases, streets became void of “life.” Stores, schools, government centers and libraries moved away from central cities, and residents who did not flee to the suburbs experienced a much reduced quality of public space and of public services. As schools were closed their mega-school replacements in outlying areas generated additional traffic; the number of cars on US roads between 7:15 and 8:15 a.m. increases 30% during the school year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Yet another impact was an increase in
      <a href="/wiki/Sedentary_lifestyle" title="Sedentary lifestyle">
       sedentary lifestyles
      </a>
      , causing and complicating a national epidemic of
      <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">
       obesity
      </a>
      , and accompanying dramatically increased health care costs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WHO-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div style="clear:both;">
     </div>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cities">
       Cities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Futurama_city.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="523" data-file-width="428" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Futurama_city.jpg/200px-Futurama_city.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Futurama_city.jpg/300px-Futurama_city.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Futurama_city.jpg/400px-Futurama_city.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Futurama_city.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Futurama_(New_York_World%27s_Fair)" title="Futurama (New York World's Fair)">
         Futurama
        </a>
        , an exhibit at the 1939 New York World's Fair, was sponsored by
        <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">
         General Motors
        </a>
        and showed a vision of the City of Tomorrow.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Transit-oriented_development" title="Transit-oriented development">
       Transit-oriented development
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Cities are shaped by their transport systems. In
      <a href="/wiki/The_City_in_History" title="The City in History">
       The City in History
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Mumford" title="Lewis Mumford">
       Lewis Mumford
      </a>
      documented how the location and layout of cities was shaped around a walkable center, often located near a port or waterway, and with suburbs accessible by animal transport or, later, by rail or tram lines.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1939, the
      <a href="/wiki/Futurama_(New_York_World%27s_Fair)" title="Futurama (New York World's Fair)">
       New York World's Fair
      </a>
      included a model of an imagined city, built around a car-based transport system. In this "greater and better world of tomorrow", residential, commercial and industrial areas were separated, and skyscrapers loomed over a network of urban motorways. These ideas captured the popular imagination, and are credited with influencing city planning from the 1940s to the 1970s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:45intoI-10_2.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/45intoI-10_2.jpg/220px-45intoI-10_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/45intoI-10_2.jpg/330px-45intoI-10_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/45intoI-10_2.jpg/440px-45intoI-10_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:45intoI-10_2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_10_(Texas)" title="Interstate 10 (Texas)">
         Interstate 10
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_45_(Texas)" title="Interstate 45 (Texas)">
         Interstate 45
        </a>
        near downtown
        <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">
         Houston
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
         Texas
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The popularity of the car in the post-war era led to major changes in the structure and function of cities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
      There was some opposition to these changes at the time. The writings of
      <a href="/wiki/Jane_Jacobs" title="Jane Jacobs">
       Jane Jacobs
      </a>
      , in particular
      <a href="/wiki/The_Death_and_Life_of_Great_American_Cities" title="The Death and Life of Great American Cities">
       The Death and Life of Great American Cities
      </a>
      provide a poignant reminder of what was lost in this transformation, and a record of community efforts to resist these changes. Lewis Mumford asked "is the city for cars or for people?"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        [46]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Appleyard" title="Donald Appleyard">
       Donald Appleyard
      </a>
      documented the consequences for communities of increasing car traffic in "The View from the Road" (1964) and in the UK,
      <a href="/wiki/Mayer_Hillman" title="Mayer Hillman">
       Mayer Hillman
      </a>
      first published research into the impacts of traffic on child independent mobility in 1971.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        [47]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite these notes of caution, trends in car ownership,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dft.gov.uk_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-dft.gov.uk-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      car use and fuel consumption continued steeply upward throughout the post-war period.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mainstream transport planning in Europe has, by contrast, never been based on assumptions that the private car was the best or only solution for urban mobility. For example, the
      <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
       Dutch
      </a>
      Transport Structure Scheme has since the 1970s required that demand for additional vehicle capacity only be met "if the contribution to societal welfare is positive", and since 1990 has included an explicit target to halve the rate of growth in vehicle traffic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some cities outside Europe have also consistently linked transport to sustainability and to
      <a href="/wiki/Land-use_planning" title="Land-use planning">
       land-use planning
      </a>
      , notably
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curitiba_Master_Plan" title="Curitiba Master Plan">
       Curitiba, Brazil
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon#Transportation" title="Portland, Oregon">
       Portland, Oregon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Transportation_in_Vancouver" title="Transportation in Vancouver">
       Vancouver, Canada
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graph_of_emissions_by_city.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="627" data-file-width="600" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Graph_of_emissions_by_city.jpg/300px-Graph_of_emissions_by_city.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Graph_of_emissions_by_city.jpg/450px-Graph_of_emissions_by_city.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7e/Graph_of_emissions_by_city.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graph_of_emissions_by_city.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Greenhouse gas emissions from transport vary widely, even for cities of comparable wealth. Source:
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UITP" title="UITP">
         UITP
        </a>
        , Mobility in Cities Database
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      There are major differences in transport energy consumption between cities; an average U.S. urban dweller uses 24 times more energy annually for private transport than a Chinese urban resident, and almost four times as much as a European urban dweller. These differences cannot be explained by wealth alone but are closely linked to the rates of walking, cycling, and public transport use and to enduring features of the city including
      <a href="/wiki/Urban_density" title="Urban density">
       urban density
      </a>
      and urban design.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenworthy_49-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kenworthy-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mk_Stettin_Hafen2.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Mk_Stettin_Hafen2.jpg/220px-Mk_Stettin_Hafen2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Mk_Stettin_Hafen2.jpg/330px-Mk_Stettin_Hafen2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Mk_Stettin_Hafen2.jpg/440px-Mk_Stettin_Hafen2.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mk_Stettin_Hafen2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A bypass the Old Town in
        <a href="/wiki/Szczecin" title="Szczecin">
         Szczecin
        </a>
        , Poland
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The cities and nations that have invested most heavily in car-based transport systems are now the least environmentally sustainable, as measured by per capita
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuel
      </a>
      use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenworthy_49-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kenworthy-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The social and economic sustainability of car-based
      <a href="/wiki/Transportation_engineering" title="Transportation engineering">
       transportation engineering
      </a>
      has also been questioned. Within the United States, residents of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_Sprawl" title="Urban Sprawl">
       sprawling
      </a>
      cities make more frequent and longer car trips, while residents of traditional urban neighbourhoods make a similar number of trips, but travel shorter distances and walk, cycle and use transit more often.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has been calculated that New York residents save $19 billion each year simply by owning fewer cars and driving less than the average American.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A less car intensive means of urban transport is
      <a href="/wiki/Carsharing" title="Carsharing">
       carsharing
      </a>
      , which is becoming popular in North America and Europe, and according to
      <a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">
       The Economist
      </a>
      , carsharing can reduce car ownership at an estimated rate of one rental car replacing 15 owned vehicles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        [52]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Car sharing has also begun in the developing world, where traffic and urban density is often worse than in developed countries. Companies like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoom_(Car-Sharing)" title="Zoom (Car-Sharing)">
       Zoom
      </a>
      in India, eHi in China, and Carrot in Mexico, are bringing car-sharing to developing countries in an effort to reduce car-related pollution, ameliorate traffic, and expand the number of people who have access to cars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        [53]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">
       European Commission
      </a>
      adopted the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Action_Plan_on_urban_mobility&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Action Plan on urban mobility (page does not exist)">
       Action Plan on urban mobility
      </a>
      on 2009-09-30 for sustainable urban mobility. The European Commission will conduct a review of the implementation of the Action Plan in the year 2012, and will assess the need for further action. In 2007, 72% of the European population lived in urban areas, which are key to growth and employment. Cities need efficient transport systems to support their economy and the welfare of their inhabitants. Around 85% of the EU’s
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">
       GDP
      </a>
      is generated in cities. Urban areas face today the challenge of making transport sustainable in environmental (
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">
       air pollution
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Noise" title="Noise">
       noise
      </a>
      ) and competitiveness (
      <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">
       congestion
      </a>
      ) terms while at the same time addressing social concerns. These range from the need to respond to
      <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">
       health
      </a>
      problems and
      <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">
       demographic
      </a>
      trends, fostering economic and social cohesion to taking into account the needs of
      <a href="/wiki/Persons_with_reduced_mobility" title="Persons with reduced mobility">
       persons with reduced mobility
      </a>
      , families and children.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        [54]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Policies_and_governance">
       Policies and governance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Policies and governance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">
       urban sprawl
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Sustainable transport policies have their greatest impact at the city level. Outside Western Europe, cities which have consistently included sustainability as a key consideration in transport and land use planning include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curitiba_Master_Plan" title="Curitiba Master Plan">
       Curitiba, Brazil
      </a>
      ;
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bogota,_Colombia" title="Bogota, Colombia">
       Bogota, Colombia
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon#Transportation" title="Portland, Oregon">
       Portland, Oregon
      </a>
      ; and
      <a href="/wiki/Transportation_in_Vancouver" title="Transportation in Vancouver">
       Vancouver, Canada
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">
       state of Victoria
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      passed legislation in 2010 - the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transport_Integration_Act" title="Transport Integration Act">
       Transport Integration Act
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        [55]
       </a>
      </sup>
      - to compel its transport agencies to actively consider sustainability issues including climate change impacts in transport policy, planning and operations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        [56]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Many other cities throughout the world have recognised the need to link sustainability and transport policies, for example by joining Cities for Climate Protection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        [57]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Community_and_grassroots_action">
       Community and grassroots action
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Community and grassroots action">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Sustainable transport is fundamentally a grassroots movement, albeit one which is now recognised as of citywide, national and international significance.
     </p>
     <p>
      Whereas it started as a movement driven by environmental concerns, over these last years there has been increased emphasis on social equity and fairness issues, and in particular the need to ensure proper access and services for lower income groups and people with mobility limitations, including the fast-growing population of older citizens. Many of the people exposed to the most vehicle noise, pollution and safety risk have been those who do not own, or cannot drive cars, and those for whom the cost of car ownership causes a severe financial burden.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        [58]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An organization called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenxc" title="Greenxc">
       Greenxc
      </a>
      started in 2011 created a national awareness campaign in the United States encouraging people to
      <a href="/wiki/Carpool" title="Carpool">
       carpool
      </a>
      by ride-sharing cross country stopping over at various destinations along the way and documenting their travel through video footage, posts and photography.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenXC_Website_59-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GreenXC_Website-59">
        [59]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ride-sharing reduces individual's carbon footprint by allowing several people to use one car instead of everyone using individual cars.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_trends">
       Recent trends
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recent trends">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Car travel increased steadily throughout the twentieth century, but trends since 2000 have been more complex.
      <a href="/wiki/2000s_energy_crisis" title="2000s energy crisis">
       Oil price rises from 2003
      </a>
      have been linked to a decline in per capita fuel use for private vehicle travel in the USA,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        [60]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Britain and Australia. In 2008, global oil consumption fell by 0.8% overall, with significant declines in consumption in North America, Western Europe, and parts of Asia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        [61]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other factors affecting a decline in driving, at least in America, include the retirement of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_Boomer" title="Baby Boomer">
       Baby Boomers
      </a>
      who now drive less, preference for other travel modes (such as transit) by younger age cohorts, the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">
       Great Recession
      </a>
      , and the rising use of technology (internet, mobile devices) which have made travel less necessary and possibly less attractive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       Green transport
      </i>
      is often used as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenwash" title="Greenwash">
       greenwash
      </a>
      marketing technique for products which are not proven to make a positive contribution to environmental sustainability. Such claims can be legally challenged. For instance Norway's
      <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Consumer_Ombudsman" title="Norwegian Consumer Ombudsman">
       consumer ombudsman
      </a>
      has targeted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automaker" title="Automaker">
       automakers
      </a>
      who claim that their cars are "green", "clean" or "environmentally friendly". Manufacturers risk fines if they fail to drop the words.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Competition_and_Consumer_Commission" title="Australian Competition and Consumer Commission">
       Australian Competition and Consumer Commission
      </a>
      (ACCC) describes
      <i>
       green
      </i>
      claims on products as
      <i>
       very vague
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       inviting consumers to give a wide range of meanings to the claim, which risks misleading them
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACCC_TPA_64-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ACCC_TPA-64">
        [64]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2008 the ACCC forced a car retailer to stop its
      <i>
       green
      </i>
      marketing of Saab cars, which was found by the Australian Federal Court as
      <i>
       misleading
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        [65]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
