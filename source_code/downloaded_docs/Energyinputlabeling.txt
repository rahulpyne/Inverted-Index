    Energy input labeling
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">

     </div>
     <p>
      The term
      <b>
       energy input labeling
      </b>
      involved producers of goods and services determining how much energy is used to produce their product, and then including that information on their product packaging.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref-1_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ref-1-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Energy input labeling is sometimes known by the acronym EIL. Energy input labeling provides the advantage of knowing how much energy was used to produce a product, but it does not indicate how much energy a product uses to operate, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union_energy_label" title="European Union energy label">
       European Union energy label
      </a>
      or the
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_rating_label" title="Energy rating label">
       Energy rating label
      </a>
      used in
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      , and is not in itself a standard for energy efficiency such as
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_Saving_Trust_Recommended" title="Energy Saving Trust Recommended">
       Energy Saving Trust Recommended
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">
       Energy Star
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_input_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Energy input labeling originated as a project by several energy and economics activists to explore
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_accounting" title="Energy accounting">
       energy accounting
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_in_industry">
       Usage in industry
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_input_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage in industry">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Energy input labeling is intended to be easy for producers to implement,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref-1_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ref-1-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      At minimum, they can report and label the energy used by their firm to produce products, which is called "Energy Inputs Added", some sometimes merely "Energy Added." If a firm is able to also account for all of the energy imputed by its suppliers, then a firm can report and label "Total Energy Inputs" or "Total Energy", but this is rare. Energy Input Labeling is being used and further developed by the European Organization for Sustainability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref-1_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ref-1-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">
       Japan
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_input_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Japan">
        edit

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Japan, the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.climatepolicy.jp/thesis/pdf/09035dp.pdf" rel="nofollow">
       Top Runner Program
      </a>
      is run in which new appliances are regularly tested on efficiency, and the most efficient ones are made the standard
