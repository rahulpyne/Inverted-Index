     <p>
      In an
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_system" title="Electric power system">
       electric power system
      </a>
      , a
      <b>
       fault
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       fault current
      </b>
      is any abnormal
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">
       electric current
      </a>
      . For example, a
      <a href="/wiki/Short_circuit" title="Short circuit">
       short circuit
      </a>
      is a fault in which current bypasses the normal load. An open-circuit fault occurs if a circuit is interrupted by some failure. In
      <a href="/wiki/Three-phase_electric_power" title="Three-phase electric power">
       three-phase
      </a>
      systems, a fault may involve one or more phases and ground, or may occur only between phases. In a "ground fault" or "earth fault", current flows into the earth. The
      <a href="/wiki/Prospective_short_circuit_current" title="Prospective short circuit current">
       prospective short circuit current
      </a>
      of a predictable fault can be calculated for most situations. In power systems, protective devices can detect fault conditions and operate
      <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">
       circuit breakers
      </a>
      and other devices to limit the loss of service due to a failure.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a
      <a href="/wiki/Polyphase_system" title="Polyphase system">
       polyphase system
      </a>
      , a fault may affect all phases equally which is a "symmetrical fault". If only some phases are affected, the resulting "asymmetrical fault" becomes more complicated to analyse. The analysis of these types of faults is often simplified by using methods such as
      <a href="/wiki/Symmetrical_components" title="Symmetrical components">
       symmetrical components
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The design of systems to detect and interrupt power system faults is the main objective of
      <a href="/wiki/Power-system_protection" title="Power-system protection">
       power-system protection
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transient_fault">
       Transient fault
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Transient fault">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       transient fault
      </b>
      is a fault that is no longer present if power is disconnected for a short time and then restored; or an insulation fault which only temporarily affects a device's dielectric properties which are restored after a short time. Many faults in
      <a href="/wiki/Overhead_power_line" title="Overhead power line">
       overhead power lines
      </a>
      are transient in nature. When a fault occurs, equipment used for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_system_protection" title="Power system protection">
       power system protection
      </a>
      operate to isolate the area of the fault. A transient fault will then clear and the power-line can be returned to service. Typical examples of transient faults include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       momentary tree contact
      </li>
      <li>
       bird or other animal contact
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Lightning_strike" title="Lightning strike">
        lightning strike
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Conductor_clashing" title="Conductor clashing">
        conductor clashing
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
       Transmission
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">
       distribution
      </a>
      systems use an automatic re-close function which is commonly used on overhead lines to attempt to restore power in the event of a transient fault. This functionality is not as common on underground systems as faults there are typically of a
      <a href="#Persistent_fault">
       persistent
      </a>
      nature. Transient faults may still cause damage both at the site of the original fault or elsewhere in the network as fault current is generated.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Persistent_fault">
       Persistent fault
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Persistent fault">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       persistent fault
      </b>
      does not disappear when power is disconnected. Faults in underground
      <a href="/wiki/Power_cable" title="Power cable">
       power cables
      </a>
      are most often persistent due to mechanical damage to the cable, but are sometimes transient in nature due to lightning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Symmetric_fault">
       Symmetric fault
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Symmetric fault">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       symmetric
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       balanced fault
      </b>
      affects each of the three phases equally. In transmission line faults, roughly 5% are symmetric.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is in contrast to an asymmetrical fault, where the three phases are not affected equally.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Asymmetric_fault">
       Asymmetric fault
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Asymmetric fault">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      An
      <b>
       asymmetric
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       unbalanced fault
      </b>
      does not affect each of the three phases equally. Common types of asymmetric faults, and their causes:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        line-to-line
       </i>
       - a
       <a href="/wiki/Short_circuit" title="Short circuit">
        short circuit
       </a>
       between lines, caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">
        ionization
       </a>
       of air, or when lines come into physical contact, for example due to a broken
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_insulation" title="Electrical insulation">
        insulator
       </a>
       . In transmission line faults, roughly 5% - 10% are asymmetric line-to-line faults.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        line-to-ground
       </i>
       - a short circuit between one line and ground, very often caused by physical contact, for example due to
       <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">
        lightning
       </a>
       or other
       <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">
        storm
       </a>
       damage. In transmission line faults, roughly 65% - 70% are asymmetric line-to-ground faults.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        double line-to-ground
       </i>
       - two lines come into contact with the ground (and each other), also commonly due to storm damage. In transmission line faults, roughly 15% - 20% are asymmetric double line-to-ground.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bolted_fault">
       Bolted fault
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bolted fault">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      One extreme is where the fault has zero impedance, giving the maximum
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prospective_short-circuit_current" title="Prospective short-circuit current">
       prospective short-circuit current
      </a>
      . Notionally, all the conductors are considered connected to ground as if by a metallic conductor; this is called a "bolted fault". It would be unusual in a well-designed power system to have a metallic short circuit to ground but such faults can occur by mischance. In one type of transmission line protection, a "bolted fault" is deliberately introduced to speed up operation of protective devices.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Realistic_faults">
       Realistic faults
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Realistic faults">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Realistically, the resistance in a fault can be from close to zero to fairly high. A large amount of power may be consumed in the fault, compared with the zero-impedance case where the power is zero. Also, arcs are highly non-linear, so a simple resistance is not a good model. All possible cases need to be considered for a good analysis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Arcing_fault">
       Arcing fault
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arcing fault">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Where the system voltage is high enough, an
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">
       electric arc
      </a>
      may form between power system conductors and ground. Such an arc can have a relatively high impedance (compared to the normal operating levels of the system) and can be difficult to detect by simple overcurrent protection. For example, an arc of several hundred amperes on a circuit normally carrying a thousand amperes may not trip overcurrent circuit breakers but can do enormous damage to bus bars or cables before it becomes a complete short circuit. Utility, industrial, and commercial power systems have additional protection devices to detect relatively small but undesired currents escaping to ground. In residential wiring, electrical regulations may now require
      <a href="/wiki/Arc-fault_circuit_interrupter" title="Arc-fault circuit interrupter">
       Arc-fault circuit interrupters
      </a>
      on building wiring circuits, to detect small arcs before they cause damage or a fire.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">
       Analysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Analysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Symmetric faults can be analyzed via the same methods as any other phenomena in power systems, and in fact many
      <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">
       software
      </a>
      tools exist to accomplish this type of analysis automatically (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_flow_study" title="Power flow study">
       power flow study
      </a>
      ). However, there is another method which is as accurate and is usually more instructive.
     </p>
     <p>
      First, some simplifying assumptions are made. It is assumed that all
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">
       electrical generators
      </a>
      in the system are in phase, and operating at the nominal
      <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">
       voltage
      </a>
      of the system.
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">
       Electric motors
      </a>
      can also be considered to be generators, because when a fault occurs, they usually supply rather than draw power. The voltages and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_(electricity)" title="Current (electricity)">
       currents
      </a>
      are then calculated for this
      <i>
       base case
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Next, the location of the fault is considered to be supplied with a negative voltage source, equal to the voltage at that location in the base case, while all other sources are set to zero. This method makes use of the principle of
      <a href="/wiki/Superposition_principle" title="Superposition principle">
       superposition
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      To obtain a more accurate result, these calculations should be performed separately for three separate time ranges:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        subtransient
       </i>
       is first, and is associated with the largest currents
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Transient_state" title="Transient state">
         transient
        </a>
       </i>
       comes between subtransient and steady-state
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        steady-state
       </i>
       occurs after all the transients have had time to settle
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      An asymmetric fault breaks the underlying assumptions used in three-phase power, namely that the
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_load" title="Electrical load">
       load
      </a>
      is balanced on all three phases. Consequently, it is impossible to
      <i>
       directly
      </i>
      use tools such as the
      <a href="/wiki/One-line_diagram" title="One-line diagram">
       one-line diagram
      </a>
      , where only one phase is considered. However, due to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">
       linearity
      </a>
      of power systems, it is usual to consider the resulting
      <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">
       voltages
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_(electricity)" title="Current (electricity)">
       currents
      </a>
      as a superposition of
      <a href="/wiki/Symmetrical_components" title="Symmetrical components">
       symmetrical components
      </a>
      , to which three-phase analysis can be applied.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the method of symmetric components, the power system is seen as a
      <a href="/wiki/Superposition_theorem" title="Superposition theorem">
       superposition
      </a>
      of three components:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       a
       <i>
        positive-sequence
       </i>
       component, in which the phases are in the same order as the original system, i.e.,
       <i>
        a-b-c
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       a
       <i>
        negative-sequence
       </i>
       component, in which the phases are in the opposite order as the original system, i.e.,
       <i>
        a-c-b
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       a
       <i>
        zero-sequence
       </i>
       component, which is not truly a three-phase system, but instead all three phases are in phase with each other.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      To determine the currents resulting from an asymmetrical fault, one must first know the
      <a href="/wiki/Per-unit_system" title="Per-unit system">
       per-unit
      </a>
      zero-, positive-, and negative-sequence impedances of the transmission lines, generators, and transformers involved. Three separate circuits are then constructed using these impedances. The individual circuits are then connected together in a particular arrangement that depends upon the type of fault being studied (this can be found in most power systems textbooks). Once the sequence circuits are properly connected, the network can then be analyzed using classical circuit analysis techniques. The solution results in voltages and currents that exist as symmetrical components; these must be transformed back into phase values by using the
      <a href="/wiki/Symmetrical_components" title="Symmetrical components">
       <b>
        A
       </b>
       matrix
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Analysis of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prospective_short-circuit_current" title="Prospective short-circuit current">
       prospective short-circuit current
      </a>
      is required for selection of protective devices such as
      <a href="/wiki/Fuse_(electrical)" title="Fuse (electrical)">
       fuses
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">
       circuit breakers
      </a>
      . If a circuit is to be properly protected, the fault current must be high enough to operate the protective device within as short a time as possible; also the protective device must be able to withstand the fault current and extinguish any resulting arcs without itself being destroyed or sustaining the arc for any significant length of time.
     </p>
     <p>
      The magnitude of fault currents differ widely depending on the type of earthing system used, the installation's supply type and earthing system, and its proximity to the supply. For example, for a domestic UK 230 V, 60 A TN-S or USA 120 V/240 V supply, fault currents may be a few thousand amperes. Large low-voltage networks with multiple sources may have fault levels of 300,000 amperes. A high-resistance-grounded system may restrict line to ground fault current to only 5 amperes. Prior to selecting protective devices, prospective fault current must be measured reliably at the origin of the installation and at the furthest point of each circuit, and this information applied properly to the application of the circuits.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Detecting_and_locating_faults">
       Detecting and locating faults
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Detecting and locating faults">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Overhead power lines are easiest to diagnose since the problem is usually obvious, e.g., a tree has fallen across the line, or a utility pole is broken and the conductors are lying on the ground.
     </p>
     <p>
      Locating faults in a cable system can be done either with the circuit de-energized, or in some cases, with the circuit under power. Fault location techniques can be broadly divided into terminal methods, which use voltages and currents measured at the ends of the cable, and tracer methods, which require inspection along the length of the cable. Terminal methods can be used to locate the general area of the fault, to expedite tracing on a long or buried cable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In very simple wiring systems, the fault location is often found through inspection of the wires. In complex wiring systems (for example, aircraft wiring) where the wires may be hidden, wiring faults are located with a
      <a href="/wiki/Time-domain_reflectometer" title="Time-domain reflectometer">
       Time-domain reflectometer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The time domain reflectometer sends a pulse down the wire and then analyzes the returning reflected pulse to identify faults within the electrical wire.
     </p>
     <p>
      In historic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submarine_telegraph_cable" title="Submarine telegraph cable">
       submarine telegraph cables
      </a>
      , sensitive
      <a href="/wiki/Galvanometer" title="Galvanometer">
       galvanometers
      </a>
      were used to measure fault currents; by testing at both ends of a faulted cable, the fault location could be isolated to within a few miles, which allowed the cable to be grappled up and repaired. The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Murray_loop_bridge" title="Murray loop bridge">
        Murray loop
       </a>
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       Varley loop
      </i>
      were two types of connections for locating faults in cables
     </p>
     <p>
      Sometimes an insulation fault in a power cable will not show up at lower voltages. A "thumper" test set applies a high-energy, high-voltage pulse to the cable. Fault location is done by listening for the sound of the discharge at the fault. While this test contributes to damage at the cable site, it is practical because the faulted location would have to be re-insulated when found in any case.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a high resistance grounded distribution system, a feeder may develop a fault to ground but the system continues in operation. The faulted, but energized, feeder can be found with a ring-type current transformer collecting all the phase wires of the circuit; only the circuit containing a fault to ground will show a net unbalanced current. To make the ground fault current easier to detect, the grounding resistor of the system may be switched between two values so that the fault current pulses.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Batteries">
       Batteries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fault_(power_engineering)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Batteries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The prospective fault current of larger batteries, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Deep_cycle_battery" title="Deep cycle battery">
       deep-cycle batteries
      </a>
      used in
      <a href="/wiki/Stand-alone_power_system" title="Stand-alone power system">
       stand-alone power systems
      </a>
      , is often given by the manufacturer.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Australia, when this information is not given, the prospective fault current in amperes "should be considered to be 6 times the nominal battery capacity at the
      <i>
       C
      </i>
      <sub>
       120
      </sub>
      A·h rate," according to AS 4086 part 2 (Appendix H).
     </p>
