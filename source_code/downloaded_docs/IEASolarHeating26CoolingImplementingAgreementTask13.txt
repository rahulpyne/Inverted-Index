     <p>
      <b>
       IEA Solar Heating &amp; Cooling Implementing Agreement - Task 13
      </b>
      (Solar Low Energy Buildings) was an international effort organised by one of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">
       International Energy Agency
      </a>
      's Implementing Agreements to build several solar
      <a href="/wiki/Low-energy_house" title="Low-energy house">
       low-energy houses
      </a>
      . This took place between 1989 and 1993(?). The Implementing Agreement for a Programme to Develop and Test Solar Heating and Cooling Systems is one of 40 research and development and deployment programmes, which are at the core of the IEA's International Energy Technology Co-operation Programme.
     </p>
     <p>
      The idea behind Task 13 was to push
      <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">
       construction
      </a>
      technology towards its limits to achieve the lowest possible total purchased
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_energy_resources" title="List of energy resources">
       energy
      </a>
      consumption. Task 13 was part of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">
       IEA
      </a>
      <i>
       Solar Heating and Cooling Programme
      </i>
      , to test the designs and techniques, and to monitor their performance.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design">
       Design
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=IEA_Solar_Heating_%26_Cooling_Implementing_Agreement_Task_13&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On average, the houses were designed to required 44
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KWh" title="KWh">
       kWh
      </a>
      /m², 75% lower than the average 172 kWh/m² that would have been required had the houses been built to normal standards. (Analysis of 11 of the houses in use indicated that total savings made in practice was actually 60%
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.forskningsdatabasen.dk/cgi-bin/egwddf2/6969/screen.tcl/host=ddfcat&amp;host=ddf&amp;gattr1=parseOp&amp;v=1&amp;fe=10&amp;e11=indoor&amp;f11=all&amp;l11=and&amp;s1=sortkey&amp;page=28&amp;name=show_record&amp;norec=1&amp;ppage=30&amp;lposddfcat=7&amp;lposddf=24&amp;service=ddf%32&amp;context1=b&amp;lang=eng" rel="nofollow">
       [1]
      </a>
      ).The 44 kWh/m² resulted from:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
        Electricity
       </a>
       – 18 kWh/m²
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_heating" title="Space heating">
        Space heating
       </a>
       – 14 kWh/m²
      </li>
      <li>
       Water heating – 11 kWh/m²
      </li>
      <li>
       Cooling – 1 kWh/m²
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In addition there was an average solar contribution designed to average 37 kWh/m², from a combination of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">
       passive solar
      </a>
      gains,
      <a href="/wiki/Active_solar" title="Active solar">
       active solar
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">
       photovoltaics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The buildings were constructed to be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airtight" title="Airtight">
       airtight
      </a>
      , be
      <a href="/wiki/Superinsulation" title="Superinsulation">
       superinsulated
      </a>
      to roughly double normal standards, and to minimise
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_bridge" title="Thermal bridge">
       thermal bridges
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">
       Masonry
      </a>
      and several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber_frame" title="Timber frame">
       timber framed
      </a>
      methods were represented, along with a novel
      <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">
       steel
      </a>
      strengthened
      <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">
       polystyrene
      </a>
      block walls were used. The Berlin "Zero Heating Energy House" included a 20m³ (700 cubic feet)
      <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">
       seasonal thermal store
      </a>
      .
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://wire.ises.org/wire/doclibs/EuroSun96.nsf/id/F7DE064B758101BDC12565E6003737C3/$File/paper.pdf" rel="nofollow">
       [2]
      </a>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lessons_learned">
       Lessons learned
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=IEA_Solar_Heating_%26_Cooling_Implementing_Agreement_Task_13&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lessons learned">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Among the lessons learned were that:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Airtightness was difficult to achieve
      </li>
      <li>
       Ventilation systems could suffer from noise and draft problems
      </li>
      <li>
       Care was needed to design out summer overheating
      </li>
      <li>
       Simple installations and systems were easier for the residents to understand
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      For a report on the Task 13 findings, see
      <i>
       Energy Design Update
      </i>
      , December 2003.
     </p>
