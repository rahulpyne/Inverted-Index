     <p>
      <b>
       Rocky Mountain Institute
      </b>
      (RMI) is an organization in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      dedicated to research, publication, consulting, and lecturing in the general field of
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
       sustainability
      </a>
      , with a special focus on profitable innovations for
      <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">
       energy and resource efficiency
      </a>
      . RMI was established in 1982 and has grown into a broad-based institution with 150+ staff and an annual budget of some $30 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      RMI's work is independent and non-adversarial, with a strong emphasis on market-based solutions. The institute, including recently merged
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_War_Room" title="Carbon War Room">
       Carbon War Room
      </a>
      , operates across 9 different initiative areas: Shipping Efficiency, Sunshine for Mines, Smart Island Economies, Reinventing Fire China, Mobility Transformation, Trucking Efficiency, Sustainable Aviation, Buildings, Electricity Platform and Renewable Solutions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The work of RMI has benefited more than 80
      <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">
       Fortune 500
      </a>
      companies in a diverse range of sectors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      RMI is headquartered in
      <a href="/wiki/Basalt,_Colorado" title="Basalt, Colorado">
       Basalt, Colorado
      </a>
      , and also maintains offices in
      <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">
       Boulder, Colorado
      </a>
      , New York City, Washington D.C. and Beijing, China.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocky_Mountain_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1978, experimental physicist
      <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">
       Amory Lovins
      </a>
      had published many books, consulted widely, and was active in energy affairs in some fifteen countries as synthesist and lobbyist. Lovins is a main theorist of the
      <a href="/wiki/Soft_energy_path" title="Soft energy path">
       soft energy path
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Later in 1979, Lovins married
      <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Lovins" title="Hunter Lovins">
       L. Hunter Sheldon
      </a>
      , a lawyer, forester, and social scientist. Hunter received her undergraduate degree in
      <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
       sociology
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political" title="Political">
       political
      </a>
      studies from
      <a href="/wiki/Pitzer_College" title="Pitzer College">
       Pitzer College
      </a>
      , and her
      <a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">
       J.D.
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Loyola_Marymount_University" title="Loyola Marymount University">
       Loyola Marymount
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Loyola_Law_School" title="Loyola Law School">
       School of Law
      </a>
      . In 1982, Amory and Hunter founded Rocky Mountain Institute, based in Colorado. Together with a group of colleagues, the Lovinses fostered efficient resource use and policy development that they believed would promote global security. RMI ultimately grew into an organization with a staff of around fifty. By the mid 1980s, the Lovinses were being featured on major network TV programs, such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">
        60 Minutes
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      At RMI's headquarters the south-facing building complex is so energy-efficient that, even with local -40 °F (-40 °C) winter temperatures, the building interiors can maintain a comfortable temperature solely from the sunlight admitted plus the body heat of the people who work there. The environment can actually nurture semi-tropical and tropical indoor plants.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Lovinses described the "hard energy path" as involving inefficient liquid-fuel automotive transport, as well as giant centralized electricity-generating facilities, often burning
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuels
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">
       petroleum
      </a>
      , or harnessing a
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">
       fission reaction
      </a>
      , greatly complicated by electricity wastage and loss. The "soft energy path" which they wholly preferred involves efficient use of energy, diversity of energy production methods (and matched in scale and quality to end uses), and special reliance on "soft technologies" (alternative technology) such as
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
       solar
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
       biofuels
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">
       geothermal
      </a>
      . According to the Institute, large-scale electricity production facilities had an important place, but it was a place that they were already filling in the middle 1970s; in general, more would not be needed. In a 1989 speech, Amory Lovins introduced the related concept of
      <a href="/wiki/Negawatt_power" title="Negawatt power">
       Negawatt power
      </a>
      , in which the creation of a market for trading increased efficiency could supply additional electrical energy to consumers without increasing generation capacity—such as building more power plants.
     </p>
     <p>
      In recent years, RMI has convened a team of designers and engineers to develop a super-efficient prototype automobile, which they've dubbed the
      <a href="/wiki/Hypercar" title="Hypercar">
       Hypercar
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 2014, RMI merged with
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_War_Room" title="Carbon War Room">
       Carbon War Room
      </a>
      , an organization with similar goals but a different approach
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMICWRmerge_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-RMICWRmerge-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_vehicles">
       Electric vehicles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocky_Mountain_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electric vehicles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In January 2008, led by John E. Waters,
      <a href="/wiki/Bright_Automotive" title="Bright Automotive">
       Bright Automotive
      </a>
      launched from RMI with the goal of building on the work of a consortium of organizations, including
      <a href="/wiki/Alcoa" title="Alcoa">
       Alcoa
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Google.org" title="Google.org">
       Google.org
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Controls" title="Johnson Controls">
       Johnson Controls
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turner_Foundation" title="Turner Foundation">
       Turner Foundation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bright_IDEA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bright IDEA (page does not exist)">
       Bright IDEA
      </a>
      is a brand new, 100 miles per US gallon (2.4 L/100 km; 120 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -imp
       </small>
      </sub>
      )
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">
       plug-in hybrid
      </a>
      electric (PHEV)
      <a href="/wiki/Fleet_vehicle" title="Fleet vehicle">
       fleet vehicle
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has launched
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bright_eSolutions" title="Bright eSolutions">
       Bright eSolutions
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      to consult on engineering, design,
      <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">
       powertrain
      </a>
      , battery technology and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid_conversion" title="Plug-in hybrid conversion">
       plug-in hybrid conversion
      </a>
      technology services. Bright Automotive has announced its first conversion contract: the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Tank-automotive_and_Armaments_Command" title="U.S. Army Tank-automotive and Armaments Command">
       U.S. Army Tank-automotive and Armaments Command
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TACOM" title="TACOM">
       TACOM
      </a>
      ) to convert military non-combat vehicles into a parallel
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PHEV" title="PHEV">
       PHEV
      </a>
      for evaluation, including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V2G" title="V2G">
       V2G
      </a>
      testing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Energy" title="Advanced Energy">
       Advanced Energy
      </a>
      , in partnership with Rocky Mountain Institute, has announced a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Request_for_Information" title="Request for Information">
       Request for Information
      </a>
      (RFI) for
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_Vehicle_Supply_Equipment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Electric Vehicle Supply Equipment (page does not exist)">
       Electric Vehicle Supply Equipment
      </a>
      (EVSE) specific to charging stations for
      <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">
       plug-in electric vehicles
      </a>
      (EV).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">
       Awards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      RMI co-founder Amory Lovins has received nine honorary doctorates, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MacArthur_Fellowship" title="MacArthur Fellowship">
       MacArthur Fellowship
      </a>
      , the Heinz, Lindbergh,
      <a href="/wiki/Right_Livelihood_Award" title="Right Livelihood Award">
       Right Livelihood Award
      </a>
      , World Technology, and Time
      <a href="/wiki/Heroes_of_the_Environment" title="Heroes of the Environment">
       Hero for the Planet
      </a>
      awards, the Benjamin Franklin and Happold Medals, the Nissan, Shingo, Mitchell, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onassis_Prize" title="Onassis Prize">
       Onassis Prizes
