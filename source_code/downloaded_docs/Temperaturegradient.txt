   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Temperature gradient
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       temperature gradient
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Physical_quantity" title="Physical quantity">
       physical quantity
      </a>
      that describes in which direction and at what rate the
      <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
       temperature
      </a>
      changes the most rapidly around a particular location. The temperature gradient is a
      <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">
       dimensional quantity
      </a>
      expressed in
      <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">
       units
      </a>
      of degrees (on a particular temperature scale) per unit length. The
      <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">
       SI
      </a>
      unit is
      <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
       kelvin
      </a>
      per meter (K/m).
     </p>
     <p>
      Temperature gradients in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
       atmosphere
      </a>
      are important in the atmospheric sciences (
      <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">
       meteorology
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">
       climatology
      </a>
      and related fields).
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_description">
       Mathematical description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_processes">
       Physical processes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperature_gradient&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical processes">
        edit

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">
       Climatology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperature_gradient&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climatology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On a global and annual basis, the dynamics of the atmosphere (and the oceans) can be understood as attempting to reduce the large difference of temperature between the
      <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">
       poles
      </a>
      and the equator by redistributing warm and cold air and water, known as Earth's heat engine.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorology">
       Meteorology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperature_gradient&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Meteorology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Differences in air temperature between different locations are critical in weather forecasting and climate. The absorption of solar light at or near the planetary surface increases the temperature gradient and may result in
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      (a major process of
      <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">
       cloud
      </a>
      formation, often associated with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">
       precipitation
      </a>
      ).
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">
       Meteorological fronts
      </a>
      are regions where the horizontal temperature gradient may reach relatively high values, as these are boundaries between
      <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">
       air masses
      </a>
      with rather distinct properties.
     </p>
     <p>
      Clearly, the temperature gradient may change substantially in time, as a result of diurnal or seasonal heating and cooling for instance. This most likely happens during an
      <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">
       inversion
      </a>
      . For instance, during the day the temperature at
      <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">
       ground level
      </a>
      may be cold while it's warmer up in the atmosphere. As the day shifts over to night the temperature might drop rapidly while at other places on the land stay warmer or cooler at the same
      <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
       elevation
      </a>
      . This happens on the
      <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">
       West Coast of the United States
      </a>
      sometimes due to geography.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Weathering">
       Weathering
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temperature_gradient&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Weathering">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Expansion and contraction of rock, caused by temperature changes during a
      <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">
       wildfire
      </a>
      , through
      <a href="/wiki/Weathering#Thermal_stress" title="Weathering">
       thermal stress weathering
      </a>
      , may result in
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_shock" title="Thermal shock">
       thermal shock
      </a>
      and subsequent structure failure.
     </p>
