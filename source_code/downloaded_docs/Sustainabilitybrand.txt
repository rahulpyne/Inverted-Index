     <p>
      <b>
       Sustainability brands
      </b>
      are products and services that are
      <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">
       branded
      </a>
      to signify a special added value in terms of environmental and social benefits to the customer and thus enable the differentiation from competitors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meffert_2010_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Meffert_2010-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_brand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Sustainability branding is the process of creating and maintaining an identity of a specific product, service, or business that reflects special added value in terms of environmental and social benefits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A brand is only perceived as being sustainable if it can credibly convey sustainability benefits which are noticeable by and relevant to the consumer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meffert_2010_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Meffert_2010-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A sustainability brand must have an integrated culture for success. The key to a sustainable brand is trust between the consumer and the brand, only when this is achieved can a sustainable brand truly generate a USP and reap the benefits of it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Opposed to the term green brands which mainly focuses on environmentally sound business practices, sustainability brands additionally acknowledge the social dimension of providing products and services. This entails, among others, health and safety issues resulting from direct or indirect product use (consumption level) as well as the conditions under which a particular product is produced (production level). The
      <a href="/wiki/Occupational_safety_and_health" title="Occupational safety and health">
       physical protection and well-being
      </a>
      of people at work (i.e. employees as well as workers within the supply chains) are important indicators of sustainability brands and sustainability marketing in general which adheres to the triple bottom line of ecological (environmental), social (equity), and financial (economic) sustainability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peattie_2001_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peattie_2001-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A brand is able to evoke positive or negative feelings, especially in the context of sensitive social and ecological issues. The more positive the perceptions and feelings are towards a brand, the higher will be the likelihood of identification and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Customer_loyalty" title="Customer loyalty">
       loyalty amongst consumers
      </a>
      . It is therefore crucial in sustainability marketing to build up strong brands. In doing so, companies face far-reaching decisions in the areas of brand positioning (1), sustainability brand name selection (2), and sustainability brand development (3), in order to create and build sustainability brands that consumers associate with social and environmental added value.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_marketing" title="Environmental marketing">
       Environmental marketing
      </a>
      claims on products and packages need to be made (and read) with caution. Ambiguous
      <a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">
       greenwashing
      </a>
      titles such as
      <i>
       green product
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       green packaging
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       environmentally friendly
      </i>
      can be confusing without specific definition. Some regulators, such as the US Federal Trade Commission, are providing guidance
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability_brands_vs._Sustainable_Brands">
       Sustainability brands vs. Sustainable Brands
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_brand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sustainability brands vs. Sustainable Brands">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Since the adjective “sustainable” might convey the notion of brands that have long-lasting success, implicating durable competitive advantage without any particular reference to a sustainability agenda, the term “sustainability brand” should be used to prevent ambiguity. Albeit subtle difference, the latter explicitly emphasizes the notion of brands which have built their brand image upon sustainable business practices that consumers value. Sustainability brands are commonly referred to in the field of sustainability marketing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ottman_2010_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ottman_2010-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability_brand_positioning_and_the_8_C.27s">
       Sustainability brand positioning and the 8 C's
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_brand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sustainability brand positioning and the 8 C's">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Sustainability Brand Positioning and positioning in general is part of the brand identity and
      <a href="/wiki/Value_proposition" title="Value proposition">
       value proposition
      </a>
      that is to be actively communicated to the target audience
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aaker_and_Joachimsthaler.2C_2000_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Aaker_and_Joachimsthaler.2C_2000-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and can be described as an iterative process, consisting of deliberate and proactive actions aimed at the definition of distinct consumer perceptions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kalafatis_et_al..2C_2000_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kalafatis_et_al..2C_2000-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sustainable brand positioning is the brand positioning of Sustainable products and services. Sustainable products and services should offer an improved social and ecological performance during the whole product lifecycle and at the same time they have to satisfy consumer needs and wants. Many of the first generation sustainability brands failed in the market because companies overemphasized the positive socio-ecological product attributes, while they neglected to focus on other product attributes such as performance, functionality or design, too. As a result, many products could not compete against conventional products.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To build up and position strong sustainability brands, there are some guidelines to follow. Marc Stoiber
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      summed them up in the
      <i>
       The five Cs of Sustainability Branding
      </i>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fwww.sustainabilityconversations.com.2F2010.2F01.2F24.2Fa-sixth-c-for-sustainability-branding-consistency.2F_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fwww.sustainabilityconversations.com.2F2010.2F01.2F24.2Fa-sixth-c-for-sustainability-branding-consistency.2F-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Consumer Facing, Competitive, Core, Conversational and credible. Perrine Bouhana added to that concept “a sixth C”: Consistency. Martin Belz complemented and revised this concept to “8 C’s” of sustainability branding and describes them as followed:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fwww.sustainability-marketing.com.2F2010.2F08.2Feight-cs-of-sustainability-branding.html_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fwww.sustainability-marketing.com.2F2010.2F08.2Feight-cs-of-sustainability-branding.html-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Core
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Sustainability should be tied up to the key problems and the core business through assessing the socio-ecological impacts of products along the entire life-cycle of the products and finding out the socio-ecological “hot-spots” of the product-life-cycle.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Co-operative
      </dt>
      <dd>
       The solutions to the main socio-ecological problems associated with products along the entire
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_lifecycle_(marketing)" title="Product lifecycle (marketing)">
        life cycle
       </a>
       require - both in the process of innovating and marketing sustainable products and services - co-operations with suppliers, retailers, consumers, scientists, and other non-market actors (e.g. NGO’s).
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Credible
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Fundamentals of credibility are first the solving of key socio-ecological problems associated with companies’ products and second tying sustainability to the core business. Co-operations with trustworthy partners and the use of independent, third-party labels (e.g. labels like Bio or MSC) such as a high level of transparency (e.g. through an online tracking system, which enables consumers to see the world of behind the product) can additionally increase the credibility of sustainability brands.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Consumer Benefits
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Socio-ecological characteristics or attributes of products usually just play an auxiliary role (no core benefits). To broaden the appeal of sustainability brands, the companies should emphasize the inherent consumer benefits of socio-ecological attributes, including efficiency and cost effectiveness, health and safety, symbolism and status. Further they should align socio-ecological attributes with benefits such as functionality, design, and durability to create “motive alliances”.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Conversational
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Sustainability branding is more effective as a two-way conversation, rather than a one-way announcement. Inviting consumers to enter into dialogues about the sustainability process strengthens the brand-consumer relationship.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Consistency
      </dt>
      <dd>
       If sustainability is key to brand positioning, this requires a kind of integrated approach to sustainability communication: it is important to communicate in a consistent way, including e.g. advertising, personal selling or online communication. In addition to that, the sustainability product brand has to be consistent with the overall environmental and social performance of the company.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Commitment
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Sustainability branding not only requires the commitment of the PR department and the sustainability officers but also requires the commitment of top management and marketing decision makers.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Continuity
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Sustainability must reflect the core values of the brand and contribute to delivering the brand promise over the long term. This means that a brand cannot change its sustainability focus too often, or engage in too many non-related areas.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability_brand_name_selection">
       Sustainability brand name selection
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_brand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sustainability brand name selection">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the course of choosing the right name, Sustainability brands must first –just like brand names in the conventional sense- follow well-established rules. In general, a good brand name should consider three areas:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fbiznik.com.2Farticles.2Fthree-criteria-of-a-good-brand-name_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fbiznik.com.2Farticles.2Fthree-criteria-of-a-good-brand-name-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      memorability (distinctive short name, evoking emotions…), strategic fit (they should relate to the actual product; ability to expand to other brands) and legal (legal protection under trade mark law etc.)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kotler.2C_P._.26_Armstrong.2C_G._2004_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kotler.2C_P._.26_Armstrong.2C_G._2004-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sustainability Brands, however, go one step further and incorporate something that conveys the notion of social and/or ecological awareness.
     </p>
     <p>
      A popular example is “Better Place”, a global provider of electric vehicle networks and services that works in a joint venture with Renault Nissan Alliance Motor Company to promote the use of electric cars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fwww.betterplace.com_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fwww.betterplace.com-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The founder, Shai Agassi, was intrigued by a question posed at the World Economic Forum in 2005 "How do you make the world a better place by 2020?".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fwww.copenhagenclimatecouncil.com.2Fabout-us.2Fcouncillors.2Fshai-agassi.html_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fwww.copenhagenclimatecouncil.com.2Fabout-us.2Fcouncillors.2Fshai-agassi.html-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus the name Better Place. It is not related to the product (electric vehicle) but to much wider social and ecological issues (depletion of natural resources, CO2 emissions…), which the company addresses or rather offers the solution for.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sustainability brand names can be part of a new product launch, an extension to an existing (conventional) brand or be so new to the market that they create a product category themselves. Each of these approaches has specific strategic implications.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Enter Established Market
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Entering a saturated market such as the one for conventional household detergents with a sustainability brand might prove extremely difficult. However, there are companies that have successfully entered the market and positioned their brand as sustainability leaders. Seventh Generation, for instance, is the US market leader in four product categories (Household, Laundry, Personal and Baby Care) with products that –in the words of the company- “protect human health and the environment.”
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fwww.seventhgeneration.com.2Fabout_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fwww.seventhgeneration.com.2Fabout-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Brand Extension / Aligning Existing Brand
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Established brands can leverage their existing brand recognition to extend their name to new products, which are then marketed as socially and ecologically friendly. Tide, a popular detergent brand in the US, expanded its brand to meet the demands of the ecological conscious consumer by launching Tide Cold Water Detergent in 2005. The product requires only cold water and thus saves energy. In 2010, it was given the Green GH seal, which is only awarded after a “cradle to grave” examination of the product.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fwww.goodhousekeeping.com.2Fproduct-testing.2Ffrom-the-lab-blog.2Ftide-coldwater-detergents_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fwww.goodhousekeeping.com.2Fproduct-testing.2Ffrom-the-lab-blog.2Ftide-coldwater-detergents-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Creating entirely new Product Categories
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Sustainability brand names can also succeed if they achieve to create a product category for themselves where there is virtually no competition yet. When the car-sharing concept was fairly new, companies such as Mobility CarSharing in Switzerland did neither compete with car companies nor public transportation providers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability_brand_development">
       Sustainability brand development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_brand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sustainability brand development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sustainability_brand_development.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="615" data-file-width="1271" height="106" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Sustainability_brand_development.png/220px-Sustainability_brand_development.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Sustainability_brand_development.png/330px-Sustainability_brand_development.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Sustainability_brand_development.png/440px-Sustainability_brand_development.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sustainability_brand_development.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Sustainability brand development
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Sustainability brands are subject to constant change. A sustainability brand that is well established on the market, can be further developed into different directions. According to Belz and Peattie
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      four main options for development are possible:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Line extension
       </b>
       occurs when a company adds new products of the same product category under the same sustainability brand name.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Sustainability brand extension
       </b>
       occurs when a company introduces products of a different product category but under the same sustainability brand name.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Multi-sustainability brands
       </b>
       occur when a company manages two or more different sustainability brands but in the same product category.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        New sustainability brands
       </b>
       occur when a company creates an entire new brand name when they access a new product category. For example
       <a href="/wiki/MUD_Jeans" title="MUD Jeans">
        MUD Jeans
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      To create a sustainability brand it is furthermore important to adopt the right channels for marketing according to Lauterborns five Cs. Advertising in this case can help to create awareness of the brand and thus form the new brand experience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R_Lauterborn_1990_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-R_Lauterborn_1990-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Furthermore, it is obligatory for Sustainability brands to obtain one ore more
      <a href="/wiki/Ecolabel" title="Ecolabel">
       ecolabels
      </a>
      , leading to a higher influence on consumer behaviour and the perception of the brand.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belz_Peattie_2009_1-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belz_Peattie_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
