    Thermal mass

     <p>
      In building design,
      <b>
       thermal mass
      </b>
      is a property of the mass of a building which enables it to store heat, providing "inertia" against temperature fluctuations. It is sometimes known as the
      <b>
       thermal flywheel effect
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, when outside temperatures are fluctuating throughout the day, a large thermal mass within the insulated portion of a house can serve to "flatten out" the daily temperature fluctuations, since the thermal mass will absorb thermal energy when the surroundings are higher in temperature than the mass, and give thermal energy back when the surroundings are cooler, without reaching
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium" title="Thermal equilibrium">
       thermal equilibrium
      </a>
      . This is distinct from a material's
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">
       insulative
      </a>
      value, which reduces a building's
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
       thermal conductivity
      </a>
      , allowing it to be heated or cooled relatively separate from the outside, or even just retain the occupants' thermal energy longer.
     </p>
     <p>
      Scientifically, thermal mass is equivalent to
      <b>
       thermal capacitance
      </b>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">
       heat capacity
      </a>
      , the ability of a body to store
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">
       thermal energy
      </a>
      . It is typically referred to by the symbol
      <i>
       C
       <sub>
        th
       </sub>
      </i>
      and measured in units of J/°C or J/K (which are equivalent). Thermal mass may also be used for bodies of water, machines or machine parts, living things, or any other structure or body in engineering or biology. In those contexts, the term "heat capacity" is typically used instead.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

     <div class="hatnote" role="note">

     <p>
      The equation relating thermal energy to thermal mass is:

             </mi>

             </mspace>

            {\displaystyle Q=C_{\mathrm {th} }\Delta T\,}
           </annotation>
          </semantics>
         </math>
        </span>
        <img alt="Q = C_\mathrm{th} \Delta T\," aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9632864ea441039d8520af40cbef60b83d7cd617" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:12.411ex; height:2.676ex;"/>
       </span>

     <p>
      where
      <i>
       Q
      </i>
      is the thermal energy transferred,
      <i>
       C
       <sub>
        th
       </sub>
      </i>
      is the thermal mass of the body, and Δ
      <i>
       T
      </i>
      is the change in temperature.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example, if 250 J of heat energy is added to a copper gear with a thermal mass of 38.46 J/°C, its temperature will rise by 6.50 °C. If the body consists of a homogeneous material with sufficiently known physical properties, the thermal mass is simply the mass of material present times the specific heat capacity of that material. For bodies made of many materials, the sum of heat capacities for their pure components may be used in the calculation, or in some cases (as for a whole animal, for example) the number may simply be measured for the entire body in question, directly.
     </p>
     <p>
      As an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensive_property" title="Extensive property">
       extensive property
      </a>
      , heat capacity is characteristic of an object; its corresponding
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intensive_property" title="Intensive property">
       intensive property
      </a>
      is specific heat capacity, expressed in terms of a measure of the amount of material such as mass or number of moles, which must be multiplied by similar units to give the heat capacity of the entire body of material. Thus the heat capacity can be equivalently calculated as the product of the
      <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
       mass
      </a>
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      of the body and the specific heat capacity
      <i>
       c
      </i>
      for the material, or the product of the number of
      <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">
       moles
      </a>
      of molecules present
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      and the molar specific heat capacity
      <span>
       <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
        <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
         <semantics>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
           <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
            <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

                <!-- ¯ -->
               </mo>

            </mrow>
           </mstyle>
          </mrow>
          <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
           {\displaystyle {\bar {c}}}
          </annotation>
         </semantics>
        </math>
       </span>
       <img alt="{\bar {c}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94e1e99531b83728c7362b99dda09cc8b366168e" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.302ex; height:2.009ex;"/>
      </span>
      . For discussion of
      <i>
       why
      </i>
      the thermal energy storage abilities of pure substances vary, see
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity#Factors_that_affect_specific_heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">
       factors that affect specific heat capacity
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_mass_in_buildings">
       Thermal mass in buildings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thermal mass in buildings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Thermal mass is effective in improving building comfort in any place that experiences these types of daily temperature fluctuations—both in winter as well as in summer. When used well and combined with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar_design" title="Passive solar design">
       passive solar design
      </a>
      , thermal mass can play an important role in major reductions to energy use in
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       active heating and cooling systems
      </a>
      . The terms
      <i>
       heavy-weight
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       light-weight
      </i>
      are often used to describe buildings with different thermal mass strategies, and affects the choice of numerical factors used in subsequent calculations to describe their thermal response to heating and cooling. In
      <a href="/wiki/Building_services_engineering" title="Building services engineering">
       building services engineering
      </a>
      , the use of dynamic simulation computational modelling software has allowed for the accurate calculation of the environmental performance within buildings with different constructions and for different annual climate data sets. This allows the
      <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">
       architect
      </a>
      or engineer to explore in detail the relationship between heavy-weight and light-weight constructions, as well as insulation levels, in reducing energy consumption for
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       mechanical heating or cooling systems
      </a>
      , or even removing the need for such systems altogether.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_required_for_good_thermal_mass">
       Properties required for good thermal mass
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties required for good thermal mass">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Ideal materials for thermal mass are those materials that have:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" title="Specific heat capacity">
        specific heat capacity
       </a>
       ,
      </li>
      <li>
       high
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Any solid, liquid, or gas that has
      <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
       mass
      </a>
      will have some thermal mass. A common misconception is that only concrete or earth soil has thermal mass; even air has thermal mass (although very little).
     </p>
     <p>
      A table of volumetric heat capacity for building materials is available
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.yourhome.gov.au/passive-design/thermal-mass" rel="nofollow">
       here
      </a>
      , but note that their definition of thermal mass is slightly different.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_thermal_mass_in_different_climates">
       Use of thermal mass in different climates
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use of thermal mass in different climates">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The correct use and application of thermal mass is dependent on the prevailing climate in a district.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Temperate_and_cold_temperate_climates">
       Temperate and cold temperate climates
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Temperate and cold temperate climates">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar-exposed_thermal_mass">
       Solar-exposed thermal mass
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solar-exposed thermal mass">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      Thermal mass is ideally placed within the building and situated where it still can be exposed to low-angle winter sunlight (via windows) but insulated from heat loss. In summer the same thermal mass should be obscured from higher-angle summer sunlight in order to prevent overheating of the structure.
     </p>
     <p>
      The thermal mass is warmed passively by the sun or additionally by internal heating systems during the day. Thermal energy stored in the mass is then released back into the interior during the night. It is essential that it be used in conjunction with the standard principles of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar_design" title="Passive solar design">
       passive solar design
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Any form of thermal mass can be used. A concrete slab foundation either left exposed or covered with conductive materials, e.g. tiles, is one easy solution. Another novel method is to place the masonry facade of a timber-framed house on the inside ('reverse-brick veneer'). Thermal mass in this situation is best applied over a large area rather than in large volumes or thicknesses. 7.5–10 cm (3-4") is often adequate.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the most important source of thermal energy is the Sun, the ratio of glazing to thermal mass is an important factor to consider. Various formulas have been devised to determine this.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a general rule, additional solar-exposed thermal mass needs to be applied in a ratio from 6:1 to 8:1 for any area of sun-facing (north-facing in Southern Hemisphere or south-facing in Northern Hemisphere) glazing above 7% of the total floor area. For example, a 200 m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      house with 20 m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      of sun-facing glazing has 10% of glazing by total floor area; 6 m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      of that glazing will require additional thermal mass. Therefore, using the 6:1 to 8:1 ratio above, an additional 36–48 m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      of solar-exposed thermal mass is required. The exact requirements vary from climate to climate.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Classroom_thermal_mass.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Classroom_thermal_mass.JPG/220px-Classroom_thermal_mass.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Classroom_thermal_mass.JPG/330px-Classroom_thermal_mass.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Classroom_thermal_mass.JPG/440px-Classroom_thermal_mass.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Classroom_thermal_mass.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A modern school classroom with natural ventilation by opening windows and exposed thermal mass from a solid concrete floor soffit to help control summertime temperatures
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h5>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_mass_for_limiting_summertime_overheating">
       Thermal mass for limiting summertime overheating
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Thermal mass for limiting summertime overheating">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h5>
     <p>
      Thermal mass is ideally placed within a building where it is shielded from direct solar gain but exposed to the building occupants. It is therefore most commonly associated with solid concrete floor slabs in naturally ventilated or low-energy mechanically ventilated buildings where the concrete soffit is left exposed to the occupied space.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the day heat is gained from the sun, the occupants of the building, and any electrical lighting and equipment, causing the air temperatures within the space to increase, but this heat is absorbed by the exposed concrete slab above, thus limiting the temperature rise within the space to be within acceptable levels for human thermal comfort. In addition the lower surface temperature of the concrete slab also absorbs radiant heat directly from the occupants, also benefiting their thermal comfort.
     </p>
     <p>
      By the end of the day the slab has in turn warmed up, and now, as external temperatures decrease, the heat can be released and the slab cooled down, ready for the start of the next day. However this "regeneration" process is only effective if the building ventilation system is operated at night to carry away the heat from the slab. In naturally ventilated buildings it is normal to provide automated window openings to facilitate this process automatically.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hot.2C_arid_climates_.28e.g._desert.29">
       Hot, arid climates (e.g. desert)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hot, arid climates (e.g. desert)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Fe_adobe.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1340" data-file-width="2187" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Santa_Fe_adobe.jpg/220px-Santa_Fe_adobe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Santa_Fe_adobe.jpg/330px-Santa_Fe_adobe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Santa_Fe_adobe.jpg/440px-Santa_Fe_adobe.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Fe_adobe.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An adobe walled building in
        <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">
         Santa Fe, New Mexico
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      This is a classical use of thermal mass. Examples include
      <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">
       adobe
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">
       rammed earth
      </a>
      houses. Its function is highly dependent on marked
      <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">
       diurnal temperature variations
      </a>
      . The wall predominantly acts to retard heat transfer from the exterior to the interior during the day. The high
      <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_heat_capacity" title="Volumetric heat capacity">
       volumetric heat capacity
      </a>
      and thickness prevents thermal energy from reaching the inner surface. When temperatures fall at night, the walls re-radiate the thermal energy back into the night sky. In this application it is important for such walls to be massive to prevent heat transfer into the interior.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hot_humid_climates_.28e.g._sub-tropical_and_tropical.29">
       Hot humid climates (e.g. sub-tropical and tropical)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hot humid climates (e.g. sub-tropical and tropical)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The use of thermal mass is the most challenging in this environment where night temperatures remain elevated. Its use is primarily as a temporary heat sink. However, it needs to be strategically located to prevent overheating. It should be placed in an area that is not directly exposed to solar gain and also allows adequate
      <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">
       ventilation
      </a>
      at night to carry away stored energy without increasing internal temperatures any further. If to be used at all it should be used in judicious amounts and again not in large thicknesses.
     </p>
     <p>
      Cold incoming tap water may be piped through radiators to draw summer thermal energy from the air. In most areas, its initial temperature is 60 °F (16 °C) degrees. Since the existing plumbing is deep underground, it's well insulated from the heat of the day.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Materials_commonly_used_for_thermal_mass">
       Materials commonly used for thermal mass
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Materials commonly used for thermal mass">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Water:
       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
        water
       </a>
       has the highest volumetric heat capacity of all commonly used material. Typically, it is placed in large container(s),
       <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_resin" title="Acrylic resin">
        acrylic
       </a>
       tubes for example, in an area with direct sunlight. It may also be used to saturate other types material such as soil to increase heat capacity.
      </li>
      <li>
       Concrete, clay bricks and other forms of masonry: the
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">
        thermal conductivity
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">
        concrete
       </a>
       depends on its composition and curing technique. Concretes with stones are more thermally conductive than concretes with ash, perlite, fibers, and other insulating aggregates.
      </li>
      <li>
       Insulated concrete panels consist of an inner layer of concrete to provide the thermal mass factor. This is insulated from the outside by a conventional foam insulation and then covered again with an outer layer of concrete. The effect is a highly efficient building insulation envelope.
      </li>
      <li>
       Insulating concrete forms are commonly used to provide thermal mass to building structures.
       <a href="/wiki/Insulating_concrete_form" title="Insulating concrete form">
        Insulating concrete forms
       </a>
       provide the specific heat capacity and mass of concrete.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_inertia" title="Thermal inertia">
        Thermal inertia
       </a>
       of the structure is very high because the mass is insulated on both sides.
      </li>
      <li>
       Clay brick, adobe brick or mudbrick: see
       <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">
        brick
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">
        adobe
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Earth, mud and sod:
       <a href="/wiki/Dirt" title="Dirt">
        dirt's
       </a>
       heat capacity depends on its density, moisture content, particle shape, temperature, and composition. Early settlers to Nebraska built houses with thick walls made of dirt and sod because wood, stone, and other building materials were scarce. The extreme thickness of the walls provided some insulation, but mainly served as thermal mass, absorbing thermal energy during the day and releasing it during the night. Nowadays, people sometimes use
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_sheltering" title="Earth sheltering">
        earth sheltering
       </a>
       around their homes for the same effect. In earth sheltering, the thermal mass comes not only from the walls of the building, but from the surrounding earth that is in physical contact with the building. This provides a fairly constant, moderating temperature that reduces heat flow through the adjacent wall.
      </li>
      <li>
       Rammed earth:
       <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">
        rammed earth
       </a>
       provides excellent thermal mass because of its high density, and the high specific heat capacity of the soil used in its construction.
      </li>
      <li>
       Natural rock and stone: see
       <a href="/wiki/Stonemasonry" title="Stonemasonry">
        stonemasonry
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Logs are used as a building material to create the exterior, and perhaps also the interior, walls of homes. Log homes differ from some other construction materials listed above because solid wood has both moderate R-value (insulation) and also significant thermal mass. In contrast, water, earth, rocks, and concrete all have low R-values.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Phase-change_material" title="Phase-change material">
        Phase-change materials
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_energy_storage">
       Seasonal energy storage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Seasonal energy storage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      If enough mass is used it can create a seasonal advantage. That is, it can heat in the winter and cool in the summer. This is sometimes called
      <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">
       passive annual heat storage
      </a>
      or PAHS. The PAHS system has been successfully used at 7000 ft. in Colorado and in a number of homes in Montana.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Earthship" title="Earthship">
       Earthships of New Mexico
      </a>
      utilize passive heating and cooling as well as using recycled tires for foundation wall yielding a maximum PAHS/STES. It has also been used successfully in the UK at
      <a href="/wiki/Hockerton_Housing_Project" title="Hockerton Housing Project">
       Hockerton Housing Project
      </a>
      .
     </p>
