     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Carnot cycle
      </b>
      is a theoretical
      <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">
       thermodynamic cycle
      </a>
      proposed by
      <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot" title="Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot">
       Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot
      </a>
      in 1824 and expanded upon by others in the 1830s and 1840s. It provides an upper limit on the efficiency that any classical thermodynamic engine can achieve during the conversion of
      <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">
       heat
      </a>
      into
      <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">
       work
      </a>
      , or conversely, the efficiency of a
      <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">
       refrigeration
      </a>
      system in creating a temperature difference (e.g. refrigeration) by the application of work to the system. It is not an actual thermodynamic cycle but is a theoretical construct.
     </p>
     <p>
      Every single thermodynamic system exists in a particular state. When a system is taken through a series of different states and finally returned to its initial state, a thermodynamic cycle is said to have occurred. In the process of going through this cycle, the system may perform work on its surroundings, thereby acting as a
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">
       heat engine
      </a>
      . A system undergoing a Carnot cycle is called a
      <a href="/wiki/Carnot_heat_engine" title="Carnot heat engine">
       Carnot heat engine
      </a>
      , although such a "perfect" engine is only a theoretical construct and cannot be built in practice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giordano2009_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Giordano2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Essentially, there are two systems at temperatures
      <i>
       T
      </i>
      <sub>
       h
      </sub>
      and
      <i>
       T
      </i>
      <sub>
       c
      </sub>
      (hot and cold respectively), which are so large that their temperatures are practically unaffected by a single cycle. As such, they are called "heat reservoirs". Since the cycle is reversible, there is no generation of entropy during the cycle; entropy is conserved. During the cycle, an arbitrary amount of entropy Δ
      <i>
       S
      </i>
      is extracted from the hot reservoir, and deposited in the cold reservoir. Since there is no volume change in either reservoir, they do no work, and during the cycle, an amount of energy
      <i>
       T
      </i>
      <sub>
       h
      </sub>
      ΔS is extracted from the hot reservoir and a smaller amount of energy
      <i>
       T
      </i>
      <sub>
       c
      </sub>
      ΔS is deposited in the cold reservoir. The difference in the two energies (
      <i>
       T
      </i>
      <sub>
       h
      </sub>
      -T
      <sub>
       c
      </sub>
      )Δ
      <i>
       S
      </i>
      is equal to the work done by the engine.
     </p>

     </p>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isothermal" title="Isothermal">

         </i>
        </sub>

        1
       </sub>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Reversed_Carnot_cycle">
       The Reversed Carnot cycle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Reversed Carnot cycle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Carnot heat-engine cycle described is a totally reversible cycle. That is, all the processes that comprise it can be reversed, in which case it becomes the Carnot refrigeration cycle. This time, the cycle remains exactly the same except that the directions of any heat and work interactions are reversed. Heat is absorbed from the low-temperature reservoir, heat is rejected to a high-temperature reservoir, and a work input is required to accomplish all this. The P-V diagram of the reversed Carnot cycle is the same as for the Carnot cycle except that the directions of the processes are reversed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Carnot.27s_theorem">
       Carnot's theorem
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Carnot's theorem">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)" title="Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)">
       Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      It can be seen from the above diagram, that for any cycle operating between temperatures

     <p>
      <b>
       Carnot's theorem
      </b>
      is a formal statement of this fact:
      <i>
       No engine operating between two heat reservoirs can be more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between those same reservoirs.
      </i>
      Thus, Equation 3 gives the maximum efficiency possible for any engine using the corresponding temperatures. A corollary to Carnot's theorem states that:
      <i>
       All reversible engines operating between the same heat reservoirs are equally efficient.
      </i>
      Rearranging the right side of the equation gives what may be a more easily understood form of the equation. Namely that the theoretical maximum efficiency of a heat engine equals the difference in temperature between the hot and cold reservoir divided by the absolute temperature of the hot reservoir. To find the absolute temperature in kelvin, add 273.15 degrees to the Celsius temperature. Looking at this formula an interesting fact becomes apparent. Lowering the temperature of the cold reservoir will have more effect on the ceiling efficiency of a heat engine than raising the temperature of the hot reservoir by the same amount. In the real world, this may be difficult to achieve since the cold reservoir is often an existing ambient temperature.
     </p>
     <p>
      In other words, maximum efficiency is achieved if and only if no new entropy is created in the cycle, which would be the case if e.g. friction leads to
      <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">
       dissipation
      </a>
      of work into heat. In that case the cycle is not reversible and the
      <a href="/wiki/Clausius_theorem" title="Clausius theorem">
       Clausius theorem
      </a>
      becomes an inequality rather than an equality. Otherwise, since entropy is a
      <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">
       state function
      </a>
      , the required dumping of heat into the environment to dispose of excess entropy leads to a (minimal) reduction in efficiency. So Equation 3 gives the efficiency of any
      <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">
       reversible
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">
       heat engine
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In mesoscopic heat engines, work per cycle of operation fluctuates due to thermal noise. For the case when work and heat fluctuations are counted, there is exact equality that relates average of exponents of work performed by any heat engine and the heat transfer from the hotter heat bath.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This relation transforms Carnot's inequality into an exact equality that applies to an arbitrary heat engine coupled to two heat reservoirs and operating at an arbitrary rate.
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Efficiency of real heat engines">

       </a>

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        See also:
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_Engine#Efficiency" title="Heat Engine">
         Heat engine efficiency
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine#Other_criteria_of_heat_engine_performance" title="Heat engine">

       </i>

     <p>
      Carnot realized that in reality it is not possible to build a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_reversibility" title="Thermodynamic reversibility">
       thermodynamically reversible
      </a>
      engine, so real heat engines are even less efficient than indicated by Equation 3. In addition, real engines that operate along this cycle are rare. Nevertheless, Equation 3 is extremely useful for determining the maximum efficiency that could ever be expected for a given set of thermal reservoirs.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although
      <b>

          All reversible engines operating between the same heat reservoirs are equally efficient.
          <i>
           Rearranging the right side of the equation gives what may be a more easily understood form of the equation. Namely that the theoretical maximum efficiency of a heat engine equals the difference in temperature between the hot and cold reservoir divided by the absolute temperature of the hot reservoir. To find the absolute temperature in kelvin, add 273.15 degrees to the Celsius temperature. Looking at this formula an interesting fact becomes apparent. Lowering the temperature of the cold reservoir will have more effect on the ceiling efficiency of a heat engine than raising the temperature of the hot reservoir by the same amount. In the real world, this may be difficult to achieve since the cold reservoir is often an existing ambient temperature. In other words, maximum efficiency is achieved if and only if no new entropy is created in the cycle.
