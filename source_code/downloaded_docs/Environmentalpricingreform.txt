     <p>
      <b>
       Environmental pricing reform
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       EPR
      </b>
      ) is the process of adjusting market prices to include environmental costs and benefits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An
      <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">
       externality
      </a>
      (a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Market_failure" title="Market failure">
       market failure
      </a>
      ) exists where a market price omits environmental costs and/or benefits. In such a situation, rational (self-interested) economic decisions can lead to environmental harm, as well as to economic distortions and inefficiencies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Environmental pricing reform can be economy-wide, or more focused (e.g. specific to a sector (such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_power_generation" title="Electric power generation">
       electric power generation
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">
       mining
      </a>
      ) or a particular
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issue" title="Environmental issue">
       environmental issue
      </a>
      (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
       climate change
      </a>
      ). A "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_based_instruments" title="Market based instruments">
       market based instruments
      </a>
      " or "economic instrument for environmental protection" is an individual instance of Environmental Pricing Reform. Examples include green tax-shifting (
      <a href="/wiki/Ecotax" title="Ecotax">
       ecotaxation
      </a>
      ), tradeable pollution permits, or the creation of markets for ecological services.
     </p>
     <p>
      A similar term, "ecological fiscal reform" differs in more narrowly dealing with fiscal (i.e. tax) policies as opposed to using non-fiscal regulations to achieve the government's environmental goals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
