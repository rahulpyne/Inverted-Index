     <p>
      <b>
       Behavior change
      </b>
      is a central objective in
      <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">
       public health
      </a>
      interventions,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      with an increased focus on
      <a href="/wiki/Preventive_healthcare" title="Preventive healthcare">
       prevention
      </a>
      prior to onset of disease.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is particularly important in
      <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">
       low and middle income countries
      </a>
      , where efficiency of health spending and costs and benefits of health interventions has come under increased scrutiny in recent decades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Behavior change in public health is also known as Social and Behavior Change Communication (SBCC).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavior_change_(public_health)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many health conditions are caused by risk behaviors, such as problem drinking, substance use, smoking, reckless driving, overeating, or unprotected sexual intercourse. The key question in health behavior research is how to predict and modify the adoption and maintenance of health behaviors. Fortunately, human beings have, in principle, control over their conduct. Health-compromising behaviors can be eliminated by self-regulatory efforts, and health-enhancing behaviors can be adopted instead, such as physical exercise, weight control, preventive nutrition, dental hygiene, condom use, or accident prevention. Health behavior change refers to the motivational, volitional, and actional processes of abandoning such health-compromising behaviors in favor of adopting and maintaining health-enhancing behaviors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Behavior change programs, which have evolved over time, encompass a broad range of activities and approaches, which focus on the individual, community, and environmental influences on behavior. Behavior change, a relatively recent public health-related term, should not be confused with
      <a href="/wiki/Behavior_modification" title="Behavior modification">
       behavior modification
      </a>
      , a term with specific meaning in a clinical psychiatry setting.
     </p>
     <p>
      One emerging concept in the American health system is that of small, manageable changes. It is not necessary to make sweeping, drastic alterations to one's whole lifestyle in order to see benefit; something is better than nothing. Dietary and exercise contexts, in particular show the benefit of moderate, slow changes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">
       Theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavior_change_(public_health)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Behavior change programs tend to focus on a few
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioral_change_theories" title="Behavioral change theories">
       behavioral change theories
      </a>
      which gained ground in the 1980s. These theories share a major commonality in defining individual actions as the locus of change. Behavior change programs that are usually focused on activities that help a person or a community to reflect upon their risk behaviors and change them to reduce their risk and vulnerability are known as
      <a href="/wiki/Intervention_(counseling)" title="Intervention (counseling)">
       interventions
      </a>
      . Examples include: "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transtheoretical_Model" title="Transtheoretical Model">
       Transtheoretical (Stages of Change) Model
      </a>
      of Behavior Change", "
      <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_reasoned_action" title="Theory of reasoned action">
       theory of reasoned action
      </a>
      ", "
      <a href="/wiki/Health_belief_model" title="Health belief model">
       health belief model
      </a>
      ", "
      <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_planned_behavior" title="Theory of planned behavior">
       theory of planned behavior
      </a>
      ",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffusion_of_innovation" title="Diffusion of innovation">
       diffusion of innovation
      </a>
      ",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Health_action_process_approach" title="Health action process approach">
       health action process approach
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tools">
       Tools
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavior_change_(public_health)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tools">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Care groups are groups of 10-15 volunteer, community-based health educators who regularly meet together.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrier_Analysis" title="Barrier Analysis">
        Barrier Analysis
       </a>
       is a rapid assessment tool used in behavior change projects to identify behavioral determinants.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Community-led_total_sanitation" title="Community-led total sanitation">
        Community-led total sanitation
       </a>
       is a behaviour change tool used in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">
        sanitation
       </a>
       sector for mainly rural settings in developing countries with the aim to stop
       <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">
        open defecation
       </a>
       . The method uses shame, disgust and to some extent peer pressure which leads to the "spontaneous" construction and long-term use of toilets after an initial triggering process has taken place.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior_change_communication_.28BCC.29">
       Behavior change communication (BCC)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Behavior_change_(public_health)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior change communication (BCC)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Behavior_change_communication" title="Behavior change communication">
       Behavior change communication
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Behavior_change_communication" title="Behavior change communication">
       Behavior change communication
      </a>
      , or BCC, is an approach to behavior change focused on communication. It is also known as Social and Behavior Change Communication, or SBCC. The assumptions is that through communication of some kind, individuals and communities can somehow be persuaded to behave in ways that will make their lives safer and healthier. BCC was first employed in HIV and TB prevention projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      More recently, its ambit has grown to encompass any communication activity whose goal is to help individuals and communities select and practice behavior that will positively impact their health, such as immunization, cervical cancer check up, employing single-use syringes, etc.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
       Examples
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johns_Hopkins_Center_for_Communication_Programs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johns Hopkins Center for Communication Programs (page does not exist)">
        Johns Hopkins Center for Communication Programs
       </a>
       specializes in health-related BCC (behavior change communication) programs, primarily in developing countries. It includes programs in reproductive health and family planning, malaria, and HIV/AIDS.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Development_Media_International" title="Development Media International">
        Development Media International
       </a>
       uses mass media to promote healthy behaviors in Burkina Faso, DRC and Mozambique.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Young_1ove" title="Young 1ove">
        Young 1ove
       </a>
       provides information to youth to reduce the spread of HIV/AIDS in Botswana.

        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
