     <p>
      <b>
       Consolidated Edison, Inc.
      </b>
      , commonly known as
      <b>
       Con Edison
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Con Ed
      </b>
      , is one of the largest
      <a href="/wiki/Investor-owned_utility" title="Investor-owned utility">
       investor-owned
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_company" title="Energy company">
       energy companies
      </a>
      in the United States, with approximately $13 billion in annual revenues as of 2016, and over $47 billion in assets. The company provides a wide range of energy-related products and services to its customers through its subsidiaries:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc.
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        (CECONY)
       </b>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Public_utilities_commission" title="Public utilities commission">
        regulated utility
       </a>
       providing electric and gas service in
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York City
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Westchester_County,_New_York" title="Westchester County, New York">
        Westchester County, New York
       </a>
       , and steam service in the borough of
       <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">
        Manhattan
       </a>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Orange and Rockland Utilities, Inc.
       </b>
       , a regulated utility serving customers in a 1,300-square-mile (3,400 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ) area in southeastern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">
        New York
       </a>
       and northern
       <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">
        New Jersey
       </a>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Con Edison Solutions
       </b>
       , an energy services company;
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Con Edison Energy
       </b>
       , a wholesale energy services company;
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Con Edison Development
       </b>
       , a company that owns and operates renewable and energy infrastructure projects, and,
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Con Edison Transmission
       </b>
       , Inc., which invests in electric and natural gas transmission projects.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In 2015, electric revenues accounted for 70.35% of consolidated sales (70.55% in 2014); gas revenues 13.61% (14.96% in 2014); steam revenues 5.01% (4.86% in 2014); and non-utility revenues of 11.02% (9.63 in 2014%).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Though the company provides an indispensable service to New York residents, a number of major incidents and service problems have negatively impacted its reputation with the public.
     </p>

     <p>
      In 1823, Con Edison’s earliest corporate predecessor, the New York Gas Light Company, was founded by a consortium of New York City investors. A year later, it was listed on the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">
       New York Stock Exchange
      </a>
      . In 1884, six gas companies combined into the Consolidated Gas Company.
     </p>
     <p>
      The New York Steam Company began providing service in lower
      <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">
       Manhattan
      </a>
      in 1882. Today, Con Edison operates the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">
       largest commercial steam system
      </a>
      in the world, providing steam service to nearly 1,600 commercial and residential establishments in Manhattan from
      <a href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">
       Battery Park
      </a>
      to 96th Street.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Con Edison’s electric business also dates back to 1882, when
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">
       Thomas Edison
      </a>
      ’s
      <a href="/wiki/Edison_Illuminating_Company" title="Edison Illuminating Company">
       Edison Illuminating Company
      </a>
      of New York began supplying electricity to 59 customers in a square-mile area in lower Manhattan. After the “
      <a href="/wiki/War_of_Currents" title="War of Currents">
       War of Currents
      </a>
      ”, there were more than 30 companies generating and distributing electricity in New York City and Westchester County. But by 1920 there were far fewer, and the New York Edison Company (then part of Consolidated Gas) was clearly the leader.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1936, with electric sales far outstripping gas sales, the company incorporated and the name was changed to Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc. The years that followed brought further amalgamations as Consolidated Edison acquired or merged with more than a dozen companies between 1936 and 1960. Con Edison today is the result of
      <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">
       acquisitions
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(law)" title="Dissolution (law)">
       dissolutions
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merger" title="Merger">
       mergers
      </a>
      of more than 170 individual
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric" title="Electric">
       electric
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
       gas
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">
       steam
      </a>
      companies.
     </p>
     <p>
      On January 1, 1998, following the
      <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">
       deregulation
      </a>
      of the utility industry in New York state, a holding company, Consolidated Edison, Inc., was formed. It is one of the nation’s largest investor-owned energy companies, with approximately $13 billion in annual revenues and $47 billion in assets. The company provides a wide range of energy-related products and services to its customers through two regulated utility subsidiaries and three competitive energy businesses. Under a number of corporate names, the company has been traded on the NYSE without interruption since 1824—longer than any other NYSE stock. Its largest subsidiary, Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc. provides electric, gas and steam service to more than 3 million customers in New York City and Westchester County, New York, an area of 660 square miles (1,700 km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) with a population of nearly 9 million.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical">
       Electrical
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electrical">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Con Edison electrical transmission system utilizes voltages of 138 kilovolts (kV), 345 kV, and 500 kV. The company has two 345 kV interconnections with upstate New York that enable it to import power from
      <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Hydro-Québec">
       Hydro-Québec
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      and one 345 kV interconnection each with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Service_Electric_and_Gas" title="Public Service Electric and Gas">
       Public Service Electric and Gas
      </a>
      in New Jersey and
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Power_Authority" title="Long Island Power Authority">
       LIPA
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">
       Long Island
      </a>
      . Con Edison is also interconnected with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Service_Electric_and_Gas" title="Public Service Electric and Gas">
       Public Service Electric and Gas
      </a>
      via the Branchburg-Ramapo 500 kV line. Con Ed's distribution voltages are 33 kV, 27 kV, 13 kV, and 4 kV.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 93,000 miles (150,000 km) of underground cable in the Con Edison system could wrap around the Earth 3.6 times. Nearly 36,000 miles (58,000 km) of overhead electric wires complement the underground system—enough cable to stretch between New York and Los Angeles 13 times.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gas">
       Gas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Con Edison gas system has nearly 7,200 miles (11,600 km) of pipes—if laid end to end, long enough to reach Paris and back to New York City, and serves Westchester County, the Bronx, Manhattan and parts of Queens and Westchester County. Gas service in Brooklyn, Staten Island and the rest of Queens is provided by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Grid_USA" title="National Grid USA">
       National Grid USA
      </a>
      , with the exception of the Rockaway peninsula, which is serviced by PSEG-LI. The average volume of gas that travels through Con Edison’s gas system annually could fill the Empire State Building nearly 6,100 times.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Steam">
       Steam
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Steam">

       </span>

     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">
       New York City steam system
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Con Edison produces 30 billion pounds of steam each year through its seven power plants which boil water to 1,000 °F (538 °C) before distributing it to hundreds of buildings in the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">
       New York City steam system
      </a>
      , which is the biggest
      <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">
       district steam system
      </a>
      in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Steam traveling through the system is used to heat and cool some of New York’s most famous addresses, including the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Building" title="United Nations Building">
       United Nations complex
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">
       Empire State Building
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">
       Metropolitan Museum of Art
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bribery_prosecution">
       Bribery prosecution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bribery prosecution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On January 14, 2009, eleven Con Edison supervisors were arrested for demanding more than $1 million in kickbacks related to work done by a construction company that was repairing the midtown stream pipe eruption of 2007. According to federal prosecutors, the employees had approved payment for work that was unnecessary or not performed, and promised faster payment for some work performed by the construction company in exchange for the bribes. The FBI had two retired Con Edison employees and the president of the construction company wear recording devices that recorded the suspects demanding bribes of between $1000 to $5000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later that year Con Edison sued Brendan Maher, one of the construction supervisors who was arrested and later admitted taking bribes that the utility company claimed amounted to $10,000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In April 2016, Con Edison agreed to pay over $171 million, about 1.5% of its annual revenue, back to its customers in compensation for harm resulting from the bribery. The Public Service Commission had found that Con Edison failed to supervise the employees. Con Edison admitted no wrongdoing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
