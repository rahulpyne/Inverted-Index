     <p>
      <b>
       Micro combined heat and power
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       micro-CHP
      </b>
      is an extension of the idea of
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       cogeneration
      </a>
      to the single/multi family home or small office building in the range of up to 50
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">
       kW
      </a>
      . Local generation has a higher efficiency as it lacks the 8-10 % energy losses when transporting
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>
      over long distances and the 10–15 % energy losses on
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
       heat transfer
      </a>
      in district heating networks due to the difference between the thermal energy carrier (hot water) and the colder external environment. The most common systems use natural gas as their primary energy source and emit
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Combined heat and power (CHP) systems for homes or small commercial buildings are often fueled by
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      to produce electricity and heat. A micro-CHP system is a small
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">
       fuel cell
      </a>
      or a
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">
       heat engine
      </a>
      driving a generator which provide
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">
       electric power
      </a>
      and heat for an individual building's
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       heating, ventilation, and air conditioning
      </a>
      . A micro-CHP may primarily follow heat demand, delivering
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>
      as the by-product, or may follow electrical demand to
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">
       generate electricity
      </a>
      and heat is the by-product. When used primarily for heat in circumstances of fluctuating electrical demand, micro-CHP systems may generate more electricity than is instantly being demanded.
     </p>
     <p>
      The heat engine version is a small scale example of
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       cogeneration
      </a>
      schemes which have been used with large electric power plants. The purpose is to utilize more of the energy in the fuel. The reason for using such systems is that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_engines" title="Heat engines">
       heat engines
      </a>
      , such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_power_plant" title="Steam power plant">
       steam power plants
      </a>
      which generate the electric power needed for modern life by burning fuel, are not very efficient. Due to
      <a href="/wiki/Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)" title="Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)">
       Carnot's theorem
      </a>
      , a heat engine cannot be 100% efficient; it cannot convert anywhere near all the heat produced from the fuel it burns into useful forms such as electricity. So heat engines always produce a surplus of low-temperature
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">
       waste heat
      </a>
      , called "secondary heat" or "low-grade heat". Modern plants are limited to efficiencies of about 33–60% at most, so 40–67% of the energy is exhausted as waste heat. In the past this energy was usually wasted to the environment.
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       Cogeneration
      </a>
      systems, built in recent years in cold-climate countries, utilize the waste heat produced by large power plants for heating, piping hot water from the plant into buildings in the surrounding community.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, it is not practical to transport heat long distances, due to heat loss from the pipes. Since electricity can be transported practically, it is more efficient to generate the electricity near where the waste heat can be used. So in a "micro-combined heat and power system" (micro-CHP), small power plants are instead located where the secondary heat can be used, in individual buildings. Micro-CHP are defined by the EC as being of less than 50 kW electrical power output.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a central power plant, the supply of "waste heat" may exceed the local heat demand. In such cases, if it is not desirable to reduce the power production, the excess waste heat must be disposed in e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Cooling_tower" title="Cooling tower">
       cooling towers
      </a>
      or
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_cooling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sea cooling (page does not exist)">
       sea cooling
      </a>
      without being used. A way to avoid excess waste heat is to reduce the fuel input to the CHP plant, reducing both the heat and power output to balance the heat demand. In doing this, the power production is limited by the heat demand.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a traditional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">
       power plant
      </a>
      delivering electricity to consumers, about 30% of the heat content of the primary heat energy source, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_thermal" title="Solar thermal">
       solar thermal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">
       petroleum
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">
       uranium
      </a>
      , reaches the consumer, although the efficiency can be 20% for very old plants and 45% for newer gas plants. In contrast, a CHP system converts 15%–42% of the primary heat to electricity, and most of the remaining heat is captured for
      <a href="/wiki/Water_heating" title="Water heating">
       hot water
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_heating" title="Space heating">
       space heating
      </a>
      . In total, as much as 90% of the heat from the primary energy source goes to useful purposes when heat production does not exceed the demand.
     </p>
     <p>
      CHP systems are able to increase the total energy utilization of primary energy sources, such as fuel and concentrated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_thermal" title="Solar thermal">
       solar thermal
      </a>
      energy. Thus CHP has been steadily gaining popularity in all sectors of the energy economy, due to the increased costs of electricity and fuel, particularly fossil fuels, and due to environmental concerns, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
       climate change
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      CHP systems have benefited the industrial sector since the beginning of the industrial revolution. For three decades, these larger CHP systems were more economically justifiable than micro-CHP, due to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economy_of_scale" title="Economy of scale">
       economy of scale
      </a>
      . After the year 2000, micro-CHP has become cost effective in many markets around the world, due to rising energy costs. The development of micro-CHP systems has also been facilitated by recent technological developments of small heat engines. This includes improved performance and cost-effectiveness of
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">
       fuel cells
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">
       Stirling engines
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_engines" title="Steam engines">
       steam engines
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_turbines" title="Gas turbines">
       gas turbines
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">
       diesel engines
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_engine" title="Otto engine">
       Otto engines
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PEMFC" title="PEMFC">
       PEMFC
      </a>
      fuel cell mCHP operates at low temperature (50 to 100 °C) and needs high purity hydrogen, its prone to contamination, changes are made to operate at higher temperatures and improvements on the fuel reformer.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SOFC" title="SOFC">
       SOFC
      </a>
      fuel cell mCHP operates at a high temperature (500 to 1,000 °CP) and can handle different energy sources well but the high temperature requires expensive materials to handle the temperature, changes are made to operate at a lower temperature. Because of the higher temperature SOFC in general has a longer start-up time and need continuous heat output even in times when there is no thermal demand.
     </p>
     <p>
      CHP systems linked to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_chiller" title="Absorption chiller">
       absorption chillers
      </a>
      can use waste heat for
      <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">
       refrigeration
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2013 UK report from Ecuity Consulting stated that MCHP is the most cost-effective method of utilising gas to generate energy at the domestic level.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Delta-ee consultants stated in 2013 that with 64% of global sales the fuel cell micro-combined heat and power passed the conventional engine-based micro-CHP systems in sales in 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Micro-CHP engine systems are currently based on several different technologies:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHP_Review_2015_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CHP_Review_2015-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
        Internal combustion engines
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">
        Stirling engines
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">

       </a>
      </li>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microturbine" title="Microturbine">
        Microturbines
       </a>
      </li>

        Steam engine
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Steam_motor" title="Steam motor">
        Steam motor
       </a>
       (using either the traditional water or
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_Rankine_cycle" title="Organic Rankine cycle">
        organic chemicals such as refrigerants
       </a>
       )
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fuels">
       Fuels
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fuels">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are many types of fuels and sources of heat that may be considered for micro-CHP. The properties of these sources vary in terms of system cost, heat cost, environmental effects, convenience, ease of transportation and storage, system maintenance, and system life. Some of the heat sources and fuels that are being considered for use with micro-CHP include:
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">
       LPG
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      , vegetable oil (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">
       rapeseed
      </a>
      oil),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodgas" title="Woodgas">
       woodgas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
       solar thermal
      </a>
      , and lately also
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      , as well as multi-fuel systems. The energy sources with the lowest emissions of particulates and net-carbon dioxide include solar power, hydrogen, biomass (with two-stage gasification into
      <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">
       biogas
      </a>
      ), and natural gas. Due to the high efficiency of the CHP process, cogeneration has still lower carbon emissions compared to energy transformation in fossil driven boilers or thermal power plants.
     </p>
     <p>
      The majority of cogeneration systems use natural gas for fuel, because natural gas burns easily and cleanly, it can be inexpensive, it is available in most areas and is easily transported through pipelines, which already exist for many homes.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Engine_types">
       Engine types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Engine types">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Natural gas is suitable for
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       internal combustion engines
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_engine" title="Otto engine">
       Otto engine
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">
       gas turbine
      </a>
      systems. Gas turbines are used in many small systems due to their high efficiency, small size, clean combustion, durability and low maintenance requirements. Gas turbines designed with
      <a href="/wiki/Foil_bearing" title="Foil bearing">
       foil bearings
      </a>
      and air-cooling operate without lubricating oil or coolants. The waste heat of gas turbines is mostly in the exhaust, whereas the waste heat of
      <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_engine" title="Reciprocating engine">
       reciprocating internal combustion engines
      </a>
      is split between the exhaust and cooling system.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/External_combustion_engines" title="External combustion engines">
       External combustion engines
      </a>
      can run on any high-temperature heat source. These engines include the
      <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">
       Stirling engine
      </a>
      , hot "gas" turbocharger, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">
       steam engine
      </a>
      . Both range from 10%-20% efficiency, and as of 2014, small quantities are in production for micro-CHP products.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other possibilities include the
      <a href="/wiki/Organic_Rankine_cycle" title="Organic Rankine cycle">
       Organic Rankine cycle
      </a>
      , which operates at lower temperatures and pressures using low-grade heat sources. The primary advantage to this is that the equipment is essentially an air-conditioning or refrigeration unit operating as an engine, whereby the piping and other components need not be designed for extreme temperatures and pressures, reducing cost and complexity. Electrical efficiency suffers, but it is presumed that such a system would be utilizing waste heat or a heat source such as a wood stove or gas boiler that would exist anyway for purposes of space heating.
     </p>
     <p>
      The future of combined heat and power, particularly for homes and small businesses, will continue to be affected by the price of fuel, including natural gas. As fuel prices continue to climb, this will make the economics more favorable for energy conservation measures, and more
      <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">
       efficient energy use
      </a>
      , including CHP and micro-CHP.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_cells">
       Fuel cells
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fuel cells">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Fuel cells generate electricity and heat as a by product. The advantages for a
      <a href="/wiki/Stationary_fuel_cell_applications" title="Stationary fuel cell applications">
       stationary fuel cell application
      </a>
      over stirling CHP are no moving parts, less maintenance, and quieter operation. The surplus electricity can be delivered back to the grid.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      PEMFC fuel cells fueled by natural gas or propane use a
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming#Reforming_for_fuel_cells" title="Steam reforming">
       steam reformer
      </a>
      to convert methane in the gas supply into
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      and hydrogen; the hydrogen then reacts with oxygen in the fuel cell to produce electricity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PEMFC" title="PEMFC">
       PEMFC
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">
       fuel cell
      </a>
      based micro-CHP has an electrical efficiency of 37%
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" title="Lower heating value">
       LHV
      </a>
      and 33%
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higher_heating_value" title="Higher heating value">
       HHV
      </a>
      and a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_recovery" title="Heat recovery">
       heat recovery
      </a>
      efficiency of 52% LHV and 47% HHV with a
      <a href="/wiki/Service_life" title="Service life">
       service life
      </a>
      of 40,000 hours or 4000 start/stop cycles which is equal to 10 year use. An estimated 138,000 Fuel cell CHP systems below 1 kW had been installed in Japan by the end of 2014.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHP_Review_2015_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CHP_Review_2015-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most of these CHP systems are PEMFC based (85%) and the remaining are SOFC systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2013
      <a href="/wiki/Service_life" title="Service life">
       Lifetime
      </a>
      is around 60,000 hours. For PEM fuel cell units, which shut down at night, this equates to an estimated lifetime of between ten and fifteen years.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoelectrics">
       Thermoelectrics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thermoelectrics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoelectric" title="Thermoelectric">
       Thermoelectric
      </a>
      generators operating on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seebeck_Effect" title="Seebeck Effect">
       Seebeck Effect
      </a>
      show promise due to their total absence of moving parts. Efficiency, however, is the major concern as most thermoelectric devices fail to achieve 5% efficiency even with high temperature differences.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_micro-CHP">
       Solar micro-CHP
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Solar micro-CHP">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="CPVT">
       CPVT
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: CPVT">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      This can be achieved by
      <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_thermal_hybrid_solar_collector" title="Photovoltaic thermal hybrid solar collector">
       Photovoltaic thermal hybrid solar collector
      </a>
      , another option is
      <b>
       Concentrated photovoltaics and thermal
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       CPVT
      </b>
      ), also sometimes called
      <b>
       combined heat and power solar
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       CHAPS
      </b>
      ), is a
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       cogeneration
      </a>
      technology used in concentrated photovoltaics that produce both electricity and heat in the same module. The heat may be employed in
      <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">
       district heating
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Water_heating" title="Water heating">
       water heating
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_cooling" title="Solar cooling">
       air conditioning
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">
       desalination
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Process_heat" title="Process heat">
       process heat
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      CPVT systems are currently in production in Europe,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Zenith_Solar" title="Zenith Solar">
       Zenith Solar
      </a>
      developing CPVT systems with a claimed efficiency of 72%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Sopogy" title="Sopogy">
       Sopogy
      </a>
      produces a micro
      <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">
       concentrated solar power
      </a>
      (microCSP) system based on
      <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough" title="Parabolic trough">
       parabolic trough
      </a>
      which can be installed above building or homes, the heat can be used for water heating or
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_air_conditioning" title="Solar air conditioning">
       solar air conditioning
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">
       steam turbine
      </a>
      can also be installed to produce electricity.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="CHP.2BPV">
       CHP+PV
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: CHP+PV">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The recent development of small scale CHP systems has provided the opportunity for in-house power backup of residential-scale
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">
       photovoltaic
      </a>
      (PV) arrays.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1947_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1947-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The results of a recent study show that a PV+CHP hybrid system not only has the potential to radically reduce energy waste in the status quo electrical and heating systems, but it also enables the share of solar PV to be expanded by about a factor of five.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1947_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1947-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some regions, in order to reduce waste from excess heat, an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_chiller" title="Absorption chiller">
       absorption chiller
      </a>
      has been proposed to utilize the CHP-produced thermal energy for cooling of PV-CHP system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      These
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigeneration" title="Trigeneration">
       trigen
      </a>
      +PV systems have the potential to save even more energy.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Net_metering">
       Net metering
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Net metering">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      To date, micro-CHP systems achieve much of their savings, and thus attractiveness to consumers, by the value of electrical energy which is replaced by the autoproduced electricity. A "generate-and-resell" or
      <a href="/wiki/Net_metering" title="Net metering">
       net metering
      </a>
      model supports this as home-generated power exceeding the instantaneous in-home needs is sold back to the electrical utility. This system is efficient because the energy used is distributed and used instantaneously over the
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">
       electrical grid
      </a>
      . The main losses are in the transmission from the source to the consumer which will typically be less than losses incurred by storing energy locally or generating power at less than the peak efficiency of the micro-CHP system. So, from a purely technical standpoint
      <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_demand_(electric_power)" title="Dynamic demand (electric power)">
       dynamic demand management
      </a>
      and net-metering are very efficient.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another positive to net-metering is the fact that it is fairly easy to configure. The user's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_meter" title="Electrical meter">
       electrical meter
      </a>
      is simply able to record electrical power exiting as well as entering the home or business. As such, it records the net amount of power entering the home. For a grid with relatively few micro-CHP users, no design changes to the electrical grid need be made. Additionally, in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , federal and now many state regulations require utility operators to compensate anyone adding power to the grid. From the standpoint of grid operator, these points present operational and technical as well as administrative burdens. As a consequence, most grid operators compensate non-
      <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">
       utility
      </a>
      power-contributors at less than or equal to the rate they charge their customers. While this compensation scheme may seem almost fair at first glance, it only represents the consumer’s cost-savings of not purchasing utility power versus the true cost of generation and operation to the micro-CHP operator. Thus from the standpoint of micro-CHP operators, net-metering is not ideal.
     </p>
     <p>
      While net-metering is a very efficient mechanism for using excess energy generated by a micro-CHP system, it does have detractors. Of the detractors' main points, the first to consider is that while the main generating source on the electrical grid is a large commercial generator, net-metering generators "spill" power to the
      <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">
       smart grid
      </a>
      in a haphazard and unpredictable fashion. However, the effect is negligible if there are only a small percentage of customers generating electricity and each of them generates a relatively small amount of electricity. When turning on an oven or space heater, about the same amount of electricity is drawn from the grid as a home generator puts out. If the percentage of homes with generating systems becomes large, then the effect on the grid may become significant. Coordination among the generating systems in homes and the rest of the grid may be necessary for reliable operation and to prevent damage to the grid.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="UK">
       UK
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: UK">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It is estimated that about 1,000 micro-CHP systems were in operation in the UK as of 2002. These are primarily
      <a href="/wiki/Applications_of_the_Stirling_engine#MicroCHP" title="Applications of the Stirling engine">
       Whispergen
      </a>
      using
      <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">
       Stirling engines
      </a>
      , and Senertec Dachs
      <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_engine" title="Reciprocating engine">
       reciprocating engines
      </a>
      . The market is supported by the government through regulatory work, and some government research money expended through the Energy Saving Trust and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Carbon_Trust" title="The Carbon Trust">
       Carbon Trust
      </a>
      , which are public bodies supporting energy efficiency in the UK.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Effective as of 7 April 2005, the UK government has cut the VAT from 20% to 5% for micro-CHP systems, in order to support demand for this emerging technology at the expense of existing, less environmentally friendly technology. The reduction in VAT is effectively a 10.63%
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">
       subsidy
      </a>
      for micro-CHP units over conventional systems, which will help micro-CHP units become more cost competitive, and ultimately drive micro-CHP sales in the UK. Of the 24 million households in the UK, as many as 14 to 18 million are thought to be suitable for micro-CHP units.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        [64]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Two fuel cell varieties of mCHP co-generation units are almost ready for mainstream production and are planned for release to commercial markets in early in 2014. With the UK Government's Feed-In-Tariff available for a 10-year period, a wide uptake of the technology is anticipated.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Research">
       Research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro_combined_heat_and_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Testing is underway in
      <a href="/wiki/Ameland" title="Ameland">
       Ameland
      </a>
      , the Netherlands for a three-year field testing until 2010 of
      <a href="/wiki/HCNG" title="HCNG">
       HCNG
      </a>
      were 20% hydrogen is added to the local
      <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">
       CNG
      </a>
      distribution net, the appliances involved are
      <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_stove" title="Kitchen stove">
       kitchen stoves
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Condensing_boiler" title="Condensing boiler">
       condensing boilers
      </a>
      , and micro-CHP boilers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        [73]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        [74]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Micro-CHP Accelerator, a field trial performed between 2005 and 2008, studied the performance of 87
      <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">
       Stirling engine
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       internal combustion engine
      </a>
      devices in residential houses in the UK. This study found that the devices resulted in average carbon savings of 9% for houses with heat demand over 54 GJ/year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
       <a href="#cite_note-75">
        [75]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An ASME (American Society of Mechanical Engineers) paper fully describes the performance and operating experience with two residential sized Combined Heat and Power units which were in operation from 1979 through 1995.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        [76]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
