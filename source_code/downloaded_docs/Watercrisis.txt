     <p>
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
        Water
       </a>
       security
      </b>
      has been defined as "the reliable availability of an acceptable quantity and quality of water for health, livelihoods and production, coupled with an acceptable level of water-related risks."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sustainable development will not be achieved without a water secure world. A water secure world integrates a concern for the intrinsic value of water with a concern for its use for human survival and well-being. A water secure world harnesses water's productive power and minimises its destructive force. Water security also means addressing environmental protection and the negative effects of poor management. It is also concerned with ending fragmented responsibility for water and integrating water resources management across all sectors – finance, planning, agriculture, energy, tourism, industry, education and health. A water secure world
      <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">
       reduces poverty
      </a>
      , advances education, and increases living standards. It is a world where there is an improved quality of life for all, especially for the most vulnerable—usually women and children—who benefit most from good water governance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_security&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Institute" title="Pacific Institute">
       Pacific Institute
      </a>
      "While regional impacts will vary, global
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
       climate change
      </a>
      will potentially alter agricultural productivity, freshwater availability and quality, access to vital minerals, coastal and island flooding, and more. Among the consequences of these impacts will be challenges to political relationships, realignment of energy markets and regional economies, and threats to security".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It impacts regions, states and countries. Tensions exist between upstream and downstream users of water within individual jurisdictions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During history there has been much conflict over use of water from
      <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">
       rivers
      </a>
      such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">
       Tigris
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">
       Euphrates
      </a>
      Rivers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormingmedia.us_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-stormingmedia.us-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another highly politicized example is
      <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
       Israel's
      </a>
      control of water resources in the
      <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">
       Levant
      </a>
      region since its creation,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      where Israel securing its water resources was one of several drivers for the 1967
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Six_Day_War" title="Six Day War">
       Six Day War
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Water security is sometimes sought by implementing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_desalination" title="Water desalination">
       water desalination
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">
       pipelines
      </a>
      between sources and users, water licences with different security levels and
      <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">
       war
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Water allocation between competing users is increasingly determined by application of market-based pricing for either water licenses or actual water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fresh_Water">
       Fresh Water
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_security&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fresh Water">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Water, in absolute terms, is not in short supply planet-wide. But, according to the United Nations water organization, UN-Water, the total usable freshwater supply for ecosystems and humans is only about 200,000 km3 of water – less than one percent (&lt;1%) of all freshwater resources. And, water use has been growing at more than twice the rate of the population increase in the last century. Specifically, water withdrawals are predicted to increase by 50 percent by 2025 in developing countries, and 18 per cent in developed countries. By 2025, 1.8 billion people will be living in countries or regions with absolute water scarcity, and two-thirds of the world population could be under stress conditions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Research">
       Research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_security&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thailand_nan_river_fishing.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="499" data-file-width="686" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Thailand_nan_river_fishing.jpg/220px-Thailand_nan_river_fishing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Thailand_nan_river_fishing.jpg/330px-Thailand_nan_river_fishing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Thailand_nan_river_fishing.jpg/440px-Thailand_nan_river_fishing.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thailand_nan_river_fishing.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Thailand nan river fishing
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">
        Nature
       </a>
      </i>
      (2010), about 80% of the world's population (5.6 billion in 2011) live in areas with threats to water security. The water security is a shared threat to human and nature and it is pandemic. Human water-management strategies can be detrimental to wildlife, such as migrating fish. Regions with intensive agriculture and dense populations, such as the US and Europe, have a high threat of water security. Water is increasingly being used as a weapon in conflict.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Water insecurity is always accompanied by one or more issues such as poverty, war and conflict, low women’s development and environmental degradation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Researchers estimate that during 2010-2015, ca US$800 billion will be required to cover the annual global investment in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_infrastructure" title="Water infrastructure">
       water infrastructure
      </a>
      . Good management of water resources can jointly manage
      <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">
       biodiversity
      </a>
      protection and human water security. Preserving flood plains rather than constructing flood-control reservoirs would provide a cost-effective way to control floods while protecting the biodiversity of wildlife that occupies such areas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Lawrence Smith, the president of the population institute, asserts that although an overwhelming majority of the planet's surface is composed of water, 97% of this water is constituted of saltwater; the
      <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">
       fresh water
      </a>
      used to sustain humans is only 3% of the total amount of water on Earth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoevel_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hoevel-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, Smith believes that the competition for water in an overpopulated world would pose a major threat to human stability,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoevel_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hoevel-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      even going so far as to postulate apocalyptic world wars being fought over the control of thinning ice sheets and nearly desiccated reservoirs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoevel_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hoevel-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nevertheless, 2 billion people have supposedly gained access to a safe water source since 1990 who may have earlier lacked it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDG_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MDG-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The proportion of people in developing countries with access to safe water is calculated to have improved from 30 percent in 1970
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      to 71 percent in 1990, 79 percent in 2000 and 84 percent in 2004, parallel with rising population. This trend is projected to continue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDG_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MDG-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
       Earth
      </a>
      has a limited though renewable
      <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">
       supply of fresh water
      </a>
      , stored in
      <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">
       aquifers
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">
       surface waters
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
       atmosphere
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">
       Oceans
      </a>
      are a good source of usable water, but the amount of energy needed to convert
      <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">
       saline water
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potable_water" title="Potable water">
       potable water
      </a>
      is prohibitive with conventional approaches, explaining why only a very small fraction of the world's water supply is derived from
      <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">

       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
        Texas
       </a>
       , and Florida use desalination for a very small part of their water supply.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       After being desalinized at
       <a href="/wiki/Jubail" title="Jubail">
        Jubail
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">
        Saudi Arabia
       </a>
       , water is pumped 200 miles (320 km) inland though a pipeline to the capital city of
       <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">
        Riyadh
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A January 17, 2008, article in the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">
        Wall Street Journal
       </a>
      </i>
      states, "World-wide, 13,080 desalination plants produce more than 12 billion gallons of water a day, according to the International Desalination Association."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The world's largest desalination plant is the
      <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Ali" title="Jebel Ali">
       Jebel Ali
      </a>
      Desalination Plant (Phase 2) in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">
       United Arab Emirates
      </a>
      . It is a dual-purpose facility that uses multi-stage flash distillation and is capable of producing 300 million cubic meters of water per year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A typical
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">
       aircraft carrier
      </a>
      in the U.S. military uses nuclear power to desalinize 400,000 US gallons (1,500,000 L) of water per day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While desalinizing 1,000 US gallons (3,800 L) of water can cost as much as $3, the same amount of
      <a href="/wiki/Bottled_water" title="Bottled water">
       bottled water
      </a>
      costs $7,945.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        [41]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, given the energy intensive nature of desalination, with associated economic and environmental costs, desalination is generally considered a last resort after
      <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">
       water conservation
      </a>
      . But this is changing as prices continue to fall.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">
       MSNBC
      </a>
      , a report by Lux Research estimated that the worldwide desalinated water supply will triple between 2008 and 2020.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, not everyone is convinced that desalination is or will be economically viable or environmentally sustainable for the foreseeable future.
      <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Cook" title="Debbie Cook">
       Debbie Cook
      </a>
      , the former mayor of
      <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Beach,_California" title="Huntington Beach, California">
       Huntington Beach, California
      </a>
      , has been a frequent critic of desalination proposals ever since she was appointed as a member of the California Desalination Task Force. Cook claims that in addition to being energy intensive, desalination schemes are very costly—often much more costly than desalination proponents claim. In her writing on the subject, Cook points to a long list of projects that have stalled or been aborted for financial or other reasons, and suggests that water-stressed regions would do better to focus on conservation or other water supply solutions than to invest in desalination plants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_energy_based_desalination">
       Solar energy based desalination
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_security&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Solar energy based desalination">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A novel approach to desalination is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seawater_Greenhouse" title="Seawater Greenhouse">
       Seawater Greenhouse
      </a>
      which takes seawater and uses solar energy to desalinate it in conjunction with growing food crops in a specially adapted greenhouse.
     </p>
