     <p>
      A
      <b>
       street light
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       light pole
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       lamppost
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       street lamp
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       light standard
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       lamp standard
      </b>
      is a raised source of
      <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
       light
      </a>
      on the edge of a
      <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">
       road
      </a>
      or walkway. Modern lamps may also have light-sensitive
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photocell" title="Photocell">
       photocells
      </a>
      that activate automatically when light is or is not needed:
      <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">
       dusk
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">
       dawn
      </a>
      , or the onset of dark
      <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">
       weather
      </a>
      . This function in older lighting systems could have been performed with the aid of a
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_dial" title="Solar dial">
       solar dial
      </a>
      . Many street light systems are being connected underground instead of wiring from one
      <a href="/wiki/Utility_pole" title="Utility pole">
       utility post
      </a>
      to another.
     </p>

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main articles:
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_lamp" title="Oil lamp">
       oil lamp
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">
       gas lighting
      </a>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Preindustrial_era">
       Preindustrial era
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preindustrial era">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Early lamps were used by Greek and Roman civilizations,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      where light primarily served the purpose of
      <a href="/wiki/Security_lighting" title="Security lighting">
       security
      </a>
      , both to protect the wanderer from tripping on the path over something or keeping the potential robbers at bay.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      At that time oil lamps were used predominantly as they provided a long-lasting and moderate flame. The Romans had a word 'lanternarius', which was a term for a slave responsible for lighting the oil lamps in front of their villas. This task remained the responsibility of a designated person up to the Middle Ages where the so-called '
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Link_boy" title="Link boy">
       link boys
      </a>
      ' escorted people from one place to another through the murky winding streets of medieval towns.
     </p>
     <p>
      Before
      <a href="/wiki/Incandescence" title="Incandescence">
       incandescent
      </a>
      lamps,
      <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">
       candle lighting
      </a>
      was employed in cities. The earliest lamps required that a
      <a href="/wiki/Lamplighter" title="Lamplighter">
       lamplighter
      </a>
      tour the town at dusk, lighting each of the lamps. According to some sources, illumination was ordered in
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      in 1417 by Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Barton" title="Henry Barton">
       Henry Barton
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">
       Mayor of London
      </a>
      though there is no firm evidence of this.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1524, Paris house owners were required to have lanterns with candles lit in front of their houses at night, but the law was often ignored. Following the invention of lanterns with glass windows, which greatly improved the quantity of light, in 1594 the police of Paris took charge of installing lanterns in each city neighborhood. Still, in 1662, it was a common practice for travelers to hire a lantern-bearer if they had to move at night through the dark, winding streets. Lantern bearers were still common in Paris until 1789. In 1667, under King
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">
       Louis XIV
      </a>
      , the royal government began installing lanterns on all the streets. There were three thousand in place by 1669, and twice as many by 1729. Lanterns with glass windows were suspended from a cord over the middle of the street at a height of twenty feet and were placed twenty yards apart. A much-improved oil lantern, called a
      <i>
       réverbère
      </i>
      , was introduced between 1745 and 1749. These lamps were attached to the top of lampposts; by 1817, there were 4694 lamps on the Paris streets. During the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
       French Revolution
      </a>
      (1789-1799), the revolutionaries found that the lampposts were a convenient place to hang aristocrats and other opponents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFierro1996836_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFierro1996836-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">
       Gas lighting
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first widespread system of street lighting used piped
      <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">
       coal gas
      </a>
      as fuel.
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hales" title="Stephen Hales">
       Stephen Hales
      </a>
      was the first person who procured a flammable fluid from the actual distillation of coal in 1726 and John Clayton, in 1735, called gas the "spirit" of coal and discovered its flammability by accident.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Murdoch_House_%26_St._Rumon%27s_Gardens.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3072" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Murdoch_House_%26_St._Rumon%27s_Gardens.jpg/220px-Murdoch_House_%26_St._Rumon%27s_Gardens.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Murdoch_House_%26_St._Rumon%27s_Gardens.jpg/330px-Murdoch_House_%26_St._Rumon%27s_Gardens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Murdoch_House_%26_St._Rumon%27s_Gardens.jpg/440px-Murdoch_House_%26_St._Rumon%27s_Gardens.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Murdoch_House_%26_St._Rumon%27s_Gardens.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/William_Murdoch" title="William Murdoch">
         William Murdoch
        </a>
        's house in
        <a href="/wiki/Redruth" title="Redruth">
         Redruth
        </a>
        , UK, the first domestic house lit by gas in the world
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/William_Murdoch" title="William Murdoch">
       William Murdoch
      </a>
      (sometimes spelled "Murdock") was the first to use the flammability of gas for the practical application of lighting. In the early 1790s, while overseeing the use of his company's steam engines in tin mining in Cornwall, Murdoch began experimenting with various types of gas, finally settling on coal-gas as the most effective. He first lit his own house in
      <a href="/wiki/Redruth" title="Redruth">
       Redruth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">
       Cornwall
      </a>
      in 1792.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1798, he used gas to light the main building of the Soho Foundry and in 1802 lit the outside in a public display of gas lighting, the lights astonishing the local population.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Paris, gas lighting was first demonstrated in November 1800 at a private residence on the rue Saint-Dominique, and was installed on a covered shopping street, the
      <a href="/wiki/Passage_des_Panoramas" title="Passage des Panoramas">
       Passage des Panoramas
      </a>
      , in 1817. The First gas lamps on the streets of Paris appeared in January 1829 on the place du Carrousel and the rue de Rivoli, then on rue de la Paix, place Vendôme, rue de Castiglione; by 1857 the Grands Boulevards were all lit with gas. A Parisian writer enthused in August, 1857: "That which most enchants the Parisians is the new lighting by gas of the boulevards...From the church of the Madeleine all the way to rue Montmartre, these two rows of lamps, shining with a clarity white and pure, have a marvelous effect." The gaslights installed on the boulevards and city monuments in the 19th century gave the city the nickname "The City of Light."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFierro1996838_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFierro1996838-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first public street lighting with gas was demonstrated in
      <a href="/wiki/Pall_Mall,_London" title="Pall Mall, London">
       Pall Mall, London
      </a>
      on January 28, 1807 by
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Albert_Winsor" title="Frederick Albert Winsor">
       Frederick Albert Winsor
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In 1812,
      <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">
       Parliament
      </a>
      granted a charter to the London and Westminster
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_Light_and_Coke_Company" title="Gas Light and Coke Company">
       Gas Light and Coke Company
      </a>
      , and the first gas company in the world came into being. Less than two years later, on December 31, 1813, the
      <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Bridge" title="Westminster Bridge">
       Westminster Bridge
      </a>
      was lit by gas.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Following this success, gas lighting spread to other countries. The use of gas lights in
      <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt_Peale" title="Rembrandt Peale">
       Rembrandt Peale
      </a>
      's Museum in
      <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">
       Baltimore
      </a>
      in 1816 was a great success. Baltimore was the first American city with gas streetlights, provided by Peale's Gas Light Company of Baltimore.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first place outside
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      to have gas lighting, was
      <a href="/wiki/Preston,_Lancashire" title="Preston, Lancashire">
       Preston, Lancashire
      </a>
      in 1825, this was due to the
      <i>
       Preston Gaslight Company
      </i>
      run by revolutionary
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Dunn_(revolutionary)" title="Joseph Dunn (revolutionary)">
       Joseph Dunn
      </a>
      , who found the most improved way of brighter gas lighting.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil-gas" title="Oil-gas">
       Oil-gas
      </a>
      appeared in the field as a rival of coal-gas guff. In 1815,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Taylor_(civil_engineer)" title="John Taylor (civil engineer)">
       John Taylor
      </a>
      patented an apparatus for the decomposition of "oil" and other animal substances. Public attention was attracted to "oil-gas" by the display of the patent apparatus at
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Apothecary%27s_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Apothecary's Hall (page does not exist)">
       Apothecary's Hall
      </a>
      , by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taylor_%26_Martineau" title="Taylor &amp; Martineau">
       Taylor &amp; Martineau
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The first modern street lamps to use kerosene were introduced in
      <a href="/wiki/Lviv" title="Lviv">
       Lviv
      </a>
      in what was then the
      <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">
       Austrian Empire
      </a>
      in 1853. In
      <a href="/wiki/Brest,_Belarus" title="Brest, Belarus">
       Brest
      </a>
      , street lighting with
      <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_lamp" title="Kerosene lamp">
       kerosene lamps
      </a>
      reappeared in 2009 in the shopping street as a tourist attraction.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Farola_fernandina">
       <i>
        Farola fernandina
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Farola fernandina">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Farola fernandina
      </i>
      is a traditional design of street light which remains popular in Spain. Essentially it is a neo-classical French gas lamp style dating from the late 18th century. It may be either a wall-bracket or standard lamp. The standard base is cast metal with an escutchion bearing two intertwined letters 'F', the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_cypher" title="Royal cypher">
       Royal cypher
      </a>
      of King
      <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VII of Spain">
       Ferdinand VII of Spain
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and commemorates the date of the birth of his daughter, the
      <a href="/wiki/Infanta_Luisa_Fernanda,_Duchess_of_Montpensier" title="Infanta Luisa Fernanda, Duchess of Montpensier">
       Infanta Luisa Fernanda, Duchess of Montpensier
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Arc_lamps_and_incandescent_lighting">
       Arc lamps and incandescent lighting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arc lamps and incandescent lighting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yablochkov_candles_illuminating_Avenue_de_l%27Opera_ca1878.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yablochkov_candles_illuminating_Avenue_de_l%27Opera_ca1878.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Demonstration of
        <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Yablochkov" title="Pavel Yablochkov">
         Yablochkov's
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Yablochkov_candle" title="Yablochkov candle">
         arc lamp
        </a>
        on the Avenue de l'Opera in Paris (1878), the first form of electric street lighting
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BCBG_Lamppost_01.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4320" data-file-width="3240" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BCBG_Lamppost_01.JPG/170px-BCBG_Lamppost_01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BCBG_Lamppost_01.JPG/255px-BCBG_Lamppost_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/BCBG_Lamppost_01.JPG/340px-BCBG_Lamppost_01.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BCBG_Lamppost_01.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Heritage lamp post in the
        <a href="/wiki/City_Botanic_Gardens" title="City Botanic Gardens">
         City Botanic Gardens
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">
         Brisbane
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first electric street lighting employed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_lamps" title="Arc lamps">
       arc lamps
      </a>
      , initially the 'Electric candle', 'Jablotchkoff candle' or '
      <a href="/wiki/Yablochkov_candle" title="Yablochkov candle">
       Yablochkov candle
      </a>
      ' developed by the Russian
      <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Yablochkov" title="Pavel Yablochkov">
       Pavel Yablochkov
      </a>
      in 1875. This was a carbon arc lamp employing alternating current, which ensured that both electrodes were consumed at equal rates. In 1876, the common council of the City of Los Angeles ordered four arc lights installed in various places in the fledgling town for street lighting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On May 30, 1878, the first electric street lights in Paris were installed on the avenue de l'Opera and the Place d'Etoile, around the
      <a href="/wiki/Arc_de_Triomphe" title="Arc de Triomphe">
       Arc de Triomphe
      </a>
      , to celebrate the opening of the
      <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1878)" title="Exposition Universelle (1878)">
       Paris Universal Exposition
      </a>
      . In 1881, to coincide with the Paris International Exposition of Electricity, street lights were installed on the major boulevards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFierro1996628_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFierro1996628-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first streets in London lit with the electrical arc lamp were by the
      <a href="/wiki/Holborn_Viaduct" title="Holborn Viaduct">
       Holborn Viaduct
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Thames_Embankment" title="Thames Embankment">
       Thames Embankment
      </a>
      in 1878. More than 4,000 were in use by 1881, though by then an improved differential arc lamp had been developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_von_Hefner-Alteneck" title="Friedrich von Hefner-Alteneck">
       Friedrich von Hefner-Alteneck
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Siemens_%26_Halske" title="Siemens &amp; Halske">
       Siemens &amp; Halske
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      was quick in adopting arc lighting, and by 1890 over 130,000 were in operation in the US, commonly installed in exceptionally tall
      <a href="/wiki/Moonlight_tower" title="Moonlight tower">
       moonlight towers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Arc lights had two major disadvantages. First, they emit an intense and harsh light which, although useful at industrial sites like dockyards, was discomforting in ordinary city streets. Second, they are maintenance-intensive, as carbon electrodes burn away swiftly. With the development of cheap, reliable and bright
      <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">
       incandescent light bulbs
      </a>
      at the end of the 19th century, arc lights passed out of use for street lighting, but remained in industrial use longer.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first street to be lit by an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_lightbulb" title="Incandescent lightbulb">
       incandescent lightbulb
      </a>
      was Mosley Street, in
      <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">
       Newcastle upon Tyne
      </a>
      . The street was lit by
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Swan" title="Joseph Swan">
       Joseph Swan
      </a>
      's incandescent lamp on 3 February 1879.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Consequently, Newcastle was the first city in the world to be fully lit up by lighting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mosely_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mosely-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first street to be lit with modern electricity as we know it was Electric Avenue in London's Brixton in 1880. The first city in the United States, and second overall after Newcastle (England), was the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_Square,_Cleveland" title="Public Square, Cleveland">
       Public Square
      </a>
      road system in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">
       Cleveland, Ohio
      </a>
      , on April 29, 1879.
      <a href="/wiki/Wabash,_Indiana" title="Wabash, Indiana">
       Wabash, Indiana
      </a>
      holds the title of being the third electrically lit city in the world, which took place on February 2, 1880. Four 3,000
      <a href="/wiki/Candlepower" title="Candlepower">
       candlepower
      </a>
      Brush arc lamps suspended over the courthouse rendered the town square "as light as midday.
      <a href="/wiki/Kimberley,_Northern_Cape" title="Kimberley, Northern Cape">
       Kimberley
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
       South Africa
      </a>
      , was the first city in the
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
       Southern Hemisphere
      </a>
      and in
      <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
       Africa
      </a>
      to have electric street lights - first lit on 1 September 1882 .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Central America,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Jose,_Costa_Rica" title="San Jose, Costa Rica">
       San Jose
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
       Costa Rica
      </a>
      was the first city, the system was launched on August 9, 1884, with 25 lamps powered by a hydroelectric plant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      , was the first city in
      <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">
       Continental Europe
      </a>
      to have electric public lighting on 12 November 1884. 731 lamps were used. In 1886, the tiny, isolated mining town of Waratah in NW Tasmania was the first to have electrically powered street lighting in Australia. On 9 December 1882,
      <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">
       Brisbane
      </a>
      , Queensland, Australia was introduced to electricity by having a demonstration of using eight arc lights, erected along
      <a href="/wiki/Queen_Street_Mall,_Brisbane" title="Queen Street Mall, Brisbane">
       Queen Street Mall
      </a>
      . The power to supply these arc lights was taken from a 10 hp Crompton DC generator driven by a Robey steam engine in a small foundry in Adelaide Street and occupied by
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_William_Sutton" title="Joseph William Sutton">
       J. W. Sutton and Co.
      </a>
      The lamps were erected on cast iron standards, 20 ft in height. In 1888
      <a href="/wiki/Tamworth,_New_South_Wales" title="Tamworth, New South Wales">
       Tamworth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">
       New South Wales
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      became the first location in New South Wales to have electric street lighting, for which that city erroneously maintains the title of "First City of Light".
     </p>
     <p>
      Incandescent lamps were primarily used for street lighting until the advent of high-intensity discharge lamps. They were often operated at high-voltage
      <a href="/wiki/Series_and_parallel_circuits#Series_circuits" title="Series and parallel circuits">
       series circuits
      </a>
      . Series circuits were popular since the higher voltage in these circuits produced more light per watt consumed. Furthermore, before the invention of photoelectric controls, a single switch or clock could control all the lights in an entire district.
     </p>
     <p>
      To avoid having the entire system go dark if a single lamp burned out, each street lamp was equipped with a device that ensured that the circuit would remain intact. Early series street lights were equipped with isolation transformers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      that would allow current to pass across the transformer whether the bulb worked or not. Later the
      <b>
       film cutout
      </b>
      was invented. The film cutout was a small disk of insulating film that separated two contacts connected to the two wires leading to the lamp. If the lamp failed (an
      <a href="/wiki/Open-circuit_voltage" title="Open-circuit voltage">
       open circuit
      </a>
      ), the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_(electricity)" title="Current (electricity)">
       current
      </a>
      through the string became zero, causing the voltage of the circuit (thousands of volts) to be imposed across the insulating film, penetrating it (see
      <a href="/wiki/Ohm%27s_law" title="Ohm's law">
       Ohm's law
      </a>
      ). In this way, the failed lamp was bypassed and power restored to the rest of the district. The street light circuit contained an automatic current regulator, preventing the current from increasing as lamps burned out, preserving the life of the remaining lamps. When the failed lamp was replaced, a new piece of film was installed, once again separating the contacts in the cutout. This system was recognizable by the large
      <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">
       porcelain
      </a>
      insulator separating the lamp and reflector from the mounting arm. This was necessary because the two contacts in the lamp's base may have operated at several thousand volts above ground/earth.
     </p>

     <p>
      Today, street lighting commonly uses
      <a href="/wiki/High-intensity_discharge_lamp" title="High-intensity discharge lamp">
       high-intensity discharge lamps
      </a>
      , often HPS
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_sodium_lamp" title="High pressure sodium lamp">
       high pressure sodium lamps
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Such lamps provide the greatest amount of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photopic" title="Photopic">
       photopic
      </a>
      illumination for the least consumption of electricity. However, white light sources have been shown to double driver peripheral vision and improve driver brake reaction time by at least 25%; to enable pedestrians to better detect pavement trip hazards
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and to facilitate visual appraisals of other people associated with interpersonal judgements. Studies comparing metal halide and high-pressure sodium lamps have shown that at equal photopic light levels, a street scene illuminated at night by a metal halide lighting system was reliably seen as brighter and safer than the same scene illuminated by a high pressure sodium system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Two national standards now allow for variation in illuminance when using lamps of different spectra. In Australia, HPS lamp performance needs to be reduced by a minimum value of 75%. In the UK, illuminances are reduced with higher values S/P ratio
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      New street lighting technologies, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">
       LED
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Induction_lights" title="Induction lights">
       induction lights
      </a>
      , emit a white light that provides high levels of scotopic lumens allowing street lights with lower wattages and lower photopic lumens to replace existing street lights. However, there have been no formal specifications written around Photopic/Scotopic adjustments for different types of light sources, causing many municipalities and street departments to hold back on implementation of these new technologies until the standards are updated. Eastbourne in East Sussex UK is currently undergoing a project to see 6000 of its street lights converted to LED and will be closely followed by Hastings in early 2014.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milan,_Italy" title="Milan, Italy">
       Milan, Italy
      </a>
      , is the first major city to have entirely switched to LED lighting.

       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      In North America, the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Mississauga" title="Mississauga">
       Mississauga
      </a>
      (Canada) was one of the first and biggest LED conversion projects with over 46,000 lights converted to LED technology between 2012 and 2014. It is also one of the first cities in North America to use
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smart_City" title="Smart City">
       Smart City
      </a>
      technology to control the lights.
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DimOnOff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DimOnOff (page does not exist)">
       DimOnOff
      </a>
      , a company based in
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">
       Quebec City
      </a>
      , was chosen as a Smart City partner for this project.

       <a href="#cite_note-23">

       </a>
      </sup>

      Two very similar measurement systems were created to bridge the scotopic and photopic luminous efficiency functions,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>

       </a>
      </sup>
      creating a Unified System of Photometry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This new measurement has been well-received because the reliance on V(λ) alone for characterizing night-time light illuminations requires more electric energy. The cost-savings potential of using a new way to measure mesopic lighting scenarios is tremendous.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Outdoor Site-Lighting Performance (OSP) is a method for predicting and measuring three different aspects of light pollution: glow, trespass and glare.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Using this method, lighting specifiers can quantify the performance of existing and planned lighting designs and applications to minimize excessive or obtrusive light leaving the boundaries of a property.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">
       Advantages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Advantages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Major advantages of street lighting include prevention of accidents and increase in safety.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Studies have shown that darkness results in a large number of crashes and fatalities, especially those involving pedestrians; pedestrian fatalities are 3 to 6.75 times more likely in the dark than in daylight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Street lighting has been found to reduce pedestrian crashes by approximately 50%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Furthermore, lighted intersections and highway interchanges tend to have fewer crashes than unlighted intersections and interchanges.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Towns, cities, and villages use the unique locations provided by lampposts to hang decorative or commemorative banners.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many communities in the US use lampposts as a tool for fund raising via lamppost banner sponsorship programs first designed by a US based lamppost banner manufacturer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        [41]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">
       Disadvantages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The major criticisms of street lighting are that it can actually cause accidents if misused, and cause
      <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">
       light pollution
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers">
       Dangers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dangers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are two optical phenomena that need to be recognized in street light installations.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The loss of
       <a href="/wiki/Night_vision" title="Night vision">
        night vision
       </a>
       because of the
       <a href="/wiki/Accommodation_reflex" title="Accommodation reflex">
        accommodation reflex
       </a>
       of drivers' eyes is the greatest danger. As drivers emerge from an unlighted area into a pool of light from a street light their
       <a href="/wiki/Pupil" title="Pupil">
        pupils
       </a>
       quickly constrict to adjust to the brighter light, but as they leave the pool of light the dilation of their pupils to adjust to the dimmer light is much slower, so they are driving with impaired vision. As a person gets older the eye's recovery speed gets slower, so driving time and distance under impaired vision increases.
      </li>
      <li>
       Oncoming headlights are more visible against a black background than a grey one. The contrast creates greater awareness of the oncoming vehicle.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Stray_voltage" title="Stray voltage">
        Stray voltage
       </a>
       is also a concern in many cities. Stray voltage can accidentally electrify lampposts and has the potential to injure or kill anyone who comes into contact with the post.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      There are also physical dangers other than children climbing them for recreational purposes. Street light stanchions (lampposts) pose a collision risk to motorists and pedestrians, particularly those affected by poor eyesight or under the influence of alcohol. This can be reduced by designing them to break away when hit (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frangible" title="Frangible">
       frangible
      </a>
      or collapsible supports), protecting them by guardrails, or marking the lower portions to increase their visibility. High winds or accumulated
      <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">
       metal fatigue
      </a>
      also occasionally topple street lights.
     </p>

       </a>

     <p>
      In urban areas
      <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">
       light pollution
      </a>
      can hide the stars and interfere with
      <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
       astronomy
      </a>
      and the migration of many bird species
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . In settings near astronomical
      <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
       telescopes
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">
       observatories
      </a>
      , low pressure
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_lamp" title="Sodium lamp">
       sodium lamps
      </a>
      may be used. These lamps are advantageous over other lamps such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_lamp" title="Mercury lamp">
       mercury
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_halide_lamp" title="Metal halide lamp">
       metal halide lamps
      </a>
      because low pressure sodium lamps emit lower intensity,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">
       monochromatic
      </a>
      light. Observatories can filter the sodium
      <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
       wavelength
      </a>
      out of their observations and virtually eliminate the interference from nearby urban lighting. Full cutoff streetlights also reduce light pollution by reducing the amount of light that is directed at the sky which also improves the luminous efficiency of the light.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_consumption">
       Energy consumption
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Energy consumption">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Globally 70% of all electricity is generated by burning fossil fuels, a source of air pollution including greenhouse gases, and also globally there are approximately 300 million street lights using that electricity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cities are exploring more
      <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">
       efficient energy use
      </a>
      , reducing street light power consumption by dimming lights during off-peak hours and switching to high-efficiency LED lamps.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A British county council has turned off 5% of its street lights on a trial basis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Street_light_control_systems">
       Street light control systems
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Street light control systems">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A number of street light control systems have been developed to control and reduce energy consumption of a town's public lighting system. These range from controlling a circuit of street lights and/or individual lights with specific ballasts and network operating protocols. These may include sending and receiving instructions via separate data networks,at high frequency over the top of the low voltage supply or wireless.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        [46]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Image-based_street_light_control">
       Image-based street light control
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Image-based street light control">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A number of companies are now manufacturing
      <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_street_lighting" title="Intelligent street lighting">
       Intelligent street lighting
      </a>
      that adjust light output based on usage and occupancy, i.e. automating classification of pedestrian versus cyclist, versus automotive, sensing also velocity of movement and illuminating a certain number of streetlights ahead and fewer behind, depending on velocity of movement. Also the lights adjust depending on road conditions, for example, snow produces more reflectance therefore reduced light is required.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">
       Purpose
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Purpose">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are three distinct main uses of street lights, each requiring different types of lights and placement. Misuse of the different types of lights can make the situation worse by compromising visibility or safety.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Beacon_lights">
       Beacon lights
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Beacon lights">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Plaza_at_sunset,_Samaipata,_Bolivia.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4912" data-file-width="3264" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/The_Plaza_at_sunset%2C_Samaipata%2C_Bolivia.jpg/170px-The_Plaza_at_sunset%2C_Samaipata%2C_Bolivia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/The_Plaza_at_sunset%2C_Samaipata%2C_Bolivia.jpg/255px-The_Plaza_at_sunset%2C_Samaipata%2C_Bolivia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/The_Plaza_at_sunset%2C_Samaipata%2C_Bolivia.jpg/340px-The_Plaza_at_sunset%2C_Samaipata%2C_Bolivia.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Plaza_at_sunset,_Samaipata,_Bolivia.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Decorative but functional lamps in the Plaza at sunset,
        <a href="/wiki/Samaipata,_Bolivia" title="Samaipata, Bolivia">
         Samaipata, Bolivia
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A modest steady light at the intersection of two roads is an aid to navigation because it helps a driver see the location of a side road as they come closer to it and they can adjust their braking and know exactly where to turn if they intend to leave the main road or see vehicles or pedestrians. A beacon light's function is to say "here I am" and even a dim light provides enough contrast against the dark night to serve the purpose. To prevent the dangers caused by a car driving through a pool of light, a beacon light must never shine onto the main road, and not brightly onto the side road. In residential areas, this is usually the only appropriate lighting, and it has the bonus side effect of providing spill lighting onto any sidewalk there for the benefit of pedestrians. On Interstate highways this purpose is commonly served by placing reflectors at the sides of the road.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Roadway_lights">
       Roadway lights
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Roadway lights">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Highway_401_Night_Lapse_Busy.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2592" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Highway_401_Night_Lapse_Busy.jpg/220px-Highway_401_Night_Lapse_Busy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Highway_401_Night_Lapse_Busy.jpg/330px-Highway_401_Night_Lapse_Busy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Highway_401_Night_Lapse_Busy.jpg/440px-Highway_401_Night_Lapse_Busy.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">

       </a>

        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:401_widest_point.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Conventional streetlights are used instead of high mast lighting near airport runway approaches due to the negative effects they cause.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Because of the dangers discussed above, roadway lights are properly used sparingly and only when a particular situation justifies increasing the risk. This usually involves an intersection with several turning movements and much signage, situations where drivers must take in much information quickly that is not in the headlights' beam. In these situations (A freeway junction or exit ramp) the intersection may be lit so that drivers can quickly see all hazards, and a well designed plan will have gradually increasing lighting for approximately a quarter of a minute before the intersection and gradually decreasing lighting after it. The main stretches of highways remain unlighted to preserve the driver's night vision and increase the visibility of oncoming headlights. If there is a sharp curve where headlights will not illuminate the road, a light on the outside of the curve is often justified.
     </p>
     <p>
      If it is desired to light a roadway (perhaps due to heavy and fast multi-lane traffic), to avoid the dangers of casual placement of street lights it should not be lit intermittently, as this requires repeated eye readjustment which implies
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asthenopia" title="Asthenopia">
       eyestrain
      </a>
      and temporary blindness when entering and leaving light pools. In this case the system is designed to eliminate the need for headlights. This is usually achieved with bright lights placed on high poles at close regular intervals so that there is consistent light along the route. The lighting goes from curb to curb.
     </p>
     <p>
      For more information, see the lighting section of
      <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_crossing" title="Pedestrian crossing">
       "Pedestrian Crossings"
      </a>
      article.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">
       Maintenance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Street_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Maintenance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_York_City_Street_Light_Status.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2364" data-file-width="3174" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/New_York_City_Street_Light_Status.jpg/220px-New_York_City_Street_Light_Status.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/New_York_City_Street_Light_Status.jpg/330px-New_York_City_Street_Light_Status.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/New_York_City_Street_Light_Status.jpg/440px-New_York_City_Street_Light_Status.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_York_City_Street_Light_Status.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        New York City street light status indicator
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Street lighting systems require ongoing maintenance, which can be classified as either reactive or preventative. Reactive maintenance is a direct response to a lighting failure, such as replacing a discharge lamp after it has failed, or replacing an entire lighting unit after it has been hit by a vehicle. Preventative maintenance is scheduled replacement of lighting components, for example replacing all of the discharge lamps in an area of the city when they have reached 85% of their expected life. In the United Kingdom the Roads Liaison Group has issued a
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.ukroadsliaisongroup.org/en/guidance/codes-of-practice.cfm" rel="nofollow">
       Code of Practice
      </a>
      recommending specific reactive and preventative maintenance procedures.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some street lights in New York City have an orange or red light on top of the luminair (light fixture) or a red light attached to the lamppost. This indicates that near to this lighting pole or in the same intersection, there is a fire alarm pull box.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        [47]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other street lights have a small red light next to the street light bulb, when the small light flashes, it indicates an issue with the electrical current.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
