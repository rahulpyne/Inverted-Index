     <p>
      <b>
       The Geysers
      </b>
      is the world's largest geothermal field, containing a complex of 22
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">
       geothermal power
      </a>
      plants, drawing steam from more than 350 wells, located in the
      <a href="/wiki/Mayacamas_Mountains" title="Mayacamas Mountains">
       Mayacamas Mountains
      </a>
      approximately 72 miles (116 km) north of
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
       San Francisco
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Geysers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Geysers_Resort_Hotel.tif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1404" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/The_Geysers_Resort_Hotel.tif/lossy-page1-220px-The_Geysers_Resort_Hotel.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/The_Geysers_Resort_Hotel.tif/lossy-page1-330px-The_Geysers_Resort_Hotel.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/The_Geysers_Resort_Hotel.tif/lossy-page1-440px-The_Geysers_Resort_Hotel.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Geysers_Resort_Hotel.tif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Geysers Resort Hotel, ca 1880
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      For about 12,000 years, Native American tribes built steambaths at the Geysers and used the steam and hot water for healing purposes and cooking.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      When Euro/Americans first entered the area, six Indian tribes inhabited the area around the Geysers, three bands of
      <a href="/wiki/Pomo_people" title="Pomo people">
       Pomo people
      </a>
      , two bands of
      <a href="/wiki/Wappo_people" title="Wappo people">
       Wappo people
      </a>
      , and the Lake
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miwok_people" title="Miwok people">
       Miwok people
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Wappo also collected sulfur which they called
      <i>
       te'ke
      </i>
      and a Wappo village, named
      <i>
       tekena'ntsonoma
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       teke
      </i>
      sulphur +
      <i>
       nan
      </i>
      well containing water +
      <i>
       tso
      </i>
      ground +
      <i>
       no'ma
      </i>
      village) was located about 12 miles (19 km) southeast of
      <a href="/wiki/Cloverdale,_California" title="Cloverdale, California">
       Cloverdale
      </a>
      and on the present-day Sulphur Creek.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Geysers were first seen by Euro/Americans and named in 1847 during
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Fremont" title="John Fremont">
       John Fremont
      </a>
      's survey of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">
       Sierra Mountains
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">
       Great Basin
      </a>
      by William Bell Elliot who called the area "The Geysers," although the geothermal features he discovered were not technically
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geysers" title="Geysers">
       geysers
      </a>
      , but
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumaroles" title="Fumaroles">
       fumaroles
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Between 1848 and 1854,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Archibald_C._Godwin" title="Archibald C. Godwin">
       Archibald C. Godwin
      </a>
      developed The Geysers into a spa named
      <i>
       The Geysers Resort Hotel
      </i>
      , which attracted tourists including
      <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant" title="Ulysses S. Grant">
       Ulysses S. Grant
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">
       Theodore Roosevelt
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">
       Mark Twain
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The resort declined in popularity in the mid 1880s, and rebranded itself to appeal to lower-income people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1938, the main building was destroyed in a landslide although the bar/restaurant, small cabins and the swimming pool stayed open, despite another fire in March 1957, until about 1979.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Unocal_Corporation" title="Unocal Corporation">
       Unocal Corporation
      </a>
      dismantled the remains of the resort in 1980.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Five of the Geysers facilities were damaged in the
      <a href="/wiki/Valley_Fire" title="Valley Fire">
       Valley Fire
      </a>
      of September 2015, suffering "severe" damage to their cooling towers. The main power houses were not damaged.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal_development">
       Geothermal development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Geysers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geothermal development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GeysersGeothermalDrilling1977.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="513" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/GeysersGeothermalDrilling1977.jpg/200px-GeysersGeothermalDrilling1977.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/GeysersGeothermalDrilling1977.jpg/300px-GeysersGeothermalDrilling1977.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/GeysersGeothermalDrilling1977.jpg/400px-GeysersGeothermalDrilling1977.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GeysersGeothermalDrilling1977.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Drilling a geothermal well, 1977 (USGS)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Geysers is the world's largest geothermal field
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GeoEnergy_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GeoEnergy-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      spanning an area of around 30 square miles (78 km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Sonoma_County,_California" title="Sonoma County, California">
       Sonoma
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_County,_California" title="Lake County, California">
       Lake
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mendocino_County,_California" title="Mendocino County, California">
       Mendocino
      </a>
      counties in California, centered in the area of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Geyser_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Geyser Canyon (page does not exist)">
       Geyser Canyon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cobb_Mountain" title="Cobb Mountain">
       Cobb Mountain
      </a>
      . Power from The Geysers provides electricity to Sonoma, Lake, Mendocino,
      <a href="/wiki/Marin_County,_California" title="Marin County, California">
       Marin
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Napa_County,_California" title="Napa County, California">
       Napa
      </a>
      counties. It is estimated that the development meets 60% of the power demand for the coastal region between the
      <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">
       Golden Gate Bridge
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">
       Oregon
      </a>
      state line.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calpine_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-calpine-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unlike most geothermal resources, the Geysers is a dry steam field which mainly produces
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superheated" title="Superheated">
       superheated
      </a>
      steam.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GeoEnergy_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GeoEnergy-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Steam used at The Geysers is produced from a
      <a href="/wiki/Greywacke" title="Greywacke">
       greywacke
      </a>
      sandstone reservoir, capped by a heterogeneous mix of low permeability rocks and underlaid by a
      <a href="/wiki/Silicic" title="Silicic">
       silicic
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion" title="Intrusion">
       intrusion
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gravity and seismic studies suggest that the source of heat for the steam reservoir is a large
      <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">
       magma
      </a>
      chamber over 4 miles (6.4 km) beneath the surface, and greater than 8 miles (13 km) in diameter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first geothermal wells drilled in Geyser Canyon were the first in the Western Hemisphere.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first power plant at the Geysers was privately developed by the owner of The Geysers Resort
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgson_1-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hodgson-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and opened in 1921, producing 250 kilowatts of energy to light the resort.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1960,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Gas_and_Electric" title="Pacific Gas and Electric">
       Pacific Gas and Electric
      </a>
      began operation of their 11-megawatt plant at the Geysers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100years_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-100years-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The original turbine lasted for more than 30 years and produced 11
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>
      net power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By 1999 the steam to power extraction had begun to deplete the Geysers steam field and production began to drop.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, since October 16, 1997, the Geysers steam field has been recharged by injection of treated sewage effluent, producing approximately 77 megawatts of capacity in 2004.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakeCo_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LakeCo-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The effluent is piped up to 50 miles (80 km) from its source at the Lake County Sanitation waste water treatment plants and added to the Geysers steam field via geothermal injection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakeCo_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-LakeCo-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2004, 85% of the effluent produced by four waste-water treatment plants serving 10 Lake County communities was diverted to the Geysers steam field.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakeCo_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-LakeCo-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Injecting treated water into the Geysers field increases the amount of power that can be generated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakeCo_11-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-LakeCo-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The injection of wastewater to the Geysers protects local waterways and
      <a href="/wiki/Clear_Lake_(California)" title="Clear Lake (California)">
       Clear Lake
      </a>
      by diverting effluent which used to be put into surface waters,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakeCo_11-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-LakeCo-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and has produced electricity without releasing greenhouse gases into the atmosphere.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Seismicity">
       Seismicity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Geysers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seismicity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">
       Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory
      </a>
      Earth Sciences division, seismicity was very low prior to the use of the Geyser steam field for geothermal energy, although this may have been the result of low seismic coverage of the area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EGS_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EGS-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Before 1969, there were no earthquakes above
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquake_magnitude#Magnitude_scales" title="Earthquake magnitude">
       magnitude
      </a>
      2 recorded by the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
       United States Geological Survey
      </a>
      (USGS) in an approximately 70 square miles (180 km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) area around the Geysers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EGS_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EGS-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Studies have shown that injecting water into the Geysers field produces earthquakes from magnitude 0.5 to 3.0, although a 4.6 occurred in 1973 and magnitude four events increased thereafter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EGS_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-EGS-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Even with increasing injection rates over time, the rate of magnitude 3 earthquakes has remained relatively unchanged since the 1980s,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EGS_12-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-EGS-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      although the amount of earthquakes has increased significantly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A magnitude 4.5 earthquake struck near the Geysers on January 12, 2014.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite the increases in the number of earthquakes and the fears of local residents, it is unlikely that a large earthquake will occur at the Geysers since there is no large earthquake fault or fracture nearby.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geochemistry">
       Geochemistry
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Geysers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geochemistry">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2005, abatement equipment was installed at two of the Geysers plants to reduce the amount of mercury released by the waste vapor even though the amount released was below the legal limit for such releases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GeoEnergy_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-GeoEnergy-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Geysers Air Monitoring Programs (GAMP) has shown limited releases of arsenic, but again below a significant level.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GeoEnergy_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-GeoEnergy-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Geysers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Power plants at the Geysers are of the dry steam power plant type, where the steam directly powers the generator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GeoEnergy_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-GeoEnergy-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In general, the Geysers has
      <span class="nowrap">
       1517
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
        MW
       </a>
      </span>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      of active installed capacity with an average production factor of 63% (955 MW).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Of nearly two dozen active plants in the Geysers in 2013,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calpine_Corporation" title="Calpine Corporation">
       Calpine Corporation
      </a>
      operated 19 plants in 2004
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      but only 15 in 2013.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Two other plants are owned jointly by the
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_California_Power_Agency" title="Northern California Power Agency">
       Northern California Power Agency
      </a>
      and the City of
      <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">
       Santa Clara
      </a>
      's municipal Electric Utility (now called
      <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley_Power" title="Silicon Valley Power">
       Silicon Valley Power
      </a>
      ). The Bottle Rock Power plant, owned by the U.S. Renewables Group, was reopened in 2007.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In July 2009,
      <a href="/wiki/AltaRock_Energy" title="AltaRock Energy">
       AltaRock Energy
      </a>
      planned to drill more than 2 miles (3.2 km) down to create an "enhanced geothermal" project which was abandoned when federal agencies asked for review.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAE_2-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-SAE-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another plant was under development by Ram Power Corporation, formerly Western Geopower, in 2010, but after Ram Power lost both its CEO and CFO in 2013, it was seeking a buyer for its Geysers property.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
