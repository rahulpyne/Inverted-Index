   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Flue gas
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      <b>
       Flue gas
      </b>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
       gas
      </a>
      exiting to the atmosphere via a
      <a href="/wiki/Flue" title="Flue">
       flue
      </a>
      , which is a pipe or channel for conveying exhaust gases from a fireplace, oven,
      <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">
       furnace
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">
       boiler
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiler_(steam_generator)" title="Boiler (steam generator)">
       steam generator
      </a>
      . Quite often, the flue gas refers to the
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      exhaust gas produced at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plants" title="Power plants">
       power plants
      </a>
      . Its composition depends on what is being burned, but it will usually consist of mostly
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      (typically more than two-thirds) derived from the combustion of air,
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      (CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ), and
      <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">
       water vapor
      </a>
      as well as excess
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      (also derived from the combustion air). It further contains a small percentage of a number of pollutants, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">
       particulate matter
      </a>
      (like
      <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">
       soot
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">
       nitrogen oxides
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfur_oxides" title="Sulfur oxides">
       sulfur oxides
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scrubbing">
       Scrubbing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flue_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scrubbing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      At power plants, flue gas is often treated with a series of chemical processes and
      <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">
       scrubbers
      </a>
      , which remove pollutants.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic_Precipitator" title="Electrostatic Precipitator">
       Electrostatic precipitators
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Dust_collector#Fabric_filters" title="Dust collector">
       fabric filters
      </a>
      remove particulate matter and
      <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">
       flue-gas desulfurization
      </a>
      captures the
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">
       sulfur dioxide
      </a>
      produced by burning fossil fuels, particularly coal. Nitrogen oxides are treated either by modifications to the combustion process to prevent their formation, or by high temperature or catalytic reaction with
      <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
       ammonia
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">
       urea
      </a>
      . In either case, the aim is to produce nitrogen gas, rather than nitrogen oxides. In the United States, there is a rapid deployment of technologies to remove
      <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">
       mercury
      </a>
      from flue gas—typically by
      <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">
       absorption
      </a>
      on sorbents or by capture in inert solids as part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">
       flue-gas desulfurization
      </a>
      product. Such scrubbing can lead to meaningful recovery of sulfur for further industrial use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Technologies based on regenerative capture by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amines" title="Amines">
       amines
      </a>
      for the removal of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      from flue gas have been deployed to provide high purity CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      gas to the food industry, and for
      <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">
       enhanced oil recovery
      </a>
      . They are now under active research as a method for CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      capture for long-term storage as a means of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_remediation" title="Greenhouse gas remediation">
       greenhouse gas remediation
      </a>
      , and have begun to be implemented in a limited way commercially (e.g. the
      <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_gas_field#Carbon_capture_and_storage_project" title="Sleipner gas field">
       Sleipner West field
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">
       North Sea
      </a>
      , operating since 1996).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are a number of proven technologies for removing pollutants emitted from power plants that are now available. There is also much ongoing research into technologies that will remove even more air pollutants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
