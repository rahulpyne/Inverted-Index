    Green paper
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       European Union
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
       Commonwealth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">
       Hong Kong
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a
      <b>
       green paper
      </b>
      is a tentative government report and consultation document of policy proposals for debate and discussion. A green paper represents the best that the government can propose on the given issue, but, remaining uncommitted, it is able without loss of face to leave its final decision open until it has been able to consider the public reaction to it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      without any commitment to action—the first step in changing the law. Green papers may result in the production of a
      <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">
       white paper
      </a>
      . They may be considered as
      <a href="/wiki/Grey_literature" title="Grey literature">
       grey literature
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">
       Canada
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Canada">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A green paper in
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      , like a
      <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">
       white paper
      </a>
      , is an official government document. Green papers tend to be statements not of policy already determined, but of propositions put before the whole nation for discussion. They are produced early in the policy-making process, while ministerial proposals are still being formulated. Many white papers in Canada have been, in effect, green papers, while at least one green paper - that on immigration and population in 1975 - was released for public debate after the government had already drafted legislation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Similarly, in the UK, green papers are official consultation documents produced by the government for discussion both inside and outside Parliament, for instance when a government department is considering introducing a new law.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">
       European Union
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European Union">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A green paper released by the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">
       European Commission
      </a>
      is a discussion document intended to stimulate debate and launch a process of consultation, at European level, on a particular topic. A green paper usually presents a range of ideas and is meant to invite interested individuals or organizations to contribute views and information. It may be followed by a white paper, an official set of proposals that is used as a vehicle for their development into law.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discussion_of_defence_policy_in_Australia.2C_2000">
       Discussion of defence policy in Australia, 2000
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discussion of defence policy in Australia, 2000">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A major review of defence policy in Australia culminated in a white paper issued in December 2000. Prior to this a discussion paper was released in June 2000. This discussion paper was in nature what is known as a green paper (and was sometimes referred to as such).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_in_the_knowledge_economy.2C_2008">
       Copyright in the knowledge economy, 2008
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Copyright in the knowledge economy, 2008">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The purpose of the EU green paper is to foster a debate on how knowledge for research, science and education can best be disseminated in the online environment. The green paper aims to set out a number of issues connected with the role of copyright in the "
      <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_economy" title="Knowledge economy">
       knowledge economy
      </a>
      " and intends to launch a consultation on these issues (compare to
      <a class="external text" href="http://ec.europa.eu/internal_market/copyright/docs/copyright-infso/greenpaper_en.pdf" rel="nofollow">
       this document
      </a>
      ). The EU asks for answers and comments until 30 November 2008.
     </p>
