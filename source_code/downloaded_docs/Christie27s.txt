     <p>
      <b>
       Christie's,
      </b>
      founded in 1766 by James Christie, is the world's leading art business, with sales in 2015 that totalled £4.8 billion / $7.4 billion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Christie's has its main headquarters in London on King Street and Rockefeller Plaza in New York.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is owned by
      <a href="/wiki/Groupe_Art%C3%A9mis" title="Groupe Artémis">
       Groupe Artémis
      </a>
      , the holding company of
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Henri_Pinault" title="François-Henri Pinault">
       François-Henri Pinault
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">

     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peep-at-Christies-Gillray.jpeg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3696" data-file-width="2640" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Peep-at-Christies-Gillray.jpeg/220px-Peep-at-Christies-Gillray.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Peep-at-Christies-Gillray.jpeg/330px-Peep-at-Christies-Gillray.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Peep-at-Christies-Gillray.jpeg/440px-Peep-at-Christies-Gillray.jpeg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>

        In
        <i>
         A Peep at Christies
        </i>
        (1796),
        <a href="/wiki/James_Gillray" title="James Gillray">
         James Gillray
        </a>
        caricatured actress
        <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Farren" title="Elizabeth Farren">
         Elizabeth Farren
        </a>
        and huntsman
        <a href="/wiki/Edward_Smith-Stanley,_12th_Earl_of_Derby" title="Edward Smith-Stanley, 12th Earl of Derby">
         Lord Derby
        </a>
        examining paintings appropriate to their tastes and heights.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The official company literature states that founder
      <a href="/wiki/James_Christie_(auctioneer)" title="James Christie (auctioneer)">
       James Christie
      </a>
      conducted the first sale in London, England, on 5 December 1766,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the earliest auction catalogue the company retains is from December 1766. However, other sources note that James Christie rented auction rooms from 1762, and newspaper advertisements of Christie's sales dating from 1759 have also been traced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Christie's soon established a reputation as a leading auction house, and took advantage of London's new found status as the major centre of the international art trade after the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
       French Revolution
      </a>
      . From 1859, the company was called Christie, Manson &amp; Woods. In 1958, it established its first overseas office, by placing a representative in Rome. The first overseas salesroom opened in Geneva, where Christie's holds jewellery auctions.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1973.E2.80.931999">
       1973–1999
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christie%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1973–1999">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Microcosm_of_London_Plate_006_-_Auction_Room,_Christie%27s.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1334" data-file-width="1776" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Microcosm_of_London_Plate_006_-_Auction_Room%2C_Christie%27s.jpg/220px-Microcosm_of_London_Plate_006_-_Auction_Room%2C_Christie%27s.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Microcosm_of_London_Plate_006_-_Auction_Room%2C_Christie%27s.jpg/330px-Microcosm_of_London_Plate_006_-_Auction_Room%2C_Christie%27s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Microcosm_of_London_Plate_006_-_Auction_Room%2C_Christie%27s.jpg/440px-Microcosm_of_London_Plate_006_-_Auction_Room%2C_Christie%27s.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Microcosm_of_London_Plate_006_-_Auction_Room,_Christie%27s.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         The Microcosm of London
        </i>
        (1808), an engraving of Christie's auction room
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Christie's was a public company, listed on the
      <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">
       London Stock Exchange
      </a>
      from 1973 to 1999. In 1974, Jo Floyd was appointed chairman of Christie's. He served as chairman of Christie's International P.L.C. from 1976 to 1988, until handing over to
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Carington,_6th_Baron_Carrington" title="Peter Carington, 6th Baron Carrington">
       Lord Carrington
      </a>
      , and later was a non-executive member of the board of directors until 1992.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The auction house's subsidiary Christie's International Inc. held its first sale in the United States in 1977, 13 years later than
      <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby's">
       Sotheby's
      </a>
      . Christie's growth was slow but steady since 1989, when it had 42 percent of the auction market.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1990, the company reversed a longstanding policy and guaranteed a minimum price for a collection of artworks in its May auctions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1996, the auction house's sales eclipsed Sotheby's for the first time since 1954.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, its profits did not grow at the same pace;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carol_Vogel_1998_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Carol_Vogel_1998-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      from 1993 through 1997, Christie's annual pretax profits were about $60 million, whereas Sotheby's annual pretax profits were about $265 million for those years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1993, Christie's paid $10.9 million for the London gallery Spink &amp; Sons, which specialised in Oriental art and British paintings; the gallery was run as a separate entity from the auction house. The company bought Leger Gallery for $3.3 million in 1996, and merged it with Spink to become Spink-Leger.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Spink-Leger was closed in 2002. To make itself competitive with Sotheby's in real estate, Christie's bought Great Estates in 1995, then the largest network of independent real estate brokers in North America, changing its name to Christie's Great Estates Inc.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1998_takeover">
       1998 takeover
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christie%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1998 takeover">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In December 1997, under the chairmanship of
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Allsopp,_6th_Baron_Hindlip" title="Charles Allsopp, 6th Baron Hindlip">
       Lord Hindlip
      </a>
      , Christie's put itself on the auction block, but after two months of negotiations with the consortium-led investment firm
      <a href="/wiki/Warburg_Dillon_Read" title="Warburg Dillon Read">
       SBC Warburg Dillon Read
      </a>
      it did not attract a bid high enough to accept.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In May 1998,
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Pinault" title="François Pinault">
       François Pinault
      </a>
      's holding company, Groupe Artémis S.A., first bought 29.1 percent of the company for $243.2 million, and subsequently purchased the rest of it in a deal that valued the entire company at $1.2 billion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carol_Vogel_1998_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Carol_Vogel_1998-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The company has since not been reporting profits, though it gives sale totals twice a year. Its policy, in line with U.K. accounting standards, is to convert non-U.K. results using an average exchange rate weighted daily by sales throughout the year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg.com_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bloomberg.com-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2002, Christie's France held its first auction in Paris.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Like Sotheby's, Christie's became increasingly involved in high-profile private transactions. In 2006, Christie's offered a reported $21 million guarantee to the
      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Judd" title="Donald Judd">
       Donald Judd Foundation
      </a>
      and displayed the artist's works for five weeks in an exhibition that later won an
      <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Art_Critics" title="International Association of Art Critics">
       AICA award
      </a>
      for "Best Installation in an Alternative Space".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2007 the auction house brokered a $68 million deal that transferred
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Eakins" title="Thomas Eakins">
       Thomas Eakins
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Gross_Clinic" title="The Gross Clinic">
        The Gross Clinic
       </a>
      </i>
      (1875) from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jefferson_Medical_College" title="Jefferson Medical College">
       Jefferson Medical College
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson_University" title="Thomas Jefferson University">
       Thomas Jefferson University
      </a>
      in Philadelphia to joint ownership by the
      <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">
       Philadelphia Museum of Art
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Academy_of_the_Fine_Arts" title="Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts">
       Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      That same year,
      <a href="/wiki/Haunch_of_Venison" title="Haunch of Venison">
       Haunch of Venison
      </a>
      , a contemporary art gallery which since 2002 had successfully conducted back-room sales of secondary-market works by major artists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">
       Francis Bacon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">
       Andy Warhol
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Damien_Hirst" title="Damien Hirst">
       Damien Hirst
      </a>
      from its locations in London and Zürich,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      became a subsidiary of Christie's International plc.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Under the original deal, the gallery was meant to be the channel for all of Christie's private-client business as well as the focus of its primary trade.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also, the auction house originally announced that Haunch employees could not bid at auction because of conflicts of interest or issues of market manipulation, but later abandoned this rule.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      While Christie's eventually retained the brand name and repositioned Haunch as purely a primary-focused gallery, any secondary-market activities were taken over by the auction house's post-war and contemporary department.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Today, the gallery continues to operate as an independent company in London and New York, and again handles all of its secondary market activities itself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On 28 December 2008,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">
        The Sunday Times
       </a>
      </i>
      reported that Pinault's debts left him "considering" the sale of Christie's and that a number of "
      <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">
       private equity
      </a>
      groups" were thought to be interested in its acquisition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In January 2009, Christie's was reported to employ 2,100 people worldwide, though an unspecified number of staff and consultants were soon to be cut due to a worldwide downturn in the art market;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downturn_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-downturn-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      later news reports said that 300 jobs would be cut.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downturn2_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-downturn2-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      With sales for premier Impressionist, Modern, and contemporary artworks tallying only $US248.8 million in comparison to $US739 million just a year before, a second round of job cuts began after May 2009 when the auction house was still reported to employ 1,900 people worldwide.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BloombergSecondCut_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BloombergSecondCut-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      One of the auction house's "rainmakers" in the sale of Impressionist and Modern art, Guy Bennett, resigned from the auction house just prior to the beginning of the summer 2009 sales season.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the economic downturn has encouraged some collectors to sell art, others are unwilling to sell in a market which may yield only bargain prices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downturn2_25-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-downturn2-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Price-fixing_scandal">
       Price-fixing scandal
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christie%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Price-fixing scandal">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2000, allegations surfaced of a price-fixing arrangement between Christie's and
      <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby's">
       Sotheby's
      </a>
      , another major auction house. Executives from Christie's subsequently alerted the Department of Justice of their suspicions of commission-fixing
      <a href="/wiki/Collusion" title="Collusion">
       collusion
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Christie's gained immunity from prosecution in the United States as a longtime employee of Christie's confessed and cooperated with the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">
       Federal Bureau of Investigation
      </a>
      . Numerous members of Sotheby's senior management were fired soon thereafter, and
      <a href="/wiki/A._Alfred_Taubman" title="A. Alfred Taubman">
       A. Alfred Taubman
      </a>
      , the largest shareholder of Sotheby's at the time, took most of the blame; he and Dede Brooks (the CEO) were given jail sentences, and Christie's, Sotheby's and their owners also paid a civil lawsuit settlement of $512 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AR-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CM_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CM-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KW_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KW-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_auctions">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Christie.27s_Fine_Art_Storage_Services_.28CFASS.29">
       Christie's Fine Art Storage Services (CFASS)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christie%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Christie's Fine Art Storage Services (CFASS)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Christie's first ventured into storage services for outside clients in 1984, when it opened a 100,000 square feet brick warehouse in London that was granted "Exempted Status" by
      <a href="/wiki/HM_Revenue_and_Customs" title="HM Revenue and Customs">
       HM Revenue and Customs
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online.wsj.com_61-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-online.wsj.com-61">
        [61]
       </a>
      </sup>
      meaning that property may be imported into the United Kingdom and stored without incurring import duties and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VAT" title="VAT">
       VAT
      </a>
      . Christie's Fine Art Storage Services, or CFASS, is a wholly owned subsidiary that runs Christie's storage operation. In September 2008, Christie's signed a 50-year lease on an early 1900s warehouse of the historic
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_Dock_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="New York Dock Company (page does not exist)">
       New York Dock Company
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-An_exodus_from_Red_Hook_62-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-An_exodus_from_Red_Hook-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Hook,_Brooklyn" title="Red Hook, Brooklyn">
       Red Hook, Brooklyn
      </a>
      , and subsequently spent $30 million converting it into a six-storey, 250,000 square feet
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
      art-storage facility.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online.wsj.com_61-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-online.wsj.com-61">
        [61]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The facility opened in 2010 and features high-tech security and climate controls that maintain a virtually constant 70° and 50% relative humidity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        [64]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since 2009, Christie's has been the main tenant of the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_FreePort&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Singapore FreePort (page does not exist)">
       Singapore FreePort
      </a>
      , taking up 40 per cent of the space to offer its fine art storage services to its global clients.
     </p>
     <p>
      Located near the Upper Bay tidal waterway near the Atlantic Ocean, the Brooklyn facility was hit by at least one storm surge during
      <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">
       Hurricane Sandy
      </a>
      in 2012. CFASS subsequently faced client defections and complaints arising from damage to works of art.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-An_exodus_from_Red_Hook_62-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-An_exodus_from_Red_Hook-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2013,
      <a href="/wiki/AXA" title="AXA">
       AXA Art Insurance
      </a>
      filed a lawsuit in New York court alleging that CFASS' "gross negligence" during the hurricane damaged art collected by late cellist
      <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Piatigorsky" title="Gregor Piatigorsky">
       Gregor Piatigorsky
      </a>
      and his wife
      <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Piatigorsky" title="Jacqueline Piatigorsky">
       Jacqueline Rebecca Louise de Rothschild
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        [65]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later that year, StarNet Insurance Co., the insurer for the
      <a href="/wiki/LeRoy_Neiman" title="LeRoy Neiman">
       LeRoy Neiman Foundation
      </a>
      and the artist's estate, also filed a lawsuit in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Supreme_Court" title="New York Supreme Court">
       New York Supreme Court
      </a>
      claiming that the storage company's negligence caused more than $10 million in damages to Neiman's art.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        [66]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Christie.27s_International_Real_Estate">
       Christie's International Real Estate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christie%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Christie's International Real Estate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Christie's clients who buy and sell works of art often request real estate services. To satisfy this demand, Great Estates, founded in 1987, was acquired by the auction house in 1995.
      <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s_International_Real_Estate" title="Christie's International Real Estate">
       Christie's International Real Estate
      </a>
      is a wholly owned subsidiary of Christie's, and is the leading international network of real estate brokers dedicated to the marketing and sale of
      <a href="/wiki/Luxury_real_estate" title="Luxury real estate">
       luxury properties
      </a>
      . The network spans more than 40 countries worldwide, with 9,500 offices and approximately 27,500 sales associates
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        [67]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Christie's International Real Estate have been involved with some of the world's most high-profile residential property transactions including a New York penthouse on Central Park West for a reported US$88 million
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        [68]
       </a>
      </sup>
      as well as the sale of Copper Beech Farm in Greenwich, Connecticut, for US$120 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        [69]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Christie.27s_Education_graduate_programmes">
       Christie's Education graduate programmes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christie%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Christie's Education graduate programmes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The educational arm of Christie's auction house is called
      <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s_Education" title="Christie's Education">
       Christie's Education
      </a>
      . It offers graduate programs in London, its headquarters, and nondegree programs in London, Paris, New York and Melbourne.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        [70]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has colleges in London and New York accredited by the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">
       University of Glasgow
      </a>
      in the UK and the New York State
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Regents" title="Board of Regents">
       Board of Regents
      </a>
      in the US. It offers master's degrees, Graduate Diplomas, Art Business Certificates and an Undergraduate Degree. Courses include: Arts of China; Arts of Europe; Art, Style and Design; Modern and Contemporary Art (all in London) and History of Art and the Art Market (in New York). Evening programmes in Art Business and part-time certificates in continuing education are also offered in London and New York.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ventures">
       Ventures
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Christie%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ventures">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Christie's Images
      </i>
      is the picture library for the auction house and has an archive of several million fine and decorative art images representing items sold in its sale rooms around the world. With offices in New York and London, images are available for reproduction.
     </p>
     <p>
      With
      <a href="/wiki/Bonhams" title="Bonhams">
       Bonhams
      </a>
      , Christie's is a shareholder in the London-based
      <a href="/wiki/Art_Loss_Register" title="Art Loss Register">
       Art Loss Register
      </a>
      , a privately owned database used by law enforcement services worldwide to trace and recover stolen art.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        [71]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
