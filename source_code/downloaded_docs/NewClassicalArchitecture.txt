     <p>
      <b>
       New Classical architecture
      </b>
      is a contemporary movement in architecture that continues the practice of
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_architecture" title="Classical architecture">
       classical
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture" title="Vernacular architecture">
       traditional architecture
      </a>
      . The design and construction of buildings in these traditions is continuous throughout the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, even as
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture">
       modernist
      </a>
      and other post-classical theories of architecture have been more dominant. Since New Classical architecture is not an
      <a href="/wiki/Architectural_style" title="Architectural style">
       architectural style
      </a>
      and can appear in
      <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_classical_architecture#Classical_architectural_styles" title="Outline of classical architecture">
       various forms
      </a>
      , contemporary classical buildings might be also, although not correctly, be described with the terms
      <b>
       Traditionalism,
      </b>
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Neo-Historism" title="Neo-Historism">
        Neo-Historism
       </a>
      </b>
      (or
      <a href="/wiki/Historicism_(art)" title="Historicism (art)">
       Historicism
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Revivalism_(architecture)" title="Revivalism (architecture)">
       Revivalism
      </a>
      ), or simply
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">
        Neoclassical Architecture
       </a>
       ,
      </b>
      implying the continuation of a specific historical style.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
       Development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </span>

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Richmond_Riverside,_London_-_Sept_2008.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3085" data-file-width="12000" height="57" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Richmond_Riverside%2C_London_-_Sept_2008.jpg/220px-Richmond_Riverside%2C_London_-_Sept_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Richmond_Riverside%2C_London_-_Sept_2008.jpg/330px-Richmond_Riverside%2C_London_-_Sept_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Richmond_Riverside%2C_London_-_Sept_2008.jpg/440px-Richmond_Riverside%2C_London_-_Sept_2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_London#Richmond_Riverside" title="Richmond, London">
         Richmond Riverside

        in
        <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">

        </a>
        (1984–87).

     <p>
      At the beginning of the 20th century,
      <a href="/wiki/Historicism_(art)" title="Historicism (art)">
       historicism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">
       Jugendstil
      </a>
      were still dominant styles in
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Austrian
      </a>
      architect Adolf Loos criticized his time's architecture as too "grandiloquent" and "opulent", and longed for a complete abandonment of architectural
      <a href="/wiki/Ornament_(art)" title="Ornament (art)">
       ornaments
      </a>
      in his 1910 essay
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ornament_and_Crime" title="Ornament and Crime">
        Ornament and Crime
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As early as the first major
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture">
       modernist
      </a>
      movements like
      <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Werkbund" title="Deutscher Werkbund">
       Werkbund
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">
       Bauhaus
      </a>
      gained momentum in
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      , the desire to continue and develop
      <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_classical_architecture" title="Outline of classical architecture">
       classical styles
      </a>
      sprouted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      From 1904 until around 1955 the
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heimatschutzarchitektur" title="de:Heimatschutzarchitektur">
        Heimatschutz style
       </a>
      </i>
      prospered in Germany, which focused on
      <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture" title="Vernacular architecture">
       vernacular
      </a>
      traditions and can be roughly translated to
      <i>
       cultural protection style
      </i>
      . Examples of this early new classical style are the
      <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_Museum" title="Hamburg Museum">
       Hamburg Museum
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Prinzipalmarkt" title="Prinzipalmarkt">
       Prinzipalmarkt
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Münster">
       Münster
      </a>
      and the market square of
      <a href="/wiki/Freudenstadt" title="Freudenstadt">
       Freudenstadt
      </a>
      . After heavy Allied bombing of Germany in
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      , architects such as
      <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Abel" title="de:Adolf Abel">
       Adolf Abel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Roderich_Fick" title="Roderich Fick">
       Roderich Fick
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konstanty_Gutschow" title="de:Konstanty Gutschow">
       Konstanty Gutschow
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Werner_March" title="Werner March">
       Werner March
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Schmitthenner" title="Paul Schmitthenner">
       Paul Schmitthenner
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Julius_Schulte-Frohlinde" title="Julius Schulte-Frohlinde">
       Julius Schulte-Frohlinde
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Wolters" title="Rudolf Wolters">
       Rudolf Wolters
      </a>
      assisted in the postwar rebuilding of destroyed German cities using Heimatschutz and other traditional design methods. In Britain, architect
      <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Erith" title="Raymond Erith">
       Raymond Erith
      </a>
      continued to design classical houses into the late 1960s and early 1970s.
      <a href="/wiki/Quinlan_Terry" title="Quinlan Terry">
       Quinlan Terry
      </a>
      , a New Classical Architect who continues to practice, was an employee, later a partner and now the successor of the late
      <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Erith" title="Raymond Erith">
       Raymond Erith
      </a>
      . In the late 1970s several young architects in Europe began challenging modernist proposals in architecture and planning. To broadcast them,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon_Krier" title="Leon Krier">
       Leon Krier
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Culot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maurice Culot (page does not exist)">
       Maurice Culot
      </a>
      founded the
      <i>
       Archives d'Architecture Moderne
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">
       Brussels
      </a>
      and began publishing texts and counterprojects to modernist proposals in architecture and planning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It received a boost from the sponsorship of
      <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">
       Charles, Prince of Wales
      </a>
      , especially with
      <a href="/wiki/The_Prince%27s_Foundation_for_Building_Community" title="The Prince's Foundation for Building Community">
       The Prince's Foundation for Building Community
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      With the demand for professors knowledgeable in the history of architecture, several PhD programs in schools of architecture arose in order to differentiate themselves from art history PhD programs, where architectural historians had previously trained. In the US,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">
       MIT
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cornell" title="Cornell">
       Cornell
      </a>
      were the first, created in the mid-1970s, followed by
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
       Columbia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">
       Berkeley
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
       Princeton
      </a>
      . Among the founders of new architectural history programs were
      <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Zevi" title="Bruno Zevi">
       Bruno Zevi
      </a>
      at the Institute for the History of Architecture in Venice, Stanford Anderson and Henry Millon at MIT, Alexander Tzonis at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architectural_Association" title="Architectural Association">
       Architectural Association
      </a>
      , Anthony Vidler at Princeton,
      <a href="/wiki/Manfredo_Tafuri" title="Manfredo Tafuri">
       Manfredo Tafuri
      </a>
      at the University of Venice,
      <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Frampton" title="Kenneth Frampton">
       Kenneth Frampton
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
       Columbia University
      </a>
      , and Werner Oechslin and Kurt Forster at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ETH_Z%C3%BCrich" title="ETH Zürich">
       ETH Zürich
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The creation of these programs was paralleled by the hiring, in the 1970s, of professionally trained historians by schools of architecture: Margaret Crawford (with a PhD from U.C.L.A) at
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Institute_of_Architecture" title="Southern California Institute of Architecture">
       SCI-Arc
      </a>
      ; Elisabeth Grossman (PhD, Brown University) at
      <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_School_of_Design" title="Rhode Island School of Design">
       Rhode Island School of Design
      </a>
      ; Christian Otto
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (PhD, Columbia University) at
      <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">
       Cornell University
      </a>
      ; Richard Chafee (PhD, Courtauld Institute) at
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Williams_University" title="Roger Williams University">
       Roger Williams University
      </a>
      ; and Howard Burns (M.A. Kings College) at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">
       Harvard
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In these years
      <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_architecture" title="Postmodern architecture">
       postmodern architecture
      </a>
      developed a critique of modernist architectural aesthetics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McAlester_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-McAlester-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among them were certain influential postmodernist architects such as
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Moore_(architect)" title="Charles Moore (architect)">
       Charles Moore
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Venturi" title="Robert Venturi">
       Robert Venturi
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Graves" title="Michael Graves">
       Michael Graves
      </a>
      , who used classical elements as ironic motifs in order to criticize modernism's sterility. A broad spectrum of more than two dozen architects, theorists, and historians presented other alternatives to modernism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among them were several serious New Classical architects who saw classicism as a legitimate mode of architectural expression, several of whom would later become Driehaus Prize Laureates, including some such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Beeby" title="Thomas Beeby">
       Thomas Beeby
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_A.M._Stern" title="Robert A.M. Stern">
       Robert A.M. Stern
      </a>
      , who practice both in post modern as well as classical modes. Some postmodernist firms, such as Stern and Albert, Righter, &amp; Tittman, fully moved from postmodern design to new interpretations of traditional architecture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McAlester_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-McAlester-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thomas Gordon Smith, the 1979 Rome Prize laureate from the American Academy in Rome, was a devotee of Charles Moore. In 1988 Smith Published "Classical Architecture - Rule and Invention" and in 1989 was appointed to be chair of the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Notre_Dame" title="University of Notre Dame">
       University of Notre Dame
      </a>
      Department of Architecture, which is now the School of Architecture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Today other programs exist which teach in part New Classical Architecture at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">
       University of Miami
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Judson_University" title="Judson University">
       Judson University
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Andrews_University" title="Andrews University">
       Andrews University
      </a>
      and beginning in 2013,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <a class="external text" href="http://cap.ucdenver.edu/carta" rel="nofollow">
       Center for Advanced Research in Traditional Architecture
      </a>
      at the University of Colorado Denver.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alongside these academic and scholarly developments, a populist and professional manifestation of new classicism has existed and continues to develop. The 1963 demolition of McKim, Meade and White's
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Station_(1910%E2%80%931963)" title="Pennsylvania Station (1910–1963)">
       Pennsylvania Railroad Station
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      provoked the formation of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_America" title="Classical America">
       Classical America
      </a>
      and its regional chapters, led by
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hope_Reed,_Jr." title="Henry Hope Reed, Jr.">
       Henry Hope Reed, Jr.
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Classical America advocated the appreciation of classically inspired buildings and for the practice of contemporary classical and traditional design by teaching architects to draw the classical orders, hosting walking tours, educational events, conferences and publishing The Classical America Series in Art and Architecture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2002, the then-named Institute of Classical Architecture merged with Classical America to form the Institute of Classical Architecture &amp; Classical America (now the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_of_Classical_Architecture_%26_Art&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute of Classical Architecture &amp; Art (page does not exist)">
       Institute of Classical Architecture &amp; Art
      </a>
      ). The ICAA currently supports and is supported by regional chapters across the United States, almost all of which host awards programs
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which recognize significant accomplishments in new classical and traditional design and construction. The ICAA publishes
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Classicist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Classicist (page does not exist)">
        The Classicist
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a peer-reviewed journal exclusively dedicated to the theory and practice of contemporary classicism in architecture, urbanism, and the allied arts. The ICAA offers educational programs to architecture and design professionals, many of which follow the methodologies of the
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_nationale_sup%C3%A9rieure_des_Beaux-Arts" title="École nationale supérieure des Beaux-Arts">
       École des Beaux-Arts
      </a>
      . The ICAA also teaches courses to educate the general public,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and has created programs such as the Beaux Arts Atelier, the Advanced Program in Residential Design for the American Institute of Building Designers, and many other unique programs.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2003, Chicago philanthropist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_H._Driehaus" title="Richard H. Driehaus">
       Richard H. Driehaus
      </a>
      established
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a prize in architecture to be given to an architect "whose work embodies the principles of classical and traditional architecture and urbanism in society, and creates a positive, long lasting impact." Awarded by the
      <a href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_School_of_Architecture" title="Notre Dame School of Architecture">
       University of Notre Dame School of Architecture
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Driehaus_Architecture_Prize" title="Driehaus Architecture Prize">
       Driehaus Architecture Prize
      </a>
      is seen as the alternative to the modernist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pritzker_Prize" title="Pritzker Prize">
       Pritzker Prize
      </a>
      . The Driehaus Prize is given in conjunction with the
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hope_Reed_Award" title="Henry Hope Reed Award">
       Reed Award
      </a>
      , for an individual working outside the practice of architecture who has supported the cultivation of the traditional city, its architecture and art through writing, planning or promotion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other high-profiled classical architecture awards are the US-American
      <a href="/wiki/Palladio_Award" title="Palladio Award">
       Palladio Award
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Edmund_N._Bacon_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Edmund N. Bacon Prize (page does not exist)">
       Edmund N. Bacon Prize
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rieger_Graham_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rieger Graham Prize (page does not exist)">
       Rieger Graham Prize
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/The_Institute_of_Classical_Architecture_and_Art" title="The Institute of Classical Architecture and Art">
       Institute of Classical Architecture and Art
      </a>
      (ICAA) for architecture graduates.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">
       Philosophy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Classical_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philosophy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">
       Sustainable architecture
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fundamental tenets of the New Classicism is that it is not limited to
      <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">
       neoclassical architecture
      </a>
      and that "classical" is not a style in itself, but a way of elevating the art of building to the art of architecture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A classical building uses imitation to express its tectonic truth, which is not the same as the facts of its construction, and finds its beauty not in originality and style but in the handling of the traditional forms that have always been its vehicles. Classical buildings also always account for the differences between the public and the private realms in addressing the urban and rural conditions where they are built. New classical architects also emphasize the awareness of
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
       sustainability
      </a>
      , the aim is to create long-lasting, well-crafted buildings of great quality, with an efficient use of natural resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
