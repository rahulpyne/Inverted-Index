     <p>
      An
      <b>
       air separation
      </b>
      plant separates
      <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">
       atmospheric air
      </a>
      into its primary components, typically
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      , and sometimes also
      <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">
       argon
      </a>
      and other rare
      <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">
       inert gases
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The most common method for air separation is
      <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">
       cryogenic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">
       distillation
      </a>
      . Cryogenic air separation units (ASUs) are built to provide nitrogen or oxygen and often co-produce argon. Other methods such as membrane,
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">
       pressure swing adsorption
      </a>
      (PSA) and
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_swing_adsorption" title="Vacuum swing adsorption">
       vacuum pressure swing adsorption
      </a>
      (VPSA), are commercially used to separate a single component from ordinary air. High purity
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">
       argon
      </a>
      used for
      <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_device_fabrication" title="Semiconductor device fabrication">
       semiconductor device fabrication
      </a>
      requires cryogenic distillation. Similarly, the only viable sources of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_gases" title="Rare gases">
       rare gases
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">
       neon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">
       krypton
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">
       xenon
      </a>
      is the distillation of air using at least two
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distillation_column" title="Distillation column">
       distillation columns
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cryogenic_distillation_process">
       Cryogenic distillation process
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cryogenic distillation process">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coldbox.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2616" data-file-width="3488" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Coldbox.JPG/220px-Coldbox.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Coldbox.JPG/330px-Coldbox.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Coldbox.JPG/440px-Coldbox.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coldbox.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Distillation column in a cryogenic air separation plant
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Pure gases can be separated from air by first cooling it until it liquefies, then selectively
      <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">
       distilling
      </a>
      the components at their various boiling temperatures. The process can produce high purity gases but is energy-intensive. This process was pioneered by Dr.
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Linde" title="Carl von Linde">
       Carl von Linde
      </a>
      in the early 20th century and is still used today to produce high purity gases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cool_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cool-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cryogenic separation process
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      requires a very tight integration of heat exchangers and separation columns to obtain a good efficiency and all the energy for refrigeration is provided by the compression of the air at the inlet of the unit.
     </p>
     <p>
      To achieve the low distillation temperatures an air separation unit requires a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigeration_cycle" title="Refrigeration cycle">
       refrigeration cycle
      </a>
      that operates by means of the
      <a href="/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_effect" title="Joule–Thomson effect">
       Joule–Thomson effect
      </a>
      , and the cold equipment has to be kept within an
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">
       insulated
      </a>
      enclosure (commonly called a "cold box"). The cooling of the gases requires a large amount of energy to make this refrigeration cycle work and is delivered by an air
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">
       compressor
      </a>
      . Modern ASUs use
      <a href="/wiki/Turboexpander" title="Turboexpander">
       expansion turbines
      </a>
      for cooling; the output of the expander helps drive the air compressor, for improved efficiency. The process consists of the following main steps:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Before compression the air is pre-filtered of dust.
      </li>
      <li>
       Air is compressed where the final delivery pressure is determined by recoveries and the
       <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">
        fluid
       </a>
       state (gas or liquid) of the products. Typical pressures range between 5 and 10 bar gauge. The air stream may also be compressed to different pressures to enhance the efficiency of the ASU. During compression water is condensed out in inter-stage coolers.
      </li>
      <li>
       The process air is generally passed through a
       <a href="/wiki/Molecular_sieve" title="Molecular sieve">
        molecular sieve
       </a>
       bed, which removes any remaining water vapour, as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
        carbon dioxide
       </a>
       , which would freeze and plug the cryogenic equipment. Molecular sieves are often designed to remove any gaseous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">
        hydrocarbons
       </a>
       from the air, since these can be a problem in the subsequent air distillation that could lead to explosions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The molecular sieves bed must be regenerated. This is done by installing multiple units operating in alternating mode and using the dry co-produced waste gas to desorb the water.
      </li>
      <li>
       Process air is passed through an integrated
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">
        heat exchanger
       </a>
       (usually a
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_fin_heat_exchanger" title="Plate fin heat exchanger">
        plate fin heat exchanger
       </a>
       ) and cooled against product (and waste) cryogenic streams. Part of the air liquefies to form a liquid that is enriched in oxygen. The remaining gas is richer in nitrogen and is distilled to almost pure nitrogen (typically &lt; 1ppm) in a high pressure (HP) distillation column. The condenser of this column requires
       <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">
        refrigeration
       </a>
       which is obtained from expanding the more oxygen rich stream further across a valve or through an
       <a href="/wiki/Turboexpander" title="Turboexpander">
        Expander
       </a>
       , (a reverse compressor).
      </li>
      <li>
       Alternatively the condenser may be cooled by interchanging heat with a reboiler in a low pressure (LP) distillation column (operating at 1.2-1.3 bar abs.) when the ASU is producing pure oxygen. To minimize the compression cost the combined condenser/reboiler of the HP/LP columns must operate with a temperature difference of only 1-2 K, requiring plate fin brazed aluminium heat exchangers. Typical oxygen purities range in from 97.5% to 99.5% and influences the maximum recovery of oxygen. The refrigeration required for producing liquid products is obtained using the
       <a href="/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_effect" title="Joule–Thomson effect">
        Joule–Thomson effect
       </a>
       in an expander which feeds compressed air directly to the low pressure column. Hence, a certain part of the air is not to be separated and must leave the low pressure column as a waste stream from its upper section.
      </li>
      <li>
       Because the boiling point of argon (87.3 K at standard conditions) lies between that of oxygen (90.2 K) and nitrogen (77.4 K), argon builds up in the lower section of the low pressure column. When argon is produced, a vapor side draw is taken from the low pressure column where the argon concentration is highest. It is sent to another column rectifying the argon to the desired purity from which liquid is returned to the same location in the LP column. Use of modern structured packings which have very low pressure drops enable argon with less than 1 ppm impurities. Though argon is present in less to 1% of the incoming, the air argon column requires a significant amount of energy due to the high reflux ratio required (about 30) in the argon column. Cooling of the argon column can be supplied from cold expanded rich liquid or by liquid nitrogen.
      </li>
      <li>
       Finally the products produced in gas form are warmed against the incoming air to ambient temperatures. This requires a carefully crafted heat integration that must allow for robustness against disturbances (due to switch over of the molecular sieve beds
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ). It may also require additional external refrigeration during start-up.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      The separated products are sometimes supplied by pipeline to large industrial users near the production plant. Long distance transportation of products is by shipping liquid product for large quantities or as
      <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_storage_dewar" title="Cryogenic storage dewar">
       dewar flasks
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">
       gas cylinders
      </a>
      for small quantities.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-cryogenic_processes">
       Non-cryogenic processes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Non-cryogenic processes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">
       Pressure swing adsorption
      </a>
      provides separation of oxygen or nitrogen from air without liquefication. The process operates around ambient temperature; a
      <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">
       zeolite
      </a>
      (molecular sponge) is exposed to high pressure air, then the air is released and an adsorbed film of the desired gas is released. The size of compressor is much reduced over a liquification plant, and
      <a href="/wiki/Portable_oxygen_concentrator" title="Portable oxygen concentrator">
       portable oxygen concentrators
      </a>
      are made in this manner to provide oxygen-enriched air for medical purposes.
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_swing_adsorption" title="Vacuum swing adsorption">
       Vacuum swing adsorption
      </a>
      is a similar process, but the product gas is evolved from the zeolite at sub-atmospheric pressure.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Membrane_gas_separation" title="Membrane gas separation">
       Membrane technologies
      </a>
      can provide alternate, lower-energy approaches to air separation. For example, a number of approaches are being explored for oxygen generation. Polymeric membranes operating at ambient or warm temperatures, for example, may be able to produce oxygen-enriched air (25-50% oxygen). Ceramic membranes can provide high-purity oxygen (90% or more) but require higher temperatures (800-900 deg C) to operate. These ceramic membranes include Ion Transport Membranes (ITM) and Oxygen Transport Membranes (OTM). Air Products and Chemicals Inc and Praxair are developing flat ITM and tubular OTM systems, respectively.
     </p>
     <p>
      Membrane gas separation is used to provide oxygen poor and nitrogen rich gases instead of air to fill the fuel tanks of jet liners, thus greatly reducing the chances of accidental fires. Conversely, membrane gas separation is currently used to provide oxygen enriched air to pilots flying at great altitudes in aircraft without pressurized cabins.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
       Applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Large amounts of oxygen are required for
      <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">
       coal gasification
      </a>
      projects; cryogenic plants producing 3000 tons/day are found in some projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">
       steelmaking
      </a>
      oxygen is required for the
      <a href="/wiki/Basic_oxygen_steelmaking" title="Basic oxygen steelmaking">
       basic oxygen steelmaking
      </a>
      . Large amounts of nitrogen with low oxygen impurities are used for inerting storage tanks of ships and tanks for petroleum products, or for protecting edible oil products from oxidation.
     </p>
