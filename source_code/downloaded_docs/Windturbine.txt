     <p>
      A
      <b>
       wind turbine
      </b>
      is a device that
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       converts
      </a>
      the wind's
      <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
       kinetic energy
      </a>
      into
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_power" title="Electrical power">
       electrical power
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Wind turbines are manufactured in a wide range of vertical and horizontal axis types. The smallest turbines are used for applications such as battery charging for auxiliary power for boats or
      <a href="/wiki/Travel_trailer" title="Travel trailer">
       caravans
      </a>
      or to power traffic warning signs. Slightly larger turbines can be used for making contributions to a domestic power supply while selling unused power back to the utility supplier via the
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">
       electrical grid
      </a>
      . Arrays of large turbines, known as
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">
       wind farms
      </a>
      , are becoming an increasingly important source of intermittent
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      and are used by many countries as part of a strategy to reduce their reliance on
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuels
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_wind_power" title="History of wind power">
       History of wind power
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:James_Blyth%27s_1891_windmill.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="727" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/James_Blyth%27s_1891_windmill.jpg/220px-James_Blyth%27s_1891_windmill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/James_Blyth%27s_1891_windmill.jpg/330px-James_Blyth%27s_1891_windmill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/James_Blyth%27s_1891_windmill.jpg/440px-James_Blyth%27s_1891_windmill.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:James_Blyth%27s_1891_windmill.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        James Blyth's electricity-generating wind turbine, photographed in 1891
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Windmills were used in Persia (present-day Iran) about 500-900 A.D.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The windwheel of
      <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">
       Hero of Alexandria
      </a>
      marks one of the first known instances of wind powering a machine in history.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drachmann_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Drachmann-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lohrmann_10f._3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lohrmann_10f.-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the first known practical windmills were built in
      <a href="/wiki/Sistan" title="Sistan">
       Sistan
      </a>
      , an Eastern province of Iran, from the 7th century. These "
      <a href="/wiki/Panemone_windmill" title="Panemone windmill">
       Panemone
      </a>
      " were vertical axle windmills, which had long vertical
      <a href="/wiki/Drive_shaft" title="Drive shaft">
       drive shafts
      </a>
      with rectangular blades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Made of six to twelve
      <a href="/wiki/Windmill_sail" title="Windmill sail">
       sails
      </a>
      covered in reed matting or cloth material, these windmills were used to grind grain or draw up water, and were used in the
      <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">
       gristmilling
      </a>
      and sugarcane industries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Windmills first appeared in Europe during the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </a>
      . The first historical records of their use in England date to the 11th or 12th centuries and there are reports of German
      <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">
       crusaders
      </a>
      taking their windmill-making skills to Syria around 1190.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morthorst_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Morthorst-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      By the 14th century, Dutch windmills were in use to drain areas of the
      <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">
       Rhine
      </a>
      delta. Advanced wind mills were described by Croatian inventor
      <a href="/wiki/Fausto_Veranzio" title="Fausto Veranzio">
       Fausto Veranzio
      </a>
      . In his book Machinae Novae (1595) he described
      <a href="/wiki/Vertical_axis_wind_turbine" title="Vertical axis wind turbine">
       vertical axis wind turbines
      </a>
      with curved or V-shaped blades.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first electricity-generating wind turbine was a battery charging machine installed in July 1887 by Scottish academic
      <a href="/wiki/James_Blyth_(engineer)" title="James Blyth (engineer)">
       James Blyth
      </a>
      to light his holiday home in
      <a href="/wiki/Marykirk" title="Marykirk">
       Marykirk
      </a>
      , Scotland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODNB-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some months later American inventor
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Brush" title="Charles F. Brush">
       Charles F. Brush
      </a>
      was able to build the first automatically operated wind turbine after consulting local University professors and colleagues
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jacob_S._Gibbs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jacob S. Gibbs (page does not exist)">
       Jacob S. Gibbs
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brinsley_Coleberd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brinsley Coleberd (page does not exist)">
       Brinsley Coleberd
      </a>
      and successfully getting the blueprints peer-reviewed for electricity production in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">
       Cleveland, Ohio
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODNB-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although Blyth's turbine was considered uneconomical in the United Kingdom
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODNB-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      electricity generation by wind turbines was more cost effective in countries with widely scattered populations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morthorst_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Morthorst-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wind_turbine_1888_Charles_Brush.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="739" data-file-width="714" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Wind_turbine_1888_Charles_Brush.jpg/220px-Wind_turbine_1888_Charles_Brush.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Wind_turbine_1888_Charles_Brush.jpg/330px-Wind_turbine_1888_Charles_Brush.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Wind_turbine_1888_Charles_Brush.jpg/440px-Wind_turbine_1888_Charles_Brush.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wind_turbine_1888_Charles_Brush.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The first automatically operated wind turbine, built in Cleveland in 1887 by Charles F. Brush. It was 60 feet (18 m) tall, weighed 4 tons (3.6 metric tonnes) and powered a 12 kW generator.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In Denmark by 1900, there were about 2500 windmills for mechanical loads such as pumps and mills, producing an estimated combined peak power of about 30 MW. The largest machines were on 24-meter (79 ft) towers with four-bladed 23-meter (75 ft) diameter rotors. By 1908 there were 72 wind-driven electric generators operating in the United States from 5 kW to 25 kW. Around the time of World War I, American windmill makers were producing 100,000 farm windmills each year, mostly for water-pumping.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aermotor_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Aermotor-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By the 1930s, wind generators for electricity were common on farms, mostly in the United States where distribution systems had not yet been installed. In this period, high-tensile steel was cheap, and the generators were placed atop prefabricated open steel lattice towers.
     </p>
     <p>
      A forerunner of modern horizontal-axis wind generators was in service at
      <a href="/wiki/Yalta" title="Yalta">
       Yalta
      </a>
      , USSR in 1931. This was a 100 kW generator on a 30-meter (98 ft) tower, connected to the local 6.3 kV distribution system. It was reported to have an annual
      <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">
       capacity factor
      </a>
      of 32 percent, not much different from current wind machines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the autumn of 1941, the first megawatt-class wind turbine was synchronized to a utility grid in
      <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">
       Vermont
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Smith-Putnam_wind_turbine" title="Smith-Putnam wind turbine">
       Smith-Putnam wind turbine
      </a>
      only ran for 1,100 hours before suffering a critical failure. The unit was not repaired, because of shortage of materials during the war.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first utility grid-connected wind turbine to operate in the UK was built by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Brown_%26_Company" title="John Brown &amp; Company">
       John Brown &amp; Company
      </a>
      in 1951 in the
      <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">
       Orkney Islands
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODNB-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite these diverse developments, developments in fossil fuel systems almost entirely eliminated any wind turbine systems larger than supermicro size. In the early 1970s, however, anti-nuclear protests in Denmark spurred artisan mechanics to develop microturbines of 22 kW. Organizing owners into associations and co-operatives lead to the lobbying of the government and utilities and provided incentives for larger turbines throughout the 1980s and later. Local activists in Germany, nascent turbine manufacturers in Spain, and large investors in the United States in the early 1990s then lobbied for policies that stimulated the industry in those countries. Later companies formed in India and China. As of 2012, Danish company
      <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">
       Vestas
      </a>
      is the world's biggest wind-turbine manufacturer.
     </p>

     <p>
      A quantitative measure of the wind energy available at any location is called the
      <a href="/wiki/Wind#Wind_power_density" title="Wind">
       Wind Power Density
      </a>
      (WPD). It is a calculation of the mean annual power available per square meter of swept area of a turbine, and is tabulated for different heights above ground. Calculation of
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_profile_power_law#Wind_power_density" title="Wind profile power law">
       wind power density
      </a>
      includes the effect of wind velocity and air density. Color-coded maps are prepared for a particular area described, for example, as "Mean Annual Power Density at 50 Metres". In the United States, the results of the above calculation are included in an index developed by the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">
       National Renewable Energy Laboratory
      </a>
      and referred to as "NREL CLASS". The larger the WPD, the higher it is rated by class. Classes range from Class 1 (200 watts per square meter or less at 50 m altitude) to Class 7 (800 to 2000 watts per square m). Commercial wind farms generally are sited in Class 3 or higher areas, although isolated points in an otherwise Class 1 area may be practical to exploit.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency">
       Efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Not all the energy of blowing wind can be used, but some
      <a href="/wiki/Small_wind_turbine" title="Small wind turbine">
       small wind turbines
      </a>
      are designed to work at low wind speeds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_mass" title="Conservation of mass">
       Conservation of mass
      </a>
      requires that the amount of air entering and exiting a turbine must be equal. Accordingly,
      <a href="/wiki/Betz%27s_law" title="Betz's law">
       Betz's law
      </a>
      gives the maximal achievable extraction of wind power by a wind turbine as 16/27 (59.3%) of the total kinetic energy of the air flowing through the turbine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The maximum theoretical power output of a wind machine is thus 0.59 times the kinetic energy of the air passing through the effective disk area of the machine. If the effective area of the disk is A, and the wind velocity v, the maximum theoretical power output P is:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <span>
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
          <semantics>
           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
             <mi>
              P
             </mi>
             <mo>
              =
             </mo>
             <mn>
              0.59
             </mn>
             <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
              <mfrac>
               <mn>
                1
               </mn>
               <mn>
                2
               </mn>
              </mfrac>
             </mrow>
             <mi>
              ρ
              <!-- ρ -->
             </mi>
             <msup>
              <mi>
               v
              </mi>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mn>
                3
               </mn>
              </mrow>
             </msup>
             <mi>
              A
             </mi>
            </mstyle>
           </mrow>
           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
            {\displaystyle P=0.59{\frac {1}{2}}\rho v^{3}A}
           </annotation>
          </semantics>
         </math>
        </span>
        <img alt="{\displaystyle P=0.59{\frac {1}{2}}\rho v^{3}A}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c86b8e927c93b07631ba138829129581325e827d" style="vertical-align: -1.838ex; width:16.215ex; height:5.176ex;"/>
       </span>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where
      <i>
       ρ
      </i>
      is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_density" title="Air density">
       air density
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      As wind is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gratis" title="Gratis">
       free
      </a>
      (no fuel cost), wind-to-rotor efficiency (including rotor blade
      <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">
       friction
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">
       drag
      </a>
      ) is one of many aspects impacting the final
      <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">
       price
      </a>
      of wind power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Further inefficiencies, such as gearbox losses, generator and converter losses, reduce the power delivered by a wind turbine. To protect components from undue wear, extracted power is held constant above the rated operating speed as theoretical power
      <a href="/wiki/Cube_(algebra)" title="Cube (algebra)">
       increases at the cube
      </a>
      of wind speed, further reducing theoretical efficiency. In 2001, commercial utility-connected turbines deliver 75% to 80% of the Betz limit of power extractable from the wind, at rated operating speed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enercon_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Enercon-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (April 2016)">
          needs update
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Efficiency can decrease slightly over time due to wear. Analysis of 3128 wind turbines older than 10 years in Denmark showed that half of the turbines had no decrease, while the other half saw a production decrease of 1.2% per year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Vertical turbine designs have much lower efficiency than standard horizontal designs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
       Types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types">

       </a>

       </span>

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HAWT_and_VAWTs_in_operation_medium.gif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="338" data-file-width="631" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/HAWT_and_VAWTs_in_operation_medium.gif/220px-HAWT_and_VAWTs_in_operation_medium.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/HAWT_and_VAWTs_in_operation_medium.gif/330px-HAWT_and_VAWTs_in_operation_medium.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/HAWT_and_VAWTs_in_operation_medium.gif/440px-HAWT_and_VAWTs_in_operation_medium.gif 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

     <p>
      Wind turbines can rotate about either a horizontal or a vertical axis, the former being both older and more common.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      They can also include blades (transparent or not)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      or be bladeless.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Vertical designs produce less power and are less common.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Horizontal_axis">
       Horizontal axis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">

        <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine_design#Blades" title="Wind turbine design">

        , UK
       </div>

     <p>
      Horizontal-axis wind turbines (HAWT) have the main
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotor_(turbine)" title="Rotor (turbine)">
       rotor
      </a>
      shaft and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">
       electrical generator
      </a>
      at the top of a tower, and must be pointed into the wind. Small turbines are pointed by a simple
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_vane" title="Wind vane">
       wind vane
      </a>
      , while large turbines generally use a wind sensor coupled with a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Servo_motor" title="Servo motor">
       servo motor
      </a>
      . Most have a gearbox, which turns the slow rotation of the blades into a quicker rotation that is more suitable to drive an electrical generator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since a tower produces
      <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
       turbulence
      </a>
      behind it, the turbine is usually positioned upwind of its supporting tower. Turbine blades are made stiff to prevent the blades from being pushed into the tower by high winds. Additionally, the blades are placed a considerable distance in front of the tower and are sometimes tilted forward into the wind a small amount.
     </p>
     <p>
      Downwind machines have been built, despite the problem of turbulence (mast wake), because they don't need an additional mechanism for keeping them in line with the wind, and because in high winds the blades can be allowed to bend which reduces their swept area and thus their wind resistance. Since cyclical (that is repetitive) turbulence may lead to
      <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">
       fatigue
      </a>
      failures, most HAWTs are of upwind design.
     </p>
     <p>
      Turbines used in
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">
       wind farms
      </a>
      for commercial production of electric power are usually three-bladed and pointed into the wind by computer-controlled motors. These have high tip speeds of over 320 km/h (200 mph), high efficiency, and low
      <a href="/wiki/Torque_ripple" title="Torque ripple">
       torque ripple
      </a>
      , which contribute to good reliability. The blades are usually colored white for daytime visibility by aircraft and range in length from 20 to 40 meters (66 to 131 ft) or more. The tubular steel towers range from 60 to 90 meters (200 to 300 ft) tall.
     </p>
     <p>
      The blades rotate at 10 to 22 revolutions per minute. At 22 rotations per minute the tip speed exceeds 90 meters per second (300 ft/s).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A gear box is commonly used for stepping up the speed of the generator, although designs may also use direct drive of an annular generator. Some models operate at constant speed, but more energy can be collected by variable-speed turbines which use a solid-state power converter to interface to the transmission system. All turbines are equipped with protective features to avoid damage at high wind speeds, by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feathering_(propeller)" title="Feathering (propeller)">
       feathering
      </a>
      the blades into the wind which ceases their rotation, supplemented by
      <a href="/wiki/Brake" title="Brake">
       brakes
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Year by year the size and height of turbines increase. Offshore wind turbines are built up to 8MW today and have a blade length up to 80m. Onshore wind turbines are installed in low wind speed areas and getting higher and higher towers. Usual towers of multi megawatt turbines have a height of 70
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      m to 120
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      m and in extremes up to 160
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      m, with blade tip speeds reaching 80 m/s to 90 m/s. Higher tip speeds means more noise and blade erosion.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_axis">
       Vertical axis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vertical axis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:170px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Twisted_Savonius_wind_turbine_in_operation@60rpm.gif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="262" data-file-width="168" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/37/Twisted_Savonius_wind_turbine_in_operation%4060rpm.gif" width="168"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Twisted_Savonius_wind_turbine_in_operation@60rpm.gif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A vertical axis Twisted Savonius type turbine.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical-axis_wind_turbine" title="Vertical-axis wind turbine">
       Vertical-axis wind turbines
      </a>
      (or VAWTs) have the main rotor shaft arranged vertically. One advantage of this arrangement is that the turbine does not need to be pointed into the wind to be effective, which is an advantage on a site where the wind direction is highly variable. It is also an advantage when the turbine is integrated into a building because it is inherently less steerable. Also, the generator and gearbox can be placed near the ground, using a direct drive from the rotor assembly to the ground-based gearbox, improving accessibility for maintenance. However, these designs produce much less energy averaged over time, which is a major drawback.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The key disadvantages include the relatively low rotational speed with the consequential higher
      <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
       torque
      </a>
      and hence higher cost of the drive train, the inherently lower
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_turbine_aerodynamics" title="Wind turbine aerodynamics">
       power coefficient
      </a>
      , the 360-degree rotation of the aerofoil within the wind flow during each cycle and hence the highly dynamic loading on the blade, the pulsating torque generated by some rotor designs on the drive train, and the difficulty of modelling the wind flow accurately and hence the challenges of analysing and designing the rotor prior to fabricating a prototype.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When a turbine is mounted on a rooftop the building generally redirects wind over the roof and this can double the wind speed at the turbine. If the height of a rooftop mounted turbine tower is approximately 50% of the building height it is near the optimum for maximum wind energy and minimum wind turbulence. Wind speeds within the built environment are generally much lower than at exposed rural sites,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      noise may be a concern and an existing structure may not adequately resist the additional stress.
     </p>

      Subtypes of the vertical axis design include:

     <dl>
      <dt>
       <a href="/wiki/Darrieus_wind_turbine" title="Darrieus wind turbine">
        Darrieus wind turbine
       </a>
      </dt>
      <dd>
       "Eggbeater" turbines, or Darrieus turbines, were named after the French inventor, Georges Darrieus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They have good efficiency, but produce large
       <a href="/wiki/Torque_ripple" title="Torque ripple">
        torque ripple
       </a>
       and cyclical stress on the tower, which contributes to poor reliability. They also generally require some external power source, or an additional Savonius rotor to start turning, because the starting torque is very low. The torque ripple is reduced by using three or more blades which results in greater solidity of the rotor. Solidity is measured by blade area divided by the rotor area. Newer Darrieus type turbines are not held up by
       <a href="/wiki/Guy-wire" title="Guy-wire">
        guy-wires
       </a>
       but have an external superstructure connected to the top bearing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giromill" title="Giromill">
        Giromill
       </a>
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A subtype of Darrieus turbine with straight, as opposed to curved, blades. The cycloturbine variety has variable pitch to reduce the torque pulsation and is self-starting.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The advantages of variable pitch are: high starting torque; a wide, relatively flat torque curve; a higher coefficient of performance; more efficient operation in turbulent winds; and a lower blade speed ratio which lowers blade bending stresses. Straight, V, or curved blades may be used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       <a href="/wiki/Savonius_wind_turbine" title="Savonius wind turbine">
        Savonius wind turbine
       </a>
      </dt>
      <dd>
       These are drag-type devices with two (or more) scoops that are used in anemometers,
       <i>
        Flettner
       </i>
       vents (commonly seen on bus and van roofs), and in some high-reliability low-efficiency power turbines. They are always self-starting if there are at least three scoops.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Twisted Savonius
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Twisted Savonius is a modified savonius, with long helical scoops to provide smooth torque. This is often used as a rooftop windturbine and has even been
       <a href="/wiki/Hornblower_Hybrid" title="Hornblower Hybrid">
        adapted for ships
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Another type of vertical axis is the Parallel turbine, which is similar to the crossflow fan or centrifugal fan. It uses the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_effect_(aircraft)" title="Ground effect (aircraft)">
       ground effect
      </a>
      . Vertical axis turbines of this type have been tried for many years: a unit producing 10 kW was built by Israeli wind pioneer Bruce Brill in the 1980s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>

        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Wind turbines are designed to exploit the wind energy that exists at a location.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_turbine_aerodynamics" title="Wind turbine aerodynamics">
       Aerodynamic modeling
      </a>
      is used to determine the optimum tower height, control systems, number of blades and blade shape.
     </p>
     <p>
      Wind turbines convert wind energy to electricity for distribution. Conventional horizontal axis turbines can be divided into three components:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The rotor component, which is approximately 20% of the wind turbine cost, includes the blades for converting wind energy to low speed rotational energy.
      </li>
      <li>
       The generator component, which is approximately 34% of the wind turbine cost, includes the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">
        electrical generator
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the control electronics, and most likely a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gearbox" title="Gearbox">
        gearbox
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Epicyclic_gearing" title="Epicyclic gearing">
        planetary gearbox
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Adjustable-speed_drive" title="Adjustable-speed drive">
        adjustable-speed drive
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Continuously_variable_transmission" title="Continuously variable transmission">
        continuously variable transmission
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       component for converting the low speed incoming rotation to high speed rotation suitable for generating electricity.
      </li>
      <li>
       The structural support component, which is approximately 15% of the wind turbine cost, includes the tower and rotor yaw mechanism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A 1.5 MW wind turbine of a type frequently seen in the United States has a tower 80 meters (260 ft) high. The rotor assembly (blades and hub) weighs 22,000 kilograms (48,000 lb). The nacelle, which contains the generator component, weighs 52,000 kilograms (115,000 lb). The concrete base for the tower is constructed using 26,000 kilograms (58,000 lb) of reinforcing steel and contains 190 cubic meters (250 cu yd) of concrete. The base is 15 meters (50 ft) in diameter and 2.4 meters (8 ft) thick near the center.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        [46]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Among all renewable energy systems wind turbines have the highest effective
      <a href="/wiki/Intensity_(physics)" title="Intensity (physics)">
       intensity
      </a>
      of power-harvesting surface
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        [47]
       </a>
      </sup>
      because turbine blades not only harvest wind power, but also concentrate it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>

      to extract the energy due to air heated by the sun.
     </p>
     <p>
      Wind turbines which utilise the
      <a href="/wiki/Magnus_effect" title="Magnus effect">
       Magnus effect
      </a>
      have been developed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Ram_air_turbine" title="Ram air turbine">
       ram air turbine
      </a>
      (RAT) is a special kind of small turbine that is fitted to some aircraft. When deployed, the RAT is spun by the airstream going past the aircraft and can provide power for the most essential systems if there is a loss of all on-board electrical power,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
      as in the case of the "
      <a href="/wiki/Gimli_Glider" title="Gimli Glider">
       Gimli Glider
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      The two-bladed turbine SCD 6MW offshore turbine designed by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodyn_Energiesysteme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aerodyn Energiesysteme (page does not exist)">
       aerodyn Energiesysteme
      </a>
      , built by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MingYang_Wind_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MingYang Wind Power (page does not exist)">
       MingYang Wind Power
      </a>
      has a helideck for helicopters on top of its nacelle. The prototype was erected in 2014 in Rudong China.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Turbine_monitoring_and_diagnostics">
       Turbine monitoring and diagnostics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Turbine monitoring and diagnostics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine_prognostics" title="Wind turbine prognostics">
       Wind turbine prognostics
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Due to data transmission problems,
      <a href="/wiki/Structural_health_monitoring" title="Structural health monitoring">
       structural health monitoring
      </a>
      of wind turbines is usually performed using several accelerometers and strain gages attached to the nacelle to monitor the gearbox and equipments. Currently, digital image correlation and stereophotogrammetry are used to measure dynamics of wind turbine blades. These methods usually measure displacement and strain to identify location of defects. Dynamic characteristics of non-rotating wind turbines have been measured using digital image correlation and photogrammetry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Three dimensional point tracking has also been used to measure rotating dynamics of wind turbines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        [52]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Materials_and_durability">
       Materials and durability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Materials and durability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Currently serving wind turbine blades are mainly made of composite materials. These blades are usually made of a polyester resin, a vinyl resin, and epoxy thermosetting matrix resin and E-glass fibers, S- glass fibers and carbon fiber reinforced materials. Construction may use manual layup techniques or composite resin injection molding. As the price of glass fibers is only about one tenth the price of carbon fiber,
      <a href="/wiki/Glass_fiber" title="Glass fiber">
       glass fiber
      </a>
      is still dominant. One of the predominant ways wind turbines have gain performance is by increasing rotor diameters, and thus blade length. Longer blades place more demands on the strength and stiffness of the materials. Stiffness is especially important to avoid having blades flex to the degree that they hit the tower of the wind turbine. Carbon fiber is between 4 and 6 times stiffer than glass fiber,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        [53]
       </a>
      </sup>
      so carbon fiber is becoming more common in wind turbine blades.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        [54]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Small_wind_turbines">
       Small wind turbines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Small wind turbines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Small_wind_turbine" title="Small wind turbine">
       Small wind turbine
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quietrevolution_Bristol_3513051949.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="581" data-file-width="451" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Quietrevolution_Bristol_3513051949.jpg/170px-Quietrevolution_Bristol_3513051949.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Quietrevolution_Bristol_3513051949.jpg/255px-Quietrevolution_Bristol_3513051949.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Quietrevolution_Bristol_3513051949.jpg/340px-Quietrevolution_Bristol_3513051949.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quietrevolution_Bristol_3513051949.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A small
        <a href="/wiki/Quietrevolution_wind_turbine" title="Quietrevolution wind turbine">
         Quietrevolution QR5
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Gorlov_helical_turbine" title="Gorlov helical turbine">
         Gorlov type
        </a>
        vertical axis wind turbine in Bristol, England. Measuring 3 m in diameter and 5 m high, it has a nameplate rating of 6.5 kW to the grid.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Small wind turbines may be used for a variety of applications including on- or off-grid residences, telecom towers, offshore platforms, rural schools and clinics, remote monitoring and other purposes that require energy where there is no electric grid, or where the grid is unstable. Small wind turbines may be as small as a fifty-watt generator for boat or
      <a href="/wiki/Travel_trailer" title="Travel trailer">
       caravan
      </a>
      use. Hybrid solar and wind powered units are increasingly being used for traffic signage, particularly in rural locations, as they avoid the need to lay long cables from the nearest mains connection point.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        [56]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">
       U.S. Department of Energy's National Renewable Energy Laboratory
      </a>
      (NREL) defines small wind turbines as those smaller than or equal to 100 kilowatts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        [57]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Small units often have direct drive generators,
      <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">
       direct current
      </a>
      output, aeroelastic blades, lifetime bearings and use a vane to point into the wind.
     </p>
     <p>
      Larger, more costly turbines generally have geared power trains, alternating current output, flaps and are actively pointed into the wind. Direct drive generators and aeroelastic blades for large wind turbines are being researched.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_turbine_spacing">
       Wind turbine spacing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Wind turbine spacing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On most horizontal windturbine farms, a spacing of about 6-10 times the rotor diameter is often upheld. However, for large wind farms distances of about 15 rotor diameters should be more economically optimal, taking into account typical wind turbine and land costs. This conclusion has been reached by research
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        [58]
       </a>
      </sup>
      conducted by Charles Meneveau of the Johns Hopkins University,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        [59]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Johan Meyers of Leuven University in Belgium, based on computer simulations
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        [60]
       </a>
      </sup>
      that take into account the detailed interactions among wind turbines (wakes) as well as with the entire turbulent atmospheric boundary layer. Moreover, recent research by John Dabiri of Caltech suggests that vertical wind turbines may be placed much more closely together so long as an alternating pattern of rotation is created allowing blades of neighbouring turbines to move in the same direction as they approach one another.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        [61]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Operability">
       Operability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Operability">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">
       Maintenance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Maintenance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Wind turbines need regular
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maintenance,_repair_and_operations" title="Maintenance, repair and operations">
       maintenance
      </a>
      to stay
      <a href="/wiki/Reliability_engineering" title="Reliability engineering">
       reliable
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Availability_factor" title="Availability factor">
       available
      </a>
      , reaching 98%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Modern turbines usually have a small onboard
      <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">
       crane
      </a>
      for hoisting maintenance tools and minor components. However, large heavy components like generator, gearbox, blades and so on are rarely replaced and a
      <a href="/wiki/Mobile_crane" title="Mobile crane">
       heavy lift external crane
      </a>
      is needed in those cases. If the turbine has a difficult access road, a
      <a href="/wiki/Liftra" title="Liftra">
       containerized crane
      </a>
      can be lifted up by the internal crane to provide heavier lifting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        [64]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Repowering">
       Repowering
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Repowering">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Repowering" title="Repowering">
       Repowering
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Installation of new wind turbines can be controversial. An alternative is repowering, where existing wind turbines are replaced with bigger, more powerful ones, sometimes in smaller numbers while keeping or increasing capacity.
     </p>

      A
      <a href="/wiki/Demolition" title="Demolition">

       was the tallest wind turbine, standing in
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%98sterild_Wind_Turbine_Test_Field" title="Østerild Wind Turbine Test Field">
        Østerild
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
        Denmark
       </a>
       , 220 meters tall, constructed in 2014. It has a steel tube tower.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Highest tower
      </dt>
      <dd>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fuhrl%C3%A4nder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fuhrländer (page does not exist)">
        Fuhrländer
       </a>
       installed a 2.5MW turbine on a 160m lattice tower in 2003 (see
       <a href="/wiki/Fuhrl%C3%A4nder_Wind_Turbine_Laasow" title="Fuhrländer Wind Turbine Laasow">
        Fuhrländer Wind Turbine Laasow
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nowy_Tomy%C5%9Bl_Wind_Turbines" title="Nowy Tomyśl Wind Turbines">
        Nowy Tomyśl Wind Turbines
       </a>
       ).
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Most rotors
      </dt>
      <dd>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lagerwey_Wind&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lagerwey Wind (page does not exist)">
        Lagerwey
       </a>
       has build Four-in-One, a multi rotor wind turbine with one tower and four rotors near Maasvlakte.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In April 2016, Vestas installed a 900 kW
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadrotor" title="Quadrotor">
        quadrotor
       </a>
       test wind turbine at
       <a href="/wiki/Ris%C3%B8_DTU_National_Laboratory_for_Sustainable_Energy" title="Risø DTU National Laboratory for Sustainable Energy">
        Risø
       </a>
       , made from 4 recycled 225 kW V29 turbines.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
        <a href="#cite_note-76">
         [76]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
        <a href="#cite_note-77">
         [77]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
        <a href="#cite_note-78">
         [78]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Most productive
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Four turbines at Rønland
       <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">
        wind farm
       </a>
       in Denmark share the record for the most productive wind turbines, with each having generated 63.2 GWh by June 2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
        <a href="#cite_note-79">
         [79]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Highest-situated
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Since 2013 the world's highest-situated wind turbine was made and installed by WindAid and is located at the base of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pastoruri_Glacier" title="Pastoruri Glacier">
        Pastoruri Glacier
       </a>
       in Peru at 4,877 meters (16,001 ft) above sea level.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
        <a href="#cite_note-80">
         [80]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The site uses the WindAid 2.5 kW wind generator to supply power to a small rural community of micro entrepreneurs who cater to the tourists who come to the Pastoruri glacier.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81">
        <a href="#cite_note-81">
         [81]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Largest floating wind turbine
      </dt>
      <dd>
       The world's largest—and also the first operational deep-water
       <i>
        large-capacity
       </i>
       —
       <a href="/wiki/Floating_wind_turbine" title="Floating wind turbine">
        floating wind turbine
       </a>
       is the 2.3 MW
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hywind" title="Hywind">
        Hywind
       </a>
       currently operating 10 kilometers (6.2 mi) offshore in 220-meter-deep water, southwest of
       <a href="/wiki/Karm%C3%B8y" title="Karmøy">
        Karmøy
       </a>
       , Norway. The turbine began operating in September 2009 and utilizes a
       <a href="/wiki/Siemens_Wind_Power" title="Siemens Wind Power">
        Siemens
       </a>
       2.3 MW turbine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patel2009_82-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Patel2009-82">
         [82]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC20090908_83-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BBC20090908-83">
         [83]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
