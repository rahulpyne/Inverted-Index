     <p>
      A
      <b>
       green job
      </b>
      , also called a
      <b>
       green-collar job
      </b>
      is, according to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Program" title="United Nations Environment Program">
       United Nations Environment Program
      </a>
      , "work in agricultural, manufacturing,
      <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">
       research and development
      </a>
      (R&amp;D), administrative, and service activities that contribute(s) substantially to preserving or restoring environmental quality. Specifically, but not exclusively, this includes jobs that help to protect ecosystems and biodiversity; reduce energy, materials, and water consumption through high efficiency strategies; de-carbonize the economy; and minimize or altogether avoid generation of all forms of waste and pollution."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Net_jobs">
       Net jobs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_job&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Net jobs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A 2004 study by the Renewable and Appropriate Energy Laboratory (RAEL) at UC Berkeley reported that the renewable energy sector generates more jobs than the fossil fuel-based energy sector per unit of energy delivered (i.e., per average megawatt) across a broad range of scenarios.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Contrarily, a report by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriel_Calzada_Alvarez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gabriel Calzada Alvarez (page does not exist)">
       Gabriel Calzada Alvarez
      </a>
      analyzing the impact of an eleven-year Green energy project in Spain concluded that the U.S. should expect a loss of at least 2.2 jobs on average for each "green job" created, or about 9 jobs lost for every 4 created, in addition to those jobs that non-subsidized investments with the same resources would have created.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A rebuttal to the Spanish study by the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">
       National Renewable Energy Laboratory
      </a>
      (NREL) presented numerous allegations of methodological flaws in the Spanish study, citing outdated data, non-standard measures of job creation, and a lack of accompanying statistical analysis among the limitations of the study.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2010, the US
      <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Labor_Statistics" title="Bureau of Labor Statistics">
       Bureau of Labor Statistics
      </a>
      (BLS) received funding to analyze data about green jobs. The goal of this initiative is to [map] "(1) the number of and trend over time in green jobs, (2) the industrial, occupational, and geographic distribution of the jobs, and (3) the wages of the workers in these jobs."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In September 2010, the BLS published its final definition of green jobs in the Federal Register,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      organizing them in 2 types: A) Jobs in businesses that produce goods and provide services that benefit the environment or conserve natural resources. Type A) green jobs are further divided in 5 categories. B) Jobs in which workers' duties involve making their establishment's production processes more environmentally friendly or use fewer natural resources. Type B) jobs are further divided in 4 categories.
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2011, Canadian report by the
      <a href="/wiki/Pembina_Institute" title="Pembina Institute">
       Pembina Institute
      </a>
      found that the
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      ’s governments could create more jobs by implementing strong climate policies than by continuing with business as usual.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Jobs_and_Workforce_Education">
       Green Jobs and Workforce Education
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The National Council for Workforce Education and AED published a report,
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.ncwe.org/wp-content/uploads/2007/11/GoingGreensmaller.pdf" rel="nofollow">
       Going Green: Going Green: The Vital Role of Community Colleges in Building a Sustainable Future and a Green Workforce
      </a>

        </i>
        ]
       </span>
      </sup>
      that examines how workforce education and community colleges contribute to the overall efforts in the move toward renewable and clean energy. The report gives examples of initiatives currently in effect nationally as well as offering information as to how to implement programs.
     </p>
     <p>
      In response to high unemployment and a distressed economy workers need skills that are relevant to their specific geographical locations. "Instead of making green jobs we need to make jobs green" says Ken Warden, an administrator in workforce education.
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2016 study indicates that the declining
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      industry could protect their workers by retraining them for the
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Solar" title="Solar">
       solar
      </a>
      industry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is also some indications that the solar industry “welcomes coal workers with open arms”.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Jobs_Initiatives">
       Green Jobs Initiatives
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_job&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Green Jobs Initiatives">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="UNEP_Green_Jobs_Initiative">
       UNEP Green Jobs Initiative
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_job&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: UNEP Green Jobs Initiative">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 2007 the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">
       United Nations Environment Programme
      </a>
      (UNEP), the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Labor_Organization" title="International Labor Organization">
       International Labor Organization
      </a>
      (ILO), and the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Trade_Union_Confederation" title="International Trade Union Confederation">
       International Trade Union Confederation
      </a>
      (ITUC) jointly launched the Green Jobs Initiative. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Employers_Organization" title="International Employers Organization">
       International Employers Organization
      </a>
      (IEO) .
     </p>
     <p>
      <br/>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="USA_Green_Jobs_Act_2007">
       USA Green Jobs Act 2007
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_job&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: USA Green Jobs Act 2007">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Green Jobs Act of 2007 (H.R. 2847), introduced by Reps.
      <a href="/wiki/Hilda_Solis" title="Hilda Solis">
       Hilda Solis
      </a>
      (D-CA) and
      <a href="/wiki/John_F._Tierney" title="John F. Tierney">
       John Tierney
      </a>
      (D-MA), "authorized up to $125 million in funding to establish national and state job training programs, administered by the U.S. Department of Labor, to help address job shortages that are impairing growth in green industries, such as energy efficient buildings and construction, renewable electric power, energy efficient vehicles, and biofuels development."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Energy Independence and Security Act passed in December 2007 incorporates the Green Jobs Act of 2007.
     </p>
     <p>
      Additionally, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act">
       American Recovery and Reinvestment Act
      </a>
      (ARRA), passed in early 2009, includes provisions for new jobs in industries such as energy, utilities, construction, and manufacturing with a focus toward energy efficiency and more environmentally-friendly practices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 2009, U.S. President
      <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">
       Barack Obama
      </a>
      appointed
      <a href="/wiki/Van_Jones" title="Van Jones">
       Van Jones
      </a>
      as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Advisor_for_Green_Jobs,_Enterprise_and_Innovation" title="Special Advisor for Green Jobs, Enterprise and Innovation">
       Special Advisor for Green Jobs, Enterprise and Innovation
      </a>
      at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_House_Council_on_Environmental_Quality" title="White House Council on Environmental Quality">
       White House Council on Environmental Quality
      </a>
      (CEQ). Following Jones' resignation in September 2009, no further candidates appear to have been appointed to this position.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pathways_out_of_Poverty">
       Pathways out of Poverty
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_job&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pathways out of Poverty">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Pathways_out_of_Poverty" title="Pathways out of Poverty">
        Pathways out of Poverty
       </a>
      </b>
      (POP) is a national workforce training program that was established on August 14, 2009 by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obama_administration" title="Obama administration">
       Obama administration
      </a>
      and funded by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act">
       American Recovery and Reinvestment Act
      </a>
      (ARRA) of 2009. POP targets individuals living below or near the poverty level to provide them with skills needed to enter the green job market, focusing on the energy efficiency and renewable energy industries. The training programs focus on teaching basic literacy and job readiness skills. Some of the programs also provide supportive assistance with childcare and transportation to overcome barriers to employment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
