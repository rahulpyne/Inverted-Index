     <p>
      <b>
       SunEdison, Inc.
      </b>
      is a global
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      company headquartered in the U.S. In addition to developing, building, owning, and operating
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_power_plant" title="Solar power plant">
       solar power plants
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_energy" title="Wind energy">
       wind energy
      </a>
      plants, it also manufactures high purity
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysilicon" title="Polysilicon">
       polysilicon
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Monocrystalline_silicon" title="Monocrystalline silicon">
       monocrystalline
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Boule_(crystal)" title="Boule (crystal)">
       silicon ingots
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicon_wafer" title="Silicon wafer">
       silicon wafers
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_module" title="Solar module">
       solar modules
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">
       solar energy systems
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_mounting_system" title="Photovoltaic mounting system">
       solar module racking systems
      </a>
      . Originally a silicon-wafer manufacturer established in 1959 as the Monsanto Electronic Materials Company, a former business unit of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monsanto_Company" title="Monsanto Company">
       Monsanto Company
      </a>
      , Monsanto sold the company in 1989. Prior to May 30, 2013, the company was known as
      <b>
       MEMC Electronic Materials
      </b>
      ; the name change to SunEdison reflects the company's focus on solar energy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      SunEdison's corporate headquarters are in
      <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Heights,_Missouri" title="Maryland Heights, Missouri">
       Maryland Heights, Missouri
      </a>
      , and the company's operational and solar headquarters are in
      <a href="/wiki/Belmont,_California" title="Belmont, California">
       Belmont, California
      </a>
      with offices throughout the world.
     </p>
     <p>
      Established in the early stages of the semiconductor electronics industry in 1959, MEMC was for the next half century a major and pioneering manufacturer of silicon wafers, the most basic element of semiconductor-chip manufacturing. The company entered the solar market in a major way beginning in 2006, with longterm contracts to manufacture and sell solar wafers to several large Asian solar-energy companies. Contracts with other solar-energy companies followed, as did joint-venture projects on solar power plants. With the acquisition of the large, successful, and pioneering solar-energy systems company SunEdison LLC in late 2009 and the smaller solar-power companies Axio and Fotowatio in 2011, MEMC focused increasingly on the burgeoning solar-energy industry to offset the cyclical downturns in the semiconductor market. The company now develops, finances, and maintains solar power systems and plants for a widespread commercial, public-sector, and power-plant customer base, and its name change in 2013 to SunEdison, Inc. reflects its main focus. It is one of the leading solar-power companies worldwide, and with its acquisition of wind-energy company
      <a href="/wiki/First_Wind" title="First Wind">
       First Wind
      </a>
      in 2014, SunEdison is the leading
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      development company in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pentland_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pentland-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      SunEdison's subsidiaries TerraForm Power and TerraForm Global own and operate renewable-energy projects around the globe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2015, SunEdison sold off its subsidiary SunEdison Semiconductor, which manufactures silicon wafers for the electronics industry. The divestiture marked the completion of SunEdison, Inc.'s transition from a semiconductor-wafer company to a dedicated renewable-energy corporation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Following years of major expansion and the announcement of the intent – which eventually fell through – to acquire the residential-rooftop solar company
      <a href="/wiki/Vivint_Solar" title="Vivint Solar">
       Vivint Solar
      </a>
      in 2015, SunEdison's stock plummeted and its more than $11
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      billion in debt caused it to file for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_11" title="Chapter 11">
       Chapter 11
      </a>
      bankruptcy protection on April 21, 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">
       Foundation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SunEdison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation">

     </h3>
     <p>
      The establishment of
      <b>
       Monsanto Electronic Materials Company (MEMC)
      </b>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicon_wafer" title="Silicon wafer">
       silicon wafer
      </a>
      –manufacturing division to serve the emerging electronics industry, was announced on August 6, 1959, as an arm of the U.S.-based multinational corporation
      <a href="/wiki/Monsanto" title="Monsanto">
       Monsanto
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In February 1960 MEMC started production of 19mm silicon ingots at its location in
      <a href="/wiki/St._Peters,_Missouri" title="St. Peters, Missouri">
       St. Peters, Missouri
      </a>
      , 30 miles west of Monsanto's headquarters in St. Louis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As one of the first companies to produce semiconductor wafers, MEMC was a pioneer in the field, and some of its innovations became industry standards into the 21st century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      MEMC used the
      <a href="/wiki/Czochralski_process" title="Czochralski process">
       Czochralski process
      </a>
      (CZ process) of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicon_crystal" title="Silicon crystal">
       silicon crystal
      </a>
      production,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and developed the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_mechanical_polishing" title="Chemical mechanical polishing">
       Chemical Mechanical Polishing
      </a>
      (CMP) process of wafer finishing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1966 MEMC installed its first reactors for the production of
      <a href="/wiki/Epitaxial_wafer" title="Epitaxial wafer">
       epitaxial wafers
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-timeline-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and developed zero-dislocation crystal growing, which made large-diameter silicon crystals possible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">
       Expansion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SunEdison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expansion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the early 1970s, MEMC opened a production plant in
      <a href="/wiki/Kuala_Lumpur" title="Kuala Lumpur">
       Kuala Lumpur
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
       Malaysia
      </a>
      , and sent its St. Peters–produced 2.25-inch ingots there for slicing and polishing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1979, MEMC became the first company to manufacture 125mm (5-inch) wafers; in 1981 the first to produce 150mm (6-inch) wafers; and, in partnership with
      <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">
       IBM
      </a>
      , in 1984 the first to produce 200mm (8-inch) wafers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-timeline-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slideshare_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-slideshare-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1986 MEMC opened its production and R&amp;D facility in
      <a href="/wiki/Utsunomiya" title="Utsunomiya">
       Utsunomiya
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      , to serve the Japanese semiconductor market, becoming the first non-Japanese wafer maker with manufacturing and research facilities in Japan.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]

      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Change_of_ownership">
       Change of ownership
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SunEdison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Change of ownership">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      MEMC experienced heavy price-pressure from Japanese competition during the mid 1980s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite its success and increasing revenues, MEMC had to account for losses for a few years, leading to the decision of Monsanto, which was refocusing on chemicals, agriculture, and biotechnology products, to sell the electronic materials division.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1989 the German company
      <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemiepark_Marl" title="de:Chemiepark Marl">
       Hüls AG
      </a>
      , the chemicals arm of the German conglomerate
      <a href="/wiki/VEBA" title="VEBA">
       VEBA
      </a>
      , acquired Monsanto Electronic Materials and combined it with Hüls' previous acquisition
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamit_Nobel_AG" title="Dynamit Nobel AG">
       Dynamite Nobel
      </a>
      Silicon (DNS) to form MEMC Electronic Materials.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1997_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt1997-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      DNS already operated silicon wafer plants in
      <a href="/wiki/Merano" title="Merano">
       Merano
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Novara" title="Novara">
       Novara
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
       Italy
      </a>
      and integrated them within the new MEMC Electronic Materials.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hüls supported the new subsidiary with $50
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      million, for research and development and for manufacturing expansion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1991 MEMC developed the first process using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granular" title="Granular">
       granular
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysilicon" title="Polysilicon">
       polysilicon
      </a>
      , which provided cost and productivity advantages over "chunk" polysilicon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Four years later MEMC acquired
      <a href="/wiki/Albemarle_Corporation" title="Albemarle Corporation">
       Albemarle Corporation
      </a>
      's granular polysilicon production facility in
      <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_Texas" title="Pasadena, Texas">
       Pasadena
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
       Texas
      </a>
      , which had been producing granular polysilicon since 1987.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      MEMC's stock began trading on the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">
       New York Stock Exchange
      </a>
      with an
      <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">
       initial public offering
      </a>
      in 1995.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-timeline-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The IPO raised over $400
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      million, which went to finance an aggressive growth plan and repay some of the debt to its parent company, and Hüls/VEBA retained a majority interest in the company.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1997_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt1997-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cyclical downturn in the semiconductor business in the late 1990s hit MEMC hard.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1997_26-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt1997-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1998 the company reported a loss of $316
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      million on revenues of $759
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In June 2000 VEBA AG, still holding 72% of MEMC, was merged with
      <a href="/wiki/VEBA" title="VEBA">
       VIAG
      </a>
      to form the new
      <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">
       E.ON
      </a>
      AG.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swinger68_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-swinger68-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
      E.ON wanted to focus on its core business of electric utilities, and assigned
      <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" title="Merrill Lynch">
       Merrill Lynch
      </a>
      to sell MEMC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swinger68_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-swinger68-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shinkle1_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-shinkle1-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Merrill was unable to find a buyer until MEMC announced that it was on the verge of illiquidity in the middle of 2001.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-miller-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Finally in October 2001 E.ON was able to agree on a deal with the
      <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">
       private equity
      </a>
      company
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Texas_Pacific_Group" title="Texas Pacific Group">
       Texas Pacific Group
      </a>
      (TPG), which purchased E.ON's stake in MEMC for a symbolic dollar and offered MEMC $150
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      million in credit lines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shinkle1_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-shinkle1-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_35-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-miller-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
      TPG restructured MEMC's debt, increased its stake in the company to 90%, and cut one third of its workforce.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sunshine_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sunshine-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      MEMC returned to profitability following the appointment of
      <a href="/wiki/Nabeel_Gareeb" title="Nabeel Gareeb">
       Nabeel Gareeb
      </a>
      , who was CEO of MEMC from April 2002 to November 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sunshine_38-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sunshine-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000ceos_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-1000ceos-40">
        [40]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        [41]
       </a>
      </sup>
      MEMC's market share rose again and by 2003 it reported positive earning figures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sunshine_38-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sunshine-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        [42]
       </a>
      </sup>
      MEMC's sales topped $1
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      billion in 2004, and it was number three in market share.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-directory_9-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-directory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swinger69_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-swinger69-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Through a secondary offering, TPG reduced its share of MEMC in 2005 to 34%, and by the end of 2007, to zero.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sunshine_38-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-sunshine-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swinger69_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-swinger69-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_market_entry">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">

       </a>
      </sup>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1000ceos_40-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1000ceos-40">

      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Q-Cells" title="Q-Cells">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>

       Bavaria
      </a>
      ,

       Germany

      <span class="nowrap">

       <a href="#cite_note-bavaria-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordcapital" title="de:Nordcapital">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_SunEdison">
       Acquisition of SunEdison
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SunEdison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acquisition of SunEdison">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In November 2009 MEMC acquired the privately owned company SunEdison LLC, North America's largest
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
       solar energy
      </a>
      services provider.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scope_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-scope-54">
        [54]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Founded by
      <a href="/wiki/Jigar_Shah" title="Jigar Shah">
       Jigar Shah
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shah_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-shah-55">
        [55]
       </a>
      </sup>
      SunEdison had been developing, financing, building, operating, and monitoring large-scale photovoltaic plants for commercial customers, including many national retail outlets, government agencies, and utilities, since 2003.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        [56]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        [57]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        [58]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The company had pioneered solar-as-a-service, and the solar
      <a href="/wiki/Power_purchase_agreement" title="Power purchase agreement">
       power purchase agreement
      </a>
      (PPA) for no-money-down customer financing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shah_55-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-shah-55">
        [55]

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bankruptcy">
       Bankruptcy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=SunEdison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bankruptcy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Following years of major expansion and the announcement of the intent – which eventually fell through – to acquire the residential-rooftop solar company
      <a href="/wiki/Vivint_Solar" title="Vivint Solar">
       Vivint Solar
      </a>
      in 2015, SunEdison's stock plummeted and its more than $11
      <span class="nowrap">
      </span>
      billion in debt caused it to face bankruptcy in April 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-big_106-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-big-106">
        [106]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-why_107-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-why-107">
        [107]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-messy_108-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-messy-108">
        [108]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brink_109-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-brink-109">
        [109]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panel_110-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-panel-110">
        [110]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It filed for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_11" title="Chapter 11">
       Chapter 11
      </a>
      bankruptcy protection on April 21, 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ftch11_111-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ftch11-111">
        [111]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsjch11_112-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsjch11-112">
        [112]
       </a>
      </sup>
      To continue its operations and pay staff, the company received $300 million in bankruptcy debt financing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_113-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-113">
        [113]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It will continue operations during bankruptcy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114">
       <a href="#cite_note-114">
        [114]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115">
       <a href="#cite_note-115">
        [115]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The debt financing money came from first-lien and second-lien lenders. The bankruptcy court must approve the money.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_116-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:1-116">
        [116]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When it filed for bankruptcy, the company asked the court for an independent examiner to audit the company’s recent financial transactions. SunEdison requested that the examiner’s work start immediately and finish within 60 days, and that the maximum budget be $1 million.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">
        Reuters
       </a>
      </i>
      noted that, comparatively, the 2015 independent examination in the bankruptcy of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesars_Entertainment_Corp." title="Caesars Entertainment Corp.">
       Caesars Entertainment Corp.
      </a>
      took one year and cost $40 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_116-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:1-116">
        [116]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the summer of 2015, SunEdison was worth almost $10 billion,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_117-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:2-117">
        [117]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and in July 2015 shares traded upward of $33.44.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_116-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-:1-116">
        [116]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the day of the bankruptcy filing, the company’s trading price on the New York Stock Exchange was 34 cents per share.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_116-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-:1-116">
        [116]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the
      <i>
       Wall Street Journal
      </i>
      : “SunEdison used a combination of financial engineering and cheap debt to buy up renewable-power projects around the world before the market turned sour last summer and investors soured on its business model.”
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_117-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:2-117">
        [117]
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the three years preceding the bankruptcy filing, SunEdison invested $18 billion in acquisitions. During that time, the company also raised $24 billion in debt and equity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_117-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-:2-117">
        [117]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As of late April 2016, the U.S. Department of Justice is conducting an investigation into the company regarding its financial practices. Internally, SunEdison’s board completed its own investigation, concluding that the company’s leaders were “overly optimistic” but did not make “material misstatements” or commit any fraud.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_113-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-113">
        [113]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
