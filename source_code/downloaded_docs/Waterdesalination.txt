     <p>
      <b>
       Desalination
      </b>
      is a process that removes
      <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">
       minerals
      </a>
      from saline
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      . More generally, desalination refers to the removal of salts and minerals from a target substance,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      as in
      <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity_control" title="Soil salinity control">
       soil desalination
      </a>
      , which is an issue for agriculture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">
       Saltwater
      </a>
      is desalinated to produce water suitable for
      <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">
       human consumption
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">
       irrigation
      </a>
      . One
      <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">
       by-product
      </a>
      of desalination is
      <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">
       salt
      </a>
      . Desalination is used on many seagoing
      <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">
       ships
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">
       submarines
      </a>
      . Most of the modern interest in desalination is focused on cost-effective provision of
      <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">
       fresh water
      </a>
      for human use. Along with recycled
      <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">
       wastewater
      </a>
      , it is one of the few rainfall-independent water sources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischetti2007_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fischetti2007-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to its energy consumption, desalinating sea water is generally more costly than fresh water from rivers or
      <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">
       groundwater
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_recycling" title="Water recycling">
       water recycling
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">
       water conservation
      </a>
      . However, these alternatives are not always available and depletion of reserves is a critical problem worldwide. Currently, approximately 1% of the world's population is dependent on desalinated water to meet daily needs, but the UN expects that 14% of the world's population will encounter water scarcity by 2025.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gwi_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gwi-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Desalination is particularly relevant in dry countries such as
      <a href="/wiki/Seawater_desalination_in_Australia" title="Seawater desalination in Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      , which traditionally have relied on collecting rainfall behind dams for water.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the International Desalination Association, in June 2015, 18,426 desalination plants operated worldwide, producing 86.8 million cubic meters per day, providing water for 300 million people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This number increased from 78.4 million cubic meters in 2013,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gwi_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gwi-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a 57% increase in just 5 years. The single largest desalination project is
      <a href="/wiki/Ras_Al-Khair" title="Ras Al-Khair">
       Ras Al-Khair
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">
       Saudi Arabia
      </a>
      , which produced 1,025,000 cubic meters per day in 2014,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gwi_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-gwi-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      although this plant is expected to be surpassed by a plant in California.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
       Israel
      </a>
      produces a higher proportion of its water than any other country, totaling 40% of its water use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">
       Methods
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methods">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The traditional process used in these operations is
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_distillation" title="Vacuum distillation">
       vacuum distillation
      </a>
      —essentially boiling it to leave impurities behind. In desalination, atmospheric pressure is reduced, thus lowering the required temperature needed. Liquids boil when the
      <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">
       vapor pressure
      </a>
      equals the ambient pressure and vapor pressure increases with temperature. Thus, because of the reduced temperature, low-temperature "waste" heat from electrical power generation or industrial processes can be employed.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2816" data-file-width="2112" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG/220px-Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG/330px-Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG/440px-Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Reverse osmosis desalination plant in Barcelona, Spain
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The principal competing processes use membranes to desalinate, principally applying
      <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">
       reverse osmosis
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Membrane processes use semipermeable membranes and pressure to separate salts from water.
      <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis_plant" title="Reverse osmosis plant">
       Reverse osmosis plant
      </a>
      membrane systems typically use less energy than thermal distillation. Desalination remains energy intensive, however, and future costs will continue to depend on the energy prices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Considerations_and_criticism">
       Considerations and criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Considerations and criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_consumption">
       Energy consumption
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy consumption">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Energy consumption of seawater desalination has reached as low as 3 kWh/m
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      including pre-filtering and ancillaries, similar to the energy consumption of other fresh water supplies transported over large distances,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      but much higher than local fresh water supplies that use 0.2 kWh/m
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      or less.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A minimum energy consumption for seawater desalination of around 1 kWh/m
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      has been determined,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      excluding prefiltering and intake/outfall pumping. Under 2 kWh/m
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      has been achieved with
      <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">
       reverse osmosis
      </a>
      membrane technology, leaving limited scope for further energy reductions.
     </p>
     <p>
      Supplying all US domestic water by desalination would increase
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_States" title="Energy in the United States">
       energy consumption
      </a>
      by around 10%, about the amount of energy used by domestic refrigerators.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Domestic consumption is a relatively small fraction of the total water usage.

       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cogeneration">
       Cogeneration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cogeneration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
       Cogeneration
      </a>
      is generating excess heat and electricity generation from a single process. Cogeneration can provide usable heat for desalination in an integrated, or "dual-purpose", facility where a power plant provides the energy for desalination. Alternatively, the facility's energy production may be dedicated to the production of potable water (a stand-alone facility), or excess energy may be produced and incorporated into the energy grid. Cogeneration takes various forms, and theoretically any form of energy production could be used. However, the majority of current and planned cogeneration desalination plants use either
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">
       fossil fuels
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">
       nuclear power
      </a>
      as their source of energy. Most plants are located in the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
       Middle East
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">
       North Africa
      </a>
      , which use their petroleum resources to offset limited water resources. The advantage of dual-purpose facilities is they can be more efficient in energy consumption, thus making desalination more viable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="339" data-file-width="490" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg/300px-Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg/450px-Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8b/Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Shevchenko BN350, a nuclear-heated desalination unit
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The current trend in dual-purpose facilities is hybrid configurations, in which the permeate from reverse osmosis desalination is mixed with distillate from thermal desalination. Basically, two or more desalination processes are combined along with power production. Such facilities have been implemented in Saudi Arabia at
      <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">
       Jeddah
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Yanbu" title="Yanbu">
       Yanbu
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A typical
      <a href="/wiki/Supercarrier" title="Supercarrier">
       Supercarrier
      </a>
      in the US military uses nuclear power to desalinate 400,000 US gallons (1,500,000 l; 330,000 imp gal) of water per day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">
       Economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Costs of desalinating sea water (infrastructure, energy, and maintenance) are generally higher than fresh water from rivers or
      <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">
       groundwater
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_recycling" title="Water recycling">
       water recycling
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">
       water conservation
      </a>
      , but alternatives are not always available. Desalination costs in 2013 ranged from US$0.45 to $1.00/cubic metre ($US2 to 4/kgal). (1 cubic meter is about 264 gallons.) More than half of the cost comes directly from energy cost, and since energy prices are very volatile, actual costs can vary substantially.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang_2012_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zhang_2012-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cost of untreated fresh water in the developing world can reach US$5/cubic metre.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Factors that determine the costs for desalination include capacity and type of facility, location, feed water, labor, energy, financing and concentrate disposal. Desalination
      <a href="/wiki/Still" title="Still">
       stills
      </a>
      control pressure, temperature and brine concentrations to optimize efficiency.
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">
       Nuclear-powered
      </a>
      desalination might be economical on a large scale.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While noting costs are falling, and generally positive about the technology for affluent areas in proximity to oceans, a 2004 study argued, "Desalinated water may be a solution for some water-stress regions, but not for places that are poor, deep in the interior of a continent, or at high elevation. Unfortunately, that includes some of the places with biggest water problems.", and, "Indeed, one needs to lift the water by 2,000 m (6,600 ft), or transport it over more than 1,600 km (990 mi) to get transport costs equal to the desalination costs. Thus, it may be more economical to transport fresh water from somewhere else than to desalinate it. In places far from the sea, like
      <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">
       New Delhi
      </a>
      , or in high places, like
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
       Mexico City
      </a>
      , transport costs could match desalination costs. Desalinated water is also expensive in places that are both somewhat far from the sea and somewhat high, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">
       Riyadh
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Harare" title="Harare">
       Harare
      </a>
      . By contrast in other locations transport costs are much less, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">
       Beijing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">
       Bangkok
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">
       Zaragoza
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">
       Phoenix
      </a>
      , and, of course, coastal cities like
      <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">
       Tripoli
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      After desalination at
      <a href="/wiki/Jubail" title="Jubail">
       Jubail
      </a>
      , Saudi Arabia, water is pumped 200 mi (320 km) inland to
      <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">
       Riyadh
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      For coastal cities, desalination is increasingly viewed as a competitive choice.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2014, the Israeli facilities of Hadera, Palmahim, Ashkelon, and Sorek were desalinizing water for less than US$0.40 per cubic meter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 2006, Singapore was desalinating water for US$0.49 per cubic meter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The city of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">
       Perth
      </a>
      began operating a reverse osmosis seawater desalination plant in 2006.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A desalination plant now operates in
      <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">
       Sydney
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wonthaggi_desalination_plant" title="Wonthaggi desalination plant">
       Wonthaggi desalination plant
      </a>
      was under construction in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wonthaggi,_Victoria" title="Wonthaggi, Victoria">
       Wonthaggi, Victoria
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The Perth desalination plant is powered partially by renewable energy from the
      <a href="/wiki/Emu_Downs_Wind_Farm" title="Emu Downs Wind Farm">
       Emu Downs Wind Farm
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-npr-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A wind farm at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bungendore" title="Bungendore">
       Bungendore
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">
       New South Wales
      </a>
      was purpose-built to generate enough
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      to offset the Sydney plant's energy use,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">

       </a>
      </sup>

      </a>
      emissions.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental">
       Environmental
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Factors that determine the costs for desalination include capacity and type of facility, location, feed water, labor, energy, financing and concentrate disposal.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Intake">
       Intake
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Intake">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , cooling water intake structures are regulated by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">
       Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      (EPA). These structures can have the same impacts to the environment as desalination facility intakes. According to EPA, water intake structures cause adverse environmental impact by sucking fish and shellfish or their eggs into an industrial system. There, the organisms may be killed or injured by heat, physical stress, or chemicals. Larger organisms may be killed or injured when they become trapped against screens at the front of an intake structure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Alternative intake types that mitigate these impacts include beach wells, but they require more energy and higher costs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kwinana_Desalination_Plant" title="Kwinana Desalination Plant">
       Kwinana Desalination Plant
      </a>
      opened in Perth in 2007. Water there and at Queensland's
      <a href="/wiki/Gold_Coast_Desalination_Plant" title="Gold Coast Desalination Plant">
       Gold Coast Desalination Plant
      </a>
      and Sydney's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurnell_Desalination_Plant" title="Kurnell Desalination Plant">
       Kurnell Desalination Plant
      </a>
      is withdrawn at 0.1 m/s (0.33 ft/s), which is slow enough to let fish escape. The plant provides nearly 140,000 m
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      (4,900,000 cu ft) of clean water per day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-npr-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Desalination processes produce large quantities of
      <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">
       brine
      </a>
      , possibly at above ambient temperature, and contain residues of pretreatment and cleaning chemicals, their reaction byproducts and heavy metals due to corrosion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Chemical pretreatment and cleaning are a necessity in most desalination plants, which typically includes prevention of biofouling, scaling, foaming and corrosion in thermal plants, and of biofouling, suspended solids and scale deposits in membrane plants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        [46]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To limit the environmental impact of returning the brine to the ocean, it can be diluted with another stream of water entering the ocean, such as the outfall of a
      <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">
       wastewater treatment
      </a>
      or power plant. With medium to large power plant and desalination plants, the power plant's cooling water flow is likely to be several times larger than that of the desalination plant, reducing the salinity of the combination. Another method to dilute the brine is to mix it via a diffuser in a mixing zone. For example, once a pipeline containing the brine reaches the sea floor, it can split into many branches, each releasing brine gradually through small holes along its length. Mixing can be combined with power plant or wastewater plant dilution.
     </p>
     <p>
      Brine is denser than seawater and therefore sinks to the ocean bottom and can damage the ecosystem. Careful reintroduction can minimize this problem. Typical ocean conditions allow for rapid dilution, thereby minimizing harm.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_without_pollution">
       Alternatives without pollution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Alternatives without pollution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Some methods of desalination, particularly in combination with
      <a href="/wiki/Evaporation_pond" title="Evaporation pond">
       evaporation ponds
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_still" title="Solar still">
       solar stills
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condensation_trap" title="Condensation trap">
       condensation trap
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_desalination" title="Solar desalination">
       solar desalination
      </a>
      ), do not discharge brine. They do not use chemicals or burn fossil fuels. They do not work with membranes or other critical parts, such as components that include heavy metals, thus do not produce toxic waste (and high maintenance).
     </p>
     <p>
      A new approach that works like a solar still, but on the scale of industrial evaporation ponds is the
      <a href="/wiki/Integrated_biotectural_system" title="Integrated biotectural system">
       integrated biotectural system
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PrototypeCreation_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PrototypeCreation-47">
        [47]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It can be considered "full desalination" because it converts the entire amount of saltwater intake into distilled water. One of the advantages of this system is the feasibility for inland operation. Standard advantages also include no air pollution and no temperature increase of endangered natural water bodies from power plant cooling-water discharge. Another important advantage is the production of sea salt for industrial and other uses. As of 2015, 50% of the world's sea salt production relies on fossil energy sources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_desalination">
       Alternatives to desalination
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Alternatives to desalination">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Increased
      <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">
       water conservation
      </a>
      and efficiency remain the most cost-effective approaches in areas with a large potential to improve the efficiency of water use practices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Wastewater reclamation provides multiple benefits over desalination.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Urban runoff and storm water capture also provide benefits in treating, restoring and recharging groundwater.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A proposed alternative to desalination in the American Southwest is the commercial importation of bulk water from water-rich areas either by
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">
       oil tankers
      </a>
      converted to water carriers, or pipelines. The idea is politically unpopular in Canada, where governments imposed trade barriers to bulk water exports as a result of a
      <a href="/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="North American Free Trade Agreement">
       North American Free Trade Agreement
      </a>
      (NAFTA) claim.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        [52]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health_concerns">
       Public health concerns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Public health concerns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Desalination removes iodine from water and could increase the risk of iodine deficiency disorders. Israeli researchers claimed a possible link between seawater desalination and iodine deficiency,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        [53]
       </a>
      </sup>
      finding deficits among
      <a href="/wiki/Euthyroid" title="Euthyroid">
       euthyroid
      </a>
      adults exposed to iodine-poor water
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        [54]
       </a>
      </sup>
      concurrently with an increasing proportion of their area's drinking water from seawater reverse osmosis (SWRO).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        [55]
       </a>
      </sup>
      They later found probable iodine deficiency disorders in a population reliant on desalinated seawater.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        [56]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_techniques">
       Experimental techniques
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Experimental techniques">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Other desalination techniques include:
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_heat">
       Waste heat
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Waste heat">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diesel_generators" title="Diesel generators">
       Diesel generators
      </a>
      commonly provide electricity in remote areas. About 40%–50% of the energy output is low-grade heat that leaves the engine via the exhaust. Connecting a
      <a href="/wiki/Membrane_distillation" title="Membrane distillation">
       membrane distillation
      </a>
      system to the diesel engine exhaust repurposes this low-grade heat for desalination. The system actively cools the
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_generator" title="Diesel generator">
       diesel generator
      </a>
      , improving its efficiency and increasing its electricity output. This results in an energy-neutral desalination solution. An example plant was commissioned by Dutch company
      <a href="/wiki/Aquaver" title="Aquaver">
       Aquaver
      </a>
      in March 2014 for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulhi" title="Gulhi">
       Gulhi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">
       Maldives
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        [57]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        [58]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Low-temperature_thermal">
       Low-temperature thermal
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Low-temperature thermal">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Originally stemming from
      <a href="/wiki/Ocean_thermal_energy_conversion" title="Ocean thermal energy conversion">
       ocean thermal energy conversion
      </a>
      research,
      <a href="/wiki/Low-temperature_thermal_desalination" title="Low-temperature thermal desalination">
       low-temperature thermal desalination
      </a>
      (LTTD) takes advantage of water boiling at low pressure, even at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambient_temperature" title="Ambient temperature">
       ambient temperature
      </a>
      . The system uses pumps to create a low-pressure, low-temperature environment in which water boils at a temperature gradient of 8–10 °C (46–50 °F) between two volumes of water. Cool ocean water is supplied from depths of up to 600 m (2,000 ft). This water is pumped through coils to condense the water vapor. The resulting condensate is purified water. LTTD may take advantage of the temperature gradient available at power plants, where large quantities of warm wastewater are discharged from the plant, reducing the energy input needed to create a temperature gradient.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isope1_59-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-isope1-59">
        [59]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Experiments were conducted in the US and Japan to test the approach. In Japan, a spray-ﬂash evaporation system was tested by Saga University.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        [60]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Hawaii, the National Energy Laboratory tested an open-cycle OTEC plant with fresh water and power production using a temperature difference of 20 C° between surface water and water at a depth of around 500 m (1,600 ft). LTTD was studied by India's National Institute of Ocean Technology (NIOT) in 2004. Their first LTTD plant opened in 2005 at Kavaratti in the
      <a href="/wiki/Lakshadweep" title="Lakshadweep">
       Lakshadweep
      </a>
      islands. The plant's capacity is 100,000 L (22,000 imp gal; 26,000 US gal)/day, at a capital cost of INR 50 million (€922,000). The plant uses deep water at a temperature of 10 to 12 °C (50 to 54 °F).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irc_61-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-irc-61">
        [61]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2007, NIOT opened an experimental, floating LTTD plant off the coast of
      <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">
       Chennai
      </a>
      , with a capacity of 1,000,000 L (220,000 imp gal; 260,000 US gal)/day. A smaller plant was established in 2009 at the North Chennai Thermal Power Station to prove the LTTD application where power plant cooling water is available.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isope1_59-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-isope1-59">
        [59]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoionic_process">
       Thermoionic process
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Thermoionic process">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In October 2009, Saltworks Technologies announced a process that uses solar or other thermal heat to drive an
      <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
       ionic
      </a>
      current that removes all
      <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">
       sodium
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
       chlorine
      </a>
      ions from the water using ion-exchange membranes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        [64]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Evaporation_and_condensation_for_crops">
       Evaporation and condensation for crops
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Evaporation and condensation for crops">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Seawater_greenhouse" title="Seawater greenhouse">
       Seawater greenhouse
      </a>
      uses natural evaporation and condensation processes inside a
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">
       greenhouse
      </a>
      powered by solar energy to grow crops in arid coastal land.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_approaches">
       Other approaches
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Adsorption-based desalination (AD) relies on the moisture absorption properties of certain materials such as Silica Gel.
     </p>
     <p>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        [65]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="external free" href="https://www.kaust.edu.sa/en/news/desalination-project-wins-global-innovation-challenge" rel="nofollow">
       https://www.kaust.edu.sa/en/news/desalination-project-wins-global-innovation-challenge
      </a>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Forward_osmosis">
       Forward osmosis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Forward osmosis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      One process was commercialized by Modern Water PLC using
      <a href="/wiki/Forward_osmosis" title="Forward osmosis">
       forward osmosis
      </a>
      , with a number of plants reported to be in operation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        [66]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        [67]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        [68]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Small-scale_solar">
       Small-scale solar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Small-scale solar">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The United States, France and the United Arab Emirates are working to develop practical
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_desalination" title="Solar desalination">
       solar desalination
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        [69]
       </a>
      </sup>
      AquaDania's WaterStillar has been installed at Dahab, Egypt, and in Playa del Carmen, Mexico. In this approach, a solar thermal collector measuring two square metres can distill from 40 to 60 litres per day from any local water source – five times more than conventional stills. It eliminates the need for plastic
      <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">
       PET
      </a>
      bottles or energy-consuming water transport.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        [70]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Central California, a startup company WaterFX is developing a solar-powered method of desalination that can enable the use of local water, including runoff water that can be treated and used again. Salty groundwater in the region would be treated to become freshwater, and in areas near the ocean, seawater could be treated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        [71]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Passarell">
       Passarell
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Passarell">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Passarell process uses reduced atmospheric pressure rather than heat to drive evaporative desalination. The pure water vapor generated by distillation is then compressed and condensed using an advanced compressor. The compression process improves distillation efficiency by creating the reduced pressure in the evaporation chamber. The compressor
      <a href="/wiki/Centrifuge" title="Centrifuge">
       centrifuges
      </a>
      the pure water vapor after it is drawn through a demister (removing residual impurities) causing it to compress against tubes in the collection chamber. The compression of the vapor increases its temperature. The heat is transferred to the input water falling in the tubes, vaporizing the water in the tubes. Water vapor condenses on the outside of the tubes as product water. By combining several physical processes, Passarell enables most of the system's energy to be recycled through its evaporation, demisting, vapor compression, condensation, and water movement processes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        [72]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal">
       Geothermal
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Geothermal">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Geothermal energy can drive desalination. In most locations,
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_desalination" title="Geothermal desalination">
       geothermal desalination
      </a>
      beats using scarce groundwater or surface water, environmentally and economically.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nanotechnology">
       Nanotechnology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Nanotechnology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Nanotube_membrane" title="Nanotube membrane">
       Nanotube membranes
      </a>
      may prove to be effective for water filtration and desalination processes that would require substantially less energy than reverse osmosis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LLNL_73-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LLNL-73">
        [73]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Hermetic, sulphonated nano-composite membranes have shown to be capable of reducing almost all forms of contamination to the parts per billion level. These nano-materials, using a non-reverse osmosis process, have little or no susceptibility to high salt concentration levels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        [74]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
       <a href="#cite_note-75">
        [75]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        [76]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biomimesis">
       Biomimesis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Biomimesis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomimetic" title="Biomimetic">
       Biomimetic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Biological_membrane" title="Biological membrane">
       membranes
      </a>
      are another approach.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
       <a href="#cite_note-77">
        [77]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrochemical">
       Electrochemical
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Electrochemical">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In 2008, Siemens Water Technologies announced technology that applied electric fields to desalinate one cubic meter of water while using only a purported 1.5 kWh of energy. If accurate, this process would consume one-half the energy of other processes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
       <a href="#cite_note-78">
        [78]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 2012 a demonstration plant was operating in Singapore.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
       <a href="#cite_note-79">
        [79]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Researchers at the University of Texas at Austin and the University of Marburg are developing more efficient methods of electrochemically mediated seawater desalination.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
       <a href="#cite_note-80">
        [80]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Freeze-thaw">
       Freeze-thaw
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Freeze-thaw">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Freeze-thaw desalination uses freezing to remove fresh water from frozen seawater.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81">
       <a href="#cite_note-81">
        [81]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrokinetic_shocks">
       Electrokinetic shocks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Electrokinetic shocks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      A process employing electrokinetic shocks waves can be used to accomplish membraneless desalination at ambient temperature and pressure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82">
       <a href="#cite_note-82">
        [82]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this process, anions and cations in salt water are exchanged for carbonate anions and calcium cations, respectively using electrokinetic shockwaves. Calcium and carbonate ions react to form
      <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">
       calcium carbonate
      </a>
      , which precipitates, leaving fresh water. The theoretical energy efficiency of this method is on par with
      <a href="/wiki/Electrodialysis" title="Electrodialysis">
       electrodialysis
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">
       reverse osmosis
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_nature">
       In nature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: In nature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2001" data-file-width="1242" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG/100px-Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG/150px-Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG/200px-Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG 2x" width="100"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Mangrove leaf with salt crystals
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Evaporation of water over the oceans in the
      <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">
       water cycle
      </a>
      is a natural desalination process.
     </p>
     <p>
      The formation of
      <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">
       sea ice
      </a>
      produces ice with little salt, much lower than in seawater.
     </p>
     <p>
      Seabirds distill seawater using
      <a href="/wiki/Countercurrent_exchange" title="Countercurrent exchange">
       countercurrent exchange
      </a>
      in a
      <a href="/wiki/Salt_gland" title="Salt gland">
       gland
      </a>
      with a
      <a href="/wiki/Rete_mirabile" title="Rete mirabile">
       rete mirabile
      </a>
      . The gland
      <a href="/wiki/Na%2B/K%2B-ATPase" title="Na+/K+-ATPase">
       secretes highly concentrated brine
      </a>
      stored near the nostrils above the beak. The bird then "sneezes" the brine out. As freshwater is not usually available in their environments, some seabirds, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">
       pelicans
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Petrel" title="Petrel">
       petrels
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">
       albatrosses
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">
       gulls
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">
       terns
      </a>
      , possess this gland, which allows them to drink the salty water from their environments while they are far from land.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83">
       <a href="#cite_note-83">
        [83]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84">
       <a href="#cite_note-84">
        [84]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">
       Mangrove
      </a>
      trees grow in seawater; they secrete salt by trapping it in parts of the root, which are then eaten by animals (usually crabs). Additional salt is removed by storing it in leaves that fall off. Some types of mangroves have glands on their leaves, which work in a similar way to the seabird desalination gland. Salt is extracted to the leaf exterior as small
      <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">
       crystals
      </a>
      , which then fall off the leaf.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">
       Willow
      </a>
      trees and
      <a href="/wiki/Reed_(plant)" title="Reed (plant)">
       reeds
      </a>
      absorb salt and other contaminants, effectively desalinating the water. This is used in artificial
      <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">
       constructed wetlands
      </a>
      , for treating
      <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">
       sewage
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Before the Industrial Revolution, desalination was primarily of concern to oceangoing ships, which otherwise needed to keep on board supplies of fresh water. In the newly formed United States, Thomas Jefferson catalogued heat-based methods going back to the 1500s, and formulated practical advice that was publicized to all U.S. ships on the backs of sailing clearance permits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85">
       <a href="#cite_note-85">
        [85]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86">
       <a href="#cite_note-86">
        [86]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Significant research into improved desalination methods occurred in the United States after World War II. The
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Saline_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Office of Saline Water (page does not exist)">
       Office of Saline Water
      </a>
      was created in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">
       United States Department of the Interior
      </a>
      by the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saline_Water_Conversion_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saline Water Conversion Act (page does not exist)">
       Saline Water Conversion Act
      </a>
      of 1952. It was merged into the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Water_Resources_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Office of Water Resources Research (page does not exist)">
       Office of Water Resources Research
      </a>
      in 1974.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87">
       <a href="#cite_note-87">
        [87]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Research also took place at state universities in California, followed by development at the
      <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">
       Dow Chemical Company
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">
       DuPont
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88">
       <a href="#cite_note-88">
        [88]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
