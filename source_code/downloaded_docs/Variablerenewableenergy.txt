     <p>
      <b>
       Variable renewable energy
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       VRE
      </b>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      source that is non-dispatchable due to its fluctuating nature, like
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">
       solar power
      </a>
      , as opposed to a controllable renewable energy source such as
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectricity
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      , or a relatively constant source such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal" title="Geothermal">
       geothermal
      </a>
      power or
      <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">
       run-of-the-river hydroelectricity
      </a>
      . Skeptics of wind and solar power warn of their variable output, but many studies have shown that the grid can cope, and it is doing so in
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Denmark" title="Renewable energy in Denmark">
       Denmark
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Denmark" title="Renewable energy in Denmark">
       Germany
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Spain" title="Renewable energy in Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">
       International Energy Agency
      </a>
      says that there has been too much attention on issue of the variability of renewable electricity production.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IEA-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its significance depends on a range of factors which include the market penetration of the renewables concerned, the
      <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_plant" title="Balance of plant">
       balance of plant
      </a>
      and the wider connectivity of the system, as well as the demand side flexibility. Variability will rarely be a barrier to increased renewable energy deployment. But at high levels of market penetration it requires careful analysis and management, and additional costs may be required for backup or system modification.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-IEA-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Renewable electricity supply in the 20–50+% penetration range has already been implemented in several European systems, albeit in the context of an integrated European grid system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovins11_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lovins11-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Biomass and geothermal are both completely dispatchable; wind and solar production can be decreased, but not increased other than what nature provides. Between wind and solar, solar is more variable than wind and more predictable than wind. Biofuel and biomass involve a two step process in the production of energy – the production of fuel and the use of that fuel to create energy. For example wood needs to be cut to create firewood, and not only can be stored, but needs to be stored to dry adequately. Biofuel is created in one step, and is stored and then burned to create energy. In the combined power plant used by the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kassel" title="University of Kassel">
       University of Kassel
      </a>
      to simulate using
      <a href="/wiki/100%25_renewable_energy" title="100% renewable energy">
       100% renewable energy
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_farms" title="Wind farms">
       wind farms
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_farms" title="Solar farms">
       solar farms
      </a>
      were supplemented as needed by hydrostorage and biomass to minute by minute follow the total electricity demand.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-combined_power_plant_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-combined_power_plant-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">
       Wind power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_renewable_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wind power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Windpowerprediction.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Windpowerprediction.png/220px-Windpowerprediction.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Windpowerprediction.png/330px-Windpowerprediction.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Windpowerprediction.png/440px-Windpowerprediction.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Windpowerprediction.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Day ahead prediction and actual wind power
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Wind power is the least predictable of all of the variable renewable energy sources. Grid operators use day ahead forecasting to determine which of the available power sources to use the next day, and weather forecasting is used to predict the likely wind power and solar power output available. The correlation between wind output and prediction can be relatively high, with an average uncorrected error of 8.8% in Germany over a two-year period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The variability of wind power can be seen as one of its defining characteristics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Waves are primarily created by wind, so the power available from waves tends to follow that available from wind, but due to the mass of the water is less variable than wind power. Wind power is proportional to the cube of the wind speed, while wave power is proportional to the square of the wave height.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">
       Solar power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_renewable_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ATTParksolarpaneloutput.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="955" height="86" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/ATTParksolarpaneloutput.png/220px-ATTParksolarpaneloutput.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/ATTParksolarpaneloutput.png/330px-ATTParksolarpaneloutput.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/ATTParksolarpaneloutput.png/440px-ATTParksolarpaneloutput.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ATTParksolarpaneloutput.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Daily solar output at
        <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Park" title="AT&amp;T Park">
         AT&amp;T Park
        </a>
        in San Francisco
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Solar power is more predictable than wind power and less variable – while there is never any solar power available during the night, and there is a reduction in winter, the only unknown factors in predicting solar output each day is cloud cover, frost and snow. Many days in a row in some locations are relatively cloud free, just as many days in a row in either the same or other locations are overcast – leading to relatively high predictability. Wind comes from the uneven heating of the earth's surface,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and can provide about 1% of the potential energy that is available from solar power. 86,000 TW of solar energy reaches the surface of the world vs. 870 TW in all of the world's winds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Total world demand is roughly 12 TW, many times less than the amount that could be generated from potential wind and solar resources. From 40 to 85 TW could be provided from wind and about 580 TW from solar.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wws_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wws-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_power">
       Tidal power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_renewable_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tidal power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tide_type.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="818" data-file-width="539" height="334" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Tide_type.svg/220px-Tide_type.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Tide_type.svg/330px-Tide_type.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Tide_type.svg/440px-Tide_type.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tide_type.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Types of tide
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">
       Tidal power
      </a>
      is the most predictable of all the variable renewable energy sources. Twice a day the tides come and go at close to the same level each day. It is estimated that Britain could obtain 20% of energy from tidal power, but there are only approximately 20 locations world wide where tidal power stations are practical.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity">
       Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_renewable_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In many European counties and North America the environmental movement has eliminated the construction of dams with large reservoirs.
      <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">
       Run of the river projects
      </a>
      have continued to be built, such as the 695MW Keeyask Project in Canada which began construction in 2014.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The absence of a reservoir results in both seasonal and annual variations in electricity generated.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Coping_with_variability">
       Coping with variability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_renewable_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coping with variability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Historically grid operators use day ahead forecasting to choose which power stations to make up demand each hour of the next day, and adjust this forecast at intervals as short as hourly or even every fifteen minutes to accommodate any changes. Normally as much as 100% demand is retained as spinning reserve that can be integrated quickly into the grid to make up for any power station failures or unexpected demand increases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A shift in thinking is needed in 2030 when almost all of your energy comes from non-dispatchable sources – how much wind or solar power is available depends on the weather conditions, and instead of turning on and off available sources becomes one of either storing or transmission of those sources to when they can be used or to where they can be used.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wws_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wws-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some excess available energy can be diverted to hydrogen production for use in ships and airplanes, a relatively long term energy storage, in a world where almost all of our energy comes from wind, water, and solar (WWS). Hydrogen is not an energy source, but is a storage medium. A cost analysis will need to be made between long distance transmission and excess capacity. The sun is always shining somewhere, and the wind is always blowing somewhere on the Earth, but is it cost effective to bring solar power from Australia to New York?
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wws_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-wws-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      If excess capacity is created, the cost is increased because not all of the available output is used. For example, ERCOT predicts that 8.7% of nameplate wind capacity will be reliably available in summer
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      – so if Texas, which has a peak summer demand of 68,379 MW
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      built wind farms of 786,000 MW (68,379/0.087), they would generate, at a 35%
      <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">
       capacity factor
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      2.4 million MWh per year – four times use, but might be sufficient to meet summer peaks. In practice it is likely that there are times with almost no wind in the entire region, making this not a practical solution. There were 54 days in 2002 when there was little wind power available in Denmark.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The estimated wind power installed capacity potential for Texas, using 100 meter wind turbines at 35% capacity factor, is 1,757,355.6 MW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In locations like
      <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">
       British Columbia
      </a>
      , with abundant water power resources, water power can always make up any shortfall in wind power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Wind and solar are somewhat complementary. A comparison of the output of the
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">
       solar panels
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">
       wind turbine
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Maritime_Academy" title="Massachusetts Maritime Academy">
       Massachusetts Maritime Academy
      </a>
      shows the effect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In winter there tends to be more wind and less solar, and in summer more solar and less wind, and during the day more solar and less wind. There is always no solar at night, and there is often more wind at night than during the day, so solar can be used somewhat to fill in the peak demand in the day, and wind can supply much of the demand during the night. There is however a substantial need for
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">
       storage
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
       transmission
      </a>
      to fill in the gaps between demand and supply.
     </p>
     <p>
      As physicist
      <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">
       Amory Lovins
      </a>
      has said:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The variability of sun, wind and so on, turns out to be a non-problem if you do several sensible things. One is to diversify your renewables by technology, so that weather conditions bad for one kind are good for another. Second, you diversify by site so they're not all subject to the same weather pattern at the same time because they're in the same place. Third, you use standard weather forecasting techniques to forecast wind, sun and rain, and of course hydro operators do this right now. Fourth, you integrate all your resources — supply side and demand side..."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The combination of diversifying variable renewables by type and location, forecasting their variation, and integrating them with despatchable renewables, flexible fueled generators, and
      <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">
       demand response
      </a>
      can create a power system that has the potential to meet our needs reliably. Integrating ever-higher levels of renewables is being successfully demonstrated in the real world:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovins11_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lovins11-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Variability_and_reliability">
       Variability and reliability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_renewable_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Variability and reliability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Mark A. Delucchi and
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Z._Jacobson" title="Mark Z. Jacobson">
       Mark Z. Jacobson
      </a>
      identify seven ways to design and operate variable renewable energy systems so that they will reliably satisfy electricity demand:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacob2010_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jacob2010-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <ul>
       <li>
        (A) interconnect geographically dispersed, naturally variable energy sources (e.g., wind, solar, wave, tidal), which smoothes out electricity supply (and demand) significantly.
       </li>
       <li>
        (B) use complementary and non-variable energy sources (such as hydroelectric power) to fill temporary gaps between demand and wind or solar generation.
       </li>
       <li>
        (C) use “smart” demand-response management to shift flexible loads to a time when more renewable energy is available.
       </li>
       <li>
        (D) store electric power, at the site of generation, (in batteries, hydrogen gas, molten salts, compressed air, pumped hydroelectric power, and flywheels), for later use.
       </li>
       <li>
        (E) over-size renewable peak generation capacity to minimize the times when available renewable power is less than demand and to provide spare power to produce hydrogen for flexible transportation and heat uses.
       </li>
       <li>
        (F) store electric power in electric-vehicle batteries, known as "vehicle to grid" or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V2G" title="V2G">
         V2G
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        (G) forecast the weather (winds, sunlight, waves, tides and precipitation) to better plan for energy supply needs.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacob2010_25-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-jacob2010-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Jacobson and Delucchi say that wind, water and solar power can be scaled up in cost-effective ways to meet our energy demands, freeing us from dependence on both fossil fuels and nuclear power. In 2009 they published “A Plan to Power 100 Percent of the Planet With Renewables” in
      <i>
       Scientific American
      </i>
      . A more detailed and updated technical analysis has been published as a two-part article in the refereed journal
      <i>
       Energy Policy
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Renewable energy is naturally replenished and renewable power technologies increase energy security because they reduce dependence on foreign sources of fuel. Unlike power stations relying on uranium and recycled plutonium for fuel, they are not subject to the volatility of global fuel markets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bks_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bks-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Renewable power decentralises electricity supply and so minimises the need to produce, transport and store hazardous fuels; reliability of power generation is improved by producing power close to the energy consumer. An accidental or intentional outage affects a smaller amount of capacity than an outage at a larger power station.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bks_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-bks-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Future_prospects">
       Future prospects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_renewable_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Future prospects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">
       International Energy Agency
      </a>
      says that there has been too much attention on issue of the variability of renewable electricity production.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-IEA-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The issue of
      <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source" title="Intermittent energy source">
       intermittent supply
      </a>
      applies to popular renewable technologies, mainly
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_photovoltaics" title="Solar photovoltaics">
       solar photovoltaics
      </a>
      , and its significance depends on a range of factors which include the market penetration of the renewables concerned, the balance of plant and the wider connectivity of the system, as well as the demand side flexibility. Variability will rarely be a barrier to increased renewable energy deployment when
      <a href="/wiki/Dispatchable_generation" title="Dispatchable generation">
       dispatchable generation
      </a>
      is also available. But at high levels of market penetration it requires careful analysis and management, and additional costs may be required for back-up or system modification.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-IEA-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Renewable electricity supply in the 20-50+% penetration range has already been implemented in several European systems, albeit in the context of an integrated European grid system:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovins11_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lovins11-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2011, the
      <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">
       Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change
      </a>
      , the world's leading climate researchers selected by the United Nations, said "as infrastructure and energy systems develop, in spite of the complexities, there are few, if any, fundamental technological limits to integrating a portfolio of renewable energy technologies to meet a majority share of total energy demand in locations where suitable renewable resources exist or can be supplied".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_2011_17_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IPCC_2011_17-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      IPCC scenarios "generally indicate that growth in renewable energy will be widespread around the world".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The IPCC said that if governments were supportive, and the full complement of renewable energy technologies were deployed, renewable energy supply could account for almost 80% of the world's energy use within forty years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipccccc_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ipccccc-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rajendra_Pachauri" title="Rajendra Pachauri">
       Rajendra Pachauri
      </a>
      , chairman of the IPCC, said the necessary investment in renewables would cost only about 1% of global GDP annually. This approach could contain greenhouse gas levels to less than 450 parts per million, the safe level beyond which climate change becomes catastrophic and irreversible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipccccc_30-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ipccccc-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
