     <p>
      <b>
       Building information modeling
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       BIM
      </b>
      ) is a process involving the generation and management of digital representations of physical and functional characteristics of places.
      <b>
       Building information models
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       BIMs
      </b>
      ) are files (often but not always in proprietary formats and containing proprietary data) which can be extracted, exchanged or networked to support decision-making regarding a building or other built asset. Current BIM software is used by individuals, businesses and government agencies who plan, design, construct, operate and maintain diverse physical infrastructures, such as water, refuse, electricity, gas, communication utilities, roads, bridges, ports, tunnels, etc.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="BIM_origins_and_elements">
       BIM origins and elements
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: BIM origins and elements">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The concept of BIM has existed since the 1970s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eastman1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eastman1-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eastman2_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eastman2-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term 'building model' (in the sense of BIM as used today) was first used in papers in the mid-1980s: in a 1985 paper by Simon Ruffle eventually published in 1986,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and later in a 1986 paper by Robert Aish
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      - then at
      <a href="/wiki/GMW_Architects" title="GMW Architects">
       GMW Computers Ltd
      </a>
      , developer of
      <a href="/wiki/RUCAPS" title="RUCAPS">
       RUCAPS
      </a>
      software - referring to the software's use at London's Heathrow Airport.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The term 'Building Information Model' first appeared in a 1992 paper by G.A. van Nederveen and F. P. Tolman.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the terms 'Building Information Model' and 'Building Information Modeling' (including the acronym "BIM") did not become popularly used until some 10 years later. In 2002,
      <a href="/wiki/Autodesk" title="Autodesk">
       Autodesk
      </a>
      released a
      <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">
       white paper
      </a>
      entitled "Building Information Modeling,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and other software vendors also started to assert their involvement in the field.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      By hosting contributions from Autodesk,
      <a href="/wiki/Bentley_Systems" title="Bentley Systems">
       Bentley Systems
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Graphisoft" title="Graphisoft">
       Graphisoft
      </a>
      , plus other industry observers, in 2003,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Jerry Laiserin helped popularize and standardize the term as a common name for the digital representation of the building process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Facilitating exchange and interoperability of information in digital format had previously been offered under differing terminology by Graphisoft as "Virtual Building", Bentley Systems as "Integrated Project Models", and by Autodesk or
      <a href="/wiki/Vectorworks" title="Vectorworks">
       Vectorworks
      </a>
      as "Building Information Modeling".
     </p>
     <p>
      As Graphisoft had been developing such solutions for longer than its competitors, Laiserin regarded its ArchiCAD as then "one of the most mature BIM solutions on the market"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      but also highlighted the pioneering role of applications such as RUCAPS,
      <a href="/wiki/Sonata_(building_design_software)" title="Sonata (building design software)">
       Sonata
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Reflex_(building_design_software)" title="Reflex (building design software)">
       Reflex
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (on 23 June 2016, the UK's
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Engineering" title="Royal Academy of Engineering">
       Royal Academy of Engineering
      </a>
      presented its Prince Philip Gold Medal to Jonathan Ingram, the developer of Sonata and Reflex, for his pioneering work on BIM).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAEng-Ingram_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-RAEng-Ingram-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Following its launch in 1987,
      <a href="/wiki/ArchiCAD" title="ArchiCAD">
       ArchiCAD
      </a>
      became regarded by some as the first implementation of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Building_Information_Modeling" title="Building Information Modeling">
       BIM
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Forbes-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      as it was the first CAD product on a personal computer able to create both 2D and 3D geometry, as well as the first commercial BIM product for personal computers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Forbes-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quirk_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Quirk-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The US National Building Information Model Standard Project Committee has the following definition:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Building Information Modeling (BIM) is a digital representation of physical and functional characteristics of a facility. A BIM is a shared knowledge resource for information about a facility forming a reliable basis for decisions during its life-cycle; defined as existing from earliest conception to demolition.
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Traditional building design was largely reliant upon two-dimensional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technical_drawings" title="Technical drawings">
       technical drawings
      </a>
      (plans, elevations, sections, etc.). Building information modeling extends this beyond
      <a href="/wiki/3D_modeling" title="3D modeling">
       3D
      </a>
      , augmenting the three primary spatial dimensions (width, height and depth) with time as the fourth dimension (
      <a href="/wiki/4D_BIM" title="4D BIM">
       4D
      </a>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4DBIM-R-01_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-4DBIM-R-01-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and cost as the fifth (
      <a href="/wiki/5D_BIM" title="5D BIM">
       5D
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4DBIM-R-02_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-4DBIM-R-02-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      BIM therefore covers more than just geometry. It also covers spatial relationships, light analysis, geographic information, and quantities and properties of building components (for example, manufacturers' details).
     </p>
     <p>
      BIM involves representing a design as combinations of "objects" – vague and undefined, generic or product-specific, solid shapes or void-space oriented (like the shape of a room), that carry their geometry, relations and attributes. BIM design tools allow extraction of different views from a building model for drawing production and other uses. These different views are automatically consistent, being based on a single definition of each object instance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eastman.2C_C_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eastman.2C_C-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      BIM software also defines objects parametrically; that is, the objects are defined as parameters and relations to other objects, so that if a related object is amended, dependent ones will automatically also change.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eastman.2C_C_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eastman.2C_C-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Each model element can carry attributes for selecting and ordering them automatically, providing cost estimates as well as material tracking and ordering.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eastman.2C_C_22-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eastman.2C_C-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For the professionals involved in a project, BIM enables a virtual information model to be handed from the design team (
      <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">
       architects
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architect" title="Landscape architect">
       landscape architects
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">
       surveyors
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">
       civil
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineering" title="Structural engineering">
       structural
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Building_services" title="Building services">
       building services
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">
       engineers
      </a>
      , etc.) to the main contractor and subcontractors and then on to the owner/operator; each professional adds discipline-specific data to the single shared model. This reduces information losses that traditionally occurred when a new team takes 'ownership' of the project, and provides more extensive information to owners of complex structures.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="BIM_throughout_the_project_life-cycle">
       BIM throughout the project life-cycle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: BIM throughout the project life-cycle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Use of BIM goes beyond the planning and design phase of the project, extending throughout the building life cycle, supporting processes including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost_management" title="Cost management">
       cost management
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Construction_management" title="Construction management">
       construction management
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">
       project management
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facilities_management" title="Facilities management">
       facility operation
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Management_of_building_information_models">
       Management of building information models
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Management of building information models">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Building information models span the whole concept-to-occupation time-span. To ensure efficient management of information processes throughout this span, a BIM manager (also sometimes defined as a
      <a href="/wiki/Virtual_design_and_construction" title="Virtual design and construction">
       virtual design-to-construction
      </a>
      , VDC, project manager – VDCPM) might be appointed. The BIM manager is retained by a design build team on the client's behalf from the pre-design phase onwards to develop and to track the object-oriented BIM against predicted and measured performance objectives, supporting multi-disciplinary building information models that drive analysis, schedules, take-off and logistics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Companies are also now considering developing BIMs in various levels of detail, since depending on the application of BIM, more or less detail is needed, and there is varying modeling effort associated with generating building information models at different levels of detail.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="BIM_in_construction_management">
       BIM in construction management
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: BIM in construction management">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Participants in the building process is constantly challenged to deliver successful projects despite tight budgets, limited manpower, accelerated schedules, and limited or conflicting information. The significant disciplines such as
      <a href="/wiki/Architectural_engineering" title="Architectural engineering">
       architectural
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineering" title="Structural engineering">
       structural
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mechanical,_electrical,_and_plumbing" title="Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing">
       MEP
      </a>
      designs should be well coordinated, as two things can’t take place at the same place and time. Building Information Modeling aids in collision detection at the initial stage, identifying the exact location of discrepancies.
     </p>
     <p>
      The BIM concept envisages virtual construction of a facility prior to its actual physical construction, in order to reduce uncertainty, improve safety, work out problems, and simulate and analyze potential impacts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2007_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Smith_2007-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sub-contractors from every trade can input critical information into the model before beginning construction, with opportunities to pre-fabricate or pre-assemble some systems off-site. Waste can be minimised on-site and products delivered on a just-in-time basis rather than being stock-piled on-site.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2007_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Smith_2007-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Quantities and shared properties of materials can be extracted easily. Scopes of work can be isolated and defined. Systems, assemblies and sequences can be shown in a relative scale with the entire facility or group of facilities. BIM also prevents errors by enabling conflict or 'clash detection' whereby the computer model visually highlights to the team where parts of the building (e.g.: structural frame and building services pipes or ducts) may wrongly intersect.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="BIM_in_facility_operation">
       BIM in facility operation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: BIM in facility operation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      BIM can bridge the information loss associated with handling a project from design team, to construction team and to building owner/operator, by allowing each group to add to and reference back to all information they acquire during their period of contribution to the BIM model. This can yield benefits to the facility owner or operator.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example, a building owner may find evidence of a leak in his building. Rather than exploring the physical building, he may turn to the model and see that water
      <a href="/wiki/Valve" title="Valve">
       valve
      </a>
      is located in the suspect location. He could also have in the model the specific valve size, manufacturer, part number, and any other information ever researched in the past, pending adequate computing power. Such problems were initially addressed by Leite and Akinci when developing a vulnerability representation of facility contents and threats for supporting the identification of vulnerabilities in building emergencies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dynamic information about the building, such as sensor measurements and control signals from the building systems, can also be incorporated within BIM software to support analysis of building operation and maintenance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="BIM_in_land_administration_and_cadastre">
       BIM in land administration and cadastre
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: BIM in land administration and cadastre">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      BIM can potentially offer some benefits for managing stratified
      <a href="/wiki/Cadastre" title="Cadastre">
       cadastral
      </a>
      spaces in urban built environments. The first benefit would be enhancing visual communication of interweaved, stacked and complex cadastral spaces for non-specialists. The rich amount of spatial and semantic information about physical structures inside models can aid comprehension of cadastral boundaries, providing an unambiguous delineation of ownership, rights, responsibilities and restrictions. Additionally, using BIM to manage cadastral information could advance current land administration systems from a 2D-based and analogue data environment into a 3D digital, intelligent, interactive and dynamic one.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      BIM could also unlock value in the cadastral information by forming a bridge between that information and the interactive lifecycle and management of buildings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="BIM_software">
       BIM software
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: BIM software">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The first software tools developed for modelling buildings emerged in the late 1970s and early 1980s, and included workstation products such as Chuck Eastman's Building Description System and GLIDE,
      <a href="/wiki/RUCAPS" title="RUCAPS">
       RUCAPS
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sonata_(building_design_software)" title="Sonata (building design software)">
       Sonata
      </a>
      and Reflex. The early applications, and the hardware needed to run them, were expensive, which limited widespread adoption. ArchiCAD's Radar CH, released in 1984 was the first modelling software made available on a personal computer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quirk_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Quirk-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to the complexity of gathering all the relevant information when working with BIM on a building project some companies have developed software designed specifically to work in a BIM framework. These packages (e.g.:
      <a href="/wiki/Bentley_Systems" title="Bentley Systems">
       Bentley
      </a>
      AECOsim Building Designer,
      <a href="/wiki/ArchiCAD" title="ArchiCAD">
       ArchiCAD
      </a>
      , MagiCAD,
      <a href="/wiki/Tekla_Structures" title="Tekla Structures">
       Tekla Structures
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Autodesk_Revit" title="Autodesk Revit">
       Autodesk Revit
      </a>
      , Synchro PRO,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VectorWorks" title="VectorWorks">
       VectorWorks
      </a>
      , Trimble SketchUp) differ from architectural drafting tools such as
      <a href="/wiki/AutoCAD" title="AutoCAD">
       AutoCAD
      </a>
      by allowing the addition of further information (time, cost, manufacturers' details, sustainability and maintenance information, etc.) to the building model.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a BIM module in open source CAD software
      <a href="/wiki/FreeCAD" title="FreeCAD">
       FreeCAD
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-proprietary_or_open_BIM_standards">
       Non-proprietary or open BIM standards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-proprietary or open BIM standards">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      BIM is often associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Industry_Foundation_Classes" title="Industry Foundation Classes">
       Industry Foundation Classes
      </a>
      (IFCs) and
      <a href="/wiki/AecXML" title="AecXML">
       aecXML
      </a>
      – data structures for representing information. IFCs have been developed by
      <a href="/wiki/BuildingSMART" title="BuildingSMART">
       buildingSMART
      </a>
      (the former International Alliance for Interoperability), as a neutral, non-proprietary or open standard for sharing BIM data among different software applications (some proprietary data structures have been developed by CAD vendors incorporating BIM into their software).
     </p>
     <p>
      Poor software interoperability has long been regarded as an obstacle to industry efficiency in general and to BIM adoption in particular. In August 2004 a US National Institute of Standards and Technology (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIST" title="NIST">
       NIST
      </a>
      ) report
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      conservatively estimated that $15.8 billion was lost annually by the U.S. capital facilities industry due to inadequate interoperability arising from "the highly fragmented nature of the industry, the industry’s continued paperbased business practices, a lack of standardization, and inconsistent technology adoption among stakeholders".
     </p>
     <p>
      An early example of a nationally approved BIM standard is the AISC (
      <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Steel_Construction" title="American Institute of Steel Construction">
       American Institute of Steel Construction
      </a>
      )-approved CIS/2 standard, a non-proprietary standard with its roots in the UK.
     </p>
     <p>
      There have been attempts at creating a BIM for older, pre-existing facilities. They generally reference key metrics such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facility_Condition_Index" title="Facility Condition Index">
       Facility Condition Index
      </a>
      (FCI). The validity of these models will need to be monitored over time, because trying to model a building constructed in, say 1927, requires numerous assumptions about design standards, building codes, construction methods, materials, etc., and therefore is far more complex than building a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BIM" title="BIM">
       BIM
      </a>
      at time of initial design.
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="India">
       India
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: India">

       </span>

     <p>
      In India BIM is also known as VDC:
      <b>
       v
      </b>
      irtual
      <b>
       d
      </b>
      esign and
      <b>
       c
      </b>
      onstruction. India is an emerging market with an expanding construction market and huge potential for large scale residential and commercial development (because of population and economic growth). It has many qualified, trained and experienced BIM professionals who are implementing this technology in Indian construction projects and also assisting teams in the USA, Australia, UK, middle east, Singapore and North Africa to design and deliver construction projects using BIM. In spite of this, BIM usage was reported by only 22% of respondents to a 2014 survey.

        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">
       Iran
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Canada">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Several organizations support BIM adoption and implementation in Canada: the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canada_BIM_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canada BIM Council (page does not exist)">
       Canada BIM Council
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_BIM_Council_61-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Canada_BIM_Council-61">
        [61]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (CANBIM), the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_for_BIM_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute for BIM in Canada (page does not exist)">
       Institute for BIM in Canada
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=BuildingSMART_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="BuildingSMART Canada (page does not exist)">
       buildingSMART Canada
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Founded in December 2008, CANBIM is a consensus- and committee-driven organization for BIM in Canada developed by business leaders to standardize the use of models in architecture, engineering and construction. CanBIM has close to 100 architectural, engineering, contracting and trade firms, and is managed by industry volunteers, hosting events across Canada. Members fund and direct the priorities and activities through eight discipline focused committees.
     </p>
     <p>
      The mission of the IBC is: “to lead and facilitate the coordinated use of Building Information Modeling (BIM) in the design, construction and management of the Canadian built environment.” Its founding partner organizations represent specific industry sectors with keen interest in seeing BIM implemented in a way, and at a pace, that enables the primary stakeholders to understand their roles and responsibilities and to assess their capacity to participate in this process.
     </p>
     <p>
      buildingSMART Canada, the Canadian chapter of
      <a href="/wiki/BuildingSMART" title="BuildingSMART">
       buildingSMART
      </a>
      International, works in partnership with all Canadian AECOO community stakeholders including Canadian associations of architects, engineers, specification writers, contractors as well as public and private owners, government and industry. It creates standards and supports programmes and tools to ensure that Canada will be successful in its movement towards a better built environment supported through open and internationally compatible standards for BIM.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_of_America">
       United States of America
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: United States of America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Associated_General_Contractors_of_America" title="Associated General Contractors of America">
       Associated General Contractors of America
      </a>
      and U.S. contracting firms have developed various working definitions of BIM that describe it generally as:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        an object-oriented building development tool that utilizes 5-D modeling concepts, information technology and software interoperability to design, construct and operate a building project, as well as communicate its details.
       </i>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Although the concept of BIM and relevant processes are being explored by contractors, architects and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_estate_developers" title="Real estate developers">
       developers
      </a>
      alike, the term itself has been questioned and debated
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        [64]
       </a>
      </sup>
      with alternatives including
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_Building_Environment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Virtual Building Environment (page does not exist)">
       Virtual Building Environment
      </a>
      (VBE) and virtual design and construction (VDC) also considered.
     </p>
     <p>
      BIM is seen to be closely related to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Project_Delivery" title="Integrated Project Delivery">
       Integrated Project Delivery
      </a>
      (IPD) where the primary motive is to bring the teams together early on in the project.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        [65]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A full implementation of BIM also requires the project teams to collaborate from the inception stage and formulate model sharing and ownership contract documents.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Architects" title="American Institute of Architects">
       American Institute of Architects
      </a>
      has defined BIM as "a model-based technology linked with a database of project information",
      <sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_definition">
       <a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_information_modeling#endnote_definition">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and this reflects the general reliance on database technology as the foundation. In the future, structured text documents such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specifications" title="Specifications">
       specifications
      </a>
      may be able to be searched and linked to regional, national, and international standards.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">
       Africa
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Africa">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">
       Nigeria
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Nigeria">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      BIM has the potential to play a vital role in the Nigerian AEC sector. In addition to its potential clarity and transparency it may help promote standardization across the industry. For instance, Utiome
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        [66]
       </a>
      </sup>
      suggests that, in conceptualizing a BIM-based knowledge transfer framework from industrialized economies to urban construction projects in developing nations, generic BIM objects can benefit from rich building information within specification parameters in product libraries, and used for efficient, streamlined design and construction. Similarly, an assessment of the current 'state of the art' by Kori
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        [67]
       </a>
      </sup>
      found that medium and large firms were leading the adoption of BIM in the industry. Smaller firms were less advanced with respect to process and policy adherence.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">
       South Africa
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: South Africa">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The South African BIM Institute, established in May 2015, aims to enable technical experts to discuss digital construction solutions that can be adopted by professionals working within the construction sector. Its initial task was to promote the SA BIM Protocol.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are currently no mandated or national best practice BIM standards or protocols in South Africa. Organisations implement company-specific BIM standards and protocols at best (there are isolated examples of cross-industry alliances); these company-specific standards may be difficult to share or replicate and so hinder design collaboration. Furthermore, there is little interest from local government agencies, professional bodies and voluntary organisations to drive the development of national BIM standards and protocols, despite a vibrant client base. South Africa thus lags behind other countries in BIM implementation and practice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        [68]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">
       Oceania
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Oceania">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">
       Australia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Australia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In February 2016, Infrastructure Australia recommended: "Governments should make the use of Building Information Modelling (BIM) mandatory for the design of large-scale complex infrastructure projects. In support of a mandatory rollout, the Australian Government should commission the Australasian Procurement and Construction Council, working with industry, to develop appropriate guidance around the adoption and use of BIM; and common standards and protocols to be applied when using BIM.”
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIMCrunchFeb2016_69-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BIMCrunchFeb2016-69">
        [69]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: New Zealand">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In 2015, many projects in the rebuilding of
      <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">
       Christchurch
      </a>
      were being assembled in detail on a computer using BIM well before workers set foot on the site. The New Zealand government started a BIM acceleration committee, as part of a productivity partnership with the goal of 20 per cent more efficiency in the construction industry by 2020.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        [70]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Anticipated_future_potential">
       Anticipated future potential
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_information_modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Anticipated future potential">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      BIM is a relatively new technology in an industry typically slow to adopt change. Yet many early adopters are confident that BIM will grow to play an even more crucial role in building documentation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Proponents claim that BIM offers:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Improved visualization
      </li>
      <li>
       Improved productivity due to easy retrieval of information
      </li>
      <li>
       Increased coordination of construction documents
      </li>
      <li>
       Embedding and linking of vital information such as vendors for specific materials, location of details and quantities required for estimation and tendering
      </li>
      <li>
       Increased speed of delivery
      </li>
      <li>
       Reduced costs
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      BIM also contains most of the data needed for
      <a href="/wiki/Building_energy_simulation" title="Building energy simulation">
       building energy performance
      </a>
      analysis. The building properties in BIM can be used to automatically create the input file for building energy simulation and save a significant amount of time and effort.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        [71]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Moreover, automation of this process reduce errors and mismatches in the building energy simulation process.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Green_Building_XML" title="Green Building XML">
       Green Building XML
      </a>
      (gbXML) is an emerging schema, a subset of the Building Information Modeling efforts, focused on green building design and operation. gbXML is used as input in several energy simulation engines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        [72]
       </a>
      </sup>
      With the development of modern computer technology, a large number of building energy simulation tools are available. When choosing which simulation tool to use, the user must consider the tool's accuracy and reliability, considering the building information they have at hand, which will serve as input for the tool. Yezioro, Dong and Leite
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        [73]
       </a>
      </sup>
      developed an artificial intelligence approach towards assessing building performance simulation results and found that more detailed simulation tools have the best simulation performance in terms of heating and cooling electricity consumption within 3% of mean absolute error.
     </p>
     <p>
      Explorations are underway to pair computer network users' personal, private and public authentication choices, geographic mapping systems and evolving cloud computing security architecture models, together, to offer customers of geospatial securitization services intuitive new ways to organize their personal, private and public applications and storage. For individuals, businesses and government authorities who generate and manage building information, new ways to discover, share and work on data, within the context of particular places on earth, will be offered. David Plager, AIA, conjectures that today's web will give way to tomorrow's geo-web where data will be structured first by place (e.g. a postal address) and then by space (Personal (one user), Private (a group of users) and Public (all users)).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
