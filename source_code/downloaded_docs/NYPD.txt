     <p>
      The
      <b>
       New York City Police Department
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       NYPD
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       NYCPD
      </b>
      ), officially the
      <b>
       City of New York Police Department
      </b>
      , is the largest
      <a href="/wiki/Municipal_police" title="Municipal police">
       municipal police
      </a>
      force in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Established in 1845, the agency has primary responsibilities in law enforcement and investigation within the five
      <a href="/wiki/Borough_(New_York_City)" title="Borough (New York City)">
       boroughs
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      . The NYPD is one of the oldest police departments established in the U.S., tracing its roots back to the seventeenth century.
     </p>
     <p>
      The NYPD has a broad array of specialized services, including the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Emergency_Service_Unit" title="New York City Police Department Emergency Service Unit">
       Emergency Service Unit
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Police_dog" title="Police dog">
       K9
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_the_New_York_City_Police_Department#Harbor_Unit_and_Scuba_Team" title="Organization of the New York City Police Department">
       harbor patrol
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_support" title="Air support">
       air support
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bomb_disposal" title="Bomb disposal">
       bomb disposal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">
       counter-terrorism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Criminal_intelligence" title="Criminal intelligence">
       criminal intelligence
      </a>
      , anti-
      <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">
       gang
      </a>
      , anti-
      <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">
       organized crime
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Narcotic" title="Narcotic">
       narcotics
      </a>
      , public transportation and
      <a href="/wiki/Public_housing" title="Public housing">
       public housing
      </a>
      ; the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Transit_Police" title="New York City Transit Police">
       New York City Transit Police
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Housing_Authority_Police_Department" title="New York City Housing Authority Police Department">
       New York City Housing Authority Police Department
      </a>
      were fully integrated into the NYPD in 1995. According to the department, its mission is to "enforce the
      <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">
       laws
      </a>
      , preserve the peace, reduce fear, and provide for a safe environment." The department's regulations are compiled in title 38 of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Rules" title="New York City Rules">
        New York City Rules
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 2004, there were about 40,000 sworn officers plus several thousand civilian employees; in June 2005, the number of officers dropped to 35,000. As of December 2011, that figure increased slightly to over 36,600, helped by the graduation of a class of 1,500 from the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Academy" title="New York City Police Academy">
       New York City Police Academy
      </a>
      . The NYPD's current authorized uniformed strength is 34,450.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are also approximately 4,500
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Auxiliary_Police" title="New York City Police Department Auxiliary Police">
       Auxiliary Police Officers
      </a>
      , 5,000
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_School_Safety_Division" title="New York City Police Department School Safety Division">
       School Safety Agents
      </a>
      , 2,300 Traffic Enforcement Agents, and 370 Traffic Enforcement Supervisors currently employed by the department. The
      <a href="/wiki/Patrolmen%27s_Benevolent_Association_of_the_City_of_New_York" title="Patrolmen's Benevolent Association of the City of New York">
       Patrolmen's Benevolent Association of the City of New York
      </a>
      (NYC PBA), the largest municipal police union in the United States, represents over 50,000 active and retired NYC police officers.
     </p>
     <p>
      The NYPD Intelligence Division &amp; Counter-Terrorism Bureau has officers stationed in 11 cities internationally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the 1990s the department developed a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/CompStat" title="CompStat">
        CompStat
       </a>
      </i>
      system of management which has also since been established in other cities.
     </p>
     <p>
      Members of the NYPD are frequently referred to by politicians, some media and their own police cars by the nickname
      <i>
       New York's Finest
      </i>
      . The NYPD is headquartered at
      <a href="/wiki/1_Police_Plaza" title="1 Police Plaza">
       1 Police Plaza
      </a>
      , located on
      <a href="/wiki/Park_Row_(Manhattan)" title="Park Row (Manhattan)">
       Park Row
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">
       Lower Manhattan
      </a>
      across the street from
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Hall" title="New York City Hall">
       City Hall
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      NYPD has extensive
      <a href="/wiki/Crime_scene" title="Crime scene">
       crime scene
      </a>
      investigation and laboratory resources, as well as units which assist with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_crime" title="Computer crime">
       computer crime
      </a>
      investigations. The NYPD runs a "Real Time Crime Center", essentially a large search engine and data warehouse operated by detectives to assist officers in the field with their investigations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Domain_Awareness_System" title="Domain Awareness System">
       Domain Awareness System
      </a>
      , a joint project of
      <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">
       Microsoft
      </a>
      and NYPD, links 6,000
      <a href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_television_camera" title="Closed-circuit television camera">
       closed-circuit television cameras
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Automatic_number_plate_recognition" title="Automatic number plate recognition">
       license plate readers
      </a>
      , and other surveillance devices into an integrated system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to its high-profile location in the largest city and media center in the United States, fictionalized versions of the NYPD and its officers have frequently been portrayed in novels, radio, television, motion pictures and video games.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_New_York_City_Police_Department" title="History of the New York City Police Department">
       History of the New York City Police Department
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mounted_Police_Squad_on_Parade_(front).tif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2133" data-file-width="3321" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Mounted_Police_Squad_on_Parade_%28front%29.tif/lossy-page1-200px-Mounted_Police_Squad_on_Parade_%28front%29.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Mounted_Police_Squad_on_Parade_%28front%29.tif/lossy-page1-300px-Mounted_Police_Squad_on_Parade_%28front%29.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Mounted_Police_Squad_on_Parade_%28front%29.tif/lossy-page1-400px-Mounted_Police_Squad_on_Parade_%28front%29.tif.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mounted_Police_Squad_on_Parade_(front).tif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Mounted Police Squad on Parade circa 1910
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Municipal Police were established in 1845, replacing an old
      <a href="/wiki/Watchman_(law_enforcement)" title="Watchman (law enforcement)">
       night watch
      </a>
      system. In 1857, it was tumultuously replaced by a Metropolitan force, which consolidated many other local police departments in 1898. Twentieth-century trends included professionalization and struggles against corruption.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rank_structure">
       Rank structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rank structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      This section discusses ranks of the NYPD as a whole. The
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_School_Safety_Division" title="New York City Police Department School Safety Division">
       School Safety Division
      </a>
      , Traffic Control Division,
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Auxiliary_Police#Ranks" title="New York City Police Department Auxiliary Police">
       Auxiliary Police Section
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Academy" title="New York City Police Academy">
       Police Academy
      </a>
      have their own rank and grade structures despite being part of the NYPD and commanded by officers of the NYPD proper command structure.
     </div>
     <p>
      Officers begin service with the rank of
      <i>
       Probationary Police Officer
      </i>
      , also referred to as
      <i>
       Recruit Officer
      </i>
      . After successful completion of six months of Police Academy training and various academic, physical, and tactical tests, officers graduate from the Police Academy. While officially retaining the title of
      <i>
       Probationary Police Officer
      </i>
      , graduates are referred to as a
      <i>
       Police Officer
      </i>
      , or informally as a "Rookie", until they have completed an additional 18 months probationary period.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are three career "tracks" in the NYPD: supervisory, investigative, and specialist. The supervisory track consists of 12 sworn titles, referred to as ranks. Promotion to the ranks of sergeant, lieutenant and
      <a href="/wiki/Police_captain" title="Police captain">
       captain
      </a>
      are made via competitive
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_service" title="Civil service">
       civil service
      </a>
      examinations. Promotion to the ranks of deputy inspector, inspector, deputy chief, assistant chief and chief are made at the discretion of the police commissioner, after successfully passing a series of civil service exams. Promotion from the rank of police officer to
      <a href="/wiki/Detective" title="Detective">
       detective
      </a>
      is determined by the current police labor contract, with the approval of the commissioner. The entry level appointment to detective is third grade or specialist. The commissioner may grant discretionary grades of first or second. These grades offer compensation roughly equivalent to that of supervisors. Specifically, a second grade detective's pay roughly corresponds to a sergeant's and a first grade detective's pay roughly corresponds to a lieutenant's. Detectives are police officers who have been given a more investigatory position but no official supervisory authority. A
      <i>
       Detective First Grade
      </i>
      still falls under the command of a sergeant or above. Just like detectives, sergeants and lieutenants can receive pay grade increases within their respective ranks.
     </p>

     <p>
      There are two basic types of
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Detective_Bureau" title="New York City Police Department Detective Bureau">
       detective in the NYPD
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       detective-investigators
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       detective-specialists
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Detective-Investigators are the type most people associate with the term "detective" and are the ones most frequently portrayed on television and in the movies. Most police officers gain their detective title by working in the Narcotics Division of the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Organized_Crime_Control_Bureau" title="New York City Police Department Organized Crime Control Bureau">
       Organized Crime Control Bureau
      </a>
      and are then moved to the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Detective_Bureau" title="New York City Police Department Detective Bureau">
       Detective Bureau
      </a>
      . Detectives assigned to squads are co-located within each precinct and are responsible for investigating
      <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">
       murders
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">
       rapes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Robbery" title="Robbery">
       robberies
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">
       burglaries
      </a>
      and other crimes within that precinct's boundaries. Other detective-investigators are assigned to specialized units at either the major command or citywide level, investigating
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorist_group" title="Terrorist group">
       terrorist groups
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">
       organized crime
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_dealing" title="Drug dealing">
       narcotics dealing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Extortion" title="Extortion">
       extortion
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bias_crime" title="Bias crime">
       bias crimes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Political_corruption" title="Political corruption">
       political corruption
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">
       kidnappings
      </a>
      , major
      <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">
       frauds
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">
       thefts
      </a>
      committed against banks or museums,
      <a href="/wiki/Police_corruption" title="Police corruption">
       police corruption
      </a>
      , contractor fraud and other complex, politically sensitive or high-profile cases. A squad of detective-investigators is also assigned to each of the city's five
      <a href="/wiki/District_attorney" title="District attorney">
       district attorneys'
      </a>
      offices. (Arsons are investigated by The Arson and Explosion Squad as well as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_City_fire_marshals" title="New York City fire marshals">
       fire marshals
      </a>
      , who are part of the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Fire_Department" title="New York City Fire Department">
       New York City Fire Department
      </a>
      .)
     </p>
     <p>
      Promotion from
      <i>
       Police Officer
      </i>
      to
      <i>
       Detective-Investigator
      </i>
      is based on investigative experience. Typically, a Police Officer who is assigned to investigative work for 18 months will be designated "Detective-Investigator" and receive the gold shield and pay increase commensurate with that designation. In the recent past, however, there has been controversy over the budget-conscious department compelling police officers to work past the 18 months without receiving the new title.
     </p>
     <p>
      Newly appointed detectives start at
      <i>
       Detective Third Grade
      </i>
      , which has a pay rate roughly between that of
      <i>
       Police Officer
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Sergeant
      </i>
      . As they gain seniority and experience, they can be "promoted" to
      <i>
       Detective Second-Grade
      </i>
      , which has a pay grade slightly less than sergeants.
      <i>
       Detective First-Grade
      </i>
      is an elite designation for the department's most senior and experienced investigators and carries a pay grade slightly less than Lieutenants. All these promotions are discretionary on the part of the Commissioner and can be revoked if warranted. And while senior detectives can give directions to junior detectives in their own squads, not even the most senior detective can lawfully issue orders to even a junior patrol officer. All Detective grades still fall under the "chain of command" of the supervisory ranks beginning with
      <i>
       Sergeant
      </i>
      through
      <i>
       Chief of Department
      </i>
      . Detectives, like Police Officers, are eligible to take the promotional civil service exams for entry into the supervisory ranks.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:5.29.10NYPDByLuigiNovi6.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/5.29.10NYPDByLuigiNovi6.jpg/220px-5.29.10NYPDByLuigiNovi6.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/5.29.10NYPDByLuigiNovi6.jpg/330px-5.29.10NYPDByLuigiNovi6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/5.29.10NYPDByLuigiNovi6.jpg/440px-5.29.10NYPDByLuigiNovi6.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

     <p>
      While carrying with them increased pay and prestige, none of these Detective grades confer on the holder any supervisory authority. Contrary to some media portrayals, there is no specific rank of "Detective Sergeant" or "Detective Lieutenant". Lieutenants and Sergeants are assigned to oversee Detective squads as Supervisors, and are responsible for all investigations.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a small percentage of Lieutenants and Sergeants who work as Investigative Supervisors (approximately equal to 10% of their respective ranks) and are granted the prestigious pay grade designations of "Sergeant—Supervisor Detective Squad" (SDS), or Lieutenant—Commander Detective Squad (CDS) therefore assuming full Investigative command responsibility as opposed to operational supervision. Their pay grade rises to an approximate midpoint between their normal rank and the next highest rank's pay grade, and similar to a Detective's "grade", is also a discretionary promotion. This pay grade designation is achieved by assignment to Investigative units, i.e.
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Detective_Bureau" title="New York City Police Department Detective Bureau">
       Detective Bureau
      </a>
      , Internal Affairs Bureau, Counter-Terrorism Bureau, Intelligence Bureau, and
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Organized_Crime_Control_Bureau" title="New York City Police Department Organized Crime Control Bureau">
       Organized Crime Control Bureau
      </a>
      . Lieutenants and Sergeants in non-investigatory assignments can be designated Lieutenant-Special Assignment or Sergeant-Special Assignment, pay equivalent to their investigative counterparts.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Detective-specialists" are a relatively new designation and one unique to the NYPD. In the 1980s, many detectives resented that some officers were being granted the rank of detective in order to give them increased pay and status, but were not being assigned to investigative duties. Examples included officers assigned as bodyguards and drivers to the mayor, police commissioner and other senior officials.
     </p>
     <p>
      To remedy this situation, the rank of
      <i>
       detective-specialist
      </i>
      was created. These officers are typically found in specialized units because they possess a unique or esoteric skill the department needs, e.g., crime-scene tech, sharpshooter, bomb technician, scuba instructor, helicopter instructor, sketch artist, etc. Like detective-investigators, detective-specialists start at third-grade and can be promoted to second- or first-grade status.
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NYPD_police_motorcycle.png">

     <p>
      The Department is administered and governed by the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Commissioner" title="New York City Police Commissioner">
       Police Commissioner
      </a>
      , who is appointed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_New_York_City" title="Mayor of New York City">
       Mayor
      </a>
      . Technically, the commissioner serves a five-year term; as a practical matter, the commissioner serves at the Mayor's pleasure. The commissioner in turn appoints numerous deputy commissioners. The commissioner and his subordinate deputies are civilians under an oath of office and are not uniformed members of the force who are sworn officers of the law. However, a police commissioner who comes up from the uniformed ranks retains that status while serving as police commissioner. This has ramifications for their police pensions and the fact that any police commissioner who is considered sworn does not need a pistol permit to carry a firearm, and does retain the statutory powers of a police officer. Some police commissioners (like Ray Kelly) do carry a personal firearm, but they also have a full-time security detail from the Police Commissioner's (Detective) Squad.
     </p>
     <p>
      A First Deputy Police Commissioner may have a security detail when he/she acts as commissioner or under other circumstances as approved by the police commissioner.
     </p>

     <p>
      These individuals are administrators who supersede the Chief of Department, and they usually specialize in areas of great importance to the Department, such as counterterrorism, operations, training, public information, legal matters, intelligence, and information technology. Despite their role, as civilian administrators of the Department, deputy commissioners are prohibited from taking operational control of a police situation (the Commissioner and the First Deputy Commissioner may take control of these situations, however).
     </p>
     <p>
      Within the rank structure, there are also designations, known as "grades", that connote differences in duties, experience, and pay. However, supervisory functions are generally reserved for the rank of sergeant and above.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Badge" title="Badge">
       Badges
      </a>
      in the New York City Police Department are referred to as "shields" (the traditional term), though not all badge designs are strictly
      <a href="/wiki/Escutcheon_(heraldry)" title="Escutcheon (heraldry)">
       shield-shaped
      </a>
      . Every rank has a different badge design (with the exception of
      <i>
       Police Officer
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Probationary Police Officer
      </i>
      ), and upon change in rank officers receive a new badge. Lower-ranked police officers are identified by their shield numbers, and tax registry number. Lieutenants and above do not have shield numbers and are identified by tax registry number. All sworn members of the NYPD have their ID card photos taken against a red background. Civilian employees of the NYPD have their ID card photos taken against a blue background, signifying that they are not commissioned to carry a firearm. All ID cards have an expiration date.
     </p>

     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_of_Police" title="Chief of Police">
       Chief of Department
      </a>
      serves as the senior sworn member of the NYPD.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Carlos M. Gomez is the 39th individual to hold the post, which prior to 1973 was known as the Chief of Operations and before that as Chief Inspector.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bureaus">
       Bureaus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bureaus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Department is divided into ten bureaus, six of which are enforcement bureaus. Each enforcement bureau is sub-divided into sections, divisions, and units, and into patrol boroughs, precincts, and detective squads. Each Bureau is commanded by a Bureau Chief (such as the Chief of Patrol and the Chief of Housing). There are also a number of specialized units (such as the Operations Unit and Compstat) that are not part of any of the Bureaus and report to the Chief of the Department.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Domain_Awareness_System">
       Domain Awareness System
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Domain Awareness System">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In August 2008, the
      <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan_Security_Initiative" title="Lower Manhattan Security Initiative">
       Lower Manhattan Security Initiative
      </a>
      in a partnership between the New York City Police Department and
      <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">
       Microsoft
      </a>
      began the
      <a href="/wiki/Domain_Awareness_System" title="Domain Awareness System">
       Domain Awareness System
      </a>
      to monitor
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The program allowed the department to track surveillance targets and gain detailed information about them. The system is connected to 6,000
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_television#United_States" title="Closed-circuit television">
       video cameras
      </a>
      around New York City as well as check
      <a href="/wiki/Particle_detector" title="Particle detector">
       radiological
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_detection" title="Nuclear detection">
       nuclear detectors
      </a>
      onboard helicopters, trucks and boats as well as detectors on police officers' gun belts that were so sensitive that people who have had medical procedures may trigger them.
      <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">
       Lower Manhattan
      </a>
      now includes thousands of surveillance cameras that can identify shapes and sizes of unidentified "suspicious" packages and can track people within seconds using descriptions such as "someone wearing a red shirt". In 2009, an extension into
      <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Manhattan" title="Midtown Manhattan">
       Midtown Manhattan
      </a>
      was announced
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and by 2012 the program was fully implemented.
     </p>
     <p>
      The system was also licensed out to other cities with New York City getting 30% of the profits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fast1_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fast1-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The system's development costs were estimated at
      <span style="white-space: nowrap">
       US$
      </span>
      40 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ap1-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This system was highlighted in a May 2013 episode of
      <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">
       PBS
      </a>
      '
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">
        Nova
       </a>
      </i>
      on tracking the Boston Marathon Bombers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">
       Demographics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Demographics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As of the end of 2010, 53% of the entire 34,526-member police force were white and 47% were members of minority groups. Of 22,199 officers on patrol, 53% (11,717) were black, Latino (of any race), or Asian or Asian-American, and 47% (10,482) were non-Hispanic white. Of 5,177 detectives, 57% (2,953) were white and 43% (2,224) were people of color. Of 4,639 sergeants, 61% (2,841) were white and 39% (1,798) were minorities. Of 1,742 lieutenants, 76% (1,323) were white and 24% (419) were people of color. Of 432 captains, 82% (356) were white and 18% (76) were minorities. Of 10 chiefs, 7 were white and 3 were people of color. In 2002, whites accounted for 60% of members in the rank of police officer. Between 2002 and 2010, the number of minorities in top-tier positions in the force increased by about 4.5%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Corruption_and_misconduct">
       Corruption and misconduct
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Corruption and misconduct">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_corruption_and_misconduct" title="New York City Police Department corruption and misconduct">
       New York City Police Department corruption and misconduct
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Complaint_Review_Board" title="Civilian Complaint Review Board">
       Civilian Complaint Review Board
      </a>
      is an all-civilian, 13-member panel tasked with investigating misconduct or lesser abuse accusations against NYPD officers, including use of excessive force, abuse of authority, discourtesy and offensive language. Complaints against officers may be filed online, by US mail, by phone or in person at any NYPD station.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The history of the New York City Police Department (NYPD) has included thousands of documented cases of
      <a href="/wiki/Police_misconduct" title="Police misconduct">
       police misconduct
      </a>
      (corruption or brutality) by its officers. Over 12,000 such cases have resulted in lawsuit settlements totaling over $400 million just during a five-year period ending in 2014. Any misconduct must be called "alleged" if it did not result in a criminal conviction of the accused officer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliations">
       Affiliations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Affiliations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The NYPD is affiliated with the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Foundation" title="New York City Police Foundation">
       New York City Police Foundation
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Museum" title="New York City Police Museum">
       New York City Police Museum
      </a>
      . It also runs a Youth Police academy to provide positive interaction with police officers and to educate young people about the challenges and responsibility of police work. The department also provides a citizen Police Academy which educates the public on basic law and policing procedures.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle_appearance">
       Vehicle appearance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Vehicle appearance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The current NYPD vehicle appearance is an all-white vehicle body with two blue stripes along each side of the car. The word "POLICE" is printed in small text above the front wheel wells, and as "NYPD Police" above the grille opening. The NYPD shoulder patch is printed on both sides just in front of the front doors or on the front doors. The letters "NYPD" are printed in blue
      <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_(typeface)" title="Rockwell (typeface)">
       Rockwell Extra Bold
      </a>
      font on the front doors, and the NYPD motto "
      <b>
       C
      </b>
      ourtesy,
      <b>
       P
      </b>
      rofessionalism,
      <b>
       R
      </b>
      espect" is printed on the rear doors. The unit's shop number is printed on the rear decklid. The shop number is also printed on the rear side panels above the gas intake, along with the number of the unit's assigned precinct.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some Traffic Enforcement units used a modified paint job, with dark blue carbody and white stripes on the sides. The text on the car is also printed in white. These are being phased out in favor of units painted in a modified version of the regular NYPD paint job, with the word "TRAFFIC" printed on the rear side panels and trunk.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some NYPD Auxiliary units used a modified paint job, with dark blue or black carbody and white stripes on the sides. The text on the car is also printed in white. These are being phased out in favor of units painted in a modified version of the regular NYPD paint job, with the word "Auxiliary" printed on the rear side panels and trunk.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="On_duty">
       On duty
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: On duty">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      New NYPD officers are allowed to choose from one of three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9mm_parabellum" title="9mm parabellum">
       9mm
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Service_pistol" title="Service pistol">
       service pistols
      </a>
      : the
      <a href="/wiki/SIG_Sauer_P226#DAO_Version" title="SIG Sauer P226">
       SIG Sauer P226
      </a>
      DAO,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glock_19" title="Glock 19">
       Glock 19
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson_Model_5906" title="Smith &amp; Wesson Model 5906">
       Smith &amp; Wesson 5946
      </a>
      (authorized, but no longer issued to new recruits).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      All are modified to a 12-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force" title="Pound-force">
       pound
      </a>
      (53
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton_(units)" title="Newton (units)">
       N
      </a>
      ) NY-2 trigger pull.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Officers who purchased
      <a href="/wiki/Revolver" title="Revolver">
       revolvers
      </a>
      prior to the transition to semi-automatic pistols on January 1, 1994, are "
      <a href="/wiki/Grandfather_clause" title="Grandfather clause">
       grandfathered
      </a>
      " and if so can choose to continue to carry a revolver as a duty weapon.
     </p>
     <p>
      Shotgun-certified officers were authorized to carry
      <a href="/wiki/Ithaca_37" title="Ithaca 37">
       Ithaca 37
      </a>
      shotguns, which are being phased out by the newer
      <a href="/wiki/Mossberg_500#Model_500_vs._Model_590_vs._Model_590A1" title="Mossberg 500">
       Mossberg 590
      </a>
      . Officers and detectives belonging to special investigative units, Organized Crime Control Bureau and NYPD's
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department_Emergency_Service_Unit" title="New York City Police Department Emergency Service Unit">
       Emergency Service Unit
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      are armed with a range of select-fire weapons and long guns, such as the Colt
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M4A1" title="M4A1">
       M4A1
      </a>
      carbine and similar-pattern
      <a href="/wiki/AR-15" title="AR-15">
       AR-15
      </a>
      rifles,
      <a href="/wiki/Ruger_Mini-14" title="Ruger Mini-14">
       Ruger Mini-14
      </a>
      rifle,
      <a href="/wiki/Heckler_%26_Koch_MP5" title="Heckler &amp; Koch MP5">
       Heckler &amp; Koch MP5
      </a>
      submachine gun, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Remington_Model_700" title="Remington Model 700">
       Remington Model 700
      </a>
      bolt-action rifle.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Off_duty">
       Off duty
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Off duty">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The firearms approved by the NYPD for off duty carry are the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glock_26" title="Glock 26">
       Glock 26
      </a>
      , Smith &amp; Wesson 3914 DAO, Smith &amp; Wesson 3953TSW,
      <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson_Model_640" title="Smith &amp; Wesson Model 640">
       Smith &amp; Wesson Model 640
      </a>
      (.38 revolver),
      <a href="/wiki/SIG_Sauer_P239" title="SIG Sauer P239">
       SIG Sauer P239
      </a>
      DAO,
      <a href="/wiki/HS2000" title="HS2000">
       Springfield XDS
      </a>
      , Smith &amp; Wesson M&amp;P Shield and the Beretta 8000D Mini Cougar. Along with those weapons, NYPD officers may also carry their authorized on duty firearms while off duty.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">

         </span>

       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discontinued_from_service">
       Discontinued from service
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_Police_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Discontinued from service">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      From 1926 until 1986 the standard weapons of the department were the
      <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson_Model_10" title="Smith &amp; Wesson Model 10">
       Smith &amp; Wesson Model 10
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Colt_Official_Police" title="Colt Official Police">
       Colt Official Police
      </a>
      .38 Special revolvers with four-inch barrels. Prior to 1994 the standard weapon of the NYPD was the
      <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson_Model_64" title="Smith &amp; Wesson Model 64">
       Smith &amp; Wesson Model 64
      </a>
      DAO (Double Action Only) .38 Special revolver with three or four inch barrels. This type of revolver was called the Model NY-1 by the department.
     </p>
     <p>
      Prior to the issuing of the 9mm semi-automatic pistol NYPD Detectives and plain clothes officers often carried the
      <a href="/wiki/Colt_Detective_Special" title="Colt Detective Special">
       Colt Detective Special
      </a>
      and/or the
      <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson_Model_36" title="Smith &amp; Wesson Model 36">
       Smith &amp; Wesson Model 36
      </a>
      "Chief's Special" .38 Special caliber snub-nosed (2-inch) barrel revolvers for their easiness to conceal while dressed in civilian clothes.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kahr_K9" title="Kahr K9">
       Kahr K9
      </a>
      9 mm pistol was an approved off-duty/backup weapon from 1998 to 2011. It was pulled from service because it could not be modified to a 12-pound NY-2 trigger pull.
     </p>
