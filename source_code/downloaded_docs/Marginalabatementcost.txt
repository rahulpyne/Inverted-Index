     <p>
      Abatement cost is the cost of reducing environmental negatives such as pollution. Marginal cost is an economic concept that measures the cost of an additional unit. The
      <b>
       marginal abatement cost
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       MAC
      </b>
      ), in general, measures the cost of reducing one more unit of pollution.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although marginal abatement costs can be negative, such as when the low carbon option is cheaper than the business-as-usual option, MACs often rise steeply as more pollution is reduced.
     </p>
     <p>
      Marginal abatement costs are typically used on a
      <b>
       marginal abatement cost curve
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       MAC curve
      </b>
      , which shows the marginal cost of additional reductions in pollution.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
       Usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marginal_abatement_cost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Carbon traders use MAC curves to derive the supply function for modelling carbon price fundamentals. Power companies may employ MAC curves to guide their decisions about long-term capital investment strategies to select among a variety of efficiency and generation options. Economists have used MAC curves to explain the economics of interregional carbon trading.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Policy-makers use MAC curves as
      <a href="/wiki/Merit_order" title="Merit order">
       merit order
      </a>
      curves, to analyze how much abatement can be done in an economy at what cost, and where policy should be directed to achieve the emission reductions.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, MAC curves should not be used as abatement supply curves (or
      <a href="/wiki/Merit_order" title="Merit order">
       merit order
      </a>
      curves) to decide which measures to implement in order to achieve a given emission-reduction target. Indeed, the options they list would take decades to implement, and it may be optimal to implement expensive but high-potential measures before introducing cheaper measures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marginal_abatement_cost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The way that MAC curves are usually built has been criticized for lack of transparency and the poor treatment it makes of uncertainty, inter-temporal dynamics, interactions between sectors and ancillary benefits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_existing_MAC_curves">
       Examples of existing MAC curves
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marginal_abatement_cost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples of existing MAC curves">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Various economists, research organizations, and consultancies have produced MAC curves. Bloomberg New Energy Finance
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/McKinsey_%26_Company" title="McKinsey &amp; Company">
       McKinsey &amp; Company
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      have produced economy wide analyses on greenhouse gas emissions reductions for the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/ICF_International" title="ICF International">
       ICF International
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      produced a California specific curve following
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AB-32" title="AB-32">
       AB-32
      </a>
      legislation as have Sweeney and Weyant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal_Institute_for_Climate,_Environment_and_Energy" title="Wuppertal Institute for Climate, Environment and Energy">
       Wuppertal Institute for Climate, Environment and Energy
      </a>
      produced several marginal abatement cost curves for Germany (also called Cost Potential Curves), depending on the perspective (end-user, utilities, society).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The US
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">
       Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      has done work on a MAC curve for non carbon dioxide emissions such as methane, N
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      O, and HFCs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Enerdata and LEPII-CNRS (France) produce MAC curves with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/POLES" title="POLES">
       POLES
      </a>
      model for the 6 Kyoto Protocol gases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      These curves have been used for various public and private actors either to assess carbon policies
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      or through the use of a carbon market analysis tool.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The World Bank 2013 low-carbon energy development plan for Nigeria,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      prepared jointly with the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      , ulitizes MAC curves created in
      <a href="/wiki/Analytica_(software)" title="Analytica (software)">
       Analytica
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
