    Black start
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      A
      <b>
       black start
      </b>
      is the process of restoring an electric
      <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">
       power station
      </a>
      or a part of an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_grid" title="Electric grid">
       electric grid
      </a>
      to operation without relying on the external
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transmission_network" title="Transmission network">
       transmission network
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight01_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Knight01-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Normally, the electric power used within the plant is provided from the station's own generators. If all of the plant's main generators are shut down, station service power is provided by drawing power from the grid through the plant's transmission line. However, during a wide-area outage, off-site power supply from the grid will not be available. In the absence of grid power, a so-called black start needs to be performed to
      <a href="/wiki/Bootstrapping#Electric_power_grid" title="Bootstrapping">
       bootstrap
      </a>
      the power grid into operation.
     </p>
     <p>
      To provide a black start, some power stations have small
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_generator" title="Diesel generator">
       diesel generators
      </a>
      , normally called the
      <i>
       black start diesel generator
      </i>
      (BSDG), which can be used to start larger generators (of several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       megawatts
      </a>
      capacity), which in turn can be used to start the main power station generators. Generating plants using steam turbines require station service power of up to 10% of their capacity for
      <a href="/wiki/Boiler_feedwater_pump" title="Boiler feedwater pump">
       boiler feedwater pumps
      </a>
      , boiler forced-draft combustion air blowers, and for fuel preparation. It is uneconomical to provide such a large standby capacity at each station, so black-start power must be provided over designated tie lines from another station. Often
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectric
      </a>
      power plants are designated as the black-start sources to restore network interconnections. A hydroelectric station needs very little initial power to start (just enough to open the intake gates and provide
      <a href="/wiki/Excitation_(magnetic)" title="Excitation (magnetic)">
       excitation
      </a>
      current to the generator field coils), and can put a large block of power on line very quickly to allow start-up of fossil-fueled or nuclear stations. Certain types of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combustion_turbine" title="Combustion turbine">
       combustion turbine
      </a>
      can be configured for black start, providing another option in places without suitable hydroelectric plants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="A_black_start_sequence">
       A black start sequence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      One method of black start (based on a real scenario) might be as follows:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">
        battery
       </a>
       starts a small
       <a href="/wiki/Diesel_generator" title="Diesel generator">
        diesel generator
       </a>
       installed in a hydroelectric generating station.
      </li>
      <li>
       The power from the diesel generator is used to bring the generating station into operation.
      </li>
      <li>
       Key
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
        transmission
       </a>
       lines between the station and other areas are energized.
      </li>
      <li>
       The power from the station is used to start one of the nuclear/fossil-fuel-fired
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_load_power_plant" title="Base load power plant">
        base load
       </a>
       plants.
      </li>
      <li>
       The power from the base load plant is used to restart all of the other power plants in the system.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Power is finally re-applied to the general
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electricity_distribution" title="Electricity distribution">
       electricity distribution
      </a>
      network and sent to the consumers. Often this will happen gradually; starting the entire grid at once may be unfeasible. In particular, after a lengthy outage during summer, all buildings will be warm, and if the power were restored at once, the demand from air conditioning units alone would be more than the grid could supply. In colder climates a similar issue can occur in winter with the use of heating devices.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a larger grid, black start will often involve starting multiple "islands" of generation (each supplying local load areas), and then synchronising and reconnecting these islands to form a complete grid. The power stations involved have to be able to accept large step changes in load as the grid is reconnected.
     </p>
     <p>
      Note that there are multiple methods of commencing a black start: hydro-electric dams, diesel generators, open cycle gas turbines, compressed air storage, and so on. Different generating networks take different approaches, dependent on factors such as cost, complexity, the availability of local resources (i.e. suitable valleys for dams), the interconnectivity with other generating networks, and the response time necessary for the black start process.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Procurement_of_black_start_services">
       Procurement of black start services
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_start&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Procurement of black start services">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ERCOTOperator_2.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1331" data-file-width="2000" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/ERCOTOperator_2.jpg/220px-ERCOTOperator_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/ERCOTOperator_2.jpg/330px-ERCOTOperator_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/ERCOTOperator_2.jpg/440px-ERCOTOperator_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ERCOTOperator_2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An operator at ERCOT
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      , the grid operator has commercial agreements in place with some generators to provide black start capacity, recognising that black start facilities are often not economic in normal grid operation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is typical of power stations from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CEGB" title="CEGB">
       CEGB
      </a>
      era to have a number of open-cycle gas turbines (i.e. no heat recovery modules attached) that can run the entirety of the plant necessary to operating a full generating unit; these would normally be started by diesel generators, fed in turn by battery backups. Once up to speed these gas turbines are capable of running the entire plant associated with the rest of the power station, negating the need to bring power in from other sources. An example of this is the
      <a href="/wiki/Didcot_power_stations" title="Didcot power stations">
       Didcot power stations
      </a>
      where the 'A' station (a large coal-fired station consisting of four 500 MW generating units) was fitted with a gas turbine for each unit, enabling black-start capability for the entire station. The newer gas-fired 'B' station built adjacent (consisting of two 700 MW generating modules) was not fitted with its own Open Cycle Gas Turbine (OCGT)'s, instead relying on the 'A' station to start first. With the closure of the 'A' station under the European Union's
      <a href="/wiki/Large_Combustion_Plant_Directive" title="Large Combustion Plant Directive">
       Large Combustion Plant Directive
      </a>
      , the supplies and controls for the small gas turbines have been transferred to the 'B' station, ensuring that the Didcot site maintains independent black-start capability.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the North American
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_system_operator" title="Independent system operator">
       independent system operators
      </a>
      , the procurement of black start varies somewhat. Traditionally, black start was provided by integrated utilities and the costs were rolled into a broad tariff for cost recovery from ratepayers. In those areas which are not part of organized electricity markets, this is still the usual procurement mechanism. In the deregulated environment, this legacy of cost-based provision has persisted, and even recent overhauls of black-start procurement practices, such as that by the
      <a href="/wiki/ISO_New_England" title="ISO New England">
       ISO New England
      </a>
      , have not necessarily shifted to competitive procurement, although deregulated jurisdictions have a bias for market solutions rather than cost-of-service (COS) solutions.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the United States, there are currently three methods of procuring black start. The most common is cost-of-service, as it is the simplest and is the traditional method. It is currently used by the
      <a href="/wiki/California_Independent_System_Operator" title="California Independent System Operator">
       California Independent System Operator
      </a>
      (CAISO), the
      <a href="/wiki/PJM_Interconnection" title="PJM Interconnection">
       PJM Interconnection
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYISO" title="NYISO">
       New York Independent System Operator
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (NYISO). Although the exact mechanisms differ somewhat the same approach is used, namely that units are identified for black start and their documented costs are then funded and rolled into a tariff for cost recovery. The second method is a new method used by the Independent System Operator of New England
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (ISO-NE). The new methodology is a flat rate payment which increases black start remuneration to encourage provision. The monthly compensation paid to a generator is determined by multiplying a flat rate (in $/KWyr and referred to as the $Y value) by the unit's Monthly Claimed Capability for that month. The purpose of this change was to simplify procurement and encourage provision of the black start service. The final method of procurement is competitive procurement as used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_Reliability_Council_of_Texas" title="Electric Reliability Council of Texas">
       Electric Reliability Council of Texas
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (ERCOT). Under this approach ERCOT runs a market for black start services. Interested participants submit an hourly standby cost in $/hr (e.g. $70 per hour), often termed an availability bid, that is unrelated to the capacity of the unit. Using various criteria ERCOT evaluates these bids and the selected units are paid as bid, presuming an 85% availability. Each black start unit must be able to demonstrate that it can startup another unit in close proximity to begin the islanding and synchronization of the grid.
     </p>
     <p>
      In other jurisdictions there are differing methods of procurement. The New Zealand System Operator
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      procures the blackstart capability via competitive tender. Other jurisdictions also appear to have some sort of competitive procurement, although not as structured as ERCOT. These include the
      <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Electric_System_Operator" title="Alberta Electric System Operator">
       Alberta Electric System Operator
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      as well as Independent Electric System Operator of Ontario,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      both of which use a long-term "request for proposals" approach similar to New Zealand and ERCOT.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_on_black_start_sources">
       Limitations on black start sources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_start&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Limitations on black start sources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Not all generating plants are suitable for black-start capability. Wind turbines are not suitable for black start because wind may not be available when needed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">
       Wind turbines
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Small_hydro" title="Small hydro">
       mini-hydro
      </a>
      , or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro-hydro" title="Micro-hydro">
       micro-hydro
      </a>
      plants, are often connected to
      <a href="/wiki/Induction_generator" title="Induction generator">
       induction generators
      </a>
      which are incapable of providing power to re-energize the network. The black-start unit must also be stable when operated with the large reactive load of a long transmission line. Many
      <a href="/wiki/High-voltage_direct_current" title="High-voltage direct current">
       high-voltage direct current
      </a>
      converter stations cannot operate into a "dead" system, either, since they require
      <a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">
       commutation
      </a>
      power from the system at the load end. This is not true of
      <a href="/wiki/Pulse-width_modulation" title="Pulse-width modulation">
       PWM
      </a>
      -based (voltage-source converter)
      <a href="/wiki/High-voltage_direct_current" title="High-voltage direct current">
       HVDC
      </a>
      schemes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
