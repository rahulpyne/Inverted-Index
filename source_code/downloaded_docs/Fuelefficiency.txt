     <p>
      <b>
       Fuel efficiency
      </b>
      is a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">
       thermal efficiency
      </a>
      , meaning the efficiency of a process that converts
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">
       chemical
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">
       potential energy
      </a>
      contained in a carrier
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">
       fuel
      </a>
      into
      <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
       kinetic energy
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">
       work
      </a>
      . Overall fuel efficiency may vary per device, which in turn may vary per application fuel efficiency, especially
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">
       fossil fuel power plants
      </a>
      or industries dealing with
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
       ammonia
      </a>
      production during the
      <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">
       Haber process
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the context of
      <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">
       transport
      </a>
      , fuel economy is the
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_efficiency_in_transportation" title="Energy efficiency in transportation">
       energy efficiency
      </a>
      of a particular vehicle, given as a
      <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">
       ratio
      </a>
      of distance traveled per unit of
      <a href="/wiki/Motor_fuel" title="Motor fuel">
       fuel
      </a>
      consumed. It is dependent on
      <a href="/wiki/Engine_efficiency" title="Engine efficiency">
       engine efficiency
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">
       transmission
      </a>
      design, and
      <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">
       tire
      </a>
      design. Fuel economy is expressed in
      <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">
       miles
      </a>
      per
      <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">
       gallon
      </a>
      (mpg) in the USA and usually also in the UK (
      <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">
       imperial
      </a>
      gallon); there is sometimes confusion as the imperial gallon is 20% larger than the US gallon so that mpg values are not directly comparable. In countries using the
      <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">
       metric system
      </a>
      fuel economy is stated as "fuel consumption" in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liter" title="Liter">
       liters
      </a>
      per 100
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilometers" title="Kilometers">
       kilometers
      </a>
      (L/100 km). Litres per
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_mile" title="Scandinavian mile">
       mil
      </a>
      are used in
      <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
       Norway
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Fuel consumption is a more accurate measure of a vehicle’s performance because it is a linear relationship while fuel economy leads to distortions in efficiency improvements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Weight-specific efficiency (efficiency per unit weight) may be stated for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freight" title="Freight">
       freight
      </a>
      , and passenger-specific efficiency (vehicle efficiency per passenger).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle_design">
       Vehicle design
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Vehicle design">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Fuel efficiency is dependent on many parameters of a vehicle, including its
      <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">
       engine
      </a>
      parameters,
      <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">
       aerodynamic drag
      </a>
      , weight, and
      <a href="/wiki/Rolling_resistance" title="Rolling resistance">
       rolling resistance
      </a>
      . There have been advances in all areas of vehicle design in recent decades.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicles" title="Hybrid vehicles">
       Hybrid vehicles
      </a>
      use two or more power sources for propulsion. In many designs, a small combustion engine is combined with electric motors. Kinetic energy which would otherwise be lost to heat during braking is recaptured as electrical power to improve fuel efficiency. Engines automatically shut off when vehicles come to a stop and start again when the accelerator is pressed preventing wasted energy from idling.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fleet_efficiency">
       Fleet efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fleet efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Fleet efficiency describes the average efficiency of a population of vehicles. Technological advances in efficiency may be offset by a change in buying habits with a propensity to heavier vehicles, which are less efficient, all else being equal.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_efficiency_terminology">
       Energy efficiency terminology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy efficiency terminology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Energy efficiency is similar to fuel efficiency but the input is usually in units of energy such as British thermal units (BTU), megajoules (MJ), gigajoules (GJ), kilocalories (kcal), or kilowatt-hours (kW·h). The inverse of "energy efficiency" is "energy intensity", or the amount of input energy required for a unit of output such as MJ/passenger-km (of passenger transport), BTU/ton-mile or kJ/t-km (of freight transport), GJ/t (for production of steel and other materials), BTU/(kW·h) (for electricity generation), or litres/100 km (of vehicle travel). Litres per 100 km is also a measure of "energy intensity" where the input is measured by the amount of fuel and the output is measured by the
      <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">
       distance
      </a>
      travelled. For example:
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">
       Fuel economy in automobiles
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Given a heat value of a fuel, it would be trivial to convert from fuel units (such as litres of gasoline) to energy units (such as MJ) and conversely. But there are two problems with comparisons made using energy units:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       There are two different heat values for any hydrogen-containing fuel which can differ by several percent (see below).
      </li>
      <li>
       When comparing transportation energy costs, it must be remembered that a
       <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">
        kilowatt hour
       </a>
       of electric energy may require an amount of fuel with heating value of 2 or 3 kilowatt hours to produce it.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_content_of_fuel">
       Energy content of fuel
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Energy content of fuel">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The specific energy content of a fuel is the heat energy obtained when a certain quantity is burned (such as a gallon, litre, kilogram). It is sometimes called the
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">
       heat of combustion
      </a>
      . There exists two different values of specific heat energy for the same batch of fuel. One is the high (or gross) heat of combustion and the other is the low (or net) heat of combustion. The high value is obtained when, after the combustion, the water in the exhaust is in liquid form. For the low value, the exhaust has all the water in vapor form (steam). Since water vapor gives up heat energy when it changes from vapor to liquid, the liquid water value is larger since it includes the latent heat of vaporization of water. The difference between the high and low values is significant, about 8 or 9%. This accounts for most of the apparent discrepancy in the heat value of gasoline. In the U.S. (and the table) the high heat values have traditionally been used, but in many other countries, the low heat values are commonly used.
     </p>

     <p>
      Neither the gross heat of combustion nor the net heat of combustion gives the theoretical amount of mechanical energy (work) that can be obtained from the reaction. (This is given by the change in
      <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">
       Gibbs free energy
      </a>
      , and is around 45.7 MJ/kg for gasoline.) The actual amount of mechanical work obtained from fuel (the inverse of the
      <a href="/wiki/Brake_specific_fuel_consumption" title="Brake specific fuel consumption">
       specific fuel consumption
      </a>
      ) depends on the engine. A figure of 17.6 MJ/kg is possible with a gasoline engine, and 19.1 MJ/kg for a diesel engine. See
      <a href="/wiki/Brake_specific_fuel_consumption" title="Brake specific fuel consumption">
       Brake specific fuel consumption
      </a>
      for more information.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_efficiency_of_motor_vehicles">
       Fuel efficiency of motor vehicles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fuel efficiency of motor vehicles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">
       Fuel economy in automobiles
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The fuel efficiency of motor vehicles can be expressed in more ways:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Fuel consumption
       </b>
       is the amount of fuel used per unit distance; for example,
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">
         litres
        </a>
        per 100
        <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">
         kilometres
        </a>
        (L/100 km)
       </b>
       . In this case, the
       <b>
        lower
       </b>
       the value, the more economic a vehicle is (the less fuel it needs to travel a certain distance); this is the measure generally used across Europe (except the UK, Denmark and The Netherlands - see below), New Zealand, Australia and Canada. Also in Uruguay, Paraguay, Guatemala, Colombia, China, and Madagascar.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       , as also in post-Soviet space.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Fuel economy
       </b>
       is the distance travelled per unit volume of fuel used; for example,
       <b>
        kilometres per litre (km/L)
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">
         miles
        </a>
        per
        <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">
         gallon
        </a>
        (MPG)
       </b>
       , where 1 MPG (imperial) ≈ 0.354006 km/L. In this case, the
       <b>
        higher
       </b>
       the value, the more economic a vehicle is (the more distance it can travel with a certain volume of fuel). This measure is popular in the USA and the UK (mpg), but in Europe, India, Japan, South Korea and Latin America the metric unit
       <i>
        km/L
       </i>
       is used instead.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Converting from mpg or to L/100 km (or vice versa) involves the use of the
      <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">
       reciprocal
      </a>
      function, which is not
      <a href="/wiki/Distributive_property" title="Distributive property">
       distributive
      </a>
      . Therefore, the average of two fuel economy numbers gives different values if those units are used, because one of the functions is reciprocal, thus not linear. If two people calculate the fuel economy average of two groups of cars with different units, the group with better fuel economy may be one or the other. However, from the point of energy used as a shared method of measure, the result shall be the same in both the cases.
     </p>

     <p>
      In parts of Europe, the two standard measuring cycles for "litre/100 km" value are "urban" traffic with speeds up to 50 km/h from a cold start, and then "extra urban" travel at various speeds up to 120 km/h which follows the urban test. A combined figure is also quoted showing the total fuel consumed in divided by the total distance traveled in both tests. A reasonably modern European
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supermini_car" title="Supermini car">
       supermini
      </a>
      and many mid-size cars, including station wagons, may manage
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorway" title="Motorway">
       motorway
      </a>
      travel at 5 L/100 km (47 mpg US/56 mpg imp) or 6.5 L/100 km in city traffic (36 mpg US/43 mpg imp), with
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      emissions of around 140 g/km.
     </p>
     <p>
      An average
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North American
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Mid-size_car" title="Mid-size car">
       mid-size car
      </a>
      travels 21 mpg (US) (11 L/100 km) city, 27 mpg (US) (9 L/100 km) highway; a
      <a href="/wiki/Full-size_car" title="Full-size car">
       full-size
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SUV" title="SUV">
       SUV
      </a>
      usually travels 13 mpg (US) (18 L/100 km) city and 16 mpg (US) (15 L/100 km) highway.
      <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">
       Pickup trucks
      </a>
      vary considerably; whereas a 4 cylinder-engined light pickup can achieve 28 mpg (8 L/100 km), a
      <a href="/wiki/V8_engine" title="V8 engine">
       V8
      </a>
      full-size pickup with extended cabin only travels 13 mpg (US) (18 L/100 km) city and 15 mpg (US) (15 L/100 km) highway.
     </p>
     <p>
      The average fuel economy is higher in Europe due to the higher cost of fuel. In the UK, a gallon of gas without tax would cost US$1.97, but with taxes cost US$6.06 in 2005. The average cost in the United States was US$2.61. Consumers prefer "muscle cars" but choose more fuel efficient ones when gas prices increase.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      European-built cars are generally more fuel-efficient than US vehicles. While Europe has many higher efficiency diesel cars, European gasoline vehicles are on average also more efficient than gasoline-powered vehicles in the USA. Most European vehicles cited in the CSI study run on diesel engines, which tend to achieve greater fuel efficiency than gas engines. Selling those cars in the United States is difficult because of emission standards, notes Walter McManus, a fuel economy expert at the University of Michigan Transportation Research Institute. "For the most part, European diesels don’t meet U.S. emission standards", McManus said in 2007. Another reason why many European models are not marketed in the United States is that labor unions object to having the big 3 import any new foreign built models regardless of fuel economy while laying off workers at home.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An example of European cars' capabilities of fuel economy is the
      <a href="/wiki/Microcar" title="Microcar">
       microcar
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Smart_Fortwo" title="Smart Fortwo">
        Smart Fortwo
       </a>
      </i>
      cdi, which can achieve up to 3.4 l/100 km (69.2 mpg US) using a
      <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">
       turbocharged
      </a>
      three-cylinder 41 bhp (30 kW) Diesel engine. The Fortwo is produced by
      <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">
       Daimler AG
      </a>
      and is currently only sold by one company in the United States. Furthermore, the current (and to date already 10-year-old) world record in fuel economy of production cars is held by the
      <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Group" title="Volkswagen Group">
       Volkswagen Group
      </a>
      , with special production models (labeled "3L") of the
      <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Lupo#Lupo_3L" title="Volkswagen Lupo">
       Volkswagen Lupo
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Audi_A2#1.2_TDI_.223L.22" title="Audi A2">
       Audi A2
      </a>
      , consuming as little as 3 L/100 km (94 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -imp
       </small>
      </sub>
      ; 78 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -US
       </small>
      </sub>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">
       Diesel engines
      </a>
      generally achieve greater fuel efficiency than petrol (gasoline) engines. Passenger car diesel engines have
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">
       energy efficiency
      </a>
      of up to 41% but more typically 30%, and petrol engines of up to 37.3%, but more typically 20%. That is one of the reasons why diesels have better fuel efficiency than equivalent petrol cars. A common margin is 25% more miles per gallon for an efficient turbodiesel.
     </p>
     <p>
      For example, the current model Skoda Octavia, using Volkswagen engines, has a combined European fuel efficiency of 41.3 mpg for the 105 bhp (78 kW) petrol engine and 52.3 mpg for the 105 bhp (78 kW) — and heavier — diesel engine. The higher compression ratio is helpful in raising the energy efficiency, but diesel fuel also contains approximately 10% more energy per unit volume than gasoline which contributes to the reduced fuel consumption for a given power output.
     </p>
     <p>

      <sub>
       <small>
        -imp
       </small>
      </sub>
      ). Large trucks, over 33,000 pounds (15,000 kg), averaged 5.7 miles per US gallon (41 L/100 km; 6.8 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -imp
       </small>
      </sub>
      ).

     <p>
      The average economy of automobiles in the United States in 2002 was 22.0 miles per US gallon (10.7 L/100 km; 26.4 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -imp
       </small>
      </sub>
      ). By 2010 this had increased to 23.0 miles per US gallon (10.2 L/100 km; 27.6 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -imp
       </small>
      </sub>
      ). Average fuel economy in the United States gradually declined until 1973, when it reached a low of 13.4 miles per US gallon (17.6 L/100 km; 16.1 mpg
      <sub>
       <small>
        -imp
       </small>
      </sub>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">

       </a>

       </a>
      </sup>
      One method by car makers to increase fuel efficiency is
      <a href="/wiki/Lightweighting" title="Lightweighting">
       lightweighting
      </a>
      in which lighter-weight materials are substituted in for improved engine performance and handling.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_efficiency_in_microgravity">
       Fuel efficiency in microgravity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fuel efficiency in microgravity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      How fuel combusts affects how much energy is produced. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">
       National Aeronautics and Space Administration
      </a>
      (NASA) has investigated fuel consumption in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity" title="Microgravity">
       microgravity
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The common distribution of a flame under normal gravity conditions depends on
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      , because soot tends to rise to the top of a flame, such as in a candle, making the flame yellow. In microgravity or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_gravity" title="Zero gravity">
       zero gravity
      </a>
      , such as an environment in
      <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
       outer space
      </a>
      , convection no longer occurs, and the flame becomes
      <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
       spherical
      </a>
      , with a tendency to become more blue and more efficient. There are several possible explanations for this difference, of which the most likely one given is the hypothesis that the temperature is evenly distributed enough that soot is not formed and complete combustion occurs., National Aeronautics and Space Administration, April 2005. Experiments by NASA in microgravity reveal that
      <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_flame" title="Diffusion flame">
       diffusion flames
      </a>
      in microgravity allow more soot to be completely oxidised after they are produced than diffusion flames on Earth, because of a series of mechanisms that behaved differently in microgravity when compared to normal gravity conditions.
      <a class="external text" href="http://microgravity.grc.nasa.gov/combustion/lsp/lsp1_results.htm" rel="nofollow">
       LSP-1 experiment results
      </a>
      , National Aeronautics and Space Administration, April 2005.
      <a href="/wiki/Premixed_flame" title="Premixed flame">
       Premixed flames
      </a>
      in microgravity burn at a much slower rate and more efficiently than even a candle on Earth, and last much longer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_efficiency_in_transportation">
       Fuel efficiency in transportation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fuel efficiency in transportation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_efficiency_in_transportation" title="Energy efficiency in transportation">
       Energy efficiency in transportation
      </a>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle_efficiency_and_transportation_pollution">
       Vehicle efficiency and transportation pollution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vehicle efficiency and transportation pollution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main articles:
      <a href="/wiki/Gas-guzzler" title="Gas-guzzler">
       Gas-guzzler
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Efficiency_Initiative" title="Vehicle Efficiency Initiative">
       Vehicle Efficiency Initiative
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fuel efficiency directly affects emissions causing pollution by affecting the amount of fuel used. However, it also depends on the fuel source used to drive the vehicle concerned. Cars for example, can run on a number of fuel types other than gasoline, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_vehicle" title="Natural gas vehicle">
       natural gas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">
       LPG
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
       biofuel
      </a>
      or electricity which creates various quantities of atmospheric pollution.
     </p>
     <p>
      A kilogram of carbon, whether contained in petrol, diesel, kerosene, or any other hydrocarbon fuel in a vehicle, leads to approximately 3.6 kg of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_CO2_est_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA_CO2_est-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Due to the carbon content of gasoline, its combustion emits 2.3 kg/l (19.4 lb/US gal) of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      ; since diesel fuel is more energy dense per unit volume, diesel emits 2.6 kg/l (22.2 lb/US gal).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_CO2_est_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA_CO2_est-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This figure is only the CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions of the final fuel product and does not include additional CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions created during the drilling, pumping, transportation and refining steps required to produce the fuel. Additional measures to reduce overall emission includes improvements to the efficiency of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_conditioner" title="Air conditioner">
       air conditioners
      </a>
      , lights and tires.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Driving_technique">
       Driving technique
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Driving technique">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Energy-efficient_driving" title="Energy-efficient driving">
       Energy-efficient driving
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Many drivers have the potential to improve their fuel efficiency significantly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beusen_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beusen-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      These
      <a class="external text" href="http://oee.nrcan.gc.ca/cars-light-trucks/driving/fuel-efficient-driving-techniques/17823" rel="nofollow">
       five basic fuel-efficient driving techniques
      </a>
      can be effective. Simple things such as keeping tires properly inflated, having a vehicle well-maintained and avoiding idling can dramatically improve fuel efficiency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a growing community of enthusiasts known as hypermilers who develop and practice driving techniques to increase fuel efficiency and reduce consumption. Hypermilers have broken records of fuel efficiency, for example, achieving 109 miles per gallon in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prius" title="Prius">
       Prius
      </a>
      . In non-hybrid vehicles these techniques are also beneficial, with fuel efficiencies of up to 59 MPG in a
      <a href="/wiki/Honda_Accord" title="Honda Accord">
       Honda Accord
      </a>
      or 30 MPG in an
      <a href="/wiki/Acura_MDX" title="Acura MDX">
       Acura MDX
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-This_Guy_Can_Get_59_MPG_in_a_Plain_Old_Accord._Beat_That.2C_Punk._15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-This_Guy_Can_Get_59_MPG_in_a_Plain_Old_Accord._Beat_That.2C_Punk.-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_technology_improvements_to_improve_fuel_efficiency">
       Advanced technology improvements to improve fuel efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Advanced technology improvements to improve fuel efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The most efficient machines for converting energy to rotary motion are electric motors, as used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_vehicles" title="Electric vehicles">
       electric vehicles
      </a>
      . However, electricity is not a primary energy source so the efficiency of the electricity production has also to be taken into account. Currently
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">
       railway
      </a>
      trains can be powered using electricity, delivered through an additional running rail, overhead
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overhead_lines" title="Overhead lines">
       catenary
      </a>
      system or by on-board generators used in
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel-electric_transmission" title="Diesel-electric transmission">
       diesel-electric
      </a>
      locomotives as common on the US and UK rail networks. Pollution produced from centralised generation of electricity is emitted at a distant power station, rather than "on site". Pollution can be reduced by using more railway electrification and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_carbon_power" title="Low carbon power">
       low carbon power
      </a>
      for electricity. Some railways, such as the French SNCF and Swiss federal railways derive most, if not 100% of their power, from hydroelectric or nuclear power stations, therefore atmospheric pollution from their rail networks is very low. This was reflected in a study by AEA Technology between a
      <a href="/wiki/Eurostar" title="Eurostar">
       Eurostar
      </a>
      train and airline journeys between London and Paris, which showed the trains on average emitting 10 times less CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      , per passenger, than planes, helped in part by French nuclear generation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the future,
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_vehicle" title="Hydrogen vehicle">
       hydrogen cars
      </a>
      may be commercially available. Toyota is test marketing hydrogen fuel cell powered vehicles in southern California where a series of hydrogen fueling stations has been established. Powered either through chemical reactions in a
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">
       fuel cell
      </a>
      that create electricity to drive very efficient electrical motors or by directly burning hydrogen in a combustion engine (near identically to a
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_vehicle" title="Natural gas vehicle">
       natural gas vehicle
      </a>
      , and similarly compatible with both natural gas and gasoline); these vehicles promise to have near-zero pollution from the tailpipe (exhaust pipe). Potentially the atmospheric pollution could be minimal, provided the hydrogen is made by
      <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">
       electrolysis
      </a>
      using electricity from non-polluting sources such as solar, wind or
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydroelectricity
      </a>
      or nuclear. Commercial
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">
       hydrogen production
      </a>
      uses fossil fuels and produces more carbon dioxide than hydrogen.
     </p>
     <p>
      Because there are pollutants involved in the manufacture and destruction of a car and the production, transmission and storage of electricity and hydrogen, the use of the label "zero pollution" should be understood as applying only to the car's conversion of stored energy into transportation.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004, a consortium of major auto-makers —
      <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">
       BMW
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">
       General Motors
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">
       Honda
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">
       Toyota
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">
       Volkswagen
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">
       Audi
      </a>
      — came up with
      <i>
       "Top Tier Detergent Gasoline Standard"
      </i>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">
       gasoline
      </a>
      brands in the US and Canada that meet their minimum standards for
      <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">
       detergent
      </a>
      content
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Top_Tier_Gasoline_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Top_Tier_Gasoline-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and do not contain metallic additives. Top Tier gasoline contains higher levels of detergent additives in order to prevent the build-up of deposits (typically, on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_injector" title="Fuel injector">
       fuel injector
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intake_valve" title="Intake valve">
       intake valve
      </a>
      ) known to reduce fuel economy and engine performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
