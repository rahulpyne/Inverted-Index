        A
        <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">
         vortex
        </a>
        is created by the passage of an aircraft wing, revealed by smoke. Vortices are one of the many phenomena associated with the study of aerodynamics.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Aerodynamics
      </b>
      , from
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      ἀήρ
      <i>
       aer
      </i>
      (air) + δυναμική (dynamics), is a branch of
      <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">
       fluid dynamics
      </a>
      concerned with studying the motion of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">
       air
      </a>
      , particularly when it interacts with a solid object, such as an
      <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">
       airplane
      </a>
      wing. Aerodynamics is a sub-field of
      <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">
       fluid dynamics
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_dynamics" title="Gas dynamics">
       gas dynamics
      </a>
      , and many aspects of aerodynamics theory are common to these fields. The term
      <i>
       aerodynamics
      </i>
      is often used synonymously with gas dynamics, with the difference being that "gas dynamics" applies to the study of the motion of all gases, not limited to air. Formal aerodynamics study in the modern sense began in the eighteenth century, although observations of fundamental concepts such as
      <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">
       aerodynamic drag
      </a>
      have been recorded much earlier. Most of the early efforts in aerodynamics worked towards achieving
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft#Heavier_than_air_.E2.80.93_aerodynes" title="Aircraft">
       heavier-than-air flight
      </a>
      , which was first demonstrated by
      <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">
       Wilbur and Orville Wright
      </a>
      in 1903. Since then, the use of aerodynamics through
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical" title="Mathematical">
       mathematical
      </a>
      analysis, empirical approximations,
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">
       wind tunnel
      </a>
      experimentation, and
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">
       computer simulations
      </a>
      has formed the scientific basis for ongoing developments in heavier-than-air flight and a number of other technologies. Recent work in aerodynamics has focused on issues related to
      <a href="/wiki/Compressible_flow" title="Compressible flow">
       compressible flow
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
       turbulence
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">
       boundary layers
      </a>
      and has become increasingly
      <a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">
       computational
      </a>
      in nature.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_aerodynamics" title="History of aerodynamics">
       History of aerodynamics
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Modern aerodynamics only dates back to the seventeenth century, but aerodynamic forces have been harnessed by humans for thousands of years in sailboats and windmills,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and images and stories of flight appear throughout recorded history,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       Ancient Greek
      </a>
      legend of
      <a href="/wiki/Icarus" title="Icarus">
       Icarus
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Daedalus" title="Daedalus">
       Daedalus
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fundamental concepts of
      <a href="/wiki/Continuum_mechanics" title="Continuum mechanics">
       continuum
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">
       drag
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">
       pressure gradients
      </a>
      appear in the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
       Aristotle
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">
       Archimedes
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andersonhist_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-andersonhist-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1726,
      <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
       Sir Isaac Newton
      </a>
      became the first person to develop a theory of air resistance,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      making him one of the first aerodynamicists.
      <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
       Dutch
      </a>
      -
      <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
       Swiss
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">
       mathematician
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli" title="Daniel Bernoulli">
       Daniel Bernoulli
      </a>
      followed in 1738 with
      <i>
       Hydrodynamica
      </i>
      in which he described a fundamental relationship between pressure, density, and flow velocity for incompressible flow known today as
      <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli%27s_principle" title="Bernoulli's principle">
       Bernoulli's principle
      </a>
      , which provides one method for calculating aerodynamic lift.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1757,
      <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">
       Leonhard Euler
      </a>
      published the more general
      <a href="/wiki/Euler_equations_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Euler equations (fluid dynamics)">
       Euler equations
      </a>
      which could be applied to both compressible and incompressible flows. The Euler equations were extended to incorporate the effects of viscosity in the first half of the 1800s, resulting in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navier-Stokes_equations" title="Navier-Stokes equations">
       Navier-Stokes equations
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Navier-Stokes equations are the most general governing equations of fluid flow and are difficult to solve.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WB_Wind_Tunnel.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/WB_Wind_Tunnel.jpg/220px-WB_Wind_Tunnel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/WB_Wind_Tunnel.jpg/330px-WB_Wind_Tunnel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/WB_Wind_Tunnel.jpg/440px-WB_Wind_Tunnel.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WB_Wind_Tunnel.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A replica of the
        <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">
         Wright brothers
        </a>
        '
        <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">
         wind tunnel
        </a>
        is on display at the Virginia Air and Space Center. Wind tunnels were key in the development and validation of the laws of aerodynamics.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1799,
      <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">
       Sir George Cayley
      </a>
      became the first person to identify the four aerodynamic forces of flight (
      <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">
       weight
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">
       lift
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">
       drag
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">
       thrust
      </a>
      ), as well as the relationships between them,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AerNav123_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AerNav123-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      outlining the work towards achieving heavier-than-air flight for the next century. In 1871,
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_Herbert_Wenham" title="Francis Herbert Wenham">
       Francis Herbert Wenham
      </a>
      constructed the first
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">
       wind tunnel
      </a>
      , allowing precise measurements of aerodynamic forces. Drag theories were developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert" title="Jean le Rond d'Alembert">
       Jean le Rond d'Alembert
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">
       Gustav Kirchhoff
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">
       Lord Rayleigh
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1889,
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Renard" title="Charles Renard">
       Charles Renard
      </a>
      , a French aeronautical engineer, became the first person to reasonably predict the power needed for sustained flight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_Lilienthal" title="Otto Lilienthal">
       Otto Lilienthal
      </a>
      , the first person to become highly successful with glider flights, was also the first to propose thin, curved airfoils that would produce high lift and low drag. Building on these developments as well as research carried out in their own wind tunnel, the
      <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">
       Wright brothers
      </a>
      flew the first powered airplane on December 17, 1903.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the time of the first flights,
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_W._Lanchester" title="Frederick W. Lanchester">
       Frederick W. Lanchester
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Wilhelm_Kutta" title="Martin Wilhelm Kutta">
       Martin Wilhelm Kutta
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Nikolay_Yegorovich_Zhukovsky" title="Nikolay Yegorovich Zhukovsky">
       Nikolai Zhukovsky
      </a>
      independently created theories that connected
      <a href="/wiki/Circulation_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Circulation (fluid dynamics)">
       circulation
      </a>
      of a fluid flow to lift. Kutta and Zhukovsky went on to develop a two-dimensional wing theory. Expanding upon the work of Lanchester,
      <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Prandtl" title="Ludwig Prandtl">
       Ludwig Prandtl
      </a>
      is credited with developing the mathematics
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      behind thin-airfoil and lifting-line theories as well as work with
      <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">
       boundary layers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As aircraft speed increased, designers began to encounter challenges associated with air
      <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">
       compressibility
      </a>
      at speeds near or greater than the speed of sound. The differences in air flows under these conditions led to problems in aircraft control, increased drag due to
      <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
       shock waves
      </a>
      , and structural dangers due to
      <a href="/wiki/Aeroelasticity" title="Aeroelasticity">
       aeroelastic flutter
      </a>
      . The ratio of the flow speed to the speed of sound was named the
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach number
      </a>
      after
      <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mach" title="Ernst Mach">
       Ernst Mach
      </a>
      who was one of the first to investigate the properties of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">
       supersonic
      </a>
      flow.
      <a href="/wiki/William_John_Macquorn_Rankine" title="William John Macquorn Rankine">
       William John Macquorn Rankine
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Henri_Hugoniot" title="Pierre Henri Hugoniot">
       Pierre Henri Hugoniot
      </a>
      independently developed the theory for flow properties before and after a
      <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
       shock wave
      </a>
      , while
      <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Ackeret" title="Jakob Ackeret">
       Jakob Ackeret
      </a>
      led the initial work on calculating the lift and drag of supersonic airfoils.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Theodore_von_K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n" title="Theodore von Kármán">
       Theodore von Kármán
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Latimer_Dryden" title="Hugh Latimer Dryden">
       Hugh Latimer Dryden
      </a>
      introduced the term
      <a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">
       transonic
      </a>
      to describe flow speeds around Mach 1 where drag increases rapidly. This rapid increase in drag led aerodynamicists and aviators to disagree on whether supersonic flight was achievable until the sound barrier was broken for the first time in 1947 using the
      <a href="/wiki/Bell_X-1" title="Bell X-1">
       Bell X-1
      </a>
      aircraft.
     </p>
     <p>
      By the time the sound barrier was broken, much of the subsonic and low supersonic aerodynamics knowledge had matured. The
      <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">
       Cold War
      </a>
      fueled an ever evolving line of high performance aircraft.
      <a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">
       Computational fluid dynamics
      </a>
      began as an effort to solve for flow properties around complex objects and has rapidly grown to the point where entire aircraft can be designed using a computer, with wind-tunnel tests followed by flight tests to confirm the computer predictions. Knowledge of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">
       supersonic
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypersonic" title="Hypersonic">
       hypersonic
      </a>
      aerodynamics has also matured since the 1960s, and the goals of aerodynamicists have shifted from understanding the behavior of fluid flow to understanding how to engineer a vehicle to interact appropriately with the fluid flow. Designing aircraft for supersonic and hypersonic conditions, as well as the desire to improve the aerodynamic efficiency of current aircraft and propulsion systems, continues to fuel new research in aerodynamics, while work continues to be done on important problems in basic aerodynamic theory related to flow turbulence and the existence and uniqueness of analytical solutions to the Navier-Stokes equations.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_concepts">
       Fundamental concepts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fundamental concepts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aeroforces.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="417" data-file-width="909" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Aeroforces.svg/220px-Aeroforces.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Aeroforces.svg/330px-Aeroforces.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Aeroforces.svg/440px-Aeroforces.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aeroforces.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Forces of flight on an
        <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">
         airfoil
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Understanding the motion of air around an object (often called a flow field) enables the calculation of forces and
      <a href="/wiki/Moment_(physics)" title="Moment (physics)">
       moments
      </a>
      acting on the object. In many aerodynamics problems, the forces of interest are the fundamental forces of flight:
      <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">
       lift
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">
       drag
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">
       thrust
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">
       weight
      </a>
      . Of these, lift and drag are aerodynamic forces, i.e. forces due to air flow over a solid body. Calculation of these quantities is often founded upon the assumption that the flow field behaves as a continuum. Continuum flow fields are characterized by properties such as
      <a href="/wiki/Flow_velocity" title="Flow velocity">
       flow velocity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
       density
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
       temperature
      </a>
      , which may be functions of spatial position and time. These properties may be directly or indirectly measured in aerodynamics experiments or calculated from equations for the conservation of mass,
      <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">
       momentum
      </a>
      , and energy in air flows. Density, flow velocity, and an additional property,
      <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
       viscosity
      </a>
      , are used to classify flow fields.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Flow_classification">
       Flow classification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flow classification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Flow velocity is used to classify flows according to speed regime. Subsonic flows are flow fields in which the air speed field is always below the local speed of sound. Transonic flows include both regions of subsonic flow and regions in which the local flow speed is greater than the local speed of sound. Supersonic flows are defined to be flows in which the flow speed is greater than the speed of sound everywhere. A fourth classification, hypersonic flow, refers to flows where the flow speed is much greater than the speed of sound. Aerodynamicists disagree on the precise definition of hypersonic flow.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">
       Compressibility
      </a>
      refers to whether or not the flow in a problem can have a varying density. Subsonic flows are often assumed to be incompressible, i.e. the density is assumed to be constant. Transonic and supersonic flows are compressible, and neglecting the changes in density in these flow fields will yield inaccurate results when performing calculations.
     </p>
     <p>
      Viscosity is associated with the frictional forces in a flow. In some flow fields, viscous effects are very small, and approximate solutions may safely neglect viscous effects. These approximations are called inviscid flows. Flows for which viscosity is not neglected are called viscous flows. Finally, aerodynamic problems may also be classified by the flow environment. External aerodynamics is the study of flow around solid objects of various shapes (e.g. around an airplane wing), while internal aerodynamics is the study of flow through passages inside solid objects (e.g. through a jet engine).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Continuum_assumption">
       Continuum assumption
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Continuum assumption">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Unlike liquids and solids, gases are composed of discrete
      <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
       molecules
      </a>
      which occupy only a small fraction of the volume filled by the gas. On a molecular level, flow fields are made up of many individual collisions between gas molecules and between gas molecules and solid surfaces. However, in most aerodynamics applications, the discrete molecular nature of gases is ignored, and the flow field is assumed to behave as a
      <a href="/wiki/Continuum_mechanics" title="Continuum mechanics">
       continuum
      </a>
      . This assumption allows fluid properties such as density and flow velocity to be defined everywhere within the flow.
     </p>
     <p>
      Validity of the continuum assumption is dependent on the density of the gas and the application in question. For the continuum assumption to be valid, the
      <a href="/wiki/Mean_free_path" title="Mean free path">
       mean free path
      </a>
      length must be much smaller than the length scale of the application in question. For example, many aerodynamics applications deal with aircraft flying in atmospheric conditions, where the mean free path length is on the order of micrometers. In these cases, the length scale of the aircraft ranges from a few meters to a few tens of meters, which is much larger than the mean free path length. For these applications, the continuum assumption is reasonable. The continuum assumption is less valid for extremely low-density flows, such as those encountered by vehicles at very high altitudes (e.g. 300,000 ft/90 km)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andersonhist_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-andersonhist-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      or satellites in
      <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
       Low Earth orbit
      </a>
      . In these cases,
      <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">
       statistical mechanics
      </a>
      is a more accurate method of solving the problem than continuous aerodynamics. The
      <a href="/wiki/Knudsen_number" title="Knudsen number">
       Knudsen number
      </a>
      can be used to guide the choice between statistical mechanics and the continuous formulation of aerodynamics.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_laws">
       Conservation laws
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation laws">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Aerodynamic problems are typically solved using
      <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics#Conservation_laws" title="Fluid dynamics">
       fluid dynamics conservation laws
      </a>
      as applied to a
      <a href="/wiki/Continuum_mechanics" title="Continuum mechanics">
       fluid continuum
      </a>
      . Three conservation principles are used:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_mass" title="Conservation of mass">
        Conservation of mass
       </a>
       : In fluid dynamics, the mathematical formulation of this principle is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Continuity_equation#Fluid_dynamics" title="Continuity equation">
        mass continuity equation
       </a>
       , which requires that mass is neither created nor destroyed within a flow of interest.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum" title="Conservation of momentum">
        Conservation of momentum
       </a>
       : In fluid dynamics, the mathematical formulation of this principle can be considered an application of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton%27s_Second_Law" title="Newton's Second Law">
        Newton's Second Law
       </a>
       . Momentum within a flow of interest is only created or destroyed due to the work of external forces, which may include both
       <a href="/wiki/Surface_force" title="Surface force">
        surface forces
       </a>
       , such as viscous (
       <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">
        frictional
       </a>
       ) forces, and
       <a href="/wiki/Body_force" title="Body force">
        body forces
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        weight
       </a>
       . The momentum conservation principle may be expressed as either a single
       <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">
        vector
       </a>
       equation or a set of three
       <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics)" title="Scalar (mathematics)">
        scalar
       </a>
       equations, derived from the components of the three-dimensional flow velocity vector. In its most complete form, the momentum conservation equations are known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navier-Stokes_equations" title="Navier-Stokes equations">
        Navier-Stokes equations
       </a>
       . The Navier-Stokes equations have no known analytical solution and are solved in modern aerodynamics using
       <a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">
        computational techniques
       </a>
       . Because of the computational cost of solving these complex equations, simplified expressions of momentum conservation may be appropriate for specific applications. The
       <a href="/wiki/Euler_equations_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Euler equations (fluid dynamics)">
        Euler equations
       </a>
       are a set of momentum conservation equations which neglect viscous forces used widely by modern aerodynamicists in cases where the effect of viscous forces is expected to be small. Additionally,
       <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli%27s_principle" title="Bernoulli's principle">
        Bernoulli's equation
       </a>
       is a solution to the momentum conservation equation of an inviscid flow, neglecting gravity.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">
        Conservation of energy
       </a>
       : The energy conservation equation states that energy is neither created nor destroyed within a flow, and that any addition or subtraction of energy is due either to the fluid flow in and out of the region of interest,
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
        heat transfer
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">
        work
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">
       ideal gas law
      </a>
      or another
      <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state" title="Equation of state">
       equation of state
      </a>
      is often used in conjunction with these equations to form a determined system to solve for unknown variables.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Branches_of_aerodynamics">
       Branches of aerodynamics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Branches of aerodynamics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Aerodynamic problems are classified by the flow environment or properties of the flow, including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flow_speed" title="Flow speed">
       flow speed
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">
       compressibility
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
       viscosity
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       External
      </i>
      aerodynamics is the study of flow around solid objects of various shapes. Evaluating the
      <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">
       lift
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">
       drag
      </a>
      on an
      <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">
       airplane
      </a>
      or the
      <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
       shock waves
      </a>
      that form in front of the nose of a
      <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">
       rocket
      </a>
      are examples of external aerodynamics.
      <i>
       Internal
      </i>
      aerodynamics is the study of flow through passages in solid objects. For instance, internal aerodynamics encompasses the study of the airflow through a
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">
       jet engine
      </a>
      or through an
      <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">
       air conditioning
      </a>
      pipe.
     </p>
     <p>
      Aerodynamic problems can also be classified according to whether the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flow_speed" title="Flow speed">
       flow speed
      </a>
      is below, near or above the
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">
       speed of sound
      </a>
      . A problem is called subsonic if all the speeds in the problem are less than the speed of sound,
      <a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">
       transonic
      </a>
      if speeds both below and above the speed of sound are present (normally when the characteristic speed is approximately the speed of sound),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">
       supersonic
      </a>
      when the characteristic flow speed is greater than the speed of sound, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypersonic" title="Hypersonic">
       hypersonic
      </a>
      when the flow speed is much greater than the speed of sound. Aerodynamicists disagree over the precise definition of hypersonic flow; a rough definition considers flows with
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach numbers
      </a>
      above 5 to be hypersonic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andersonhist_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-andersonhist-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The influence of
      <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
       viscosity
      </a>
      in the flow dictates a third classification. Some problems may encounter only very small viscous effects on the solution, in which case viscosity can be considered to be negligible. The approximations to these problems are called
      <a href="/wiki/Inviscid_flow" title="Inviscid flow">
       inviscid flows
      </a>
      . Flows for which viscosity cannot be neglected are called viscous flows.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Incompressible_aerodynamics">
       Incompressible aerodynamics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Incompressible aerodynamics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Incompressible_flow" title="Incompressible flow">
       incompressible flow
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      An incompressible flow is a flow in which density is constant in both time and space. Although all real fluids are compressible, a flow problem is often considered incompressible if the effect of the density changes in the problem on the outputs of interest is small. This is more likely to be true when the flow speeds are significantly lower than the speed of sound. Effects of compressibility are more significant at speeds close to or above the speed of sound. The
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach number
      </a>
      is used to evaluate whether the incompressibility can be assumed or the flow must be solved as compressible.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Subsonic_flow">
       Subsonic flow
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Subsonic flow">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Subsonic (or low-speed) aerodynamics studies fluid motion in flows which are much lower than the speed of sound everywhere in the flow. There are several branches of subsonic flow but one special case arises when the flow is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inviscid" title="Inviscid">
       inviscid
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">
       incompressible
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrotational" title="Irrotational">
       irrotational
      </a>
      . This case is called
      <a href="/wiki/Potential_flow" title="Potential flow">
       potential flow
      </a>
      and allows the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_equations" title="Differential equations">
       differential equations
      </a>
      used to be a simplified version of the governing equations of
      <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">
       fluid dynamics
      </a>
      , thus making available to the aerodynamicist a range of quick and easy solutions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In solving a subsonic problem, one decision to be made by the aerodynamicist is whether to incorporate the effects of compressibility. Compressibility is a description of the amount of change of
      <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
       density
      </a>
      in the problem. When the effects of compressibility on the solution are small, the aerodynamicist may choose to assume that density is constant. The problem is then an incompressible low-speed aerodynamics problem. When the density is allowed to vary, the problem is called a compressible problem. In air, compressibility effects are usually ignored when the
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach number
      </a>
      in the flow does not exceed 0.3 (about 335 feet (102 m) per second or 228 miles (366 km) per hour at 60 °F (16 °C)). Above 0.3, the problem should be solved by using compressible aerodynamics.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Compressible_aerodynamics">
       Compressible aerodynamics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Compressible aerodynamics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Compressible_flow" title="Compressible flow">
       Compressible flow
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to the theory of aerodynamics, a flow is considered to be compressible if its change in
      <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
       density
      </a>
      with respect to
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      is non-zero along a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streamlines,_streaklines_and_pathlines" title="Streamlines, streaklines and pathlines">
       streamline
      </a>
      . This means that – unlike incompressible flow – changes in density must be considered. In general, this is the case where the
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach number
      </a>
      in part or all of the flow exceeds 0.3. The Mach .3 value is rather arbitrary, but it is used because gas flows with a Mach number below that value demonstrate changes in density with respect to the change in pressure of less than 5%. Furthermore, that maximum 5% density change occurs at the
      <a href="/wiki/Stagnation_point" title="Stagnation point">
       stagnation point
      </a>
      of an object immersed in the gas flow and the density changes around the rest of the object will be significantly lower. Transonic, supersonic, and hypersonic flows are all compressible.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transonic_flow">
       Transonic flow
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transonic flow">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">
       Transonic
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The term Transonic refers to a range of flow velocities just below and above the local
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">
       speed of sound
      </a>
      (generally taken as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mach_Number" title="Mach Number">
       Mach
      </a>
      0.8–1.2). It is defined as the range of speeds between the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_mach" title="Critical mach">
       critical Mach number
      </a>
      , when some parts of the airflow over an aircraft become
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">
       supersonic
      </a>
      , and a higher speed, typically near
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach 1.2
      </a>
      , when all of the airflow is supersonic. Between these speeds, some of the airflow is supersonic, while some of the airflow is not supersonic.
     </p>
     <p>
      <span id="Supersonic_aerodynamics">
      </span>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Supersonic_flow">
       Supersonic flow
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Supersonic flow">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">
       Supersonic
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Supersonic aerodynamic problems are those involving flow speeds greater than the speed of sound. Calculating the lift on the
      <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">
       Concorde
      </a>
      during cruise can be an example of a supersonic aerodynamic problem.
     </p>
     <p>
      Supersonic flow behaves very differently from subsonic flow. Fluids react to differences in pressure; pressure changes are how a fluid is "told" to respond to its environment. Therefore, since
      <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">
       sound
      </a>
      is in fact an infinitesimal pressure difference propagating through a fluid, the
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">
       speed of sound
      </a>
      in that fluid can be considered the fastest speed that "information" can travel in the flow. This difference most obviously manifests itself in the case of a fluid striking an object. In front of that object, the fluid builds up a
      <a href="/wiki/Stagnation_pressure" title="Stagnation pressure">
       stagnation pressure
      </a>
      as impact with the object brings the moving fluid to rest. In fluid traveling at subsonic speed, this pressure disturbance can propagate upstream, changing the flow pattern ahead of the object and giving the impression that the fluid "knows" the object is there and is avoiding it. However, in a supersonic flow, the pressure disturbance cannot propagate upstream. Thus, when the fluid finally does strike the object, it is forced to change its properties –
      <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
       temperature
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
       density
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">
       Mach number
      </a>
      —in an extremely violent and
      <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">
       irreversible
      </a>
      fashion called a
      <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
       shock wave
      </a>
      . The presence of shock waves, along with the compressibility effects of high-flow velocity (see
      <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">
       Reynolds number
      </a>
      ) fluids, is the central difference between supersonic and subsonic aerodynamics problems.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hypersonic_flow">
       Hypersonic flow
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hypersonic flow">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypersonic" title="Hypersonic">
       Hypersonic
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In aerodynamics, hypersonic speeds are speeds that are highly supersonic. In the 1970s, the term generally came to refer to speeds of Mach 5 (5 times the speed of sound) and above. The hypersonic regime is a subset of the supersonic regime. Hypersonic flow is characterized by high temperature flow behind a shock wave, viscous interaction, and chemical dissociation of gas.
     </p>

     <p>
      The incompressible and compressible flow regimes produce many associated phenomena, such as boundary layers and turbulence.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_layers">
       Boundary layers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Boundary layers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">
       Boundary layer
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The concept of a
      <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">
       boundary layer
      </a>
      is important in many aerodynamic problems. The viscosity and fluid friction in the air is approximated as being significant only in this thin layer. This principle makes aerodynamics much more tractable mathematically.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Turbulence">
       Turbulence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Turbulence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
       Turbulence
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In aerodynamics, turbulence is characterized by chaotic, stochastic property changes in the flow. This includes low momentum diffusion, high momentum convection, and rapid variation of pressure and flow velocity in space and time. Flow that is not turbulent is called
      <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">
       laminar flow
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynamics_in_other_fields">
       Aerodynamics in other fields
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Aerodynamics in other fields">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Automotive_aerodynamics" title="Automotive aerodynamics">
       Automotive aerodynamics
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Aerodynamics is important in a number of applications other than aerospace engineering. It is a significant factor in any type of
      <a href="/wiki/Automotive_engineering" title="Automotive engineering">
       vehicle design
      </a>
      , including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">
       automobiles
      </a>
      . It is important in the prediction of forces and moments in
      <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">
       sailing
      </a>
      . It is used in the design of mechanical components such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_drive" title="Hard drive">
       hard drive
      </a>
      heads.
      <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineering" title="Structural engineering">
       Structural engineers
      </a>
      also use aerodynamics, and particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Aeroelasticity" title="Aeroelasticity">
       aeroelasticity
      </a>
      , to calculate
      <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">
       wind
      </a>
      loads in the design of large buildings and
      <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">
       bridges
      </a>
      . Urban aerodynamics seeks to help
      <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">
       town planners
      </a>
      and designers improve comfort in outdoor spaces, create urban microclimates and reduce the effects of urban pollution. The field of environmental aerodynamics studies the ways
      <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">
       atmospheric circulation
      </a>
      and flight mechanics affect ecosystems. The aerodynamics of internal passages is important in
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       heating/ventilation
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duct_(HVAC)" title="Duct (HVAC)">
       gas piping
      </a>
      , and in
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       automotive engines
      </a>
      where detailed flow patterns strongly affect the performance of the engine. People who do
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine_design" title="Wind turbine design">
       wind turbine design
      </a>
      use aerodynamics. A few aerodynamic equations are used as part of
      <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">
       numerical weather prediction
      </a>
      .
     </p>

          <q>
           Sir George Cayley, born in 1773, is sometimes called the Father of Aviation. A pioneer in his field, he was the first to identify the four aerodynamic forces of flight – weight, lift, drag, and thrust and their relationship. He was also the first to build a successful human-carrying glider. Cayley described many of the concepts and elements of the modern airplane and was the first to understand and explain in engineering terms the concepts of lift and thrust.
