     <p>
      <b>
       Waste management
      </b>
      is all the activities and actions required to manage waste from its inception to its final disposal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN-1997_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN-1997-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This includes amongst other things, collection, transport, treatment and disposal of
      <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">
       waste
      </a>
      together with monitoring and regulation. It also encompasses the legal and regulatory framework that relates to waste management encompassing guidance on recycling etc.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term normally relates to all kinds of waste, whether generated during the extraction of
      <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">
       raw materials
      </a>
      , the processing of raw materials into intermediate and final products, the consumption of final products, or other human activities,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN-1997_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN-1997-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      including municipal (residential, institutional, commercial), agricultural, and social (health care, household hazardous wastes, sewage sludge).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WM_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WM-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Waste management is intended to reduce adverse effects of waste on
      <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">
       health
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">
       environment
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">
       aesthetics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Waste management practices are not uniform among countries (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_nation" title="Developed nation">
       developed
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_nation" title="Developing nation">
       developing nations
      </a>
      ); regions (
      <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">
       urban
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">
       rural area
      </a>
      ), and sectors (
      <a href="/wiki/Residential_area" title="Residential area">
       residential
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">
       industrial
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       waste hierarchy
      </a>
      refers to the "3 Rs"
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduce_(waste)" title="Reduce (waste)">
       reduce
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">
       reuse
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">
       recycle
      </a>
      , which classify waste management strategies according to their desirability in terms of
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_minimisation" title="Waste minimisation">
       waste minimisation
      </a>
      . The waste hierarchy remains the cornerstone of most waste minimisation strategies. The aim of the waste hierarchy is to extract the maximum practical benefits from products and to generate the minimum amount of waste; see:
      <a href="/wiki/Resource_recovery" title="Resource recovery">
       resource recovery
      </a>
      .The waste hierarchy is represented as a pyramid because the basic premise is for policy to take action first and prevent the generation of waste. The next step or preferred action is to reduce the generation of waste i.e. by re-use. The next is recycling which would include composting. Following this step is material recovery and
      <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">
       waste-to-energy
      </a>
      . Energy can be recovered from processes i.e. landfill and combustion, at this level of the hierarchy. The final action is disposal, in landfills or through incineration without energy recovery. This last step is the final resort for waste which has not been prevented, diverted or recovered.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The waste hierarchy represents the progression of a product or material through the sequential stages of the pyramid of waste management. The hierarchy represents the latter parts of the life-cycle for each product.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life-cycle_of_a_product">
       Life-cycle of a product
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life-cycle of a product">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The life-cycle begins with design, then proceeds through manufacture, distribution, use and then follows through the waste hierarchy's stages of reduce, reuse and recycle. Each of the above stages of the life-cycle offers opportunities for policy intervention, to rethink the need for the product, to redesign to minimize waste potential, to extend its use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The key behind the life-cycle of a product is to optimize the use of the world's limited resources by avoiding the unnecessary generation of waste.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_efficiency">
       Resource efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Resource efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Resource_efficiency" title="Resource efficiency">
       Resource efficiency
      </a>
      reflects the understanding that current, global, economic growth and development can not be sustained with the current production and consumption patterns. Globally, we are extracting more resources to produce goods than the planet can replenish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Resource efficiency is the reduction of the environmental impact from the production and consumption of these goods, from final raw material extraction to last use and disposal. This process of resource efficiency can address
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
       sustainability
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Polluter_pays_principle">
       Polluter pays principle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Polluter pays principle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Polluter_pays_principle" title="Polluter pays principle">
       Polluter pays principle
      </a>
      is a principle where the polluting party pays for the impact caused to the environment. With respect to waste management, this generally refers to the requirement for a waste generator to pay for appropriate disposal of the unrecoverable material.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_waste_management" title="History of waste management">
       History of waste management
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Throughout most of history, the amount of
      <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">
       waste
      </a>
      generated by humans was insignificant due to low
      <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">
       population density
      </a>
      and low societal levels of the exploitation of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">
       natural resources
      </a>
      . Common waste produced during pre-modern times was mainly ashes and human
      <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_waste" title="Biodegradable waste">
       biodegradable waste
      </a>
      , and these were released back into the ground locally, with minimum
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">
       environmental impact
      </a>
      . Tools made out of
      <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">
       wood
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">
       metal
      </a>
      were generally reused or passed down through the generations.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, some civilizations do seem to have been more profligate in their waste output than others. In particular, the
      <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">
       Maya
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
       Central America
      </a>
      had a fixed monthly ritual, in which the people of the village would gather together and burn their rubbish in large dumps.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">
       Modern era
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern era">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SirEdwinChadwick.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1544" data-file-width="1160" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/SirEdwinChadwick.jpg/170px-SirEdwinChadwick.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/SirEdwinChadwick.jpg/255px-SirEdwinChadwick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/SirEdwinChadwick.jpg/340px-SirEdwinChadwick.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SirEdwinChadwick.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Sir
        <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Chadwick" title="Edwin Chadwick">
         Edwin Chadwick
        </a>
        's 1842 report
        <i>
         The Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population
        </i>
        was influential in securing the passage of the first legislation aimed at waste clearance and disposal.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Following the onset of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">
       industrialisation
      </a>
      and the sustained urban growth of large population centres in
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      , the buildup of waste in the cities caused a rapid deterioration in levels of
      <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">
       sanitation
      </a>
      and the general quality of urban life. The streets became choked with filth due to the lack of waste clearance regulations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Calls for the establishment of a municipal authority with waste removal powers occurred as early as 1751, when
      <a href="/wiki/Corbyn_Morris" title="Corbyn Morris">
       Corbyn Morris
      </a>
      in London proposed that "...as the preservation of the health of the people is of great importance, it is proposed that the cleaning of this city, should be put under one uniform public management, and all the filth be...conveyed by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thames" title="Thames">
       Thames
      </a>
      to proper distance in the country".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lewis-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, it was not until the mid-19th century, spurred by increasingly devastating
      <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">
       cholera
      </a>
      outbreaks and the emergence of a public health debate that the first legislation on the issue emerged. Highly influential in this new focus was the report
      <i>
       The Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population
      </i>
      in 1842
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_reform" title="Social reform">
       social reformer
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Chadwick" title="Edwin Chadwick">
       Edwin Chadwick
      </a>
      , in which he argued for the importance of adequate waste removal and management facilities to improve the health and wellbeing of the city's population.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the UK, the Nuisance Removal and Disease Prevention Act of 1846 began what was to be a steadily evolving process of the provision of regulated waste management in London. The
      <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Board_of_Works" title="Metropolitan Board of Works">
       Metropolitan Board of Works
      </a>
      was the first city-wide authority that centralized sanitation regulation for the rapidly expanding city and the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_Health_Act_1875" title="Public Health Act 1875">
       Public Health Act 1875
      </a>
      made it compulsory for every household to deposit their weekly waste in "moveable receptacles: for disposal—the first concept for a dust-bin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWRA_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NWRA-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Manlove,_Alliott_furnace.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="640" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Manlove%2C_Alliott_furnace.jpg/220px-Manlove%2C_Alliott_furnace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Manlove%2C_Alliott_furnace.jpg/330px-Manlove%2C_Alliott_furnace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Manlove%2C_Alliott_furnace.jpg/440px-Manlove%2C_Alliott_furnace.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Manlove,_Alliott_furnace.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Manlove,_Alliott_%26_Co._Ltd." title="Manlove, Alliott &amp; Co. Ltd.">
         Manlove, Alliott &amp; Co. Ltd.
        </a>
        1894 destructor furnace. The use of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incinerator" title="Incinerator">
         incinerators
        </a>
        for waste disposal became popular in the late 19th century.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The dramatic increase in waste for disposal led to the creation of the first
      <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">
       incineration
      </a>
      plants, or, as they were then called, "destructors". In 1874, the first incinerator was built in
      <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">
       Nottingham
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Manlove,_Alliott_%26_Co._Ltd." title="Manlove, Alliott &amp; Co. Ltd.">
       Manlove, Alliott &amp; Co. Ltd.
      </a>
      to the design of Albert Fryer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lewis-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, these were met with opposition on account of the large amounts of ash they produced and which wafted over the neighbouring areas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Similar municipal systems of waste disposal sprung up at the turn of the 20th century in other large cities of
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      . In 1895,
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      became the first U.S. city with public-sector garbage management.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWRA_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NWRA-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Early
      <a href="/wiki/Garbage_truck" title="Garbage truck">
       garbage removal trucks
      </a>
      were simply open bodied
      <a href="/wiki/Dump_truck" title="Dump truck">
       dump trucks
      </a>
      pulled by a team of horses. They became motorized in the early part of the 20th century and the first close body trucks to eliminate odours with a dumping lever mechanism were introduced in the 1920s in Britain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      These were soon equipped with 'hopper mechanisms' where the scooper was loaded at floor level and then hoisted mechanically to deposit the waste in the truck. The
      <a href="/wiki/Garwood_Load_Packer" title="Garwood Load Packer">
       Garwood Load Packer
      </a>
      was the first truck in 1938, to incorporate a hydraulic compactor.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_handling_and_transport">
       Waste handling and transport
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Waste handling and transport">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main articles:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_collection_vehicle" title="Waste collection vehicle">
       Waste collection vehicle
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dustbin" title="Dustbin">
       Dustbin
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_sorting" title="Waste sorting">
       Waste sorting
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bin.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1232" data-file-width="1036" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bin.JPG/220px-Bin.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bin.JPG/330px-Bin.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bin.JPG/440px-Bin.JPG 2x" width="220"/>

       <div class="thumbcaption">

         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bin.JPG" title="Enlarge">

        , England
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Waste collection methods vary widely among different countries and regions. Domestic waste collection services are often provided by local government authorities, or by private companies for industrial and commercial waste. Some areas, especially those in less developed countries, do not have formal waste-collection systems.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_handling_practices">
       Waste handling practices
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Waste handling practices">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curbside_collection" title="Curbside collection">
       Curbside collection
      </a>
      is the most common method of disposal in most European countries,
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      and many other parts of the developed world in which waste is collected at regular intervals by specialised trucks. This is often associated with curb-side waste segregation. In rural areas waste may need to be taken to a transfer station. Waste collected is then transported to an appropriate disposal facility. In some areas, vacuum collection is used in which waste is transported from the home or commercial premises by vacuum along small bore tubes. Systems are in use in Europe and North America.
     </p>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Automated_vacuum_collection" title="Automated vacuum collection">
       Automated vacuum collection
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       Pyrolysis
      </a>
      is used to dispose of some wastes including tires, a process that can produce recovered fuels, steel and heat. In some cases tires can provide the feedstock for cement manufacture. Such systems are used in USA, California, Australia, Greece, Mexico, the United Kingdom and in Israel. The RESEM pyrolysis plant that has been operational at
      <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
       Texas
      </a>
      USA since December 2011, and processes up to 60 tons per day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some jurisdictions unsegregated waste is collected at the curb-side or from waste transfer stations and then sorted into recyclables and unusable waste. Such systems are capable of sorting large volumes of solid waste, salvaging recyclables, and turning the rest into bio-gas and soil conditioner. In
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
       San Francisco
      </a>
      , the local government established its
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.sfenvironment.org/article/recycling-and-composting/mandatory-recycling-and-composting-ordinance" rel="nofollow">
       Mandatory Recycling and Composting Ordinance
      </a>
      in support of its goal of
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.sfenvironment.org/zero-waste/overview/goals" rel="nofollow">
       zero waste by 2020
      </a>
      , requiring everyone in the city to keep recyclables and compostables out of the landfill. The three streams are collected with the curbside "
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.calrecycle.ca.gov/LGCentral/Library/innovations/curbside/CaseStudy.htm" rel="nofollow">
       Fantastic 3
      </a>
      " bin system - blue for recyclables, green for compostables, and black for landfill-bound materials - provided to residents and businesses and serviced by San Francisco's sole refuse hauler, Recology. The City's "Pay-As-You-Throw" system charges customers by the volume of landfill-bound materials, which provides a financial incentive to separate recyclables and compostables from other discards. The
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.sfenvironment.org/zero-waste" rel="nofollow">
       City's Department of the Environment's Zero Waste Program
      </a>
      has led the City to achieve 80% diversion, the highest diversion rate in North America.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other businesses such as
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_Industries" title="Waste Industries">
       Waste Industries
      </a>
      use a variety of colors to distinguish between trash and recycling cans.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_models">
       Financial models
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Financial models">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In most developed countries, domestic waste disposal is funded from a national or local tax which may be related to income, or notional house value. Commercial and industrial waste disposal is typically charged for as a commercial service, often as an integrated charge which includes disposal costs. This practice may encourage disposal contractors to opt for the cheapest disposal option such as landfill rather than the environmentally best solution such as re-use and recycling. In some areas such as
      <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">
       Taipei
      </a>
      , the city government charges its households and industries for the volume of rubbish they produce. Waste will only be collected by the city council if waste is disposed in government issued rubbish bags. This policy has successfully reduced the amount of waste the city produces and increased the recycling rate.

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Incineration">
       Incineration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Incineration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">
       Incineration
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Incineration is a disposal method in which solid organic wastes are subjected to combustion so as to convert them into residue and gaseous products. This method is useful for disposal of residue of both solid waste management and solid residue from waste water management. This process reduces the volumes of solid waste to 20 to 30 percent of the original volume. Incineration and other high temperature waste treatment systems are sometimes described as "
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_treatment" title="Thermal treatment">
       thermal treatment
      </a>
      ". Incinerators convert waste materials into
      <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">
       heat
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
       gas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">
       steam
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Incineration#Solid_outputs" title="Incineration">
       ash
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Incineration is carried out both on a small scale by individuals and on a large scale by industry. It is used to dispose of solid, liquid and gaseous waste. It is recognized as a practical method of disposing of certain
      <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">
       hazardous waste
      </a>
      materials (such as biological
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_waste" title="Medical waste">
       medical waste
      </a>
      ). Incineration is a controversial method of waste disposal, due to issues such as emission of gaseous
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollutants" title="Pollutants">
       pollutants
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Incineration is common in countries such as
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      where land is more scarce, as these facilities generally do not require as much area as landfills.
      <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">
       Waste-to-energy
      </a>
      (WtE) or energy-from-waste (EfW) are broad terms for facilities that burn waste in a furnace or boiler to generate heat, steam or electricity. Combustion in an incinerator is not always perfect and there have been concerns about pollutants in gaseous emissions from incinerator stacks. Particular concern has focused on some very persistent
      <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">
       organic compounds
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">
       dioxins
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">
       furans
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">
       PAHs
      </a>
      , which may be created and which may have serious environmental consequences.
     </p>

     <p>
      Recycling is a
      <a href="/wiki/Resource_recovery" title="Resource recovery">
       resource recovery
      </a>
      practice that refers to the collection and reuse of waste materials such as empty beverage containers. The materials from which the items are made can be reprocessed into new products. Material for recycling may be collected separately from general waste using dedicated bins and collection vehicles, a procedure called
      <a href="/wiki/Kerbside_collection" title="Kerbside collection">
       kerbside collection
      </a>
      . In some communities, the owner of the waste is required to separate the materials into different bins (e.g. for paper, plastics, metals) prior to its collection. In other communities, all recyclable materials are placed in a single bin for collection, and the sorting is handled later at a central facility. The latter method is known as "
      <a href="/wiki/Single-stream_recycling" title="Single-stream recycling">
       single-stream recycling
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The most common consumer products recycled include
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
       aluminium
      </a>
      such as beverages cans,
      <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
       copper
      </a>
      such as wire,
      <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">
       steel
      </a>
      from food and aerosol cans, old steel furnishings or equipment, rubber
      <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">
       tyres
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDPE" title="HDPE">
       polyethylene
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recycling_of_PET_Bottles" title="Recycling of PET Bottles">
       PET
      </a>
      bottles,
      <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">
       glass
      </a>
      bottles and jars,
      <a href="/wiki/Paperboard" title="Paperboard">
       paperboard
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Carton" title="Carton">
       cartons
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newspapers" title="Newspapers">
       newspapers
      </a>
      , magazines and light paper, and
      <a href="/wiki/Corrugated_fiberboard" title="Corrugated fiberboard">
       corrugated fiberboard
      </a>
      boxes.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">
       PVC
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDPE" title="LDPE">
       LDPE
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">
       PP
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">
       PS
      </a>
      (see
      <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">
       resin identification code
      </a>
      ) are also recyclable. These items are usually composed of a single type of material, making them relatively easy to recycle into new products. The recycling of complex products (such as computers and electronic equipment) is more difficult, due to the additional dismantling and separation required.
     </p>
     <p>
      The type of material accepted for recycling varies by city and country. Each city and country has different recycling programs in place that can handle the various types of recyclable materials. However, certain variation in acceptance is reflected in the resale value of the material once it is reprocessed.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_recovery">
       Energy recovery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Energy recovery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">
       Waste-to-energy
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Energy recovery from waste is the conversion of non-recyclable waste materials into usable heat, electricity, or fuel through a variety of processes, including combustion, gasification, pyrolyzation, anaerobic digestion, and landfill gas recovery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USEPA_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-USEPA-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This process is often called waste-to-energy. Energy recovery from waste is part of the non-hazardous waste management hierarchy. Using energy recovery to convert non-recyclable waste materials into electricity and heat, generates a renewable energy source and can reduce carbon emissions by offsetting the need for energy from fossil sources as well as reduce methane generation from landfills.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USEPA_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-USEPA-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Globally, waste-to-energy accounts for 16% of waste management.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The energy content of waste products can be harnessed directly by using them as a direct combustion fuel, or indirectly by processing them into another type of fuel. Thermal treatment ranges from using waste as a fuel source for cooking or heating and the use of the gas fuel (see above), to fuel for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boilers" title="Boilers">
       boilers
      </a>
      to generate steam and electricity in a
      <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">
       turbine
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       Pyrolysis
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">
       gasification
      </a>
      are two related forms of thermal treatment where waste materials are heated to high temperatures with limited
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      availability. The process usually occurs in a sealed vessel under high
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      . Pyrolysis of solid waste converts the material into solid, liquid and gas products. The liquid and gas can be burnt to produce energy or refined into other chemical products (chemical refinery). The solid residue (char) can be further refined into products such as
      <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">
       activated carbon
      </a>
      . Gasification and advanced
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_arc_gasification" title="Plasma arc gasification">
       Plasma arc gasification
      </a>
      are used to convert organic materials directly into a synthetic gas (
      <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">
       syngas
      </a>
      ) composed of
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      . The gas is then burnt to produce electricity and
      <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">
       steam
      </a>
      . An alternative to pyrolysis is high temperature and pressure supercritical water decomposition (hydrothermal monophasic oxidation).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrolysis">
       Pyrolysis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Pyrolysis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       Pyrolysis
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       Pyrolysis
      </a>
      is a process of thermo-chemical decomposition of organic materials by heat in the absence of
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      which produces various hydrocarbon gases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      During pyrolysis, the molecules of object are subjected to very high temperatures leading to very high vibrations. Therefore, every molecule in the object is stretched and shaken to an extent that molecules starts breaking down. The rate of pyrolysis increases with
      <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
       temperature
      </a>
      . In industrial applications, temperatures are above 430 °C (800 °F).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fast pyrolysis produces liquid fuel for feedstocks like wood. Slow pyrolysis produces gases and solid charcoal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pyrolysis hold promise for conversion of waste biomass into useful liquid fuel. Pyrolysis of waste plastics can produce millions of litres of fuel. Solid products of this process contain metals, glass, sand and pyrolysis coke which cannot be converted to gas in the process.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_recovery">
       Resource recovery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Resource recovery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Resource_recovery" title="Resource recovery">
       Resource recovery
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Resource recovery is the systematic diversion of waste, which was intended for disposal, for a specific next use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is the processing of recyclables to extract or recover materials and resources, or convert to energy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montana_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Montana-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      These activities are performed at a resource recovery facility.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montana_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Montana-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Resource recovery is not only environmentally important, but it is also cost effective.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-traverse_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-traverse-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It decreases the amount of waste for disposal, saves space in landfills, and conserves natural resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-traverse_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-traverse-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Resource recovery (as opposed to waste management) uses LCA (life cycle analysis) attempts to offer alternatives to waste management. For mixed MSW (Municipal Solid Waste) a number of broad studies have indicated that administration, source separation and collection followed by reuse and recycling of the non-organic fraction and energy and compost/fertilizer production of the organic material via anaerobic digestion to be the favoured path.
     </p>
     <p>
      As an example of how resource recycling can be beneficial, many of the items thrown away contain precious metals which can be recycled to create a profit, such as the components in circuit boards. Other industries can also benefit from resource recycling with the wood chippings in pallets and other packaging materials being passed onto sectors such as the horticultural profession. In this instance, workers can use the recycled chips to create paths, walkways, or arena surfaces.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">
       Sustainability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sustainability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The management of waste is a key component in a business' ability to maintaining
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO14001" title="ISO14001">
       ISO14001
      </a>
      accreditation. Companies are encouraged to improve their environmental efficiencies each year by eliminating waste through
      <a href="/wiki/Resource_recovery" title="Resource recovery">
       resource recovery
      </a>
      practices, which are sustainability-related activities. One way to do this is by shifting away from waste management to resource recovery practices like
      <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">
       recycling
      </a>
      materials such as glass, food scraps, paper and cardboard, plastic bottles and metal. a lot of conferences will discuss this topic as the international Conference on Green Urabnism which will be held in Italy From 12–14 October 2016
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Avoidance_and_reduction_methods">
       Avoidance and reduction methods
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Avoidance and reduction methods">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_minimization" title="Waste minimization">
       Waste minimization
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      An important method of waste management is the prevention of waste material being created, also known as waste reduction. Methods of avoidance include reuse of second-hand products, repairing broken items instead of buying new, designing products to be refillable or reusable (such as cotton instead of plastic shopping bags), encouraging consumers to avoid using disposable products (such as disposable
      <a href="/wiki/Cutlery" title="Cutlery">
       cutlery
      </a>
      ), removing any food/liquid remains from cans and packaging,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and designing products that use less material to achieve the same purpose (for example, lightweighting of beverage cans).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="International_waste_movement">
       International waste movement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: International waste movement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While waste transport within a given country falls under national regulations,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_trade" title="Waste trade">
       trans-boundary movement of waste
      </a>
      is often subject to international treaties. A major concern to many countries in the world has been
      <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">
       hazardous waste
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Basel_Convention" title="Basel Convention">
       Basel Convention
      </a>
      , ratified by 172 countries, deprecates movement of hazardous waste from developed to less developed countries. The provisions of the Basel convention have been integrated into the EU waste shipment regulation.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_waste" title="Nuclear waste">
       Nuclear waste
      </a>
      , although considered hazardous, does not fall under the jurisdiction of the Basel Convention.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">
       Benefits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Benefits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Waste is not something that should be discarded or disposed of with no regard for future use. It can be a valuable resource if addressed correctly, through policy and practice. With rational and consistent waste management practices there is an opportunity to reap a range of benefits. Those benefits include:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Economic - Improving economic efficiency through the means of resource use, treatment and disposal and creating markets for recycles can lead to efficient practices in the production and consumption of products and materials resulting in valuable materials being recovered for reuse and the potential for new jobs and new business opportunities.
      </li>
      <li>
       Social - By reducing adverse impacts on health by proper waste management practices, the resulting consequences are more appealing settlements. Better social advantages can lead to new sources of employment and potentially lifting communities out of poverty especially in some of the developing poorer countries and cities.
      </li>
      <li>
       Environmental - Reducing or eliminating adverse impacts on the environmental through reducing, reusing and recycling, and minimizing resource extraction can provide improved air and water quality and help in the reduction of
       <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
        greenhouse gas
       </a>
       emissions.
      </li>
      <li>
       Inter-generational Equity - Following effective waste management practices can provide subsequent generations a more robust economy, a fairer and more
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_inclusion" title="Social inclusion">
        inclusive society
       </a>
       and a cleaner environment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-UN-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_in_developing_countries">
       Challenges in developing countries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Challenges in developing countries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Waste management in cities with developing economies and economies in transition experience exhausted waste collection services, inadequately managed and uncontrolled dumpsites and the problems are worsening.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_4-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Problems with governance also complicate the situation. Waste management, in these countries and cities, is an ongoing challenge and many struggle due to weak institutions, chronic under-resourcing and rapid urbanization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_4-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-UN-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      All of these challenges along with the lack of understanding of different factors that contribute to the hierarchy of waste management, affect the treatment of waste.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Technologies">
       Technologies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Technologies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Traditionally the waste management industry has been a late adopter of new technologies such as
      <a href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_identification" title="Radio-frequency identification">
       RFID
      </a>
      (Radio Frequency Identification) tags, GPS and integrated software packages which enable better quality data to be collected without the use of estimation or manual data entry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
