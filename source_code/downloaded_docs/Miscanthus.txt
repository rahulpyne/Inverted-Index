     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="M._giganteus">
       <i>
        M. giganteus
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Miscanthus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: M. giganteus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Miscanthus_giganteus" title="Miscanthus giganteus">
       Miscanthus giganteus
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The sterile
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">
       hybrid
      </a>
      between
      <i>
       M. sinensis
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       M. sacchariflorus
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Miscanthus_giganteus" title="Miscanthus giganteus">
        Miscanthus giganteus
       </a>
      </i>
      , has been trialed as a
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
       biofuel
      </a>
      in Europe since the early 1980s. It can grow to heights of more than 3.5 m in one growth season. Its dry weight annual
      <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">
       yield
      </a>
      can reach 25 tonnes per hectare (10 tonnes per acre).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is sometimes called "elephant grass", so is thus confused with the African grass
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pennisetum_purpureum" title="Pennisetum purpureum">
        Pennisetum purpureum
       </a>
      </i>
      , also called that.
     </p>
     <p>
      The rapid growth, low mineral content, and high
      <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">
       biomass
      </a>
      yield of
      <i>
       Miscanthus
      </i>
      make it a favorite choice as a
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
       biofuel
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Miscanthus can be used as input for ethanol production, often outperforming corn and other alternatives in terms of biomass and gallons of ethanol produced. Additionally, after harvest, it can be burned to produce heat and steam for power turbines. In addition to the amount of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions from burning the crop, any fossil fuels that might have been used in planting, fertilizing, harvesting, and processing the crop, as well as in transporting the biofuel to the point of use, must also be considered when evaluating its carbon load. Its advantage, though, is that it is not usually consumed by humans, making it a more available crop for ethanol and biofuel, than, say, corn and sugarcane. When mixed 50%-50% with coal,
      <i>
       Miscanthus
      </i>
      biomass can be used in some current
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">
       coal-burning power plants
      </a>
      without modifications.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="M._sinensis">
       <i>
        M. sinensis
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Miscanthus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: M. sinensis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WinterMiscanthus.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/WinterMiscanthus.jpg/220px-WinterMiscanthus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/WinterMiscanthus.jpg/330px-WinterMiscanthus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/WinterMiscanthus.jpg/440px-WinterMiscanthus.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WinterMiscanthus.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Winter miscanthus, an ornamental grass, growing in Southern Ontario, Canada
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Miscanthus_sinensis" title="Miscanthus sinensis">
       Miscanthus sinensis
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       M. sinensis
      </i>
      is cultivated as an
      <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">
       ornamental plant
      </a>
      . In Japan, where it is known as
      <i>
       susuki
      </i>
      (すすき), it is considered an iconic plant of late summer and early autumn. It is mentioned in
      <a href="/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB" title="Man'yōshū">
       Man'yōshū
      </a>
      (VIII:1538) as one of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nanakusa-no-sekku#Seven_Autumn_Flowers" title="Nanakusa-no-sekku">
       seven autumn flowers
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       aki no nana kusa
      </i>
      , 秋の七草). It is used for the eighth month in
      <a href="/wiki/Hanafuda" title="Hanafuda">
       hanafuda
      </a>
      playing cards. It is decorated with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bush_clover" title="Bush clover">
       bush clover
      </a>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/Mid-Autumn_Festival" title="Mid-Autumn Festival">
       Mid-Autumn Festival
      </a>
      . Miscanthus has also excellent
      <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">
       fiber
      </a>
      properties for
      <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">
       papermaking
      </a>
      .
     </p>
