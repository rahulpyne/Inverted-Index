   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Renewable energy in Ethiopia
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      Ethiopia generates most of its electricity from
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      , mainly
      <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">
       hydropower
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Blue_Nile" title="Blue Nile">
       Blue Nile
      </a>
      . In 2011, over 96% of Ethiopia's electricity was from hydropower.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solar_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-solar-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The country began a large program to expand electricity supply in the 2010s from 2,000 MW to 10,000 MW. This was to be done mainly with renewable sources. Wind and geothermal were included to offset seasonal differences in water levels. Ethiopia plans to export electricity to neighboring countries but transmission lines will need to be upgraded and expanded.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most of the energy needs of Ethiopia are filled by biofuels for cooking, heating, and off-grid lighting. Petroleum, including gasoline, diesel and kerosene supply less than 7% of the countries energy supply.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solar_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-solar-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Solar
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">
       photovoltaics
      </a>
      is being promoted to replace fuel-based lighting and off-grid electrical supply with a solar panel assembly plant opening in Addis Ababa in early 2013. The majority of the Ethiopia's population live in rural areas and very few have access to electricity.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ethiopia is planning for a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon-neutral" title="Carbon-neutral">
       carbon-neutral
      </a>
      status by 2025.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RGES_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-RGES-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydropower">
       Hydropower
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Ethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hydropower">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Dams_and_hydropower_in_Ethiopia" title="Dams and hydropower in Ethiopia">
       Dams and hydropower in Ethiopia
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Dams built in Ethiopia provided over 1,500 MW of capacity by 2010. The four largest dams were built between 2004-2010.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Ethiopian_Renaissance_Dam" title="Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam">
       Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam
      </a>
      will be the largest hydropower dam in Africa and among the largest in the world. When completed it will be able to generate 6,000 MW, almost triple Ethiopia's entire national capacity.
     </p>
     <p>
      Egypt has expressed concerns that their water rights are being violated by these upstream dams but Ethiopia has no water treaty with Egypt.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">
       Wind power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Ethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wind power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ethiopia plans 800 MW of
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      As the dry season is also the windy season, wind power is a good complement to hydropower. The first wind installation in the country was the 51 MW Adama I wind farm, built in 2011.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The 120 MW Ashegoda Wind Farm opened in October 2013 and was the largest wind farm in Africa at that time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The larger 153 MW Adama II wind farm went online in May 2015, bringing Ethiopia's installed wind capacity to 324MW total.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">
       Solar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Ethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solar">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SolarGIS-Solar-map-Ethiopia-en.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="900" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/SolarGIS-Solar-map-Ethiopia-en.png/170px-SolarGIS-Solar-map-Ethiopia-en.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/SolarGIS-Solar-map-Ethiopia-en.png/255px-SolarGIS-Solar-map-Ethiopia-en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/SolarGIS-Solar-map-Ethiopia-en.png/340px-SolarGIS-Solar-map-Ethiopia-en.png 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SolarGIS-Solar-map-Ethiopia-en.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">
         Solar potential
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Solar
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">
       photovoltaics
      </a>
      are being promoted to replace fuel-based lighting and off-grid electrical needs. Ethiopia is thought to have about 5 MW of off-grid solar.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Almost all current solar power is used for telecommunications.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Other uses include village well pumps, health care and school lighting. A current government initiative plans to bring solar power to 150,000 households by 2015. The first phase included 1 MW of panels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first large installation of solar was a village grid of 10 kW in 1985, expanded to 30 kW in 1989. A solar panel assembly plant opened in Addis Ababa in early 2013 capable of making 20 MW of panel per year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal">
       Geothermal
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Ethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geothermal">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ethiopia has one geothermal plant, the 7MW Aluto Langano Geothermal Pilot Plant. This is being expanded to 70MW in 2015.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ethiopia is planning to build
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal_plant" title="Geothermal plant">
       geothermal plants
      </a>
      to offset restraints on power production by hydroplants due to seasonal water variation. American-Icelandic company Reykjavik Geothermal has an agreement to develop a 1000 MW geothermal farm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first 500 MW would be completed by 2018.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuels">
       Biofuels
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Ethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biofuels">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The majority of Ethiopia's energy needs are met by
      <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
       biofuels
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuelwood" title="Fuelwood">
       fuelwood
      </a>
      ) for cooking. The government plans to distribute 9 million more efficient stoves by 2015 to reduce wood use while improving air quality and lowering CO2 emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RGES_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-RGES-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Reduced use of firewood should also help the government's goal of
      <a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">
       reforestation
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Exports">
       Exports
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Ethiopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exports">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As Ethiopia produces more power than it consumes, it has become a regional power exporter. In 2015, it sells electricity to Kenya, Sudan and Djibouti and has future contracts for power sales to Tanzania, Rwanda, South Sudan and Yemen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Eastern African Power Pool will expand transmission lines to make this possible. Exports to Egypt and Sudan are possible after the completion of the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
