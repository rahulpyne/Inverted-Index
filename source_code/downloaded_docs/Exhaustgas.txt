   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Exhaust gas
   </h1>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">

     <p>
      <b>
       Exhaust gas
      </b>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gas
      </a>
      is emitted as a result of the
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      of fuels such as
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">
       gasoline
      </a>
      , petrol, biodiesel blends,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">
       diesel fuel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">
       fuel oil
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">
       coal
      </a>
      . According to the type of engine, it is discharged into the atmosphere through an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exhaust_pipe" title="Exhaust pipe">
       exhaust pipe
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gas_stack" title="Flue gas stack">
       flue gas stack
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Propelling_nozzle" title="Propelling nozzle">
       propelling nozzle
      </a>
      . It often disperses downwind in a pattern called an
      <b>
       exhaust plume
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      It is a major component of
      <b>
       motor vehicle emissions
      </b>
      (and from stationary internal combustion engines), which can also include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Crankcase" title="Crankcase">
        Crankcase
       </a>
       blow-by
      </li>
      <li>
       Evaporation of unused gasoline
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Motor vehicle emissions contribute to
      <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">
       air pollution
      </a>
      and are a major ingredient in the creation of
      <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">
       smog
      </a>
      in some large cities. A 2013 study by
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">
       MIT
      </a>
      indicates that 53,000 early deaths occur per year in the United States alone because of vehicle emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to another study from the same university, traffic fumes alone cause the death of 5,000 people every year just in the United Kingdom.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
       Composition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The largest part of most combustion gas is
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
       nitrogen
      </a>
      (N
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ), water vapor (H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      O) (except with pure-carbon fuels), and
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      (CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) (except for fuels without carbon); these are not toxic or noxious (although carbon dioxide is a
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      that contributes to
      <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">
       global warming
      </a>
      ). A relatively small part of combustion gas is undesirable noxious or toxic substances, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      (CO) from incomplete combustion,
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">
       hydrocarbons
      </a>
      (properly indicated as C
      <sub>
       x
      </sub>
      H
      <sub>
       y
      </sub>
      , but typically shown simply as "HC" on emissions-test slips) from unburnt fuel,
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">
       nitrogen oxides
      </a>
      (NO
      <sub>
       x
      </sub>
      ) from excessive combustion temperatures, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">
       particulate matter
      </a>
      (mostly
      <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">
       soot
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Exhaust_gas_temperature">
       Exhaust gas temperature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exhaust gas temperature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Exhaust gas temperature (EGT) is important to the functioning of the
      <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">
       catalytic converter
      </a>
      of an
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       internal combustion engine
      </a>
      . It may be measured by an
      <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas_temperature_gauge" title="Exhaust gas temperature gauge">
       exhaust gas temperature gauge
      </a>
      . EGT is also a measure of engine health in gas-turbine engines (see below).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_engines">
       Cold engines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cold engines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Exhaust.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Exhaust.jpg/220px-Exhaust.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Exhaust.jpg/330px-Exhaust.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Exhaust.jpg/440px-Exhaust.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Exhaust.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Steam from tailpipe of cold car
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      During the first two minutes after starting the engine of a car that has not been operated for several hours, the amount of emissions can be very high. This occurs for two main reasons:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Rich air-fuel ratio requirement in cold engines
       </b>
       : When a cold engine is started, the fuel does not vaporize completely, creating higher emissions of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">
        hydrocarbons
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">
        nitrogen oxides
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
        carbon monoxide
       </a>
       , which diminishes only as the engine reaches operating temperature. The duration of this start-up phase has been reduced by advances in materials and technology, including
       <a href="/wiki/Fuel_injection#Electronic_injection" title="Fuel injection">
        computer-controlled fuel injection
       </a>
       , shorter intake lengths, and pre-heating of fuel and/or inducted air.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Inefficient catalytic converter under cold conditions
       </b>
       : Catalytic converters are very inefficient until up to their
       <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">
        operating temperature
       </a>
       . This time has been much reduced by moving the converter closer to the exhaust manifold and even more so placing a small yet quick-to-heat-up converter directly at the exhaust manifold. The small converter handles the start-up emissions, which allows enough time for the larger main converter to heat up. Further improvements can be realised in many ways,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including electric heating, thermal battery, chemical reaction preheating, flame heating and superinsulation.
      </li>
     </ul>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gas-turbine_engines">
       Gas-turbine engines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gas-turbine engines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <ul>
      <li>
       In aircraft gas turbine engines, "exhaust gas temperature" (EGT) is a primary measure of engine health. Typically the EGT is compared with a primary engine power indication called "engine pressure ratio" (EPR). For example: at full power EPR there will be a maximum permitted EGT limit. Once an engine reaches a stage in its life where it reaches this EGT limit, the engine will require specific maintenance in order to rectify the problem. The amount the EGT is below the EGT limit is called EGT margin. The EGT margin of an engine will be greatest when the engine is new, or has been overhauled. For most airlines, this information is also monitored remotely by the airline maintenance department by means of
       <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_Communications_Addressing_and_Reporting_System" title="Aircraft Communications Addressing and Reporting System">
        ACARS
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sources_of_Volatile_Organic_Compounds.PNG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Sources_of_Volatile_Organic_Compounds.PNG/440px-Sources_of_Volatile_Organic_Compounds.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Sources_of_Volatile_Organic_Compounds.PNG/660px-Sources_of_Volatile_Organic_Compounds.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Sources_of_Volatile_Organic_Compounds.PNG/880px-Sources_of_Volatile_Organic_Compounds.PNG 2x" width="440"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sources_of_Volatile_Organic_Compounds.PNG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Non-road equipment is mostly gasoline and diesel stations.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa.gov_12-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-epa.gov-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      When oxides of nitrogen (NOx) and
      <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">
       volatile organic compounds
      </a>
      (VOCs) react in the presence of sunlight, ground level
      <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">
       ozone
      </a>
      is formed, a primary ingredient in
      <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">
       smog
      </a>
      . A 2005 U.S. EPA report gives road vehicles as the second largest source of VOCs in the U.S. at 26% and 19% are from non road equipment which is mostly gasoline and diesel stations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa.gov_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-epa.gov-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      27% of VOC emissions are from solvents which are used in the manufacturer of paints and paint thinners and other uses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ozone">
       Ozone
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Ozone">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Ozone is beneficial in the upper atmosphere,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      but at ground level, ozone irritates the
      <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">
       respiratory system
      </a>
      , causing coughing, choking, and reduced lung capacity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It also has many bad effects throughout the ecosystem.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_monoxide_.28CO.29">
       Carbon monoxide (CO)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Carbon monoxide (CO)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MOPITT_www.acd.ucar.edu.Web-201003-mixing_ratio_at_surface.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="913" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/MOPITT_www.acd.ucar.edu.Web-201003-mixing_ratio_at_surface.png/220px-MOPITT_www.acd.ucar.edu.Web-201003-mixing_ratio_at_surface.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/MOPITT_www.acd.ucar.edu.Web-201003-mixing_ratio_at_surface.png/330px-MOPITT_www.acd.ucar.edu.Web-201003-mixing_ratio_at_surface.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/MOPITT_www.acd.ucar.edu.Web-201003-mixing_ratio_at_surface.png/440px-MOPITT_www.acd.ucar.edu.Web-201003-mixing_ratio_at_surface.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MOPITT_www.acd.ucar.edu.Web-201003-mixing_ratio_at_surface.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/MOPITT" title="MOPITT">
         MOPITT
        </a>
        satellite computer image of carbon monoxide March 2010.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">
       Carbon monoxide poisoning
      </a>
      is the most common type of fatal air poisoning in many countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toxicology2002-omaye_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Toxicology2002-omaye-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       Carbon monoxide
      </a>
      is colorless, odorless and tasteless, but highly toxic. It combines with
      <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">
       hemoglobin
      </a>
      to produce
      <a href="/wiki/Carboxyhemoglobin" title="Carboxyhemoglobin">
       carboxyhemoglobin
      </a>
      , which is ineffective for delivering oxygen to bodily tissues. In 2011, 52% of carbon monoxide emissions were created by mobile vehicles in the U.S.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hazardous_air_pollutants_.28toxics.29">
       Hazardous air pollutants (toxics)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Hazardous air pollutants (toxics)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_(medicine)" title="Chronic (medicine)">
       Chronic
      </a>
      (long-term) exposure to
      <a href="/wiki/Benzene#Health_effects" title="Benzene">
       benzene
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">
       C
       <sub>
        6
       </sub>
       H
       <sub>
        6
       </sub>
      </a>
      ) damages
      <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">
       bone marrow
      </a>
      . It can also cause excessive bleeding and depress the
      <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">
       immune system
      </a>
      , increasing the chance of
      <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">
       infection
      </a>
      . Benzene causes leukemia and is associated with other blood cancers and pre-cancers of the blood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Particulate_matter_.28PM10_and_PM2.5.29">
       Particulate matter (PM
       <sub>
        10
       </sub>
       and PM
       <sub>
        2.5
       </sub>
       )
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Particulate matter (PM10 and PM2.5)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_Matter#Health_effects" title="Particulate Matter">
       health effects
      </a>
      of inhaling
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">
       airborne particulate matter
      </a>
      have been widely studied in humans and animals and include
      <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">
       asthma
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">
       lung cancer
      </a>
      , cardiovascular issues, and premature
      <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">
       death
      </a>
      . Because of the size of the particles, they can penetrate the deepest part of the lungs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A 2011 UK study estimates 90 deaths per year due to passenger vehicle PM.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a 2006 publication, the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Highway_Administration" title="Federal Highway Administration">
       U.S. Federal Highway Administration
      </a>
      (FHWA) state that in 2002 about 1 per-cent of all PM
      <sub>
       10
      </sub>
      and 2 per-cent of all PM
      <sub>
       2.5
      </sub>
      emissions came from the exhaust of on-road motor vehicles (mostly from
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">
       diesel engines
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FHA_facts_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FHA_facts-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_.28CO2.29">
       Carbon dioxide (CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       )
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide (CO2)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       Carbon dioxide
      </a>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
       greenhouse gas
      </a>
      . Motor vehicle CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions are part of the anthropogenic contribution to the growth of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      concentrations in the atmosphere which is causing
      <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
       climate change
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Motor vehicles are calculated to generate about 20% of the European Union's man-made CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions, with passenger cars contributing about 12%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards" title="European emission standards">
       European emission standards
      </a>
      limit the CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions of new passenger cars and light vehicles. The European Union average new car CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emissions figure dropped by 5.4% in the year to the first quarter of 2010, down to 145.6
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G/km" title="G/km">
       g/km
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Water_vapor">
       Water vapor
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Water vapor">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Vehicle exhaust contains much
      <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">
       water vapor
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Water_recovery">
       Water recovery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Water recovery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      There has been research into ways that troops in deserts can recover drinkable water from their vehicles' exhaust gases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution_reduction">
       Pollution reduction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Pollution reduction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Emission_standard" title="Emission standard">
       Emission standards
      </a>
      focus on reducing
      <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">
       pollutants
      </a>
      contained in the exhaust gases from vehicles as well as from industrial flue gas stacks and other
      <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">
       air pollution
      </a>
      exhaust sources in various large-scale industrial facilities such as
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">
       petroleum refineries
      </a>
      , natural gas processing plants,
      <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">
       petrochemical
      </a>
      plants and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">
       chemical
      </a>
      production plants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, these are often referred to as
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gases
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">
       Catalytic converters
      </a>
      in cars intend to break down the pollution of exhaust gases using a catalyst.
      <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">
       Scrubbers
      </a>
      in ships intend to remove the sulfur dioxide (SO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) of marine exhaust gases. The regulations on marine sulfur dioxide emissions are tightening, however only a small number of special areas worldwide have been designated for low sulfur diesel fuel use only.
     </p>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Air_suction_valve" title="Air suction valve">
       Air suction valve
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      One of the advantages claimed for
      <a href="/wiki/Advanced_steam_technology" title="Advanced steam technology">
       advanced steam technology
      </a>
      engines is that they produce smaller quantities of toxic pollutants (e.g. oxides of nitrogen) than petrol and diesel engines of the same power.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      They produce larger quantities of carbon dioxide but less carbon monoxide due to more efficient combustion.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Health_studies">
       Health studies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exhaust_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Health studies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Researchers from the University of California, Los Angeles School of Public Health say preliminary results of their statistical study of children listed in the California Cancer Registry born between 1998 and 2007 found that traffic pollution may be associated with a 5% to 15% increase in the likelihood of some cancers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">
       World Health Organization
      </a>
      study found that diesel fumes cause an increase in lung cancer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Localised_effects">
       Localised effects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">
       California Air Resources Board
      </a>
      (C.A.R.B.) found in studies that 50% or more of the
      <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">
       air pollution
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">
       smog
      </a>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">
       Southern California
      </a>
      is due to car emissions.
