     <p>
      <b>
       Johnson Controls
      </b>
      is an American
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">
       multinational
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">
       conglomerate
      </a>
      producing automotive parts such as
      <a href="/wiki/Automotive_battery" title="Automotive battery">
       batteries
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">
       electronics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       HVAC
      </a>
      equipment for buildings. It employs 170,000 people in more than 1,300 locations across six continents. As of 2012
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      it was listed as 67th in the Fortune 500
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2012Fortune_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-2012Fortune-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and 251st in Global 500.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On January 25, 2016, Johnson announced its merger with
      <a href="/wiki/Tyco_International" title="Tyco International">
       Tyco International
      </a>
      to form "Johnson Controls International plc". The merger led to the avoidance of U.S. taxes of its foreign market operations and a financial windfall for the CEO.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Surane_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Surane-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-believe_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-believe-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="1952" height="368" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg/220px-Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg/330px-Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg/440px-Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A Johnson Super-Sensitive Thermometer on an air conditioning unit
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1883,
      <a href="/wiki/Warren_S._Johnson" title="Warren S. Johnson">
       Warren S. Johnson
      </a>
      , a professor at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin-Whitewater" title="University of Wisconsin-Whitewater">
       State Normal School
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Whitewater,_Wisconsin" title="Whitewater, Wisconsin">
       Whitewater, Wisconsin
      </a>
      , received a patent for the first electric room thermostat. His invention helped launch the building control industry and was the impetus for a new company. Johnson and a group of Milwaukee investors incorporated the Johnson Electric Service Company in 1885 to manufacture, install and service automatic temperature regulation systems for buildings. After Johnson's death in 1911, the company decided to focus on its temperature control business for nonresidential buildings. The company was renamed Johnson Controls in 1974.
     </p>
     <p>
      On November 21, 2005, Johnson Controls Inc. said it must restate financial results for fiscal years 2003 and 2004 and the first three quarters of fiscal 2005. The changes would have no impact on net income, earnings per share or the financial position as previously reported.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBJ_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MBJ-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Johnson Controls plant in Lakeshore, Ontario closed in late March 2010 and the property was sold.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cbc-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2013, Stephen Roell retired and Alex Molinaroli took his position as CEO and chairman of the board.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Content_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Content-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_with_Tyco">
       Merger with Tyco
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Merger with Tyco">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In January 2016, Johnson Controls announced plans to merge with
      <a href="/wiki/Tyco_International" title="Tyco International">
       Tyco International
      </a>
      to create a company based in Ireland. The merger will save the company about $150-million per year in taxes. The new company will be called Johnson Controls International Plc.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Globe_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Globe-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">
       Hillary Clinton
      </a>
      condemned the company for wanting to escape U.S. taxes through the merger after having "begged" the government for financial help in 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gilbert-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Johnson deal, termed "outrageous"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fortune_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fortune-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">
        Fortune
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine, qualifies as a
      <a href="/wiki/Tax_inversion" title="Tax inversion">
       "super inversion"
      </a>
      because Tyco shareholders will own 44% of the company, thus avoiding penalties that the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">
       United States Department of the Treasury
      </a>
      has imposed on other inversion deals.
     </p>

       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Women's work rights">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1982, Johnson Controls enacted what it called a "
      <a href="/wiki/Fetal_protection_policies_in_the_United_States" title="Fetal protection policies in the United States">
       fetal protection policy
      </a>
      ", which denied women the right to work on the battery production line because of the potential harm to a
      <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">
       fetus
      </a>
      they might conceive. Women were allowed to work on the production line only if they could prove that "...their inability to bear children had been medically documented." In April 1984, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Auto_Workers" title="United Auto Workers">
       United Auto Workers
      </a>
      sued Johnson Controls on behalf of three employees. These employees were Mary Craig, who had chosen to be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_sterilization_(surgical_procedure)" title="Human sterilization (surgical procedure)">
       sterilized
      </a>
      to avoid losing her job, Elsie Nason, a 50-year-old divorcee, who had suffered a loss of compensation when she was transferred from a high paying job that exposed her to lead, and Donald Penney, who had been denied a request for a
      <a href="/wiki/Leave_of_absence" title="Leave of absence">
       leave of absence
      </a>
      for the purpose of lowering his blood lead levels because he intended to become a father.
      <a href="/wiki/United_Automobile_Workers_v._Johnson_Controls,_Inc." title="United Automobile Workers v. Johnson Controls, Inc.">
       The case
      </a>
      was argued before the
      <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">
       Supreme Court
      </a>
      on October 10, 1990 and was decided on March 20, 1991. The Court ruled in favor of the plaintiffs. This was a landmark ruling because it affirmed that "...it is no more important for the courts than it is for individual employers to decide whether a woman's reproductive role is more important to herself and her family than her economic role."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UAW_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-UAW-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Business_units">
       Business units
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Business units">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The companyâs operations are segmented into four business units: Building Efficiency, Global WorkPlace Solutions, Power Solutions and Automotive Experience.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Building_Efficiency">
       Building Efficiency
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Building Efficiency">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Building Efficiency business unit designs, produces, installs and services heating, ventilation and air conditioning systems, industrial refrigeration,
      <a href="/wiki/Building_management_system" title="Building management system">
       building management systems
      </a>
      , fire and security systems and mechanical equipment for commercial and residential buildings. The brands produced under this business unit are York, TempMaster, Metasys, Panoptix, Frick and Sabroe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This unit also works with organizations to reduce the energy consumption and operating costs of their buildings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lightoff_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lightoff-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This includes retrofitting existing buildings such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">
       Empire State Building
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crain_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Crain-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and working on maximizing efficiency in new construction such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Burj_Khalifa" title="Burj Khalifa">
       Burj Khalifa
      </a>
      in Dubai.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hope_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hope-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Building Efficiency is the companyâs longest-running business unit, dating to 1885 when Johnson founded the Johnson Electric Service Company
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adler_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Adler-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      after patenting the electric
      <a href="/wiki/Thermostat" title="Thermostat">
       thermostat
      </a>
      in 1883.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USembassy_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-USembassy-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 2012, the business unit operated from 700 branch offices in more than 150 countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Johnson Controls was one of the defendants in a multimillion-dollar federal court lawsuit in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">
       San Juan, Puerto Rico
      </a>
      in a case where 98 people perished and 140 were injured in a
      <a href="/wiki/Dupont_Plaza_Hotel_arson" title="Dupont Plaza Hotel arson">
       fire at the DuPont Plaza Hotel
      </a>
      and its casino on New Yearâs Eve, December 31, 1986. The plaintiffs claimed that Johnson Controls sold and installed an energy management system that failed to give early warning of the fire.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      After nine months of trial, the company and its energy management system were absolved of blame when the court issued a directed verdict.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      When this trial was completed the plaintiffs had accumulated approximately $220,908,549.00 in damages as a result of various settlements and a jury verdict against some other defendants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Global_WorkPlace_Solutions">
       Global WorkPlace Solutions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Global WorkPlace Solutions">
        edit

        ]

      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Global WorkPlace Solutions business unit provides outsourced
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facilities_management" title="Facilities management">
       facilities management
      </a>
      services globally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MJSverizon_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MJSverizon-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It also manages corporate real estate on behalf of its customers including acquiring and disposing of property, administering leases, and managing building related projects such as equipment replacements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FMWorldbrookfield_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FMWorldbrookfield-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On September 23, 2015,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBRE,_Inc." title="CBRE, Inc.">
       CBRE, Inc.
      </a>
      purchased the Global Workplace Solutions business unit, retaining the name "Global Workplace Solutions".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Power_Solutions">
       Power Solutions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Power Solutions business unit designs and manufactures automotive batteries for passenger cars, heavy and light duty trucks, utility vehicles, motorcycles, golf carts and boats.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smallvehicle_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Smallvehicle-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSelectricboat_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JSelectricboat-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greencarcongress_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Greencarcongress-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mustangmonthly_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mustangmonthly-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It supplies more than one third of the worldâs lead-acid batteries
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motleyfool_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Motleyfool-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      to automakers and aftermarket retailers including Wal-Mart, Sears and BMW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbesbattery_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Forbesbattery-32">
        [32]
       </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSonlinewalmart_33-0">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>

      </sup>

       Start-Stop

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motleyfool_31-1">

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSonlinelandrover_38-0">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Automotive_Experience">
       Automotive Experience
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Automotive Experience">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Automotive Experience business unit supplies automotive seating, interiors and electronics to automakers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motleyfool_31-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Motleyfool-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is one of the largest suppliers of car interiors in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJpartsdeal_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSJpartsdeal-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The seating division designs and manufactures automotive seats and supplies them on a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Just_in_time_(business)" title="Just in time (business)">
       just-in-time
      </a>
      schedule. It also designs and manufactures seating components, including mechanisms, tracks, structures foams, fabrics and trim, making Johnson Controls the largest automotive seat supplier in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTauto_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FTauto-44">
        [44]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A separate interiors division produces overhead systems, headliners, door panels, instrument panels, and overhead and floor consoles for automotive interiors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additionally, an electronics division designs and manufactures analog and digital instrument clusters, infotainment systems and hands free electronics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloombergauto_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bloombergauto-45">
        [45]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brands produced under this business unit include RECARO automotive seats and Keiper.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJpartsdeal_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSJpartsdeal-43">
        [43]
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 2012, the business unit operated from 240 locations worldwide.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Form10-K_14-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On January 12, 2016 Johnson Controls announced that its remaining Automotive Experience holdings will spin-off and become Adient. This will be finalized on October 1, 2016, and begin to be publicly traded on the NYSE as ticker symbol ADNT on October 3, 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adient_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Adient-46">
        [46]
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Johnson_Controls-Saft_Advanced_Power_Solutions">
       Johnson Controls-Saft Advanced Power Solutions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Johnson Controls-Saft Advanced Power Solutions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Johnson Controls-Saft Advanced Power Solutions (JCS) was a joint venture between Johnson Controls and French battery company
      <a href="/wiki/Saft_Groupe_S.A." title="Saft Groupe S.A.">
       Saft Groupe S.A.
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        [47]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was officially launched in January 2006.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_48-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varta" title="Varta">
       Varta
      </a>
      established a JCS development centre at its German HQ, following the setting-up of Varta-SAFT joint venture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_48-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Johnson Controls is exhibiting a plug-in hybrid concept called the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Re3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Re3 (page does not exist)">
       re3
      </a>
      . Johnson Controls produced cells for lithium-ion hybrid vehicle batteries in France under the joint venture with Saft. Battery assemblies were developed and produced in Hannover(Germany) and Milwaukee(USA)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite some signs of promise, Johnson Controls was increasingly dissatisfied with the restrictions of the agreement and also sought a more important ally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A21_50-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-A21-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A22_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-A22-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In May 2011, the American company requested the dissolution of Johnson Controls-Saft Advanced Power Solutions LLC to the
      <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Court_of_Chancery" title="Delaware Court of Chancery">
       Delaware Court of Chancery
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A21_50-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-A21-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A22_51-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-A22-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The two companies agreed the separation and Johnson Controls paid Saft 145 million dollars for its shares in the joint venture as well as for the right to use certain technology developed by it. Johnson Controls retained the Michigan facility built by the partnership. The French joint facility was transferred to Saft.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        [52]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
