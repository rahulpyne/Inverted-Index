     <p>
      <b>
       Walney Wind Farm
      </b>
      is an offshore
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">
       wind farm
      </a>
      14 km west of
      <a href="/wiki/Walney_Island" title="Walney Island">
       Walney Island
      </a>
      off the coast of
      <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">
       Cumbria
      </a>
      , in the
      <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">
       Irish Sea
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      . It has a capacity of 367 MW, which makes it one of the world's largest offshore wind farms. The wind farm was developed by Walney (UK) Offshore Windfarms Limited, a partnership between
      <a href="/wiki/DONG_Energy" title="DONG Energy">
       DONG Energy
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_and_Southern_Energy" title="Scottish and Southern Energy">
       Scottish and Southern Energy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The farm is immediately north west of the
      <a href="/wiki/West_of_Duddon_Sands_Wind_Farm" title="West of Duddon Sands Wind Farm">
       West of Duddon Sands Wind Farm
      </a>
      and also to the west of
      <a href="/wiki/Ormonde_Wind_Farm" title="Ormonde Wind Farm">
       Ormonde Wind Farm
      </a>
      . The farm is in water depths ranging from 19m to 23m and covers an area of approximately 73 km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walney_Wind_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Both phases have 51
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">
       turbines
      </a>
      giving a
      <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source#Terminology" title="Intermittent energy source">
       nameplate capacity
      </a>
      of 367
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Until September 2012 it was the world's largest operational offshore wind farm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is expected to generate about 1,300 GW·h/year of electricity, with a load factor of 43%
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">
       Construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walney_Wind_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2004 DONG Energy was awarded a 50-year lease from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Crown_Estate" title="The Crown Estate">
       The Crown Estate
      </a>
      to develop a wind farm off Walney Island, as part of the second UK offshore wind farm tendering process known as
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_the_United_Kingdom#Round_2" title="Wind power in the United Kingdom">
       "Round 2"
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The farm was constructed sequentially in two phases with overlapping installation activities to reduce the overall construction timeframe.
     </p>
     <p>
      The project involved constructing the wind turbines and their
      <a href="/wiki/Foundation_(engineering)" title="Foundation (engineering)">
       foundations
      </a>
      , building two offshore substations and installing two
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undersea" title="Undersea">
       undersea
      </a>
      power cables, one for each phase, and two short onshore cables to connect to two existing onshore
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_substation" title="Electrical substation">
       Electrical substations
      </a>
      for connection into the UK
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Grid_(UK)" title="National Grid (UK)">
       National Grid
      </a>
      . A cable was laid by
      <a href="/wiki/Stemat_Spirit" title="Stemat Spirit">
       Stemat Spirit
      </a>
      . Phase 1 connects to a substation at
      <a href="/wiki/Heysham" title="Heysham">
       Heysham
      </a>
      and Phase 2 connects to substation at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanah" title="Stanah">
       Stanah
      </a>
      , south of
      <a href="/wiki/Fleetwood" title="Fleetwood">
       Fleetwood
      </a>
      . Both undersea cables pass close by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrow_Offshore_Wind" title="Barrow Offshore Wind">
       Barrow Wind Farm
      </a>
      . All the construction work was expected to take less than 2 years, with both phases operational by the end of 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 11 July 2011 Phase 1 became operational, comprising 51 turbines with an installed capacity of 183.6MW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Walney 2 began sending power to the grid on 1 November 2011. In February 2012, DONG Energy claimed to have installed the 51 turbines in Walney 2 in 5 months and 14 days, including monopiles and complete turbines; about 3.25 days per turbine. Walney 1 took 7 months. The improvement is due to commonality of projects and resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The wind farm was officially opened on 9 February 2012 by the new energy secretary,
      <a href="/wiki/Ed_Davey" title="Ed Davey">
       Ed Davey
      </a>
      , MP,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      although the last of the 51 turbines in Walney 2 were only activated in April 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Walney_Extension">
       Walney Extension
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walney_Wind_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Walney Extension">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In November 2014 DONG Energy was given development consent for an extension to the Walney offshore wind farm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rec1_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rec1-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The development consent allowed a maximum of 207 turbines to be added to the existing 102 turbines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rec1_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-rec1-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The maximum generating capacity of the extension was said to be 750MW although DONG was reported to be proceeding with a project based around 660MW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rec1_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-rec1-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Construction is expected to begin in 2017.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rec1_12-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-rec1-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2015 DONG chose the 8MW Vestas for Phase 1,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the 7MW Siemens gearless turbine for Phase 2.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Involvement_in_oil_spill">
       Involvement in oil spill
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walney_Wind_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Involvement in oil spill">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2014 a dive vessel, owned by Danish firm Offshore Marine Services, was carrying out routine inspection work when an anchor cable broke and the ship slammed into one of 102 turbines installed at the Walney Offshore Wind Farm. The UK’s Maritime and Coastguard Agency surveyed the crash site and reported that a surface sheen stretching 33 feet wide and 0.7 nautical miles long was trailing the vessel. The agency said that, unlike heavier crude oil, the marine engine oil should evaporate or disperse naturally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
