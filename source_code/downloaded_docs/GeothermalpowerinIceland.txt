   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Geothermal power in Iceland
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Borehole.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="300" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Borehole.jpg/120px-Borehole.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Borehole.jpg/180px-Borehole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Borehole.jpg/240px-Borehole.jpg 2x" width="120"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">

     </div>
     <p>
      Due to the geological location of
      <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">
       Iceland
      </a>
      (over a rift in continental plates), the high concentration of
      <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
       volcanoes
      </a>
      in the area is often an advantage in the generation of
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">
       geothermal energy
      </a>
      , the heating and production of
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      During
      <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">
       winter
      </a>
      , pavements near these areas (such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reykjavik" title="Reykjavik">
       Reykjavik
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Akureyri" title="Akureyri">
       Akureyri
      </a>
      ) are heated up.
     </p>
     <p>
      Five major
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">
       geothermal
      </a>
      power plants exist in Iceland, which produce approximately 26.2% (2010)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      of the nation's electricity. In addition,
      <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">
       geothermal heating
      </a>
      meets the heating and hot water requirements of approximately 87% of all buildings in Iceland. Apart from geothermal energy, 73.8% of the nationâ€™s electricity is generated by
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydro power
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and 0.1% from
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">
       fossil fuels
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Consumption of primary geothermal energy in 2004 was 79.7
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petajoule" title="Petajoule">
       petajoules (PJ)
      </a>
      , approximately 53.4% of the total national consumption of
      <a href="/wiki/Primary_energy" title="Primary energy">
       primary energy
      </a>
      , 149.1 PJ. The corresponding share for
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">
       hydro power
      </a>
      was 17.2%,
      <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">
       petroleum
      </a>
      was 26.3%, and coal was 3%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Plans are underway to turn Iceland into a 100% fossil-fuel-free nation in the near future.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, Iceland's abundant geothermal energy has enabled renewable energy initiatives, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Recycling_International" title="Carbon Recycling International">
       Carbon Recycling International
      </a>
      's carbon dioxide to methanol fuel process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The following are the five largest power stations in Iceland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <li>

       </a>
       (303
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
        MW
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Nesjavellir_Geothermal_Power_Station" title="Nesjavellir Geothermal Power Station">
        Nesjavellir Geothermal Power Station
       </a>
       (120
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
        MW
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Reykjanes_Power_Station" title="Reykjanes Power Station">
        Reykjanes Power Station

       (100
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
        MW
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Svartsengi_Power_Station" title="Svartsengi Power Station">

       )
      </li>
