     <p>
      <b>
       Gas to liquids
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       GTL
      </b>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">
       refinery
      </a>
      process to convert
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      or other gaseous
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">
       hydrocarbons
      </a>
      into longer-chain hydrocarbons, such as gasoline or diesel fuel.
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       Methane
      </a>
      -rich gases are converted into liquid
      <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">
       synthetic fuels
      </a>
      either via direct conversion—using non-catalytic processes that convert methane to methanol in one step—or via
      <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">
       syngas
      </a>
      as an intermediate, such as in the
      <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">
       Fischer Tropsch
      </a>
      , Mobil and
      <a href="/wiki/Syngas_to_gasoline_plus" title="Syngas to gasoline plus">
       syngas to gasoline plus
      </a>
      processes.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fischer.E2.80.93Tropsch_process">
       Fischer–Tropsch process
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fischer–Tropsch process">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">
       Fischer–Tropsch process
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GTL_process.GIF">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1058" data-file-width="1138" height="325" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/GTL_process.GIF/350px-GTL_process.GIF" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/GTL_process.GIF/525px-GTL_process.GIF 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/GTL_process.GIF/700px-GTL_process.GIF 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GTL_process.GIF" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        GTL process using the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer_Tropsch" title="Fischer Tropsch">
         Fischer Tropsch
        </a>
        method
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Fischer–Tropsch process starts with partial oxidation of
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      (natural gas) to
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_gas" title="Hydrogen gas">
       hydrogen gas
      </a>
      and water. The ratio of
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      to hydrogen is adjusted using the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">
       water gas shift reaction
      </a>
      , while the excess
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      is removed with aqueous solutions of
      <a href="/wiki/Alkanolamine" title="Alkanolamine">
       alkanolamines
      </a>
      (or physical solvents). Removing the water yields
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" title="Synthesis gas">
       synthesis gas
      </a>
      (syngas) which is chemically reacted over an iron or cobalt catalyst to produce liquid hydrocarbons and other byproducts.
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       Oxygen
      </a>
      is provided from a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">
       cryogenic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">
       air separation
      </a>
      unit.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_to_methanol_process">
       Methane to methanol process
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methane to methanol process">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Methanol is made from
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      (natural gas) in a series of three reactions:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_reforming" title="Fossil fuel reforming">
        Steam reforming
       </a>
      </dt>
      <dd>
       CH
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       + H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O → CO + 3 H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       <span style="padding-left:2em;">
       </span>
       Δ
       <sub>
        r
       </sub>
       H = +206 kJ mol
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">
        Water shift reaction
       </a>
      </dt>
      <dd>
       CO + H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O → CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       + H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       <span style="padding-left:2.5em;">
       </span>
       Δ
       <sub>
        r
       </sub>
       H = -41 kJ mol
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Synthesis
      </dt>
      <dd>
       2 H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       + CO → CH
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       OH
       <span style="padding-left:3.5em;">
       </span>
       Δ
       <sub>
        r
       </sub>
       H = -92 kJ mol
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The methanol thus formed may be converted to gasoline by the
      <b>
       Mobil process
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Methanol_to_gasoline_process_.28MTG.29">
       Methanol to gasoline process (MTG)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methanol to gasoline process (MTG)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the early 1970s,
      <a href="/wiki/Mobil" title="Mobil">
       Mobil
      </a>
      developed an alternative procedure in which natural gas is converted to syngas, and then
      <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">
       methanol
      </a>
      . The methanol polymerized over a
      <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">
       zeolite
      </a>
      catalyst to form
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">
       alkanes
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      First methanol is
      <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">
       dehydrated
      </a>
      to give
      <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">
       dimethyl ether
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       2 CH
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       OH → CH
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       OCH
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       + H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This is then further dehydrated over a zeolite catalyst such as
      <a href="/wiki/ZSM-5" title="ZSM-5">
       ZSM-5
      </a>
      , which would theoretically yield
      <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">
       ethylene
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       CH
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       OCH
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       → C
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       H
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       + H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      but which in practice is polymerized and hydrogenated to give a gasoline with hydrocarbons of five or more carbon atoms making up 80% of the fuel by weight.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Syngas_to_gasoline_plus_process_.28STG.2B.29">
       Syngas to gasoline plus process (STG+)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Syngas to gasoline plus process (STG+)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Syngas_to_gasoline_plus" title="Syngas to gasoline plus">
       Syngas to gasoline plus
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STG%2B_Process.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="312" data-file-width="675" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/STG%2B_Process.jpg/350px-STG%2B_Process.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/STG%2B_Process.jpg/525px-STG%2B_Process.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d0/STG%2B_Process.jpg 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:STG%2B_Process.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The STG+ Process
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A third gas-to-liquids process builds on the MTG technology by converting natural gas-derived syngas directly into drop-in gasoline and jet fuel via a thermochemical single-loop process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bridge2Biofuels_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bridge2Biofuels-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The STG+ process follows four principal steps in one continuous process loop. This process consists of four
      <a href="/wiki/Packed_bed" title="Packed bed">
       fixed bed reactors
      </a>
      in series in which a
      <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">
       syngas
      </a>
      is converted to synthetic fuels. The steps for producing high-octane synthetic gasoline are as follows:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STG.2B_White_Paper_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-STG.2B_White_Paper-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">
        Methanol Synthesis
       </a>
       : Syngas is fed to Reactor 1, the first of four reactors, which converts most of the syngas (CO and H2) to methanol (CH3OH) when passing through the catalyst bed.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">
        Dimethyl Ether
       </a>
       (DME) Synthesis: The methanol-rich gas from Reactor 1 is next fed to Reactor 2, the second STG+ reactor. The methanol is exposed to a
       <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">
        catalyst
       </a>
       and much of it is converted to DME, which involves a dehydration from methanol to form DME (CH3OCH3).
      </li>
      <li>
       Gasoline synthesis: The Reactor 2 product gas is next fed to Reactor 3, the third reactor containing the catalyst for conversion of DME to hydrocarbons including paraffins (
       <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">
        alkanes
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">
        aromatics
       </a>
       , naphthenes (
       <a href="/wiki/Cycloalkane" title="Cycloalkane">
        cycloalkanes
       </a>
       ) and small amounts of olefins (
       <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">
        alkenes
       </a>
       ), mostly from C6 (number of carbon atoms in the hydrocarbon molecule) to C10.
      </li>
      <li>
       Gasoline Treatment: The fourth reactor provides
       <a href="/wiki/Transalkylation" title="Transalkylation">
        transalkylation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">
        hydrogenation
       </a>
       treatment to the products coming from Reactor 3. The treatment reduces
       <a href="/wiki/Durene" title="Durene">
        durene
       </a>
       (tetramethylbenzene)/isodurene and trimethylbenzene components that have high freezing points and must be minimized in gasoline. As a result, the synthetic gasoline product has high octane and desirable viscometric properties.
      </li>
      <li>
       Separator: Finally, the mixture from Reactor 4 is condensed to obtain gasoline. The non-condensed gas and gasoline are separated in a conventional condenser/separator. Most of the non-condensed gas from the product separator becomes recycled gas and is sent back to the feed stream to Reactor 1, leaving the synthetic gasoline product composed of paraffins, aromatics and naphthenes.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_uses">
       Commercial uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial uses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Using gas-to-liquids processes, refineries can convert some of their gaseous waste products (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flare_gas" title="Flare gas">
       flare gas
      </a>
      ) into valuable
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">
       fuel oils
      </a>
      , which can be sold as is or blended only with
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">
       diesel fuel
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      estimates that over 150 billion cubic metres (5.3
      <span style="margin-left:0.2em">
       ×
       <span style="margin-left:0.1em">
        10
       </span>
      </span>
      <s style="display:none">
       ^
      </s>
      <sup>
       12
      </sup>
      cu ft) of natural gas are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flared_gas" title="Flared gas">
       flared
      </a>
      or vented annually, an amount worth approximately $30.6 billion, equivalent to 25% of the United States' gas consumption or 30% of the European Union's annual gas consumption,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a resource that could be useful using GTL. Gas-to-liquids processes may also be used for the economic extraction of gas deposits in locations where it is not economical to build a pipeline. This process will be increasingly significant as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">
       crude oil
      </a>
      resources are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peak_Oil" title="Peak Oil">
       depleted
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The use of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microchannel_reactor" title="Microchannel reactor">
       microchannel reactors
      </a>
      , such as those developed by Velocys, shows promise for the conversion of unconventional, remote and problem gas into valuable liquid fuels. GTL plants based on microchannel reactors are significantly smaller than those using conventional fixed bed or slurry bed reactors, enabling modular plants that can be deployed cost effectively in remote locations and on smaller fields than is possible with competing systems.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modular_design_of_smaller_scale_GTL_plants_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Modular_design_of_smaller_scale_GTL_plants-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Construction is underway for one such plant: ENVIA Energy's Oklahoma City GTL plant being built adjacent to Waste Management's East Oak landfill site. The project is being financed by a joint venture between Waste Management, NRG Energy, Ventech and Velocys. The feedstock for this plant will be a combination of landfill gas and pipeline natural gas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On August 1, 2014, Biofuels Power Corporation (BFLS) signed a letter of intent with ThyssenKrupp Industrial Solutions and Liberty GTL, Inc. to build a small scale gas-to-liquid demonstration facility in Houston,Texas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The parties have established a non-binding target date to complete installation and commissioning of the GTL Pilot Plant on or before December 31, 2014. The purpose of the GTL Pilot Plant is to commercially demonstrate converting stranded natural gas resources to synthetic crude oil. BFLS will operate the GTL Pilot Plant for the 2-year demonstration. ThyssenKrupp will provide technical services and contribute a previously operating auto-thermal reformer pilot plant of proven design (“ATR”), which will be used to generate synthesis gas feedstock for the production of synthetic crude oil. Liberty will provide intellectual property and operating know-how regarding crude oil synthesis along with the relevant catalyst supply. The Liberty technical team is also credited for designing the FT (Fischer Tropsch) Reactor which will convert the synthetic gas to synthetic crude oil. The GTL Pilot Plant will be assembled at the Houston Clean Energy Park,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which is an industrial estate owned by BFLS. The Houston site is located between the Eagle Ford Natural Gas Field and numerous refineries.
     </p>
     <p>
      One other proposed solution is to use a novel
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating_Production_Storage_and_Offloading" title="Floating Production Storage and Offloading">
       FPSO
      </a>
      for offshore conversion of gas to liquids such as
      <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">
       methanol
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">
       diesel
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrol" title="Petrol">
       petrol
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_crude" title="Synthetic crude">
       synthetic crude
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">
       naphtha
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Two companies,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SASOL" title="SASOL">
       SASOL
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">
       Royal Dutch Shell
      </a>
      , have technology proven to work on a commercial scale.
      <a href="/wiki/PetroSA" title="PetroSA">
       PetroSA
      </a>
      completed semi-commercial demonstrations of gas-to-liquids used by the company in 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Royal Dutch Shell produces a diesel from natural gas in a factory in
      <a href="/wiki/Bintulu" title="Bintulu">
       Bintulu
      </a>
      , Malaysia. Another Shell GTL facility is the
      <a href="/wiki/Pearl_GTL" title="Pearl GTL">
       Pearl GTL
      </a>
      plant in Qatar, the world's largest GTL facility and there are reports that Shell is looking at the feasibility of a GTL facility in Louisiana, US.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      SASOL has recently built the
      <a href="/wiki/Oryx_GTL" title="Oryx GTL">
       Oryx GTL
      </a>
      facility in
      <a href="/wiki/Ras_Laffan_Industrial_City" title="Ras Laffan Industrial City">
       Ras Laffan Industrial City
      </a>
      , Qatar and together with
      <a href="/wiki/Uzbekneftegaz" title="Uzbekneftegaz">
       Uzbekneftegaz
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Petronas" title="Petronas">
       Petronas
      </a>
      builds the
      <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan_GTL" title="Uzbekistan GTL">
       Uzbekistan GTL
      </a>
      plant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upstream080409_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-upstream080409-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters140509_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-reuters140509-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ogj080310_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ogj080310-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">
       Chevron Corporation
      </a>
      , in a joint venture with the
      <a href="/wiki/Nigerian_National_Petroleum_Corporation" title="Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation">
       Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation
      </a>
      is commissioning the
      <a href="/wiki/Escravos_GTL" title="Escravos GTL">
       Escravos GTL
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">
       Nigeria
      </a>
      , which uses Sasol technology.
     </p>
     <p>
      On 1 February 2008, an
      <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">
       Airbus A380
      </a>
      flew a three-hour test flight between Britain and France, with one of the A380's four
      <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Trent_900" title="Rolls-Royce Trent 900">
       Rolls-Royce Trent 900
      </a>
      engines using a mix of 60% standard jet
      <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">
       kerosene
      </a>
      and 40% gas to liquids fuel supplied by Shell.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_global_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-flight_global-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The aircraft engine needed no modification to use the GTL fuel, which was designed to be mixed with normal jet fuel. The fuel used was no cleaner in
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      terms than standard fuel but it had local air quality benefits because the GTL portion contains no sulphur.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-reuters-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 12 October 2009, a
      <a href="/wiki/Qatar_Airways" title="Qatar Airways">
       Qatar Airways
      </a>
      Airbus A340-600 conducted the world's first commercial passenger flight using a mixture of kerosene and synthetic GTL fuel in its flight from London's
      <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">
       Gatwick Airport
      </a>
      to Doha.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downstream1_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-downstream1-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Brazilian oil company
      <a href="/wiki/Petrobras" title="Petrobras">
       Petrobras
      </a>
      has ordered two small experimental GTL production facilities intended to be posted at offshore oil fields too distant or deep to justify gas pipelines to onshore GTL plant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flare2Fuel_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Flare2Fuel-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ThinkSmall_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ThinkSmall-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petrobras_pilot_plant_-_CompactGTL_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Petrobras_pilot_plant_-_CompactGTL-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In January 2012 Petrobras'
      <a href="/wiki/Cenpes" title="Cenpes">
       Cenpes
      </a>
      Research and Development Centre approved for commercial deployment the technology supplied by UK-based gas-to-liquids company CompactGTL.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petrobras_puts_gas_flares_out_of_fashion_with_GTL_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Petrobras_puts_gas_flares_out_of_fashion_with_GTL-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Petrobras" title="Petrobras">
       Petrobras
      </a>
      has also completed its assessment of technology supplied by Velocys.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flare2Fuel_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Flare2Fuel-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The STG+ technology is currently operating at pre-commercial scale in Hillsborough, New Jersey at a plant owned by alternative fuels company Primus Green Energy. The plant produces approximately 100,000 gallons of high-quality, drop-in gasoline per year directly from natural gas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Further, the company announced the findings of an independent engineer’s report prepared by E3 Consulting, which found that STG+ system and catalyst performance exceeded expectations during plant operation. The pre-commercial demonstration plant has also achieved 720 hours of continuous operation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On March 2, 2016 INFRA Technology LLC held a ground-breaking ceremony for the construction of its innovative modular 100 BPD GTL plant Mark100 in Wharton, Texas, in close proximity to Houston. The plant will be producing synthetic crude from natural gas. It will serve as a demonstration of INFRA’s proprietary technology which provides solution to one of the industry’s most pressing problem – processing associated gas in an economically feasible manner.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
