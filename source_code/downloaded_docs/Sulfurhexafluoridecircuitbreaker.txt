     <p>
      <b>
       Sulfur hexafluoride circuit breakers
      </b>
      protect electrical
      <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">
       power stations
      </a>
      and distribution systems by interrupting electric currents, when tripped by a
      <a href="/wiki/Protective_relay" title="Protective relay">
       protective relay
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Operating_principle">
       Operating principle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Operating principle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Current interruption in a high-voltage
      <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">
       circuit breaker
      </a>
      is obtained by separating two contacts in a medium, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">
       sulfur hexafluoride
      </a>
      (SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      ), having excellent
      <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">
       dielectric
      </a>
      and arc-quenching properties. After contact separation, current is carried through an arc and is interrupted when this arc is cooled by a gas blast of sufficient intensity.
     </p>
     <p>
      The sulfur hexaflouride gas (SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      ) is an
      <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">
       electronegative
      </a>
      gas and has a strong tendency to absorb free electrons. The contacts of the breaker are opened in a high pressure flow of sulphur hexaflouride gas and an arc is struck between them. The gas captures the conducting free electrons in the arc to form relatively immobile negative ions. This loss of conducting electrons in the arc quickly builds up enough insulation strength to extinguish the arc.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A gas blast applied to the arc must be able to cool it rapidly so that gas temperature between the contacts is reduced from 20,000 K to less than 2000 K in a few hundred microseconds, so that it is able to withstand the
      <a href="/wiki/Transient_recovery_voltage" title="Transient recovery voltage">
       transient recovery voltage
      </a>
      that is applied across the contacts after current interruption. Sulfur hexafluoride is generally used in present high-voltage circuit breakers at rated voltage higher than 52 kV.
     </p>
     <p>
      Into the 1980s, the pressure necessary to blast the arc was generated mostly by gas heating using arc energy. It is now possible to use low-energy
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring-loaded" title="Spring-loaded">
       spring-loaded mechanisms
      </a>
      to drive high-voltage circuit breakers up to 800 kV.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Brief_history">
       Brief history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Brief history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker#High-voltage_circuit_breakers" title="Circuit breaker">
       High-voltage circuit breakers
      </a>
      have changed since they were introduced in the mid-1950s, and several interrupting principles have been developed that have contributed successively to a large reduction of the operating energy. These breakers are available for indoor or outdoor applications, the latter being in the form of breaker poles housed in ceramic insulators mounted on a structure. The first patents on the use of SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      as an interrupting medium were filed in Germany in 1938 by Vitaly Grosse (
      <a href="/wiki/AEG" title="AEG">
       AEG
      </a>
      ) and independently later in the United States in July 1951 by H. J. Lingal, T. E. Browne and A. P. Storm (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_(1886)" title="Westinghouse Electric (1886)">
       Westinghouse
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      The first industrial application of SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      for current interruption dates to 1953. High-voltage 15 kV to 161 kV load switches were developed with a breaking capacity of 600 A. The first high-voltage SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      circuit breaker built in 1956 by Westinghouse, could interrupt 5 kA under 115 kV, but it had six interrupting chambers in series per pole.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1957, the puffer-type technique was introduced for SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      circuit breakers, wherein the relative movement of a piston and a cylinder linked to the moving part is used to generate the pressure rise necessary to blast the arc via a nozzle made of insulating material (Figure 1). In this technique, the pressure rise is obtained mainly by gas compression.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first high-voltage SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      circuit breaker with a high short-circuit current capability was produced by Westinghouse in 1959. This dead tank circuit breaker could interrupt 41.8 kA under 138 kV (10,000 MV·A) and 37.6 kA under 230 kV (15,000 MV·A). This performance was already significant, but the three chambers per pole and the high-pressure source needed for the blast (1.35
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megapascal" title="Megapascal">
       MPa
      </a>
      ) was a constraint that had to be avoided in subsequent developments.
     </p>
     <p>
      The excellent properties of SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      led to the fast extension of this technique in the 1970s and to its use for the development of circuit breakers with high interrupting capability, up to 800 kV.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gas_circuit_breaker_operation_1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="498" data-file-width="913" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Gas_circuit_breaker_operation_1.jpg/220px-Gas_circuit_breaker_operation_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Gas_circuit_breaker_operation_1.jpg/330px-Gas_circuit_breaker_operation_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Gas_circuit_breaker_operation_1.jpg/440px-Gas_circuit_breaker_operation_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gas_circuit_breaker_operation_1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Gas circuit breaker operation. Orange and red areas show high-pressure gas produced by motion of the breaker components.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The achievement around 1983 of the first single-break 245 kV and the corresponding 420 kV to 550 kV and 800 kV, with respectively 2, 3, and 4 chambers per pole, led to the dominance of SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      circuit breakers in the complete range of high voltages.
     </p>
     <p>
      Several characteristics of SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      circuit breakers can explain their success:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Simplicity of the interrupting chamber which does not need an auxiliary breaking chamber
      </li>
      <li>
       Autonomy provided by the puffer technique
      </li>
      <li>
       The possibility to obtain the highest performance, up to 63 kA, with a reduced number of interrupting chambers
      </li>
      <li>
       Short break time of 2 to 2.5 cycles
      </li>
      <li>
       High electrical endurance, allowing at least 25 years of operation without reconditioning
      </li>
      <li>
       Possible compact solutions when used for
       <a href="/wiki/Switchgear#Types" title="Switchgear">
        gas insulated switchgear
       </a>
       or hybrid switchgear
      </li>
      <li>
       Integrated closing resistors or synchronized operations to reduce switching over-voltages
      </li>
      <li>
       Reliability and availability
      </li>
      <li>
       Low noise levels
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The reduction in the number of interrupting chambers per pole has led to a considerable simplification of circuit breakers as well as the number of parts and seals required. As a direct consequence, the reliability of circuit breakers improved, as verified later on by
      <a href="/wiki/International_Council_on_Large_Electric_Systems" title="International Council on Large Electric Systems">
       International Council on Large Electric Systems
      </a>
      (CIGRE) surveys.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_blast_chambers">
       Thermal blast chambers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thermal blast chambers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      New types of SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      breaking chambers, which implement innovative interrupting principles, have been developed over the past 30 years,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2011)">
          when?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      with the objective of reducing the operating energy of the circuit breaker. One aim of this evolution was to further increase the reliability by reducing the dynamic forces in the pole. Developments since 1980 have seen the use of the self-blast technique of interruption for SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      interrupting chambers.
     </p>
     <p>
      These developments have been facilitated by the progress made in digital simulations that were widely used to optimize the geometry of the interrupting chamber and the linkage between the poles and the mechanism.
     </p>
     <p>
      This technique has proved to be very efficient and has been widely applied for high-voltage circuit breakers up to 550 kV. It has allowed the development of new ranges of circuit breakers operated by low energy spring-operated mechanisms.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Disjoncteur-autosoufflage.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="514" data-file-width="850" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Disjoncteur-autosoufflage.jpg/220px-Disjoncteur-autosoufflage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Disjoncteur-autosoufflage.jpg/330px-Disjoncteur-autosoufflage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Disjoncteur-autosoufflage.jpg/440px-Disjoncteur-autosoufflage.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Disjoncteur-autosoufflage.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Auto-blast circuit breaker
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The reduction of operating energy was mainly achieved by lowering energy used for gas compression and by making increased use of arc energy to produce the pressure necessary to quench the arc and obtain current interruption. Low current interruption, up to about 30% of rated short-circuit current, is obtained by a puffer blast. Also includes more of extensive energy available .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Self-blast_chambers">
       Self-blast chambers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Self-blast chambers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Further development in the thermal blast technique was made by the introduction of a valve between the expansion and compression volumes. When interrupting low currents the valve opens under the effect of the overpressure generated in the compression volume. The blow-out of the arc is made as in a puffer circuit breaker thanks to the compression of the gas obtained by the piston action. In the case of high currents interruption, the arc energy produces a high overpressure in the expansion volume, which leads to the closure of the valve and thus isolating the expansion volume from the compression volume. The overpressure necessary for breaking is obtained by the optimal use of the thermal effect and of the nozzle clogging effect produced whenever the cross-section of the arc significantly reduces the exhaust of gas in the nozzle. In order to avoid excessive energy consumption by gas compression, a valve is fitted on the piston in order to limit the overpressure in the compression to a value necessary for the interruption of low short circuit currents.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Disjoncteur-selfblast.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="938" data-file-width="1625" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Disjoncteur-selfblast.svg/220px-Disjoncteur-selfblast.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Disjoncteur-selfblast.svg/330px-Disjoncteur-selfblast.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Disjoncteur-selfblast.svg/440px-Disjoncteur-selfblast.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Disjoncteur-selfblast.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Self-blast circuit breaker chamber (1) closed, (2) interrupting low current, (3) interrupting high current, and (4) open.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      This technique, known as "self-blast" has now been used extensively since 1980 for the development of many types of interrupting chambers. The increased understanding of arc interruption obtained by digital simulations and validation through breaking tests, contribute to a higher reliability of these self-blast circuit breakers. In addition the reduction in operating energy, allowed by the self-blast technique, leads to longer service life.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Double_motion_of_contacts">
       Double motion of contacts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Double motion of contacts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      An important decrease in operating energy can also be obtained by reducing the kinetic energy consumed during the tripping operation. One way is to displace the two arcing contacts in opposite directions so that the arc speed is half that of a conventional layout with a single mobile contact.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Disjoncteur_HT-fig5.svg">
        <img alt="Disjoncteur HT-fig5.svg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1063" data-file-width="1625" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Disjoncteur_HT-fig5.svg/220px-Disjoncteur_HT-fig5.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Disjoncteur_HT-fig5.svg/330px-Disjoncteur_HT-fig5.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Disjoncteur_HT-fig5.svg/440px-Disjoncteur_HT-fig5.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Disjoncteur_HT-fig5.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The thermal and self-blast principles have enabled the use of low-energy spring mechanisms for the operation of high-voltage circuit breakers. They progressively replaced the puffer technique in the 1980s; first in 72.5 kV breakers, and then from 145 kV to 800 kV.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_single_motion_and_double_motion_techniques">
       Comparison of single motion and double motion techniques
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Comparison of single motion and double motion techniques">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The double motion technique halves the tripping speed of the moving part. In principle, the kinetic energy could be quartered if the total moving mass were not increased. However, as the total moving mass
      <i>
       is
      </i>
      increased, the practical reduction in kinetic energy is closer to 60%. The total tripping energy also includes the compression energy, which is almost the same for both techniques. Thus, the reduction of the total tripping energy is lower, about 30%, although the exact value depends on the application and the operating mechanism. Depending on the specific case, either the double motion or the single motion technique can be cheaper. Other considerations, such as rationalization of the circuit breaker range, can also influence the cost.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_blast_chamber_with_arc-assisted_opening">
       Thermal blast chamber with arc-assisted opening
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Thermal blast chamber with arc-assisted opening">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In this interruption principle arc energy is used, on the one hand to generate the blast by thermal expansion and, on the other hand, to accelerate the moving part of the circuit breaker when interrupting high currents. The overpressure produced by the arc energy downstream of the interruption zone is applied on an auxiliary piston linked with the moving part. The resulting force accelerates the moving part, thus increasing the energy available for tripping. With this interrupting principle it is possible, during high-current interruptions, to increase by about 30% the tripping energy delivered by the operating mechanism and to maintain the opening speed independently of the current. It is obviously better suited to circuit breakers with high breaking currents, such as generator circuit breakers.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Generator_circuit_breakers">
       Generator circuit breakers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Generator circuit breakers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Generator circuit breakers (GCB) are connected between a generator and the step-up voltage transformer. They are generally used at the outlet of high-power generators (100 MVA to 1800 MVA) in order to protect them in a reliable, fast and economic manner. Such circuit breakers must be able to allow the passage of high permanent currents under continuous service (6.3 kA to 40 kA), and have a high breaking capacity (63 kA to 275 kA).
     </p>
     <p>
      They belong to the medium voltage range, but the transient recovery voltage withstand capability required by ANSI/IEEE Standard C37.013 is such that the interrupting principles developed for the high-voltage range must be used. A particular embodiment of the thermal blast technique has been developed and applied to generator circuit breakers. The self-blast technique described above is also widely used in SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      generator circuit breakers, in which the contact system is driven by a low-energy, spring-operated mechanism. An example of such a device is shown in the figure below; this circuit breaker is rated for 17.5 kV and 63 kA.
     </p>

     <h2>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Issues_related_to_SF6_circuit_breakers">
       Issues related to SF
       <sub>
        6
       </sub>
       circuit breakers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Issues related to SF6 circuit breakers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The following issues are associated with SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      circuit breakers:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Toxic lower order gases
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      When an arc is formed in SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      gas small quantities of lower order gases are formed. Some of these byproducts are toxic and can cause irritation to eyes and respiratory systems.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Oxygen displacement
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      is heavier than air, so care must be taken when entering low confined spaces due to the risk of oxygen displacement.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Greenhouse gas
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride#Greenhouse_gas" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">
       Sulfur hexafluoride § Greenhouse gas
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      is the most potent greenhouse gas that the
      <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">
       Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change
      </a>
      has evaluated. It has a global warming potential that is 23,900 times worse than CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some governments have implemented systems to monitor and control the emission of SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      to the atmosphere.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_SF6_circuit_breakers">
       Alternatives to SF
       <sub>
        6
       </sub>
       circuit breakers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Alternatives to SF6 circuit breakers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Circuit breakers are usually classed on their insulating medium. The follow types of circuit breakers may be an alternative to SF
      <sub>
       6
      </sub>
      types.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Air blast
      </li>
      <li>
       Oil
      </li>
      <li>
       vacuum
      </li>
      <li>
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </li>
     </ul>
