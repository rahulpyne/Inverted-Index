     <p>
      <b>
       Enthalpy
      </b>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="noexcerpt">
        <a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/En-us-enthalpy.ogg" title="Listen">
         <img alt="Listen" data-file-height="11" data-file-width="11" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/11px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/17px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/22px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
        </a>
        <sup>
         <span class="IPA" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;padding:0 .1em">
          <a href="/wiki/File:En-us-enthalpy.ogg" title="File:En-us-enthalpy.ogg">
           i
          </a>
         </span>
        </sup>
       </span>
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">
           ˈ
          </span>
          <span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">
           ɛ
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
          <span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">
           θ
          </span>
          <span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">
           əl
          </span>
          <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
           p
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">
           i
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      (Symbol:
      <b>
       H
      </b>
      ) is a measurement of
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       energy
      </a>
      in a
      <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system" title="Thermodynamic system">
       thermodynamic system
      </a>
      . It is the thermodynamic quantity equivalent to the total heat content of a system. It is equal to the internal energy of the system plus the product of pressure and volume.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      More technically, it includes the
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">
       internal energy
      </a>
      , which is the energy required to create a system, and the amount of energy required to make room for it by displacing its
      <a href="/wiki/Environment_(systems)" title="Environment (systems)">
       environment
      </a>
      and establishing its volume and pressure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Enthalpy is defined as a
      <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">
       state function
      </a>
      that depends only on the prevailing equilibrium state identified by the variables internal energy, pressure, and volume. It is an
      <a href="/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties" title="Intensive and extensive properties">
       extensive
      </a>
      quantity. The unit of measurement for enthalpy in the
      <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">
       International System of Units
      </a>
      (SI) is the
      <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">
       joule
      </a>
      , but other historical, conventional units are still in use, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">
       British thermal unit
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">
       calorie
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The enthalpy is the preferred expression of system energy changes in many chemical, biological, and physical measurements at constant pressure, because it simplifies the description of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_transfer" title="Energy transfer">
       energy transfer
      </a>
      . At constant pressure, the enthalpy change equals the energy transferred from the environment through heating or work other than expansion work.
     </p>
     <p>
      The total enthalpy,
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      , of a system cannot be measured directly. The same situation exists in classical mechanics: only a change or difference in energy carries physical meaning. Enthalpy itself is a thermodynamic potential, so in order to measure the enthalpy of a system, we must refer to a defined reference point; therefore what we measure is the change in enthalpy, Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      . The Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      is a positive change in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">
       endothermic
      </a>
      reactions, and negative in heat-releasing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">
       exothermic
      </a>
      processes.
     </p>
     <p>
      For processes under constant pressure, Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      is equal to the change in the internal energy of the system, plus the
      <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)#Pressure-volume_work" title="Work (thermodynamics)">
       pressure-volume work
      </a>
      that the system has done on its surroundings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This means that the change in enthalpy under such conditions is the heat absorbed (or released) by the material through a chemical reaction or by external heat transfer. Enthalpies for chemical substances at constant pressure assume
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">
       standard state
      </a>
      : most commonly 1 bar pressure. Standard state does not, strictly speaking, specify a temperature (see
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">
       standard state
      </a>
      ), but expressions for enthalpy generally reference the standard heat of formation at 25 °C.
     </p>
     <p>
      Enthalpy of
      <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">
       ideal gases
      </a>
      and incompressible solids and liquids does not depend on pressure, unlike
      <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">
       entropy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">
       Gibbs energy
      </a>
      . Real materials at common temperatures and pressures usually closely approximate this behavior, which greatly simplifies enthalpy calculation and use in practical designs and analyses.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
       Origins
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       enthalpy
      </i>
      stems from the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       Ancient Greek
      </a>
      verb
      <i>
       enthalpein
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       ἐνθάλπειν
      </span>
      ), which means "to warm in".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It combines the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Greek_language" title="Classical Greek language">
       Classical Greek
      </a>
      prefix
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%90%CE%BD" title="wikt:ἐν">
        ἐν-
       </a>
      </span>
      <i>
       en-
      </i>
      , meaning "to put into", and the verb
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B8%CE%AC%CE%BB%CF%80%CF%89" title="wikt:θάλπω">
        θάλπειν
       </a>
      </span>
      <i>
       thalpein
      </i>
      , meaning "to heat". The word
      <i>
       enthalpy
      </i>
      is often incorrectly attributed to
      <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Paul_%C3%89mile_Clapeyron" title="Benoît Paul Émile Clapeyron">
       Benoît Paul Émile Clapeyron
      </a>
      (1799-1864) and
      <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius" title="Rudolf Clausius">
       Rudolf Clausius
      </a>
      through the 1850 publication of their
      <a href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation" title="Clausius–Clapeyron relation">
       Clausius–Clapeyron relation
      </a>
      . This misconception was popularized by the 1927 publication of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy%E2%80%93entropy_chart" title="Enthalpy–entropy chart">
        The Mollier Steam Tables and Diagrams
       </a>
      </i>
      . However, neither the concept, the word, nor the symbol for enthalpy existed until well after Clapeyron's death.
     </p>
     <p>
      The earliest writings to contain the concept of enthalpy did not appear until 1875,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      when
      <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs" title="Josiah Willard Gibbs">
       Josiah Willard Gibbs
      </a>
      introduced "a heat function for constant pressure". However, Gibbs did not use the word "enthalpy" in his writings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [note 1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The actual word first appears in the scientific literature in a 1909 publication by J. P. Dalton. According to that publication,
      <a href="/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes" title="Heike Kamerlingh Onnes">
       Heike Kamerlingh Onnes
      </a>
      (1853–1926) actually coined the word.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Over the years, scientists used many different symbols to denote enthalpy. In 1922 Alfred W. Porter proposed the symbol "
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      " as a standard,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      thus finalizing the terminology still in use today.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_interpretation">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

     <p>
      The
      <i>
       U
      </i>
      term can be interpreted as the energy required to create the system, and the
      <i>
       pV
      </i>
      term as the energy that would be required to "make room" for the system if the pressure of the environment remained constant. When a system, for example,
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">
       moles
      </a>
      of a gas of
      <a href="/wiki/Volume_(thermodynamics)" title="Volume (thermodynamics)">
       volume
      </a>
      <i>
       V
      </i>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      <i>
       p
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
       temperature
      </a>
      <i>
       T
      </i>
      , is created or brought to its present state from
      <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">
       absolute zero

      <i>

      plus

      </i>

      <i>

      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">

      </a>

     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
       physics

      <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
       chemistry
      </a>
      , experiments are often conducted at constant
      <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">
       atmospheric pressure
      </a>
      , and the pressure-volume work represents an energy exchange with the atmosphere that cannot be accessed or controlled, so that Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      is the expression chosen for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_reaction" title="Heat of reaction">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
       Applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In thermodynamics, one can calculate enthalpy by determining the requirements for creating a system from "nothingness"; the mechanical work required,
      <i>
       pV
      </i>
      , differs based upon the conditions that obtain during the creation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system" title="Thermodynamic system">
       thermodynamic system
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       Energy
      </a>
      must be supplied to remove particles from the surroundings to make space for the creation of the system, assuming that the pressure
      <i>
       p
      </i>
      remains constant; this is the
      <i>
       pV
      </i>
      term. The supplied energy must also provide the change in internal energy,
      <i>
       U
      </i>
      , which includes
      <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">
       activation energies
      </a>
      , ionization energies, mixing energies, vaporization energies, chemical bond energies, and so forth. Together, these constitute the change in the enthalpy
      <i>
       U
      </i>
      +
      <i>
       pV
      </i>
      . For systems at constant pressure, with no external work done other than the
      <i>
       pV
      </i>
      work, the change in enthalpy is the heat received by the system.
     </p>
     <p>
      For a simple system, with a constant number of particles, the difference in enthalpy is the maximum amount of thermal energy derivable from a thermodynamic process in which the pressure is held constant.

         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_of_reaction">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">

      <a href="/wiki/Standard_enthalpy_of_reaction" title="Standard enthalpy of reaction">

     <p>
      The total enthalpy of a system cannot be measured directly, the
      <i>
       enthalpy change
      </i>
      of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_(thermodynamics)" title="System (thermodynamics)">

     </p>

             </mo>

           </annotation>

         </math>
        </span>

     <p>
      where
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Δ
       <i>
        H
       </i>

      </dd>
      <dd>

       </i>

       </sub>

      </dd>
      <dd>
       <i>

       </i>

      For an
      <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">
       exothermic reaction
      </a>
      at constant
      <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
       pressure
      </a>
      , the system's change in enthalpy equals the energy released in the reaction, including the energy retained in the system and lost through expansion against its surroundings. In a similar manner, for an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">
       endothermic
      </a>
      reaction, the system's change in enthalpy is equal to the energy
      <i>
       absorbed
      </i>
      in the reaction, including the energy
      <i>
       lost by
      </i>
      the system and
      <i>
       gained
      </i>
      from compression from its surroundings. A relatively easy way to determine whether or not a reaction is exothermic or endothermic is to determine the sign of Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      . If Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      is positive, the reaction is endothermic, that is heat is absorbed by the system due to the products of the reaction having a greater enthalpy than the reactants. On the other hand, if Δ
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      is negative, the reaction is exothermic, that is the overall decrease in enthalpy is achieved by the generation of heat.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Enthalpy_changes">
       Enthalpy changes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Enthalpy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Enthalpy changes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      An enthalpy change describes the change in enthalpy observed in the constituents of a thermodynamic system when undergoing a transformation or chemical reaction. It is the difference between the enthalpy after the process has completed, i.e. the enthalpy of the
      <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">
       products
      </a>
      , and the initial enthalpy of the system, i.e. the reactants. These processes are reversible
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="(March 2015)">
          why?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and the enthalpy for the reverse process is the negative value of the forward change.
     </p>
     <p>
      A common standard enthalpy change is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_formation" title="Enthalpy of formation">
       enthalpy of formation
      </a>
      , which has been determined for a large number of substances. Enthalpy changes are routinely measured and compiled in chemical and physical reference works, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/CRC_Handbook_of_Chemistry_and_Physics" title="CRC Handbook of Chemistry and Physics">
       CRC Handbook of Chemistry and Physics
      </a>
      . The following is a selection of enthalpy changes commonly recognized in thermodynamics.
     </p>
     <p>
      When used in these recognized terms the qualifier
      <i>
       change
      </i>
      is usually dropped and the property is simply termed
      <i>
       enthalpy of 'process'
      </i>
      . Since these properties are often used as reference values it is very common to quote them for a standardized set of environmental parameters, or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_conditions" title="Standard conditions">
       standard conditions
      </a>
      , including:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       of 25 °C or 298 K,
      </li>
      <li>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
        pressure
       </a>
       of one atmosphere (1 atm or 101.325 kPa),
      </li>
      <li>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">
        concentration
       </a>
       of 1.0 M when the element or compound is present in solution,
      </li>
      <li>
       Elements or compounds in their normal physical states, i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">
        standard state
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      For such standardized values the name of the enthalpy is commonly prefixed with the term
      <i>
       standard
      </i>
      , e.g.
      <i>
       standard enthalpy of formation
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Chemical properties:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_reaction" title="Enthalpy of reaction">
        Enthalpy of reaction
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of substance reacts completely.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_formation" title="Enthalpy of formation">
        Enthalpy of formation
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of a compound is formed from its elementary antecedents.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_combustion" title="Enthalpy of combustion">
        Enthalpy of combustion
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of a substance burns completely with oxygen.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_hydrogenation" title="Enthalpy of hydrogenation">
        Enthalpy of hydrogenation
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of an unsaturated compound reacts completely with an excess of hydrogen to form a saturated compound.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_atomization" title="Enthalpy of atomization">
        Enthalpy of atomization
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change required to atomize one mole of compound completely.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_neutralization" title="Enthalpy of neutralization">
        Enthalpy of neutralization
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of water is formed when an acid and a base react.
      </li>
      <li>
       Standard
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_solution" title="Enthalpy of solution">
        Enthalpy of solution
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change observed in a constituent of a thermodynamic system when one mole of a solute is dissolved completely in an excess of solvent, so that the solution is at infinite dilution.
      </li>
      <li>
       Standard enthalpy of
       <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">
        Denaturation (biochemistry)
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change required to denature one mole of compound.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Hydration_energy" title="Hydration energy">
        Enthalpy of hydration
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change observed when one mole of gaseous ions are completely dissolved in water forming one mole of aqueous ions.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Physical properties:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_fusion" title="Enthalpy of fusion">
        Enthalpy of fusion
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change required to completely change the state of one mole of substance between solid and liquid states.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_vaporization" title="Enthalpy of vaporization">
        Enthalpy of vaporization
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change required to completely change the state of one mole of substance between liquid and gaseous states.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_sublimation" title="Enthalpy of sublimation">
        Enthalpy of sublimation
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change required to completely change the state of one mole of substance between solid and gaseous states.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lattice_enthalpy" title="Lattice enthalpy">
        Lattice enthalpy
       </a>
       , defined as the energy required to separate one mole of an ionic compound into separated gaseous ions to an infinite distance apart (meaning no force of attraction).
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_mixing" title="Enthalpy of mixing">
        Enthalpy of mixing
       </a>
       , defined as the enthalpy change upon mixing of two (non-reacting) chemical substances.
      </li>
     </ul>
