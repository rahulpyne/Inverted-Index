     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Clinton Foundation
      </b>
      (founded in 1997 as the
      <b>
       William J. Clinton Foundation
      </b>
      ),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and called beginning in 2013 the
      <b>
       Bill, Hillary &amp; Chelsea Clinton Foundation
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_corporation" title="Nonprofit corporation">
       nonprofit corporation
      </a>
      under section
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/501(c)(3)" title="501(c)(3)">
       501(c)(3)
      </a>
      of the U.S. tax code. It was established by former President of the United States
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">
       Bill Clinton
      </a>
      with the stated mission to "strengthen the capacity of people in the United States and throughout the world to meet the challenges of global interdependence."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its offices are located in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">
       Little Rock, Arkansas
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Through 2016 the foundation had raised an estimated $2Â billion from U.S. corporations, foreign governments and corporations, political donors, and various other groups and individuals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-2b_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-2b-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The acceptance of funds from wealthy donors has been a source of controversy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-2b_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-2b-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hunt-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The foundation "has won accolades from philanthropy experts and has drawn bipartisan support".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-2b_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-2b-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Charitable grants are not a major focus of the Clinton Foundation, which instead keeps most of its money in house and hires staff to carry out its own humanitarian programs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pf-org_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pf-org-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In September 2016, the charity watchdog group
      <a href="/wiki/Charity_Navigator" title="Charity Navigator">
       Charity Navigator
      </a>
      gave it its highest possible rating, four out of four stars, after its customary review of the Foundation's financial records and tax statements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A different charity monitor,
      <a href="/wiki/CharityWatch" title="CharityWatch">
       CharityWatch
      </a>
      , says that 88% of the foundation's money goes toward its charitable mission and gave the foundation an A rating for 2016. In 2015, based on revenue of $223 million and an expense ratio of 12% the foundation spent in excess of $26 million to complete its mission.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This foundation is a public organization to which anyone may donate and is distinct from the
      <b>
       Clinton Family Foundation
      </b>
      , a private organization for personal Clinton family philanthropy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJCFF_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSJCFF-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCAA_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCAA-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the Foundation's website, neither Bill Clinton, nor his daughter Chelsea Clinton (both board members), draw any salary or receive any income from the Foundation. When Hillary Clinton was a board member she received no income from the Foundation either.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The origins of the foundation go back to 1997, when then-president
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">
       Bill Clinton
      </a>
      was focused mostly on
      <a href="/wiki/Fundraising" title="Fundraising">
       fundraising
      </a>
      for the future
      <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Presidential_Center" title="Clinton Presidential Center">
       Clinton Presidential Center
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">
       Little Rock, Arkansas
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-story_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-story-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bill_Clinton_(2015).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1999" data-file-width="1499" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Bill_Clinton_%282015%29.jpg/220px-Bill_Clinton_%282015%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Bill_Clinton_%282015%29.jpg/330px-Bill_Clinton_%282015%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Bill_Clinton_%282015%29.jpg/440px-Bill_Clinton_%282015%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bill_Clinton_(2015).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Former
        <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">
         U.S. President
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">
         Bill Clinton
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Bill founded the William J. Clinton Foundation in 2001 following the completion of his presidency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Longtime Clinton advisor,
      <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lindsey" title="Bruce Lindsey">
       Bruce Lindsey
      </a>
      , became the CEO in 2004.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pr-ceo_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pr-ceo-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later, Lindsey moved from being CEO to being chair, largely for health reasons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other Clinton hands who played an important early role included
      <a href="/wiki/Doug_Band" title="Doug Band">
       Doug Band
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Clark-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ira_Magaziner" title="Ira Magaziner">
       Ira Magaziner
      </a>
      . Additional Clinton associates who have had senior positions at the foundation include
      <a href="/wiki/John_Podesta" title="John Podesta">
       John Podesta
      </a>
      and Laura Graham.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Most of the foundation's successes came from Bill's worldwide fame and his ability to bring together corporate executives, celebrities, and government officials.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly, the foundation areas of involvement have often corresponded to whatever Bill suddenly felt an interest in.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Preceding
      <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">
       Barack Obama
      </a>
      's 2009 nomination of Hillary Clinton as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_State" title="U.S. Secretary of State">
       U.S. Secretary of State
      </a>
      , Bill Clinton agreed to accept a number of conditions and restrictions regarding his ongoing activities and fundraising efforts for the Clinton Presidential Center and the Clinton Global Initiative.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Accordingly, a list of donors was released in December 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eclectic_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eclectic-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By 2011,
      <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Clinton" title="Chelsea Clinton">
       Chelsea Clinton
      </a>
      was taking a dominant role in the foundation and had a seat on its board.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      To raise money for the Foundation, she gave paid speeches, such as her $65,000 2014 address at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Missouri%E2%80%93Kansas_City" title="University of MissouriâKansas City">
       University of Missouri
      </a>
      in Kansas City for the opening of the Starr Women's Hall of Fame.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2013, Hillary Clinton joined the foundation following her
      <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton%27s_tenure_as_Secretary_of_State" title="Hillary Clinton's tenure as Secretary of State">
       tenure as Secretary of State
      </a>
      . She planned to focus her work on issues regarding women and children,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      as well as economic development.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Accordingly, at that point, it was renamed the "Bill, Hillary &amp; Chelsea Clinton Foundation".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Extra attention was paid to the foundation due to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2016" title="United States presidential election, 2016">
       2016 United States presidential election
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In July 2013, Eric Braverman was named CEO of the foundation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pr-ceo_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-pr-ceo-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is a friend and former colleague of Chelsea Clinton from
      <a href="/wiki/McKinsey_%26_Company" title="McKinsey &amp; Company">
       McKinsey &amp; Company
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the same time, Chelsea Clinton was named vice chair of the foundation's board.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pr-ceo_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-pr-ceo-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The foundation was also in the midst of a move to two floors of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-Life_Building" title="Time-Life Building">
       Time-Life Building
      </a>
      in Midtown Manhattan.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Chelsea Clinton moved the organization to an outside review, conducted by the firm of
      <a href="/wiki/Simpson_Thacher_%26_Bartlett" title="Simpson Thacher &amp; Bartlett">
       Simpson Thacher &amp; Bartlett
      </a>
      . Its conclusions were made public in mid-2013.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The main focus was to determine how the foundation could achieve firm financial footing that was not dependent upon the former president's fundraising abilities, how it could operate more like a permanent entity rather than a start-up organization, and thus how it could survive and prosper beyond Bill Clinton's lifetime.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Cheng" title="Dennis Cheng">
       Dennis Cheng
      </a>
      , a former Hillary Clinton campaign official and State Department deputy chief, was named to oversee a $250 million endowment drive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The review also found the management and structure of the foundation needed improvements, including an increase in the size of its board of directors that would have a more direct involvement in planning and budget activities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additionally, the review said that all employees needed to understand the foundation's conflict of interest policies and that expense reports needed a more formal review process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pol-aug13_14-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-pol-aug13-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In January 2015, Braverman announced his resignation.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Politico" title="Politico">
        Politico
       </a>
      </i>
      attributed the move to being "partly from a power struggle inside the foundation between and among the coterie of Clinton loyalists who have surrounded the former president for decades and who helped start and run the foundation."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was succeeded at first in an acting capacity by former deputy assistant secretary, Maura Pally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On February 18, 2015,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">
        The Washington Post
       </a>
      </i>
      reported that, "the foundation has won accolades from philanthropy experts and has drawn bipartisan support, with members of the George W. Bush administration often participating in its programs."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-2b_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-2b-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 2015, former

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Clinton_Health_Access_Initiative_.28CHAI.29">
       Clinton Health Access Initiative (CHAI)
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As of January 1, 2010, the Clinton HIV/AIDS Initiative, an initiative of the Clinton Foundation, became a separate nonprofit organization called the Clinton Health Access Initiative (CHAI).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Organizations such as the Clinton Foundation continue to supply anti-malarial drugs to Africa and other affected areas; according to director
      <a href="/wiki/Inder_Singh_(philanthropist)" title="Inder Singh (philanthropist)">
       Inder Singh
      </a>
      , in 2011 more than 12 million individuals will be supplied with subsidized anti-malarial drugs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In May 2007, CHAI and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNITAID" title="UNITAID">
       UNITAID
      </a>
      announced agreements that help middle-income and low-income countries save money on second-line drugs. The partnership also reduced the price of a once-daily first-line treatment to less than $1 per day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Clinton Foundation HIV/AIDS Initiative's work on the ground has been subject of some criticism. The
      <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">
       American Enterprise Institute
      </a>
      , a conservative think tank, wrote that governments and organizations in Africa and Asia that partnered with the Foundation expressed caution and alarm at the Foundation's focus on treating a large number of patients with less regard for the importance of adherence, follow-up and quality of care.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      CHAI was spun off into a separate organization in 2010; Ira Magaziner became its CEO (he had been a key figure in the
      <a href="/wiki/Clinton_health_care_plan_of_1993" title="Clinton health care plan of 1993">
       Clinton health care plan of 1993
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Chelsea Clinton joined its board in 2011, as did
      <a href="/wiki/Tachi_Yamada" title="Tachi Yamada">
       Tachi Yamada
      </a>
      , former President of the Global Health Program at the
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_%26_Melinda_Gates_Foundation" title="Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation">
       Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aug13_13-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aug13-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Clinton_Climate_Initiative_.28CCI.29">
       Clinton Climate Initiative (CCI)
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      President Clinton started a program to fight climate change, the Clinton Foundation's Climate Initiative (CCI) in August 2006.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        [48]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On August 1, 2006, the Foundation entered into a partnership with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large_Cities_Climate_Leadership_Group" title="Large Cities Climate Leadership Group">
       Large Cities Climate Leadership Group
      </a>
      , agreeing to provide resources to allow the participating cities to enter into an energy-saving product purchasing consortium and to provide technical and communications support.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        [49]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In May 2007, CCI announced its first project which will help some large cities cut greenhouse gas emissions by facilitating retrofitting of existing buildings. Five large banks committed $1 billion each to help cities and building owners make energy-saving improvements aimed at lowering energy use and energy costs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        [50]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the 2007 Clinton Global Initiative, President Clinton announced the
      <a href="/wiki/1Sky" title="1Sky">
       1Sky
      </a>
      campaign to accelerate bold federal policy on global warming.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        [51]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 1Sky campaign supports at least an 80% reduction in climate pollution levels by 2050.
     </p>
     <p>
      On May 19, 2009, CCI announced the global Climate Positive Development Program where it will work with the
      <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">
       U.S. Green Building Council
      </a>
      to promote "climate positive" city growth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        [52]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

        [54]
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Clinton_Health_Matters_Initiative_.28CHMI.29">
       Clinton Health Matters Initiative (CHMI)
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In November 2012, Bill Clinton announced the launch of the Clinton Health Matters Initiative (CHMI). CHMI is a national initiative, building on the Clinton Foundation's work on global health and childhood obesity, that works to improve the health and well-being of people across the United States by activating individuals, communities, and organizations to make meaningful contributions to the health of others. CHMI holds an annual Health Matters conference every January in the
      <a href="/wiki/Coachella_Valley" title="Coachella Valley">
       Coachella Valley
      </a>
      .

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transparency">
       Transparency
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Around 2007, the Clinton Foundation was criticized for a lack of transparency. Although U.S. law did not require nonprofit charities â including presidential foundations â to disclose the identities of their contributors, critics said that the names of donors should be disclosed because Hillary was
      <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton_presidential_campaign,_2008" title="Hillary Clinton presidential campaign, 2008">
       running to be the Democratic nominee for President of the United States
      </a>
      . Commentator
      <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Yglesias" title="Matthew Yglesias">
       Matthew Yglesias
      </a>
      wrote in a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">
        Los Angeles Times
       </a>
      </i>
      op-ed that the Clintons should make public the names of foundation donors to avoid any appearance of impropriety.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        [68]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A lengthy donors list was then released by the Foundation in December 2008,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eclectic_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eclectic-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which included several politically sensitive donors, such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Kingdom of Saudi Arabia">
       Kingdom of Saudi Arabia
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackwater_Worldwide" title="Blackwater Worldwide">
       Blackwater Worldwide
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ny_69-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ny-69">
        [69]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Foundation stated that the disclosures would ensure that "not even the appearance of a conflict of interest" would exist once Hillary Clinton was Secretary of State.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ny_69-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ny-69">
        [69]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The ethics agreement between the State Department and the Clinton Foundation that was put into force at the beginning of the Secretary of State Clinton's tenure came under scrutiny from the news media during February 2015. A
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">
        Wall Street Journal
       </a>
      </i>
      report found that the Clinton Foundation had resumed accepting donations from foreign governments once Secretary Clinton's tenure had ended.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        [70]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Contributions from foreign donors who are prohibited by law from contributing to political candidates in the U.S. constitute a major portion of the foundation's income. The foundation's chief communications officer Craig Minassian explained that it is a "false choice to suggest that people who may be interested in supporting political causes wouldnât also support philanthropic work."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-2b_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-2b-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">
        Washington Post
       </a>
      </i>
      inquiry into donations by foreign governments to the Clinton Foundation during the secretary's tenure found six cases where such governments continued making donations at the same level they had before Clinton became secretary, which was permissible under the agreement, but also one instance of a new donation, $500,000 from Algeria for earthquake relief in Haiti, that was outside the bounds of the continuation provision and should have received a special ethics review, but did not.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-6-1_71-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-6-1-71">
        [71]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Foundation officials said that if the former secretary decided to run for president in 2016, they would again consider what steps to take in reference to foreign donations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-6-1_71-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-6-1-71">
        [71]
       </a>
      </sup>
      But in general, they stressed that, "As with other global charities, we rely on the support of individuals, organizations, corporations and governments who have the shared goal of addressing critical global challenges in a meaningful way. When anyone contributes to the Clinton Foundation, it goes towards foundation programs that help save lives."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo-6-1_71-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-wapo-6-1-71">
        [71]
       </a>
      </sup>
      State Department spokesperson
      <a href="/wiki/Jen_Psaki" title="Jen Psaki">
       Jen Psaki
      </a>
      attested that the foundation's commitment to the ethics agreement in question "has been over and above the letter of the law".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc-2-26_72-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-msnbc-2-26-72">
        [72]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In August 2016, with less than 90 days before the upcoming presidential election, the Clinton Foundation announced that it will stop accepting foreign donations if Clinton is elected.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        [73]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 2015,
      <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">
       Reuters
      </a>
      reported that the Clinton Foundation had broken its promise to publish all of its donors, as well as its promise to let the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Department" title="State Department">
       State Department
      </a>
      review all of its donations from foreign governments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        [74]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In April 2015, the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">
        New York Times
       </a>
      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/Uranium_One" title="Uranium One">
       Uranium One
      </a>
      whose chairman had donated to the Clinton Foundation, and that Clinton had broken her promise to publicly identify such donations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
       <a href="#cite_note-75">
        [75]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The State Department "was one of nine government agencies, not to mention independent federal and state nuclear regulators, that had to sign off on the deal."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QiuUranium_76-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-QiuUranium-76">
        [76]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/FactCheck.org" title="FactCheck.org">
       FactCheck.org
      </a>
      notes that there is "no evidence" that the donations influenced Clintonâs official actions or that she was involved in the State Department's decision to approve the deal,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KielyUranium_77-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KielyUranium-77">
        [77]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PolitiFact" title="PolitiFact">
       PolitiFact
      </a>
      concluded that any "suggestion of a quid pro quo is unsubstantiated."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QiuUranium_76-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-QiuUranium-76">
        [76]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After her January 2009 appointment as
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">

      </a>
      , Hillary Clinton proposed hiring long-time Clinton friend and confidant
      <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Blumenthal" title="Sidney Blumenthal">

      <a href="/wiki/Rahm_Emanuel" title="Rahm Emanuel">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_78-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Baker-78">

      <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">

      , Blumenthal prepared, from public and other sources, about 25 memos which he sent as
      <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton_email_controversy" title="Hillary Clinton email controversy">
       emails to Clinton
      </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Private_philanthropy">
       Private philanthropy

Due to their similar names, the public foundation has sometimes been confused with the Clinton Family Foundation, which is reserved for the Clintons' private philanthropy. The two foundations have sometimes been conflated by news sources.

       <a href="#cite_note-WSJCFF-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCAA_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCAA-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Headquartered in Chappaqua, New York, it received nearly all of the approximately $14 million the Clintons gave to charity from 2007â13.

       <a href="#cite_note-WSJCFF-10">
