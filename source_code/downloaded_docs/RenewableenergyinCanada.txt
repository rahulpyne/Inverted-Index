     <p>
      Canada generates a significant majority of its electricity from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_dam" title="Hydroelectric dam">
       hydroelectric dams
      </a>
      (59% in 2006).
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Canada" title="Wind power in Canada">
       Wind power
      </a>
      is growing quickly with Canada in 2011 being the 6th largest producer of wind power in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Canada has built a number of photovoltaic power plants, mainly in Ontario, with one in Sarnia being the largest in the world at the time of construction. A 15 megawatt tidal plant sits at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annapolis_Royal,_Nova_Scotia" title="Annapolis Royal, Nova Scotia">
       Annapolis, Nova Scotia
      </a>
      , and uses the daily tides of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Fundy" title="Bay of Fundy">
       Bay of Fundy
      </a>
      . Politicians have expressed interest in increasing the percentage of Canada's electricity generated by renewable methods. Ontario has created a subsidy to assist wind and solar power producers.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity">
       Hydroelectricity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity_in_Canada" title="Hydroelectricity in Canada">
       Hydroelectricity in Canada
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">
       Manitoba
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">
       British Columbia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">
       Newfoundland and Labrador
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">
       Yukon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
       Quebec
      </a>
      produce over 90% of their power from hydroelectricity. Quebec generates half of Canada's hydroelectric power.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">
       Solar power
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solar power">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Canada" title="Solar power in Canada">
       Solar power in Canada
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Canada has plentiful
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
       solar energy
      </a>
      resources, with the most extensive resources being found in southern
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
       Quebec
      </a>
      and the Prairies. The territories have a smaller potential, and less direct sunlight, because of their higher latitude.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sol_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sol-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      With 1210 MWp of installed photovoltaics in 2013, Canada ranked 15th among the world's countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ontario has a program of moving away from coal and promoting renewable resources which has led to a number of industrial-scale photovoltaic plants being built. Located in Sarnia, Ontario, the 97 megawatt
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Sarnia_Photovoltaic_Power_Plant" title="Sarnia Photovoltaic Power Plant">
       Sarnia Photovoltaic Power Plant
      </a>
      was briefly the largest solar farm in the world (in October 2010) and can power more than 12,000 homes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other plants include the 23.4 MW
      <a href="/wiki/Arnprior_Solar_Generating_Station" title="Arnprior Solar Generating Station">
       Arnprior Solar Generating Station
      </a>
      and a 68 MW solar farm is in Sault Ste. Marie.
     </p>
     <p>
      Until recently, the main applications of solar energy technologies in Canada have been for non-electric
      <a href="/wiki/Active_solar" title="Active solar">
       active solar
      </a>
      system applications for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_heating" title="Space heating">
       space heating
      </a>
      , water heating and drying crops and lumber. In 2001, there were more than 12,000 residential
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">
       solar water heating
      </a>
      systems and 300 commercial/ industrial solar hot water systems in use. These systems presently comprise a small fraction of Canada’s energy use, but some government studies suggest they could make up as much as five per cent of the country’s energy needs by the year 2025.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sol_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sol-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Canada has many regions that are sparsely populated and difficult to access.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">
       Photovoltaic
      </a>
      (PV) cells are increasingly used as standalone units, mostly as off-grid distributed electricity generation to power remote homes, telecommunications equipment, oil and pipeline monitoring stations and navigational devices. The Canadian PV market has grown quickly and Canadian companies make solar modules, controls, specialized water pumps, high efficiency refrigerators and solar lighting systems.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sol_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sol-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the most important uses for PV cells is in northern communities, many of which depend on high-cost
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">
       diesel fuel
      </a>
      to generate electricity. Since the 1970s, the federal government and industry has encouraged the development of solar technologies for these communities. Some of these efforts have focused on the use of hybrid systems that provide power 24 hours a day, using solar power when sunlight is available, in combination with another energy source.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sol_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-sol-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Canada" title="Wind power in Canada">
       Wind power in Canada
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      At the end of 2011, wind power generating capacity in Canada was 5,265
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatts" title="Megawatts">
       megawatts
      </a>
      (MW), capable of generating 2.3% of
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity_sector_in_Canada" title="Electricity sector in Canada">
       Canada's electricity demand
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ontario, Quebec and Alberta each had more than 1000 MW of nameplate capacity. All provinces and territories, except Nunavut, had some commercial wind power in 2012.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ontario">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario#Energy" title="Ontario">
       Ontario's
       <i>
        Green Energy and Green Economy Act, 2009
       </i>
       (GEGEA)
      </a>
      , now in force and effect, takes a two-pronged approach to creating a
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      economy. The first is to bring more
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">
       renewable energy sources
      </a>
      to the province and the second is the creation of more
      <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">
       energy efficiency
      </a>
      measures to help
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">
       conserve energy
      </a>
      . The bill also appoints a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_Energy_Facilitator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Renewable Energy Facilitator (page does not exist)">
       Renewable Energy Facilitator
      </a>
      to provide "
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=One-window&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="One-window (page does not exist)">
       one-window
      </a>
      " assistance and support to project developers in order to facilitate project approvals. The approvals process for transmission projects are also
      <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/streamline" title="wikt:streamline">
       streamlined
      </a>
      and for the first time in Ontario, the bill enacts standards for renewable energy projects. Homeowners now have access to incentives to develop small-scale renewables such as low- or no-interest
      <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">
       loans
      </a>
      to finance the capital cost of renewable energy generating facilities like solar panels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
